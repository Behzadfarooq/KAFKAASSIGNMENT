Beilstein test

The Beilstein test is a simple chemical test used in chemistry as a qualitative test for halides. It was developed by Friedrich Konrad Beilstein.

A copper wire is cleaned and heated in a Bunsen burner flame to form a coating of copper(II) oxide. It is then dipped in the sample to be tested and once again heated in a flame. A positive test is indicated by a green flame caused by the formation of a copper halide.

This test is little used nowadays; one reason why it is not popular is that it is possible to generate the highly toxic chloro-dioxins if the test material is a polychloroarene. An alternative wet test for halide is to use the sodium fusion test and when done carefully with a small bit of sodium they are safe. But a number of universities have banned this test from undergraduate labs because of the large number of fires which have occurred as a result of people using too much sodium or not being careful.
