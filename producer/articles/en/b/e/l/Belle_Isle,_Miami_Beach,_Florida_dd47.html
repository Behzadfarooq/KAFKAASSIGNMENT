Belle Isle, Miami Beach, Florida

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Main article: Venetian Islands, Miami Beach, Florida
Post card depicting the eastern bridge of the Venetian Causeway to Belle Isle from Miami Beach. (Date unknown.) Florida Photographic Collection

Belle Isle is the easternmost of the Venetian Islands, a chain of artificial islands in Biscayne Bay near Miami Beach, Florida. It is home to apartment buildings, a portion of the Venetian Causeway, a City of Miami Beach park, and the Standard Hotel.[1]

[edit] Renovations

Belle Isle Park, in the center of Belle Isle, is currently undergoing a renovation project[2] that will include sidewalks, lighting and landscape improvements, picnic areas, and a dog park.[3] A streetscape improvement project for the Venetian bridges is planned for 2008 and 2009.[4]

[edit] Announcement

In June of 2006, after construction had already begun, the City of Miami Beach sent a post card to residents of Belle Isle, announcing that construction would begin in the area in May of 2006. The announcement stated that the construction is "anticipated to be completed between 18-20 months."[5] Residents were also informed that:

  • Parking will be made available to all residents whose spaces have been displaced due to the construction.
  • Keeping pets in mind, a section of greenspace will remain open during construction within the park.
  • For residents' safety, a fence will be placed around the construction site.
  • Notifications of planned disruptions will be provided to residents in a timely manner.

[edit] Parking

Parking was free on Island Avenue on Belle Isle until 2005, when residents petitioned the City of Miami Beach to create a new residential parking zone in order to ensure parking for residents after the opening of the Standard Hotel. Island Avenue is now a residential parking zone, and you must buy a permit for Zone 14.[6] The Miami Beach parking department office at 11th and Washington is temporarily closed during renovation of the city hall building,[7] so the department is handling many permits by mail. Belle Isle residents can purchase a one-year residential decal or one visitor permit hang tag per household for $46.00 or a six-month pass for $23.00. Belle Isle residents can also purchase up to five scratch-off one-day visitor permits per month for $1.07 each.

During park renovations, the city will need to close about 20 parking spaces at a time to make way for roadway construction. They will do the road work in sections, using a temporary 20-space parking lot toward the East end of the park to compensate for the closed parking spaces.[8]

[edit] Community

The Belle Isle Residents Association was established in December, 2004. Board meets approximately 6 times per year. General Membership Meetings are called as needed at a minimum of once a year.[9] The association's mission is to "enhance the quality of life for the residents of Belle Isle", specifically regarding "security, traffic control, ease of parking, noise control, park beautification, cleanliness, open spaces, planning, development, licensing, permitting, and all issues that may affect the residents and their quality of life on Belle Isle".[10]

[edit] History

Arial photo of Belle Isle with Fisher's Flamingo Hotel visible in the background. (Date unknown.) Florida Photographic Collection
The Joseph H. Adams estate on the southeast corner of Belle Isle. The Venetian Causeway, visible, was established the year the photo was taken. Florida Photographic Collection 1927

Belle Isle was originally called "Bull Isle", and was later renamed[11]. Unlike the other Venetian Islands, Belle Isle is not completely artificial. Like the Sunset Islands, Belle Isle was originally a rough mangrove hammock island sitting in north Biscayne Bay near the Miami barrier islands, before the use of the term "Miami Beach".

Before fruit farmer John S. Collins partnered with the wealthy investor Carl G. Fisher to build the Collins Bridge from Miami in 1913, the new luxury properties under development in Miami Beach and Collins' large avocado orchards were inaccessible except by ferry boat. When Collins dug the Collins Canal, work crews deposited dredged sand around the rough island at the mouth of the canal, increasing its land mass and defining its shape. The "improved" island, now cleared of mangroves and platted into small parcels of land[12] for single-family homes, extended into Biscayne Bay and allowed Collins and Fisher to build a relatively short wooden bridge across the bay by running the road over Belle Isle. The gateway to Miami Beach earned a reputation as an enclave for fashionable millionaires, such as Joseph H. Adams, whose sprawling estate occupied the southeast corner of the island.

During the Florida land boom of the 1920's, Belle Isle and Fisher's nearby Flamingo Hotel were the site of the famous Biscayne Bay Speed Boat Regattas. Fisher had successfully promoted automobile races in Indianapolis, and he used his skills to stage gasoline-powered speed boat races in the smooth waters of Biscayne Bay just south of Belle Isle as a spectacle to attract the wealthy and sophisticated tourists that he was seeking as a target audience for his new exotic vacation destination.

In 1942, the University of Miami turned a boat house on the Joseph H. Adams estate into the first "Marine Lab" for the Rosenstiel School of Marine and Atmospheric Science.[13] Belle Isle was also the site of the All Souls Episcopal Church as late as 1947.[14]
