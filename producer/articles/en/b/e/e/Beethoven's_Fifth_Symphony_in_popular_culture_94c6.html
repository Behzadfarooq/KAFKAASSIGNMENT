Beethoven's Fifth Symphony in popular culture

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Ludwig van Beethoven's Symphony No. 5 in C Minor is one of the most famous of all works of classical music. Not surprisingly, it has appeared frequently in popular culture. This article documents such appearances, which take the form of musical adaptations, parodies, quotations, and other uses.

For information about the symphony itself, see Symphony No. 5 (Beethoven).

[edit] Musical adaptations

Editors are requested to add their contributions in chronological order, providing links to the original artist.

[edit] 1920s

  • The first movement is parodied in Beethoven's Binge, the first of Two Cod-Pieces by Peter Warlock. (The second Cod-Piece is based on the Symphony in D minor by César Franck.)

[edit] 1970s

  • The 1970s rock band Electric Light Orchestra included the opening bars of the symphony as an introduction to their cover of Chuck Berry's Roll Over Beethoven.
  • A disco version, called "A Fifth of Beethoven," was made by Walter Murphy during the disco era of the 1970s. This version was featured in the Saturday Night Fever soundtrack.
  • A short passage appeared in the 1970s disco medley "Hooked on Classics 1".

[edit] 1980s

  • A heavy metal rendition was created by guitarist Yngwie Malmsteen.
  • An a cappella rendition with lyrics and a surrealist music video was performed by a major metropolitan choral group and shown in rotation on cable TV

[edit] 1990s

  • 1999 : Danney Alkana recorded a rock version of the symphony on his album Rock the Bach.
  • Microsoft included Beethoven's 5th Symphony as one of several preinstalled MIDI files in Windows 95, 98, and 2000.
  • Portions of the first movement with a hip hop a drum loop underneath them serve as the basis for a humorous interlude on Prince Paul and The Automator's first edition of Handsome Boy Modeling School (1999).
  • A sample of the opening four-note motif was featured in the chorus of Irish band A House's 1992 single Endless Art.

[edit] 2000s

  • The first movement also appeared in Disney's Fantasia 2000 in the sequence that opens the film. As with the much earlier Disney Beethoven Sixth in the original Fantasia, the music was severely cut. The animation depicts paper butterflies being chased by a storm of darker, evil paper bats.
  • The 1970s hit "A Fifth of Beethoven" (see above) was used in turn as the backing track for some other songs, such as "Enjoy Yourself" by rapper A+ (1999), and "When I Get You Alone" by Californian R&B artist Thicke, released in 2002, with songwriting credits listed as "Walter Murphy / Robin Thicke".

[edit] Other appearances

Editors are requested to add their contributions in chronological order, providing links to the original context.

[edit] 1940s

  • During World War II the BBC adopted the first four notes as the call sign of the European Service principally because they represent the letter V for Victory in Morse code. The irony that they were composed by a German was not lost on many of the audience or for the more musically educated that it was "Fate knocking on the door" of the Third Reich.[1].

[edit] 1970s

  • In Douglas Adams' 1979 novel "The Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy", Ford Prefect hums the symphony's first bar to a Vogon guard, in a last-ditch effort to persuade the guard to abandon his brutal ways; the attempt is unsuccessful, although Prefect evades imminent death by other means.
  • On Peter Schickele's album P.D.Q. Bach On the Air (Vanguard CD-79268 LP-79268) two broadcasters provide a running commentary on a performance of the first movement, as if it were a sports event. In this recording the commentary is simply dubbed over a recording of the music. When Schickele performs the Beethoven's Fifth Sportscast on tour the music is played by a live orchestra. The act includes a referee as well as commentators and the music stops for instant replays, player substitutions and penalties.

[edit] 1980s

  • In Chapter 8, Book 1 of the graphic novel V for Vendetta, the Symphony is identified as the piece played while title character V commits an assassination. The song is significant because, as is noted by one of the detectives, the initial "da da da dum!" is morse code for the letter "V".
  • A TV ad for novelty pre-recorded greetings for telephone answering machines included the opening segment of the symphony, accompanied by a strong male voice singing, "Nobody's home! Nobody's home!" [1]

[edit] 1990s

  • In the film Beethoven, a family adopts a Saint Bernard puppy and cannot decide what to name him. The young daughter, who is playing the piano while the discussion is going on, plays a famous part of Ludwig van Beethoven's Fifth Symphony, and the dog immediately begins to bark. Thus, the family decides to name him Beethoven.
  • In the live action film 101 Dalmatians, Horace and Jasper are continually tricked by animals that someone is knocking at the door, but when they arrive no one is there. They begin to leave, when suddenly they hear a car horn honk, and the 4-note motif plays, possibly suggesting to the theme of "fate knocking at the door", a recurring theme in lore concerning the symphony.
  • In an episode of Animaniacs, The Warner Brothers and Sister visit Beethoven. They work as chimney sweeps and hum the first few notes continuously, thus giving Beethoven the idea for his piece.
  • In the 1995 The Simpsons Episode Lisa's Wedding a grown up Martin Prince appears briefly as Phantom of the Opera and plays "A Fifth of Beethoven".
  • In the Mystery Science Theater 3000 episode Hamlet, Mike Nelson identifies Hamlet's famous "to be or not to be" as "the literary equivalent of [hums the famous four note motif of the Fifth Symphony]."
  • On June 30, 1997, the Handover Ceremony of Hong Kong was held at the Hong Kong Convention and Exhibition Centre. Demonstrators including Leung Kwok-Hung, the "Long Hair", held an empty coffin and demostrated loudly at the entrance of the Centre to show their dissatification to the Communist Party of China and worrying about the future of the newly-established HKSAR. The Assistant Commissioner of the Royal Hong Kong Police Force, Lee Ming-kwai, played the Symphony to cover the noise from the demonstrators so that the leaders of the CCP who were entering the venue could not hear anything of the demonstrators.

[edit] 2000s

  • In the September 25th 2006 episode of Family Guy, "A Fifth of Beethoven" was featured during a brief scene in which Peter Griffin and friends perform an impromptu roller disco dance. This dance, despite being amazing, was entirely by accident.
  • In the 2005 episode of The Simpsons entitled The Seven-Beer Snitch the residents of Springfield build a concert hall to make their town more cultural. Beethoven's 5th Symphony is played on the opening night, but after the first four notes have been played the audience gets bored and leaves. When the conductor asks where everyone is going, Clancy Wiggum sneers "Hey, we heard the 'Duh-duh-duh-dum' bit already, the rest is just filler." In addition, in the 2004 episode Margical History Tour, Nelson as Beethoven adapted his customary laugh to the first four notes of the work.
  • In Disney's "Mickey, Donald, Goofy: The Three Musketeers," (2004) when Mickey is sent to prison, his best friends Goofy and Donald attempt to save him. At the last minute, Donald backs out (being the coward that he's portrayed to be). This scene is then accompanied by The Troubadour (the narrator of the story) singing the 5th Symphony with different lyrics. The starting lyric includes "This is the end!" Eventually, Donald joins Goofy in saving Mickey.
  • The indie rock band Cake featured a song about the 5th Symphony on their 2001 album, Comfort Eagle. The song, Commissioning A Symphony in C, recounts the commissioning of the song by the Austrian nobleman, Count Franz von Oppersdorff, the song's subsequent performance, and his resulting discontent.
  • "A Fifth of Beethoven" was used in the 2002 movie The Rules of Attraction starring James Van Der Beek.
  • A modified version of the Fifth Symphony became the theme song for Judge Judy in 2005.
  • In the anime series Fullmetal Alchemist, the symphony makes a notable appearance in episodes 49 & 50, where it heralds protagonist Edward Elric's arrival in our world and the subsequent firebombing of London by the German Air Force.
  • The first movement of Beethoven's 5th Symphony was used in the film V for Vendetta (2006) when V plays a recording of the piece to mask his voice from surveillance while he makes a deal with a corrupt government official.
