Bee and badger act

The "Bee and Badger" Act was a nickname applied to the 1875 Illegal Activities (Animals) Act of the Disraeli government of 1874-1880. The name was most likely the creation of Charles Dilke, the Liberal MP for Chelsea, though others suggest it was the creation of Hugh Childers. This last interpretation seems unlikely, as Childers was suffering from ill-health in the mid-1870s and would have been a rare presence in Parliament. The possibility remains that he did label the act at some form of social engagement, however.

The name derives from the (virtually non-existant) fixation of the Act with the practices of badger-baiting and apiculture. Badger-baiting, by far the most barbarous act of the pair, was originally banned in 1835 but had continued to be practiced in covert form in several cities. Apiculture - the keeping of bees - was not considered aggressive, but the act dedicated several clauses stipulating the minimum space in which bees could be kept. This was less to do with the "harm" that may be caused to bees as a concern as to the damage that bees could do. In Bishopsgate in 1872, three children had been stung to death after disturbing the hives of an apiculturist - an event that most consider to have prompted the apiculture clauses of the Act.

The derogatory labelling of this act was apparently designed to belittle the achievements of the Disraeli ministry.
