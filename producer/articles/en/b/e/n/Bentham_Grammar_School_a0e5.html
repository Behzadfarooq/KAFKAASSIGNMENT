[edit] Founding and Location

Bentham Grammar School was founded as a charity in 1726 in the village of Bentham (now in North Yorkshire) by William Collingwood, a gentleman of York. It educated, first, the boys of the local villages, and later, in the twentieth century, also fee-paying boarders from a wider area. Girls were educated in small numbers until the 1930’s when the school became fully co-educational. The first school was situated on School Hill in High Bentham, but after the 1870 Education Act, the building was required for state elementary education and the school moved to a site at Moon’s Acre in 1878.

[edit] Recent history

In 1948 under its new post-war headmaster John Webb, the school moved again, this time, to the beautiful Norman Shaw rectory building in Low Bentham where it remained for 54 years. In 2002 the school's governing body judged that financial difficulties demanded closure. In its earlier days the school educated about 40 - 50 local boys. After 1946, numbers steadily grew until BGS was admitting almost 400 boys and girls in the 1970’s. In the 80’s and 90’s numbers diminished - as they did universally - but the school still provided a full range of academic and practical subjects from nursery to A-level. It was a very successful and much loved school with an often remarked-upon family atmosphere which, together with its lovely site in the arm of the river Wenning, inspired much affection from its students. The school closed in August 2002 and the site and the foundation charities were taken over by Sedbergh School. The site is now occupied by Sedbergh Junior School.
