Beat Crazy Records is a UK indie record label formed in late 2004 by former The Bluetones, White Rose Movement and Archie Bronson Outfit manager, Neil Burrow. Having received significant financial investment from two northen businessmen, Burrow set about building an independent label using his friend Laurence Bell's Domino Records as the model on which Beat Crazy Records would be based (Domino Records had become the leading independent UK label of the mid-noughties due to the commercial success of Franz Ferdinand and the Arctic Monkeys).

Burrow's first two signings to the label were Amsterdam and Ricky. Amsterdam were fronted by former Pele singer/songwriter Ian Prowse and had gained superlative praise from legendary BBC Radio 1 DJ John Peel for their 'Does This Train Stop on Merseyside?' song.

Ricky meanwhile had released a self-funded debut album, 'The Summer Sun Still Echoes' to superlative praise in 2004, including the prestigious 4-star rating from Britain's biggest music publication, Q. Later that year they had released one of the highest-ranking singles ever to be released in the UK by an unsigned band when 'That Extra Mile' reached number 50 in the UK Singles Chart and made the Top Ten of the UK Indie Chart.

Beat Crazy Records' first release was a split double a-side single, featuring Ricky's 'Stop Knocking The Walls Down' and Amsterdam's 'The Journey'. It was released on January 24th 2005 and crashed in at number 32 in the UK Singles Chart.

The next single released by the label was Amsterdam's 'Does This Train Stop on Merseyside?' which fared less well, peaking at Number 53 in the UK Singles Chart. The band's debut album, titled 'The Journey', soon followed but failed to chart, despite superlative praise from the likes of The Sun.

In August 2005 Neil Burrow parted company with Beat Crazy Records.

Amsterdam's third single, 'Takin' On The World', was released in October 2005 but failed to make the Top 75 of the UK Singles Chart despite being playlisted by the UK's most listened-to station, BBC Radio 2.

Beat Crazy Records' next release was 'We Are England', an NSPCC-fundraising single released to coincide with the 2006 FIFA World Cup. The single featured vocal contributions from 54,000 English schoolchildren after the band members completed a mammoth tour of the country where they recorded the vocals of hundreds of teenagers at 180 different English secondary schools. The single generated a great deal of media interest, including a daily campaign of support from the The Daily Mirror, a live interview on BBC Radio 1's Jo Whiley show, two interviews on BBC Radio 5 Live and a huge show of support from British regional media, who were keen to promote the involvement of schools from their respective regions.

In the weeks building up to the single's release, the media regularly reported that 'We Are England' was far outselling the rival World Cup singles on HMV's pre-order chart. However, the record company failed to synchronise the release of the single's four formats (CD, 7" vinyl, DVD and digital download) and after an initially promising start - where the single featured at Number 17 in the first midweek chart update - the single finished the week at Number 54 in the UK Singles Chart. The only consolation was that 'We Are England' finished at Number 3 in the UK Indie Singles Chart.

Despite having recorded a lavishly-produced album for the label - titled 'High Speed Silence' - Beat Crazy had failed to release the album. Coupled with the label's failure to release 'We Are England's four formats on the same day, Ricky decided to disband just days later.

Amsterdam also parted company with the label, leaving it with no artists on its roster. Beat Crazy Records has not released a record since.
