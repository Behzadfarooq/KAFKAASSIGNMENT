Berger v. New York This case was argued before the Supreme Court on April 13, 1967 and was decided on June 12 of the same year.

Berger was convicted of attempting to bribe a public official, the chairman of the New York State Liquor Authority. His conviction was based on evidence of the bribe obtained from eavesdropping. There was a court order allowing a recording device to be placed in Berger's office for 60 days.

Under New York law, wiretapping was legal if the district attorney swore an oath that there was "a reasonable ground to believe that evidence of a crime may be thus obtained." The wiretap could be placed for no longer than two months, although an extension of the two months could be granted if such an extension was in the public interest.

Similarly, if leads were obtained through these wiretaps, a wiretap could be placed elswhere, also for 60 days.

The Supreme Court ruled that this law violated the 14 and 4th Amendment which protect the "life, liberty, and property" of American citizens. The Supreme Court felt that conversation was included in personal property and that it should be protected.

Based on this inclusion of conversation in personal property, the Supreme Court felt that allowing 60 days of wiretapping was equivalent to allowing search and seizures for 60 days with one warrant.

Another problem the Supreme Court found was that there was no "return on the warrant." As such, it was to the officer's full discretion what to do with the conversations of both innocent and guilty citizens who had been under surveillance.

This case is important because it extends the right to property to the more abstract property, such as conversation, rather than the purely physical property.
