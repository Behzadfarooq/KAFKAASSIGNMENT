Berlinde De Bruyckere (1964 in Ghent, Belgium ) is an artist based in Ghent.

She works in a range of sculptural media, including wax, wood, wool, horse skin and hair. These are combined to create compelling figures that suggest distorted human and animal forms. Her figures are often faceless, malformed and fragmentary. They perch precariously on high stools or are suspended from the walls or ceiling. At first their shape seems familiar although their forms resist interpretation, offering a disturbing vision of fragility and suffering.

Berlinde de Bruyckere’s work is centred around the dichotomies of life and death, power and frailty, protection and harm. Her main motif is her consistent use of blankets as a metaphor for both shelter and suffocation. De Bruyckere’s sculptures and installations often involve piles of blankets in various situations and configurations intimating potential comfort lying wasted and the sadly ubiquitous news images of refugees fleeing from war-torn corners of the globe. She also builds female figures – although only identifiable as such through occasional limbs – from blankets. These dispossessed women are vulnerable yet safe: hidden under a cosy protective layer, but hidden from what? Another of de Bruyckere’s semi-regular images is horses; her horse sculptures are casts of equine bodies modelled into twisted near-death effigies of formally powerful creatures. The casts are covered in hide giving the appearance of badly taxidermied animals and the facial features are doctored in order to remove any sign of ‘cuteness’ or sentimentality. Their massive crippled forms are ominous, articulated by shadows, seams, and holes. The brutal nature of their physical form suggests not only past suffering, but also some unknown terror to come. De Bruyckere purposefully situates her work in the uncomfortable middle-ground rather than allowing an easy emotional response – she makes it hard to empathise with her de-humanised ‘blanket women’ and faceless horses forcing the audience to confront the reality of death’s closeness to life and without trite emotional distractions.

Berlinde de Bruyckere won international acclaim at the 2003 Venice Biennale, when her sculptures were shown in the Italian Pavilion. Since then, her solo exhibitions have included Hauser & Wirth Zurich ( 2004 ); 'One' at La Maison Rouge , Fondation Antoine de Galbert , Paris ( 2005 ); and De Pont Foundation for Contemporary Art , Tilburg (2005). In 2006 her work was included in the 4th Berlin Biennial for Contemporary Art and exhibited in a two-artist show at the Kunsthalle Düsseldorf .
