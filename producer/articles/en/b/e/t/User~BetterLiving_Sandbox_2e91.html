From guaiacol

Numerous routes exist to convert guaiacol, or 2-methoxyphenol, to vanillin.[1]. This reaction was first reported by Karl Reimer in 1876,[2] and from the 1920s through the mid-1950s,[3] guaiacol, extracted from wood tar, coal tar, and later synthesized as a petrochemical product, was the major starting material for the synthesis of synthetic vanillin.

While vanillin production from guaiacol was eventually eclipsed by production from lignin, during the 1970s, Rhône-Poulenc (now Rhodia) introduced a new process for producing vanilling from guaiacol.[4] In this process, guaiacol first reacts with glyoxylic acid by electrophilic aromatic substitution. Then, the resulting vanilmandelic acid undergoes oxidative decarboxylation to produce vanillin.[5]

With the closure of most of the world's lignin-based vanillin producers during the 1980s, guaiacol has again become the principal raw material for vanillin synthesis.[6]
