Bedford Springs, a nineteenth-century mineral springs resort located just off the Pennsylvania Turnpike in Bedford, Pennsylvania, United States, is a large complex of buildings erected throughout the nineteenth century to accommodate the growing popularity of seven mineral springs in close proximity to one another. The multitude of small, freshwater springs, which were believed to have miraculous healing powers, at first attracted those suffering from illnesses, but the popularity of the resort grew quickly due to its location at the foot of the Alleghany Mountains along Shober's Run (sometimes called Shover's Run), and its appealing architecture, which includes Stone House, erected in 1804, the Colonial Building, a monumental, columned revival building constructed in 1842, as well as a long series of porticoed wings built in the Carpenter Gothic style from the 1850s to the 1890s. The popularity of the resort benefitted from the emphasis on outdoor life in the mid-nineteenth century as east coast American cities became increasingly industrialized and polluted, and from the establishment of stops in Bedford for the B&O and Pennsylvania Railroads beginning in 1872, providing easy accessibility from cities such as Washington, D.C., Philadelphia, and New York.

Bedford Springs became an attraction for politicians, and, in addition to serving as the "Summer White House" for President Buchanan from 1857 to 1861, also served as a getaway and meeting place for other presidents such as William Henry Harrison, James K. Polk and Zachary Taylor, as well as a multitude of senators and congressmen, and their families. The first transatlantic cable sent from England to the United States was received by President Buchanan at the hotel on August 12, 1858. The hotel's golf course, began by the golf course architect, A.W. Tillinghast, was completed by another golf great, Donald Ross, in the 1920s. During World War II, the hotel and grounds were used as a U.S. Navy radio training center, and also as a detention center for interned Japanese diplomats.

The decades of the 1870s through the 1890s, the "golden age" of America's grand resorts, saw the "resort gold standard" set at Bedford Springs. A series of colorful spring houses, bridges, and gates dotted the expansive grounds, and a "serpentine trail" led party excursions from one spring to the next. In the beginning of the 20th century, as medicine and health-awareness advanced, the validity of the Springs' healing powers decreased slightly, but the hotel still flourished, and in 1905, a major renovation of the complex included a monumental double-decked colonnade, which connected the hotel's main dining room to a columned pavilion at the Magnesium Springs across Shober's Run, as well as a new building with a spring-fed indoor pool, purportedly the first such facility in the United States at the time it was built.

In the 1930s, the hotel's resident physician, Dr. William E. Fitch, established the "Bedford Cure," a health regimen that required a three-week stay at the resort, which still operated successfully throughout the 1930s and 1940s.

[edit] The Springs

The Bedford Springs are known to each have different medicinal value because each spring has its own mineral makeup. The "Bedford Cure" required the patient to drink several gulps of each type of water (or a specific type of water depending on the individual's ailment) on a daily basis during their three-week stay. The separate springs are named "Magnesium Spring," which is used for stomach ailments; "Iron Spring," which was prescribed for iron deficiencies and healthy bones; the "Black Spring" which was mostly used to irrigate the golf course; and the "Sweet Spring" whose waters tasted the best. The Sweet Spring was capped for some time.

[edit] Further Development

In the 1950s, Bedford Springs underwent another substantial renovation, which brought modern infrastructure and amenities into the facilities. In the 1960s and 1970s, following the pattern of most of America's grand resort hotels, Bedford Springs experienced a decline, and, by the mid 1980s, business at the hotel had diminished substantially. In 1984, the Bedford Springs hotel was designated as part of the National Register of Historic Places. However, in 1986, Bedford Springs was shut down and remained so for nearly 18 years.

[edit] Future of the Resort

In 2004, Benchmark Hospitality International brought the facility under their wing with a goal to restore the grandeur and preserve the history of the massive facility. With a target opening of Summer 2007, the $108 million restoration project is currently in progress. The hotel buildings currently are being completely restored and renovated, and additional wings are under construction. The new resort will be a 218-room hotel, resort, spa, and conference facility. The spa wing will accommodate additional rooms and a full-service spa. The hotel will contain a 130-seat restaurant along with a fully restored colonial-style tavern in the Stone House, with original, massive stone walls and beams dating to 1804.

The revitalized resort will also contain a new outdoor pool, a complete restoration of the original, mineral spring-fed indoor pool, and a kids' club. The resort will offer outdoor life activities including hiking, bicycling (led by Cannondale), cross-country skiing, fly fishing, river rafting, carriage rides, and day excursions to many of Bedford's famous historic sites and beautiful covered bridges.

[edit] Golf Course Renovation

As part of the revitalization of the resort, an extensive renovation of the Tillinghast/Ross course will be undertaken. The historical course had suffered over time from sediment filling in the margins of the waterways, and from “modern” approaches to landscape in the 1950’s, which tried to "tidy-up" the creek edges with a "mown-lawn" approach. The renovation of the course will attempt to restore the natural spring bed with native grasses and other natural materials indigenous to the flood plane. Befitting the course's siting next to Shober's Run creek, there are water features apparent at nearly every hole.
