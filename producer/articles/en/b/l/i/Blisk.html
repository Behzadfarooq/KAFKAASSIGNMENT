A blisk is a single-engine component composed of a rotor disc and blades through machining from a solid piece of material or by welding idividually manufactured blades to the rotor disc. The term is used mainly in aerospace engine design. Also known as an integrally bladed rotor or IBR.

[edit] History

Blisk manufacturing has been in steadfast for the past 20 years. It was first used by Sermatech-Lehr (now known as Teleflex Aerospace [1]) in 1985 for the compressors of the T700 helicopter engine. After that, its use has continued to increase in major applications for both compressors and fan blade rotors. Examples include the Rocketdyne RS68 rocket engine and the F110 fighter jet.

[edit] Advantages

Instead of making the compressor disks and then attaching the blades later on in the process, blisks are just single elements combining the two. This removes the need to attach the blades to the disk (via screws, bolts, etc) and thus decreases the number of components within the compressor while at the same time decreasing drag and increasing efficiency of air compression within the engine.

[edit] Process

Blisks can either be CNC milled, ECM manufactured or welded together. Via electro chemical machining, three stages must be undergone in order to produce the final product.
