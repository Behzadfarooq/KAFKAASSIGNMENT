The Black Tusk is a remarkably abrupt pinnacle of volcanic rock located in Garibaldi Provincial Park of British Columbia, Canada. At 2319 metres above sea level,[1] the upper spire is visible from a great distance in all directions. It is particularly noticeable from the Sea-to-Sky Highway just south of Whistler, British Columbia. Distinctive and immediately identifiable, the Black Tusk is probably the best known mountain in the Garibaldi Ranges of the Coast Mountains, although many mistakenly believe it to be part of the Cascade Range (it is a member of the Cascade Volcanic Arc, but not of the Cascade Range).

[edit] Geology

The Black Tusk is a member in the chain of multiple volcanic peaks that run from southwestern British Columbia to northern California. The peaks formed in the past 35 million years as the Juan de Fuca Plate and the Explorer Plate to its west have been subducting under the North American Plate. As the oceanic crust of the Juan de Fuca Plate and the Explorer Plate melts under the pressure, it creates pools of lava that drive up the Pacific Ranges and Cascade Range and power periodic eruptions in there volcanic peaks.

[edit] Natural History

The Black Tusk is considered to be the remnant of a stratovolcano.[2] According to Natural Resources Canada, the Black Tusk was "perhaps the conduit for lava within a cinder-rich volcano. The loose cinder has eroded, leaving only the hard lava core." [3] The exposed lava rock of the core is loose and friable. It is also black, giving the mountain its name and character. Cinder Cone, to the east of The Black Tusk, produced a 9-km-long lava flow during the late Pleistocene or early Holocene.

The upper summit area at the top of the lava column can only be reached by scrambling up a short but exposed rock chimney.[4] Nevertheless, the Black Tusk's lower summit area is an extremely popular backcountry hiking destination. Most hikers approach from the Taylor Meadows camping area, although there is a second route from the north that travels by way of Helm Lake.[5] Close to the highest edge of the upper summit area is an isolated and intimidating rock formation known as the "Bishop's Mitre", which is rumoured to be unclimbed.[6][7] The Black Tusk, formed between about 1.3 and 1.1 million years ago. Following glacial dissection, renewed volcanism produced the lava dome and flow forming it's summit.

[edit] Name

The Black Tusk is sacred to the Squamish First Nation, the indigenous people of the area. It is known to them as the "Landing Place of the Thunderbird", or "t'ak't'ak mu'yin tl'a in7in'a'xe7en", home of the legendary Thunderbird. The rock was said to have been burnt black by the Thunderbird's lightning.[8]
