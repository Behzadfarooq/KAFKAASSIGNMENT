Black Water is a novella by Joyce Carol Oates and was originally published in 1992.

[edit] Plot introduction

It tells the story of Kelly Kelleher, an idealistic magazine writer, who meets the United States Senator she wrote her thesis on at a Fourth of July party. The Senator, as he is referred to in the novel, plans to take her home for a romantic rendezvous, but a car accident plunges them into a swampy marsh. The story is primarily told in flashback

[edit] Critical appreciation

The novella, taught in "Art of Short Fiction" at Harvard College and classes worldwide, uses a repetitive cyclical narrative to express the many themes of the novella. While her descriptions of the interactions seem in part archetypal as part of a mythological interaction between "man-deity" and "woman-worshipper," Kelly Kelleher as a rendition of Mary Jo Kopechne is also in part a more generalized woman provoking an analysis of American culture.

The novella parallels the Chappaquiddick incident, but Oates wants the story to be seen as the "archetypal" story "of a young woman who trusts an older man and whose trust is violated." [1]

[edit] Awards and nominations

Black Water was nominated for the Pulitzer Prize in 1993 and was a finalist for the National Book Critics Circle Award in 1992.

[edit] Trivia

Black Water was adapted into an opera.
