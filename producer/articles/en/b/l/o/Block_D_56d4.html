Block D

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Block D (Блок Д in Russian) is upper stage of rockets used by USSR/Russia. The stage (and its derivatives) has been included in more than 250 launched rockets as of 2005 [1].

It was developed as fifth stage ('Д' is the fifth letter in the cyrillic alphabet) for the cancelled USSR Soviet Moonshot mission programme in 1963-1964 (part of L3 manned lunar expeditionary complex). The plan was to use Block D for midcourse corrections on the flight to the Moon, then to brake when entering circumlunar orbit, and then to cancel orbital velocity for the moon-landing LK. However, N-1 launcher was cancelled.

The technology was later included as fouth stage of Proton rocket and as such flew on Soviet missions to Moon, Mars and Venus. It was replaced by Briz M as fourth stage in recent Proton M modification, yet Block D still flies in older configuration, Proton K. It also works as the third stage for Zenit-3SL rocket, which launches from a sea platform within Sea Launch project.

Block D was modified as Block DM in 1974 for launching satellites in Earth-based orbits. By 2002 Block DM had 97% success rate in 218 flights since 1974, and 43 successive missions in 1997-2002 [2].

The stage uses liquid oxygen (LOX) and kerosine, has one single-chamber main engine RD-58. The LOX tank has a spherical shape, the kerosine tank is toroidal, inclined to 15 degrees for better fuel extraction, with the engine mounted in the center of torus. Tanks include the first pump stage for the engine; the main pump is mounted on the engine. RKK Energia, the company that created Block D, used it as a platform for many modifications over many years for different purposes; for example, the main propulsion unit on Buran started as a modification of the Block D.

[edit] Modifications

  • Block DM (1974) - Block D's modification for geosynchronous orbits and other Earth-based orbits
  • Block DM-2 (1982)
  • Block DM-3 (1996)
  • Block DM-SL (1999, derivative of DM3) - used in Sea Launch missions as upper stage of Zenit 3SL rocket, as of January 2006
