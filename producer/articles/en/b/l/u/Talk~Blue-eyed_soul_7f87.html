"Blue-eyed soul is soul music as performed by white people and usually intended for white audiences."

I do not think the artists listed would share this definition of a made up genre of music. R&B is "R&B", soul is "soul". I (an African American) grew up from the cradle listening to the likes of Hall & Oates and Tina Marie, was I surprised when I found out they were white? Yes. Did I fling their albums out of the window? No. Good music is good music.

>>>>reply. I do not believe it should say "intended" for white audiences, but rather "popular by predominately" white audiences. I don't think any artist listed in the article was/is aiming to target any racial demographic, but rather a social or cultural demographic which can include people similar in persona but diverse in racial origin. Not to say that everyone to listens to this music is the same, but only that the majority of those that listen any sort of music certainly share similar cultural/fashion/personality traits. No music genre is comprised of a completely random mixture of listeners. To deny this is silly.

[edit] Here's the deal, I think

There's a reason this article is such a hash; it's really trying to be four articles.

"Blue Eyed Soul" fits three or four different subgenres, actually:

   * "White Soul", a specific style/genre from the sixties and seventies that included Eric Burdon and the Box Tops
   * White people doing "soul" music - from the sublime (Van Morrison, Dusty Springfield (I agree, she MUST be in this piece), the flaming ember) to the ridiculous (Michael Bolton) to, yes, Joss Stone.
   * R and B singers who happen to be white - from Teena Marie to Britney Spears and Christina Aguilera.
   * British "White soul" of the '80's - from Allison Moyet to Rick Astley to, yes, Boy George.

Any term that includes Alex Chilton AND Eric Burdon AND Boy George is basically meaningless.

If it's OK with all of you, I might rewrite some stuff here. Mitchberg 09:37, 25 February 2006 (UTC)

[edit] Brown-eyed soul?

I've heard this term used specifically to describe the white, soul-influenced rock bands that came out of the New York metropolitan area in the 1960s, such as the Rascals, Vanilla Fudge, the Soul Survivors, the Vagrants, etc. ,due to the fact that the members of those bands were usually Italian-American or Jewish.
