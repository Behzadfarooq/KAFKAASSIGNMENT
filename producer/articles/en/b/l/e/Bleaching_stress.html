Bleaching stress

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Biology of Coral Bleaching: Corals are fragile animals that need very specific conditions in order to thrive in their marine habitat. The main conditions that create a productive habitat for corals are shallow water depth, water temperatures, amount of sedimentation in the water, overcrowding, predation, disease, and pollution. When corals are faced with a drastic change in any of these conditions, the polyps (the actual animal) become stressed. This stress can either lead to their mortality, expel the Zooxanthellae algae that gives them their pigment, or it could cause them to “jump ship” and break off from their colony in order to find a less stressful environment. When the polyps have either died or otherwise left, all that remains is the white, calcium based skeleton that they had been living in. Because the skeleton is white, this phenomenon has been labeled as coral bleaching.

Stressors: Corals rely heavily on their symbiotic relationship with Zooxanthellae (a photosynthetic algae) for nutrition, so it is important that they remain relatively in close to the surface. Australian scientists have found that corals contain a kind of sunblock called fluorescent pigments. These pigments form a kind of shield around the zooxanthellae and protect them from the harmful effects of sunlight at high temperatures. The pigments change the harmful UV and blue wavelengths in sunlight to softer, lower energy wavelengths like green and yellow[1]. Different corals are able to survive at different depths depending on how they are able to acquire their nutrition. Many coral reefs are found in shallow tropical waters where access to sunlight is constant and the water temperatures remain at a relatively steady rate. Just like with water depth, corals species vary when it comes to ranges of water temperatures that they can handle. Although corals are sometimes found in colder waters, for the most part they live in places where the water temperatures are warmer. Corals can get stressed very quickly when the temperature changes too drastically. Within the span of a couple weeks a colony can go from thriving to desolate. stress factor is relevant to global climate change because if the Earth’s water temperatures begin changing then these fragile organisms will not be able to survive in their previous habitat and, because they do not have a great amount of mobility if they do leave the colony based on stress, the chance of mass mortality is extremely high. Sometimes stress stems from the actions of the other organisms that inhabit the same habitat. Corals can fall victim to overcrowding in which one species needs more space than another and forces the lesser aggressive species to vacate dues to that stress. Different kinds of organisms also have an impact on corals. Corals are such delicate animals that if a part of the colony is destroyed because of disease or because the polyps were rubbed off by some bigger organisms, the colony is vulnerable to the spread of algae. Once algae gains a foothold within the colony it can easily spread and completely ruin the colony. This is the reason that divers and snorkelers are careful not to touch the corals. Just one scrape and the entire colony could be at risk. Anthropogenic disturbances can have devastating impacts on coral reefs. Because reefs are found closer to shorelines, coastal development can lead to massive increase in the amount of sand and silt that get stirred up in the water. This increase in sedimentation induces large amounts of stress on the corals. The sedimentation that is in the water not only blocks the sunlight from reaching the Zooxanthellae algae that helps the corals to eat, it also suffocates the practically immobile animals. After the coast has been developed, another disturbance comes into play; pollution. The leakage of sewage and toxic chemicals are capable of doing massive amount of damage to corals chemically but also threaten them by increasing the amount of nutrients in the water. Eutrophication enables algae to thrive and prosper at the expense of the corals. The damage that these stress factors incur is not a light matter. Corals are not very resilient animals, and in some cases is can take as long as 30 years for a coral reef community to fully recover from a disturbance!
