[edit] Criticism and 'cult' allegations

A variety of criticisms and allegations have been leveled at Opus Dei. In the English-speaking world, the most vocal criticism comes from a group called the Opus Dei Awareness Network (ODAN), a non-profit organization that exists "to provide education, outreach and support to people who have been adversely affected by Opus Dei." Other critics include former members of Opus Dei, liberal catholic theologians, and supporters of Liberation theology.

Opus Dei, as a part of the Roman Catholic Church, shares any criticisms of Catholicism in general. At the same time, Opus Dei's status as a personal prelature is unique, and is seen by critics as giving it too much independence from the rest of the Catholic Church, making it a "church within a church".[1] Critics also assert that the organization exerts a disproportionately large influence within the Catholic Church, as illustrated by irregularaties in the canonization process of Escrivá, who was canonized as a saint after an unusually short period of time following his death.[2] (See Josemaría Escrivá: Canonisation.)

One charge is that Opus Dei maintains an extreme degree of control over its members, which is seen as a cult-like feature. Critics note that numeraries in Opus Dei generally submit all their incoming and outgoing mail to their superiors to read,[3] and point to an alleged "Forbidden Books List" that details which books members are not allowed to read without the express permission of their superiors.[3] Critics assert that Opus Dei pressures numeraries to cut off social contact with non-members, including even their own families.[4], and that the organization is altogether extremely secretive. Unlike most other religious groups, Opus Dei does not publish its memberships lists, and members generally do not publicly reveal that they are part of the organization. This practice has led to intense speculation as to who may or may not be a member of Opus Dei.[5]

Opus Dei recruitment is another focus of criticism. The organization has been accused of deceptive or aggressive recruiting practices, for example a cult-like recruitment technique called "Love bombing", in which potential members are showered with flattery and admiration by members of the organization in order to entice them into joining.[4][6][7] Numeraries are encouraged to form friendships and go to social gatherings specifically as a means to recruit, and members of Opus Dei even go so far as to fill out regular written reports on friends who may be potential recruits, and attend regular meetings with their supervisors to discuss recruiting tactics.[citation needed]

One of the most controversial aspects of Opus Dei is its members' practice of regular self-mortification.[8] According to critics, the mortification used by Opus Dei is a "startling" and "questionable" practice which borders on masochism.[9][10] Critics often cite the fact the Escrivá himself engaged in more extreme forms of mortification than those typically practiced by Opus Dei numeraries.[11] For example, Escriva's closest aide has described an incident in which Escriva flailed himself over a thousand times.[12][13] Opponents of Opus Dei often criticize Escrivá's maxim on suffering: "Loved be pain. Sanctified be pain. Glorified be pain!" [14][15]

Escrivá and Opus Dei have been accused of supporting the regimes of Francisco Franco,[16][17][18] Augusto Pinochet,[19] and to a lesser extent, Adolf Hitler.[20][21][22] (see Opus Dei and politics).
