[edit] Infobytes

  • A went on a geophysical expedition to Spitsbergen 1882—83.[1] Spitsbergen or Spitzbergen is the largest island in the Svalbard archipelago. Formerly, like in Andrée's time, "Spitsbergen" was also applied to the entire archipelago of Svalbard, and occasionally still is.
  • Nils Strindberg, the youngest member of the expedition, was the first to die. The other two buried him. (They dug a hole in the ground?)
  • Gaah! One of my few references in English, John Maxtone-Graham, Safe Return Doubtful, turns out to be full of misspelt names and factual errors. Andrée was going to go up in John Wise's balloon himself? Hardly! (What, dressed as the Goddess of Liberty?) "The Svea, he discovered, could maneuver 30° to either side of the wind's direction... The Svea was no longer merely a balloon but also a dirigible." You wish!
  • The English title eschews the ironic Jules Verne-type associations of the original Swedish title, literally "Engineer Andrée's Voyage through the Air".
  • Kjellström, 46: They tried to walk to the depot at Kap (Cape?) Flora, then realized they weren't getting anywhere, because of drift, and started for the depot at Sjuöarna instead. WHAT DEPOTS? First I've heard of any depots! How would those be relevant to balloon travel...?

Ah, Kap Flora is in Franz Joseph's Land, and is on my scanned map. Whew, that helps a little. Oho, the depots are explained in Med örnen mot polen, 59. Phew.

  • Leftover on Gurli: After the death of her husband in 1904, Gurli Linder would spend the rest of her life celebrating and defending the memory of Andrée, drawing loving pen portraits of him in the media during the long period of ignorance of the expedition's fate. Her position as an authority on the subject was still unchallenged when the remains of the explorers were found on Kvitøya in 1930 and she was again entrusted with portraying the three men, giving her personal and adulatory picture of Andrée and of events. Sven Lundström, the curator of the Andrée museum in Grenna, Sweden, suggests that this picture has thereby come to influence the perception of the Andrée expedition into our own time.[2]
  • Leftover from "Promotion and fundraising": A European trip Andrée had planned for the purpose of studying the latest in balloons was hastily turned into a lecture tour whereby he garnered support and international attention for his plans, not least in Paris, the world capital of ballooning, and from the prestigious international geography conference which was just taking place in London. Well aware of the importance of his rhetorical skills to the project, Andrée wrote to a friend: "It doesn't seem to have occurred to anybody yet that we might possibly encounter difficulties. It's fun to hypnotize on a grand scale!"[3] With Lachambre, Andrée made a number of ascents around Paris to test his own drag rope technique as well as Lachambre's claims for his craft. (Did he, really? Or did I dream it?) These ascents, like Andrée's own flights with the Svea, were ungovernable and perilous. It has been remarked on by several modern writers that Andrée apparently and inexplicably failed to become alarmed by the inability of the extremely experienced aeronaut Lachambre to steer his balloons effectively with the drag rope technique.[4] John Hertzberg at the Royal Institute of Technology in Stockholm treated the five exposed rolls of film and managed to save 93 out of a theoretical maximum of 240 (each roll could hold up to 48 exposures). A selection of these photos were quickly published along with the complete diaries as Med Örnen mot Polen (British edition The Andrée diaries, 1931; American edition Andrée's Story, 1932), a book which credits the three explorers as its posthumous authors. Tyrone Martinsson has lamented the traditional focus by previous researchers on the written records — the diaries — as primary sources of information and the lack of care with which the original negatives were stored from 1944. Martinsson's article "Recovering the visual history of the Andrée expedition" (2004) makes renewed claims for the historical significance of the photos and of Nils Strindberg.[5]
