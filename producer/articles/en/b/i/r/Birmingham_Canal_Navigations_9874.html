Birmingham Canal Navigations (BCN) is a network of navigable canals connecting Birmingham, Wolverhampton, and the eastern part of the Black Country. The BCN is connected to the rest of the English Canal system at several junctions.

The BCN has more miles of canals than there are in Venice[1]. At its working peak, the BCN contained about 160 miles (257 km) of canals; today just over 100 miles (160 km) are navigable, and the majority of traffic is from tourist and residential narrowboats.

[edit] Levels

The BCN is built on three main levels, each with its own reservoirs.

  • 453 ft, the Birmingham Level;
  • 473 ft, the Wolverhampton Level;
  • 408 ft, the Walsall Level

These levels are linked by locks at various places on the network.

There are also two shorter stretches on their own levels

  • Titford Branch was built at 511 ft, it was linked to the Titford Reservoir; and a feeder supplies water to the Edgbaston Reservoir.
  • A short section of the Old Main Line, at Smethwick top lock, was built at 491 ft; a pump house, the Smethwick Engine, had to be provided to keep the top level filled with water.

[edit] Linking canals

  • Anglesey Branch
  • Birmingham and Fazeley Canal (connects the BCN eastwards to the Coventry Canal at Fazeley Junction)
  • Digbeth Branch Canal
  • Dudley Canal Line No 1 (see also Dudley Tunnel)
  • Dudley Canal Line No 2 (see also Lappal Tunnel)
  • Soho Branch Loop Line (an old circuitous route cut off by Telford's improvements)
  • Tame Valley Canal (a more modern canal cutting off some northern wiggles)
  • Walsall Branch Canal
  • Walsall Canal (a more modern canal connecting the main line with Walsall and forming a big northern loop with the Wyrely and Essington Canal)
  • Wednesbury Oak Loop Line
  • Worcester and Birmingham Canal (connects the BCN southwards to the River Severn at Worcester)
