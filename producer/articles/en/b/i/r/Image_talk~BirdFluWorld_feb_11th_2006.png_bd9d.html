[edit] The UK

The map shows the UK to have H5N1; it doesn't. A bird died in a quarantine facility but no wild birds and no poultry have been found to have the disease, and the bird contracted the disease while in storage in Taiwan. Officially, the UK is still H5N1-free. And about Japan: there have not been any confirmed H5N1 outbreaks as far as I know; there have been outbreaks of other types of bird flu recently, but not H5N1. --Jamieli 17:36, 14 November 2005 (UTC)

[edit] Misleading colouring

The map was obviously generated by simply colouring entire countries. This can be rather misleading, as some of the countries involved cover a huge territory (esp. Russia and China), while the reported cases have been restricted to much smaller areas within these countries. The colouring also gives no indication about the number of detected cases and their timing. Colouring by country may be useful for presenting national economic statistics, political systems, etc. However, since migrating birds don't even know where country boundaries are, these are rather insignificant here. A map that places dots on the nearest cities were cases have been reported would be far more illustrative. The size of the dots could show the number of detected cases and the colour of the dot the date of the first case. Ideally, added arrows that show major bird migration routes would be useful in interpreting the data. Markus Kuhn 20:11, 8 January 2006 (UTC)

I came here to make the points Markus Kuhn has mase better than I could have. A system of overlapping dots to indicate outbreaks would be distinctly more informative. The information's available on-line. --Wetman 06:46, 10 February 2006 (UTC)
