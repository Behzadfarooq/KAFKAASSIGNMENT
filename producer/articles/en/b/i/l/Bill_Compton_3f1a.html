Bill Compton, M.A., is a mental health advocate in California. After suffering a complete mental breakdown in 1989, he has been heavily involved in self-help and patient advocate issues, including running a large self-help network since 1994.

[edit] Current work

Beginning in 1994, Compton has headed Project Return: The Next Step (PR:TNS), a consumer-run self-help network supported by the National Mental Health Association of Greater Los Angeles. He oversees more than 100 self-help clubs, which employ about 100 individuals in postions ranging from stipend to full time, serving several thousand individuals with serious mental illness.[1] The network inclucdes projects that Compton developed, such as PR:TNS Discovery Centers, which provide personal and professional growth opportunities, and the Friendship Line, an after-hours phone service that provides peer-to-peer support for people with mental illness.[2]

Compton is on the Board of Directors of Mental Health America (formerly the National Mental Health Association). He is also president of the board of Protection and Advocacy, Incorporated of California,[3] which helps protect the rights of mental health and other disabled consumers through out the state. [2] Beginning in November 2003, Compton was the chairman of the board of directors for the Pacific Clinic, the largest nonprofit mental health organization in California, based in Pasadena, California; he left that position in 2005.[4][3]

[edit] Personal history

Compton earned a B.A. degree in 1969 in Theater Arts from the University of Akron and returned to earn a Master's degree in 1986. During this time he worked as an actor, producer and director in New York and California.[4] In 1989, in his mid-40s and an event salesperson for a large Los Angeles theater, he suffered a complete mental breakdown.[5]

When Compton was diagnosed as having schizophrenia, he had already left his job and he had no health insurance. Three admissions to a private hospital over a nine-month period resulted in a $120,000 bill being sent to Compton's father, who remortgaged his home and went back to work. After transfer to a public hospital and subsequent discharge, Compton was placed in a community "board and care" home. He began hearing voices again, ending up living on the streets for nine months, panhandling for money to buy food.[6]

[edit] Presentations and testimony

In 2001, while president of the California Network of Mental Health Clients, a statewide alliance of consumers, Compton testified before the state legislatureâ€™s Joint Commission on Mental Health Reform.[2]

Compton has made presentations at state and national conferences on topics such as establishing and enhancing a network of self-help clubs, running a peer support telephone help line and incorporating the role of consumer-run services into a mental health system.[7][8][3]

He regularly makes presentations to graduate students at the University of Southern California School of Social Work and psychology students at the University of California Los Angeles.[4]

[edit] Awards and recognition

  • In June 2001, the National Mental Health Association (NMHA) presented the Clifford W. Beers Award to Compton at its annual conference in Washington, D.C.[1]
  • In June 2002, Compton received the Consumer Advocacy Award from the International Association of Psychosocial Rehabilitative Agencies.[2]
  • In November 2003, Eli Lilly and Company gave Compton its "Helping Move Lives Forward Reintegration Award" for his mentorship of people with mental illness.[4]
  • In 2004, Compton was included in Who's Who in America. [9]
