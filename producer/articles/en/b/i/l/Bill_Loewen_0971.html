William H. "Bill" Loewen is an entrepreneur, philanthropist and political activist in Winnipeg, Manitoba, Canada.

Loewen was raised in Elkhorn, Manitoba, and later moved to Winnipeg.[1] His wife Shirley Loewen is a prominent figure in the Winnipeg arts community, and his nephew John Loewen is a former member of the Legislative Assembly of Manitoba. He was named to the Order of Canada in 1999.[2]

[edit] Businessman

Loewen is a chartered accountant. He launched the payroll cheque company Comcheq Services in 1968 on an investment of $15,000, and turned the company into a national success.[3] Comcheq's revenue was $24 million in 1990, and it had 453 workers.[4] Loewen was named Manitoba Executive of the Year in May 1990.[5]

He sold the company to the Canadian Imperial Bank of Commerce in 1992,[6] and expressed disappointment when the CIBC sold it to an American firm in 1998.[7] He retained control of a division called Telpay, and used this entity to secure a customer's electronic bill-paying contract with the National Bank of Canada in 1999.[8] Telpay Inc. was named "innovator of the year" by the Winnipeg Chamber of Commerce in 2004,[9] and received the Distinguished Treasury Award from the Treasury Management Association of Canada (TMAC) the following year.[10] He spoke against bank mergers in 1998, arguing that Canadian banks were already too large and powerful.[11]

Winnipeg Mayor Susan Thompson appointed Loewen to head the Winnipeg Millennium Committee in 1997. In this capacity, he was responsible for planning events for the city's millennium festivities.[12]

[edit] Political activism

Loewen was a vocal opponent of Canada's proposed free trade deal with the United States of America in 1988. He formed the Business Council for Fair Trade, and argued that the deal would undermine Canada's sovereignty. Testifying before a parliamentary committee, he said "You're proposing a deal under which I, as a businessman, can prosper - but I must become an American to do so."[13] Loewen supported the Liberal Party of Canada in the 1988 federal election, and appeared at a Winnipeg campaign rally with party leader John Turner.[14] He later supported Lloyd Axworthy's aborted bid to lead the Liberal Party in 1990, and opposed plans for a North American Free Trade Agreement the following year.[15]

The Liberal Party announced its support for NAFTA in 1991. Loewen subsequently left the party, and become president of the newly-formed National Party of Canada in 1992. This centre-left party supported economic nationalism, and was led by Mel Hurtig.[16] Loewen donated four million dollars for the party to run candidates in the 1993 federal election,[17] and was himself a candidate in the riding of Winnipeg South Centre. He finished fifth against Axworthy, the Liberal candidate. The National Party did not win any seats.

The National Party suffered an internal split in 1994, with Hurtig and Loewen leading rival factions. Hurtig resigned as party leader following an acrimonious executive meeting in February 1994, and indicated that he would run to succeed himself in a leadership contest. A National Party press release approved by Loewen suggested that Hurtig resigned due to ill health. Hurtig denied this, and withdrew his resignation.[18] Loewen's faction refused to recognize Hurtig's retraction, and held that Kurt Loeb was the party's duly recognized interim leader. Hurtig and Loewen accused one another of undermining the party, and traded insults in the press.[19]

The two factions went to Ontario court in March 1994, to determine which side would have access to the party's $480,000 election rebate provided by the federal government.[20] The presiding judge determined in favour of Hurtig's faction.[21] Loewen subsequently issued a short book entitled "National Party of Canada, The First 14 Months", in which he criticized Hurtig as an autocratic leader.[22] Hurtig subsequently defeated Loeb in an official leadership convention, at which time Loewen called for the vote to be made unanimous to demonstrate party unity.[23] The divisions continued, however, and Hurtig resigned the leadership permanently in August 1994.[24] The party fell apart a few weeks later, amid continued acrimony.[25]

Loewen subsequently launched several legal challenges to keep the National Party, and attempted to recover $610,000 in unaccounted funds.[26] Neither attempt appears to have been successful. He remained an opponent of the Canada-U.S. Free Trade Deal throughout the 1990s.[27] In 1999, he appeared at a panel discussion led by Paul Hellyer of the newly-formed Canadian Action Party.[28]

[edit] Philanthropist

Loewen is a prominent financial supporter of the Winnipeg Arts Community. He donated $100,000 to the Winnipeg Symphony Orchestra following government cuts in the 1990s, and served as president and chairman of its Board of Directors at different times.[29] Loewen was given the orchestra's "Golden Baton Award" in 1998.[30] He wrote a piece commemorating the orchestra's fiftieth anniversary in 1999, and drew attention to new music from the young Manitoba composer Glenn Buhr.[31]

Loewen resigned from the board in June 1999, to express his displeasure over recent policy decisions.[32] He subsequently accused the WSO leadership of financial mismanagement. He rejoined the executive in the mid-2000s, and was listed as WSO Treasurer in January 2005.[33]

Loewen has provided extensive funding to the Pantages Playhouse Theatre, and donated one million dollars to the Manitoba Choral Association in 1998.[34] In 1999 and 2000, he led a successful movement to turn Winnipeg's former Bank of Commerce building into a multi-use non-profit centre.[35] The building was reopened to the public in 2002.[36] Loewen has also been a benefactor of "Project Loophole", a group which sought to ensure that a prominent wealthy Canadian family paid its fair share of taxes. He himself rejected suggestions that Canada's richest citizens are overtaxed.[37]

Loewen attempted to start a Canadian Professional Hockey League in 1995.[38]
