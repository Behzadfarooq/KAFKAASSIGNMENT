Bill Symons

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Bill Symons, born in Nucla, Colorado, is a former star running back with the Toronto Argonauts of the Canadian Football League.

Symons played for the University of Colorado Buffaloes between 1962 and 1964. He was a versatile player, running, leading the team in receptions, punt returns and kickoff returns, and doing some punting.

In 1965 he was drafted by the NFL's Green Bay Packers in the 6th round (80th overall) and by the AFL's Kansas City Chiefs in the 20th round (157 overall.) He attended two Green Bay training camps, and almost made the team, but an injury led Vince Lombardi to cut him.

The British Columbia Lions took a chance on Symons in 1966. He only played 10 games, and most were at defensive safety, and rushing for only 20 yards, as the Lions were not convinced his knee injury had fully healed. At the end of the seasons the Lions were eager to get the rights to (the eventual CFL great) Jim Young. Bill Symons was the second player in the trade, and he was off to the Toronto Argonauts.

Toronto, with their new head coach Leo Cahill, was determined to turn their losing ways around, and in 1967 Symons was given a chance. He did not disappoint, picking up 349 and positioning himself as a starter.

Everything came together in 1968, when he rushed for 1,107 yards. He was the first Boatman, ever, to top the 1000 yard plateau. Added to this record was his CFL's Most Outstanding Player Award, again a first for any Double Blue player, and an all star at running back. The only thing that was missing was a trip to the Grey Cup, something that all Argonaut fans craved (waiting since 1952.) Additionally, Symons turned down an offer from the NFL's Denver Broncos (in his home state) to stay in Toronto.

The trip to the Grey Cup came in 1971. Symons just missed another 1,000 yard season twice, with 905 yards in 1969 and 908 in 1970, as injuries just held him back. Then in 1971 Cahill decided to go with a new flashy star running back, Leon McQuay, known as "X-ray." Symons unhesitatingly turned to being a great blocking back, still gaining 418 yards in the bargain. Unfortunately, their trip to the 59th Grey Cup ended in classic heartbreak, as the very same star, Leon McQuay, fumbled the ball deep in Calgary territory in the final minutes to lose the game.

Being a blocking back, Symons' final two season saw reduced statistics, as he rushed for 235 yards in 1972 and 358 in 1973.

Symons called it quits after 1973 to donate more time to his successful AFA Forest Products. He also has a farm. He was inducted into the Canadian Football Hall of Fame in 1997.
