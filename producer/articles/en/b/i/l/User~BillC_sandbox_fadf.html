KO-DANG Cho Man-Sik (Korean: 조만식; pen name: Ko-Dang, "Lofty House") was born in South Pyongyang on 1 February 1883. In his youth he was an activist within Korea's Christian community, but with Japan's 1910 annexation of Korea he became increasingly involved with his country's independence movement. His participation in the 1919 Sam-Il protest marches led to his arrest and detention, along with tens of thousands of other Koreans. After his release, he dedicated himself to non-violent resistance to the occupation, a stance which earned him the epithet “The Gandhi of Korea”.[1] He advocated self-sufficiency for the nation, and formed a number of commercial enterprises intended to encourage Koreans to buy home-produced goods and so instil a sense of nationalism.

In August 1945, with the Japanese surrender imminent, Cho was approached by the Japanese governor of Pyongyang and asked to organise a committee to maintain stability in the power vacuum that would inevitably follow. He agreed to co-operate, and formed governing councils throughout the north; they generally being composed of right-wing nationalists opposed to communism.[2] The Soviet Union arrived in Pyongyang in the days following the Japanese surrender. Cho was forced to reorganise his party, and accept more communists onto the councils.[3]

Allied talks on the future of Korea in Moscow in December 1945 proposed a four-power trusteeship for a period of several years, nominally aimed at establishing Korean independence.[4] For Cho, this would result in excessive Soviet (and therefore communist) influence over his country, and he refused to co-operate. In January 1946 he was forced to resign from his position, and was soon placed under house arrest by the Soviets, from which position he continued to oppose the communists.[5] Later transferred to a prison, he was executed on 15 October 1950, shortly after the outbreak of the Korean War. Cho's removal opened the way for Kim Il Sung to consolidate his power in the north,[6] a position he was able to hold for 48 years until his death in 1994.

In 1970 Cho's deeds gained posthumous recognition when he was was awarded the Order of Merit for National Foundation and a Republic of Korea medal by the South Korean government.
