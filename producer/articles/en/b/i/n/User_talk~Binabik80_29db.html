[edit] upsilon transcribed as f or v

I'm pretty sure this article should be at Euagoras; I'm not aware of any other instances of upsilon being transliterated as v. Are there any objections? Binabik80 16:49, 1 May 2005 (UTC)

Certainly there are many instances of upsilon being transcribed as v or f, although I think that's normally when transcribing Modern Greek rather than Classical Greek. Strictly speaking, it's transcription rather than transliteration, because the choice of which letter in the Latin alphabet is used, depends on the pronunciation. Michael Hardy 02:10, 15 July 2005 (UTC)
PS: I'd bet large sums of money -- maybe even 50 cents, that nearly all cases in which the letter f appears in transcriptions from Modern Greek are occurences of upsilon. The other Greek letter that gets pronounced that way is Ï†, and that's transcribed as ph. Michael Hardy 02:12, 15 July 2005 (UTC)

[edit] BVM

Some titles include the definite article, some don't. For example The Guardian, The Irish Times, the Irish Independent, etc. It depends on the formal name and whether in its registered form it includes or excludes the definite article. Blessed Virgin Mary does not use the definite article has part of the title. However The Blessed Virgin uses the definite article because in Catholicism in mediÃ¦val times some individuals who were honoured for their virginity were referred to as a blessed virgin. They were distinguished from the BVM by the non use of the definite article and lowercasing of the title. In contrast the BVM was described using the definite article as part of the title and by uppercasing. Though the use of the blessed virgins reference has long since died out for the other, it has stuck with the BVM. WP has a policy of not including definite articles unless they are part of the title. In those cases the The with the capital is used, usually bolded, in the first refererence, but then used grammatically, not in forms of theoretical title, in the rest of the text. (Monarchs for example, are often described as The King in the opening paragraph, but as ' . . . the King . . . ' elsewhere in the text.)
