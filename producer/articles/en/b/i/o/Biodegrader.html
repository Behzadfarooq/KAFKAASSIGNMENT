A biodegrader is a category of in-vessel composters popularized in Japan. It is possible that the earliest developer of this technology for commercial purposes was the precursor of the [1]Shinyo Industries Co Ltd., of Fukuyama, Japan. Shintaro Takenaka developed a biodegrader in-vessel composter for Tsuneishi Shipping Co., consisting of a temperature-controlled vessel containing sawdust rotated by a screw. Food waste is simply added and broken down entirely to carbon dioxide, water, and inorganic residuals. Soil microbes may also be added. Takenaka then formed Shinyo and went on to build a line of smaller machines with processing capacities of 1 kg food waste per day and up. Special installations include units for daycares that process diapers. Shinyo also market beads instead of sawdust, but these are problematic odour-wise. Failings include poor machine quality, odour, and limited distribution, but the basic function of this machine is perhaps superior to many of the new models.

This system was imitated by [2] Mitsui Homeswhen they developed the MAM machine, much the same as the Shinyo machine. Mitsui developed this machine in response to new home buyers despising the pest and smell problems associated with source-separated food waste that is commonplace in Japan. Although Mitsui Homes poured significant resources into the machine, this division was eventually closed.

During this time, the regular stable of consumer electronic giants started to make their own range of kitchen composters, some of them using sawdust, some of them using expensive and perhaps dangerous electrical drying functions. [3]Sharp has paid special attention to the real or imagined problem of airborne particulates from these composters. These have been moderately successful owing to generous rebate schemes offered by Japanese local governments eager to minimize food waste. The marked reluctance of these same companies to bring the units overseas perhaps lies in perceived liability issues by managers whose real interest lies in easier products.

As an aside, the government rebates may be almost entirely owing to the jungle crow [4] 'karasu'; this tremendously intelligent bird can defeat even the most elaborate food waste container, and generally make a mess at the waste collection stations typical of Japan. Preventing karasu incursion is a major industry in itself. Karasu are a good example of Gaian principles.

A major stumbling block to marketing these machine has been the necessity of adding microbes, particularly in the export market where microbes are rigorously controlled. Another tiny Japanese company overcame this problem by marketing a well-designed biodegrader as a biotoilet. In this instance, the microbes are supplied by the user, and the operating conditions set to match knowledge of human pathogen kill learned in the horse-manure mushroom industry, general composting, and sewage treatment. These latter biodegraders have been so successful in Japan that they are posing a challenge to traditional water-based sanitation, particularly septic tanks. The beauty of these machines is that they can be shipped empty and filled with local sawdust and, through use, microbes.

Biodegraders can be classified separately from in-vessel composters in their purpose is to make as little compost as possible, whereas the in-vessel composters (mechanized or otherwise) rely on a balanced input of carbon and nitrogen-rich substances to produce a steady stream of compost. Biodegraders differ from most compost toilets in that they require almost no input of amendments such as peat, have no drainage, and are sized and powered depending on posited frequency of use.
