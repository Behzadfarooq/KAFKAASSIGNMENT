[edit] Poetry

  • The Bab Ballads, a collection of comic verse published roughly between 1865 and 1871.

[edit] Short Stories

  • Foggerty's Fairy and Other Tales, a collection of short stories and essays, mainly from before 1874.

[edit] Plays

(Stage works that were important to Gilbert's career or artistic development, in chronological order, excluding those listed under other headings below)

  • Dulcamara, or the Little Duck and the Great Quack (1866 musical spoof of Donizetti's L'elisir d'amore). Gilbert's first successful work for the theatre.
  • An Old Score (1869)
  • The Princess (1870). Musical farce; the precursor to Princess Ida.
  • The Palace of Truth (1870). The first of Gilbert's "Fairy Comedies".
  • Pygmalion and Galatea (1871). Gilbert's most successful work up to this time. A reinterpretation of the Pygmalion myth in which the innocent former statue, Galatea, is unable to bear the cynicism and jealousies of the real world.
  • The Wicked World (1873)
  • The Happy Land (1873). This work was briefly banned for its sharp satire of government ministers.
  • The Realm of Joy (1873). Set in the box office of a thinly-disguised The Happy Land, it satirises the public who come to scandalous plays and the Lord Chamberlain's censorship of plays.
  • Charity (1874). Concerns Victorian attitudes towards sex outside of marriage. Anticipates the "problem plays" of Shaw, Ibsen, et al. in the 1890s.[1]
  • Topsyturveydom (1874). This one-act operetta concerns a country that is the opposite of England. It contains a lot of political satire.
  • Sweethearts (play) (1874)
  • Tom Cobb (1875). This was possibly Gilbert's funniest farce.
  • Broken Hearts (1875). The last of Gilbert's "fairy comedies", this was one of Gilbert's favourite plays.
  • Princess Toto (1876). A three-act opera. Gilbert's last collaboration with Frederic Clay.
  • Engaged (1877). Probably the most famous of Gilbert's non-Sullivan works for the theatre.
  • Gretchen (play) (1879)
  • Foggerty's Fairy (1881)
  • Brantinghame Hall (1888) a drama. Gilbert's biggest flop, it sent producer Rutland Barrington into bankruptcy.
  • The Hooligan (1911). Gilbert's last play, written in a new, serious style.

[edit] German Reed Entertainments

Gilbert wrote six one-act musical entertainments for the German Reeds between 1869 and 1875. They were successful in their own right and also helped form Gilbert's mature style as a dramatist. These include:

  • No Cards (1869)
  • Ages Ago (1869). Gilbert's first collaboration with Frederic Clay, and his first hit with the German Reeds, running for 350 performances.
  • Our Island Home (1870)
  • A Sensation Novel (1871)
  • Happy Arcadia (1872)
  • Eyes and No Eyes (1875)

[edit] The Gilbert and Sullivan operas

All of these Savoy Operas are full-length two-act works, except for Trial by Jury, which was a one-act piece, and Princess Ida, which is in three acts. All of these works continue to be regularly performed today except for Thespis, most of the music to which is lost (although it is occasionally produced using scores written or arranged by others).

  • Thespis (1871)
  • Trial by Jury (1875)
  • The Sorcerer (1877)
  • H.M.S. Pinafore (1878)
  • The Pirates of Penzance (1879; 1880)
  • Patience (1881)
  • Iolanthe (1882)
  • Princess Ida (1884)
  • The Mikado (1885)
  • Ruddigore (1887)
  • The Yeomen of the Guard (1888)
  • The Gondoliers (1889)
  • Utopia, Limited (1893)
  • The Grand Duke (1896)

[edit] Later operas without Sullivan

Though not as popular as the works with Sullivan, a few of Gilbert's later works arguably have stronger plots than the last two Gilbert and Sullivan operas.[2]

  • The Mountebanks (1892). The lozenge plot (with music by Alfred Cellier).
  • Haste to the Wedding (1892). A flop (with music by George Grossmith).
  • His Excellency (1894) (with music by Osmond Carr). Gilbert's best libretto outside of the G&S operas. Gilbert felt that if Sullivan had set it, the piece would have been "another Mikado".
  • Fallen Fairies (1909). Gilbert's last opera (with music by Edward German) was a failure.
