Responding to a comment

You left a note on my talk page that I thought warranted a response: "Please be careful when saying "rvv" - rvv means "revert vandalism" and any effort to improve the encyclopedia - even if ill-conceived (like the thing about the T next to the V) is not vandalism. Also, despite what you see on Sabre, VT hasn't been known as VPI in 35 years."

My response is that I interpreted the additions regarding the vandalism of UVA's facilities as being vandalism here because of the timing of the additions, the fact that it was done right after ESPN mentioned it, the fact that it was verbatim in two articles, and the fact that it was not put in any VPI-related articles (which, if it were such a big part of a rivalry, it would be). My use of "rvv" in that context was a good-faith summary of how I interpreted the intent of the edit given the totality of the circumstances. It didn't have anything to do with the act itself being childish and stupid so much as it did with the combination of things I just noted; I mean, the thing in Scott Stadium was in 2005 and someone suddenly decides to add it the day after it was shown on ESPN. Fishy timing, that's all.

Regarding VPI, the school's name is still "Virginia Polytechnic Institute and State (sic) University," so "VPI" remains fully appropriate even if their administration has tried to distance themselves from it. I'm not going to revert it because I recognize you as a serious editor who makes good contributions and because I don't have a problem with you doing it, but I felt that it bears mention that I don't feel that I'm bound by the fact that VPI's administration would prefer us to call it something else, that's all.

---1995hoo 18:36, 2 March 2007 (UTC)

I realize that you used "rvv" in good faith ... I was just making the suggestion that it's inappropriate except in the case of "simple" vandalism, eg, adding "BOBBY IS A MORON" or something. Any edit that might at all be reviewable should have an edit summary. As for VPI, Wikipedia generally refers to institutions, people, etc, by their own self-identified name. Tech has not self-identified as VPI in 35 years. You can't go into the store and buy a VPI hat. Nobody outside of the Commonwealth of Virginia would recognize the name VPI. I realize that on Sabre, it's common to refer to us by our old-school name, but Wikipedia isn't a message board. But regardless of that, anyone should be referred to by their self-identified name or the most commonly accepted name. --BigDT 18:44, 2 March 2007 (UTC)

Someone blocked him indef. Is that the right call?Rlevse 13:32, 6 March 2007 (UTC)
Goodness ... take a look at his contributions. He left a lot of mess to clean up. He inserted a bunch of photos at the beginning of articles where it didn't make sense to add them. Going back over his last 20 or so edits, I don't see much that's useful - just a big mess to clean up. Indefinitely blocked doesn't necessarilly mean forever banned. If he is willing to stop and discuss what he is doing and address the problems with it, he can always be unblocked at some future time ... but as it is, I would consider this an emergency block. --BigDT 00:30, 7 March 2007 (UTC)
So uploading photos that don't add anything is blockable, that's what I want to know. It's sort of like making nonsense edits, I guess, as opposed to the type of vandalism where there's profanity and such.Rlevse 02:39, 7 March 2007 (UTC)
Essentially, yes. It really depends on what they are uploading. If they are uploading useless images, but otherwise making good faith edits, I would discuss the issue with them, rather than blocking. Explain that Wikipedia is not free webhosting and they should upload the contents of their camera to flickr instead of Wikipedia. If someone is just uploading their porn collection, though, I would block on sight. In this case, the user was repeatedly uploading untagged images and adding them inline in articles where they didn't belong. Personally, I would have tried to have a conversation with the person rather than blocking ... and I'm not thrilled that the first action taken was a block ... but it's one of those things that I'm not going to jump up and down too loudly about. I would say that uploading unsourced photos is not the same as a nonsense edit because the photos were, unquestionably, uploaded in good faith ... probably this is a very young child who just doesn't know what they are doing ... but if they are going to ignore the warnings, something has to be done. --BigDT 03:16, 7 March 2007 (UTC)
