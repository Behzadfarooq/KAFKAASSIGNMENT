Bicycle culture

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Many cities contain subcultures of bicycle enthusiasts, including racers, bicycle messengers, bicycle transportation activists, bicycle mechanics, and cyclists who share an interest in peace and justice activism or various counter-culture groups. Group activities may involve competitive cycling, fun rides, or even civil disobedience, which is how motorists may characterize the activities of Critical Mass. Some groups work to promote bicycle transportation (community bicycle program); others fix up bicycles to give to children or the homeless, or to poor people in other countries (Bikes Not Bombs).

(Midnight Ridazz) is a group of bicycle enthusiasts who ride every second Friday of the month in Los Angeles California. Riding in numbers exceeding 1000 cyclists, this rides only political motive is to inspire more people to ride bicycles. Ride on.MidnightRidazz.com

Bicycle culture includes arts and crafts (both handmade and mass manufactured), film, music, books and magazines. Thomas Stevens with his narrative "Around the World on a Bicycle," Mark Twain with his essay "Taming the Bicycle" and H. G. Wells with his novel The Wheels of Chance were early contributors to bicycle culture.

Bicycle magazines and organizations give yearly awards to cities for being "bicycle friendly". Cities known as such, including Amsterdam, Chicago, Copenhagen, Kyoto, Ljubljana and Portland, Oregon feature "bicycle culture" as part of their urban identity.
