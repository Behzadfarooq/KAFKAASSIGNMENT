Butt Report

The Butt Report was a report prepared during World War II which revealed the wide-spread failure of bombers to deliver their payloads to the correct target.

At the start of the war, RAF Bomber Command had no real means of determining the success rate of its operations. Crews would return with only their own word as to the amount of damage caused, or even if they had bombed the correct target at all. The Air Ministry demanded that a method of verifying these claims was developed, and by 1941 cameras mounted under bombers, triggered by the bomb release, were being fitted.

[edit] The report

A personal friend of Churchill, D. M. Butt of the War Cabinet Secretariat, was given the task of assessing over 600 target photos and comparing them with crews' claims. The results, in August 1941, were a shock to all.

  1. On any given night, around one third of all aircraft returned having not bombed the primary target.
  2. Of the rest of the attacking forces, only one third of managed to bomb within 5 miles of the aiming point. When bombing in the heavily defended Ruhr Valley, only 10% managed even this.
  3. Moonlight appeared to be indispensable - on moonlit nights two crews in five managed fives miles or closer. On moonless nights this fell as low as one in fifteen.

[edit] The effects of the report

The truth about the failure of Bomber Command shook everyone, and afterwards no-one ever quite trusted Bomber Command claims of success. Bomber Command was forced to search for new methods of guiding aircraft to the target, of marking targets, and of defending the bombers en route.

Harris mistrusted technology, but slowly various technological solutions were developed: Oboe, a blind bombing aid; H2S, a ground mapping radar; GEE, a pulsed-radar navigation aid, and various other devices, such as Boozer, which gave warning of Searchlight Radar illumination, and Monica, which gave warning of illumination by the German night fighter radar Lichenstien. Window, the use of metallic strips to confuse enemy radar, was used towards the end, and specialist crew leaders such as Bombing Leader and Navigation Leader were introduced.

One effect of the Butt Report was to provide another prod towards the policy of area bombardment. The RAF was basically divided into two factions, the faction that propsed the carpet bombing of Germany to break German morale, and the faction that believed precision bombing of military targets was the key to defeating Germany. Harris was firmly an Area Bombing stalwart, as was his superior officer Portal. Both men were of the Trenchard School of tactics, developed in Mesopotamia in the early 1920s, called then "Air Control".

The "Men at the Ministry", officers such as Bufton and the Minister of Production, Sir Stafford Cripps, were horrified by the collateral damage of Area bombing and a perpetual war between Harris and the Ministry was an ongoing feature of Harris's tenure as head of Bomber Command. Eventually, however, Harris was forced to support D-Day by precision bombing of various targets, such as shipyards, bridges, rail yards and oil plants, but only after a direct order by Portal. Harris called these precision raids "panacea targets".

Harris underestimated his crews in the final analysis: the Pathfinder Force created against his wishes improved bombing accuracy of large raids hugely, and some crews, such as Guy Gibson and Mickey Martin, two of the most outstanding Heavy Bomber pilots of the war, performed extraordinary feats of target marking, such as when Gibson marked the Dortmund-Ems canal using a Mosquito at only sixty feet.
