Burke-Gilman Trail

The Burke-Gilman Sammamish Trail is a 27 mi (43.2 km) bike path and recreational rail trail of the King County Regional Trail System, Washington. The Burke-Gilman segment is managed by the City of Seattle south of NE 145th Street. The trail begins at 8th Avenue NW in Ballard and follows along the Ship Canal and north along Lake Washington. At Blyth Park in Bothell the trail becomes the Sammamish River Trail and continues for 10 miles to Marymoor King County Park, Redmond, on Lake Sammamish. [1] It runs 14.1 mi (22.5 km) between Ballard and Tracy Owen Station in Kenmore (its initial eastern end), or 17 mi to Blyth Park. The Seattle Parks Department considers the Burke-Gilman segment of the trail to end in Kenmore;[2] King County considers that the segments divide in Bothell,[1] Judge Burke and Daniel Gilman's original Road east ran past Snoqualmie Falls, before North Bend.[3]

[edit] Route and extent

The trail is a substantial part of the 90 miles (144 km) of signed bike routes in Seattle,[4] 175 miles (280 km) of the King County Trails System, planned to become 300 miles (480 km).[5] The newest segment of the Burke-Gilman part, opened in July 2005, runs for 0.7 mile (1.1 km) from NW 60th Street and Seaview Avenue NW to the Ballard Locks.[6] The main trail resumes at 11th Avenue NW and NW 45th Street and runs 17 miles to Blyth Park in Bothell. There, it becomes the Sammamish River Trail segment, which parallels the Sammamish River for 10 miles to Redmond.[1]

Currently the trail runs along the Fremont Cut, Lake Union (an old freight depot remains visible at the foot of Stone Way), and through the University of Washington campus. After passing the University Village shopping center, the trail heads up through northeast neighborhoods, alongside the Hawthorne Hills, Laurelhurst and Windermere neighborhoods; through the Sand Point neighborhood, passing Magnuson Park, then alongside Lake Washington from just before the Matthews Beach and Cedar Park neighborhoods of the former Lake City, continuing on through Lake Forest Park and Kenmore to Bothell.[2] The trail throughout is nearly level with few large intersection crossings--it's a railroad right-of-way.

[edit] Origins and extensions

See also: Ravenna, Seattle, Washington#Cowen Park–Ravenna Park and Ravenna Creek

Out for Opening Day on the Burke-Gilman, circa 1887.[7]

The trail can trace its origins to the founding of the Seattle, Lake Shore & Eastern Railroad on 15 April 1885, by ten men headed by Thomas Burke and Daniel Gilman. In its heyday Burke and Gilman's road extended from Downtown north to Arlington and east to Rattlesnake Prairie above Snoqualmie Falls. Taken over by the Northern Pacific Railway in 1890 (or 1892),[8] the line became part of the Burlington Northern Railroad in 1970, and was abandoned in 1971. In 1978, the first 12.1 miles (19.4 km) of the right-of-way, from Seattle's Gas Works Park to Kenmore's Tracy Owen Station, was opened as a public trail and named after the founders of the railroad.[9] From Jerry Wilmot Park, south Woodinville, the King County Regional Trail continues along much of the old Burke and Gilman SLS&E line as the Snoqualmie Valley Trail, past Duvall, through Carnation to Snoqualmie Falls, with the rail trail continuing east from North Bend as a cross-state trail from Iron Horse State Park.[10] The Snoqualmie Falls-North Bend link of Burke and Gilman's old SLS&E has become the line of the Northwest Railway Museum.[11]

As of 2006, there are extensions of the Burke-Gilman Trail at its western end: connecting the short and long segments between the Ballard Locks and 11th Avenue, and a northern extension along Shilshole Bay from NW 60th Street to Golden Gardens Park, and planning for connecting between the Ballard Locks and downtown Ballard at 11th Avenue NW and NW 45th Street.[6] A major point of contention regarding the remaining "missing link" project is the industrial nature of the Salmon Bay waterfront, through which this portion of the trail would pass. Many business owners are concerned about the safety and liability issues inherent in the convergence of trains, trucks, cyclists, and pedestrians, while the city and citizens groups claim the dangers are being exaggerated.[12] The trail overall can at times be busy and even crowded.[9]

Note: All Seattle "urban trails" provide an off-road path or sidewalk for pedestrians (separated from motor vehicles); for bicyclists, "urban trails" consist of off-road shared use paths, on-street striped bike lanes, and signed routes in the street right-of-way,[2] where due caution is warranted.

[edit] Neighborhoods

The trail intersects the following:

  • Seattle neighborhoods from western terminus east and north:
    • Ballard
    • Fremont
    • Northlake (also known as south Wallingford)
    • University District
    • Trail runs alongside:
      • University Village shopping center
      • Hawthorne Hills
      • Laurelhurst and
      • Windermere neighborhoods
      • Sand Point neighborhood, passing Magnuson Park
    • Lake City district of neighborhoods
      • Matthews Beach and
      • Cedar Park.[13]
  • Suburban Seattle towns, continuing north and east:
    • Lake Forest Park
    • Kenmore
    • Bothell
    • Woodinville
    • Redmond
