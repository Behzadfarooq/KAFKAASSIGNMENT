Bulgarisation (or Bulgarization, Bulgarianisation or Bulgarianization; Bulgarian: побългаряване or българизация) is a term used to describe a cultural change in which something ethnically non-Bulgarian is made to become Bulgarian.

The term is often used in relation to Turks, Romanians, Aromanians, Macedonians and Greeks.

According to the 1926 census, there were 79,728 Balkan Romance speakers in Bulgaria. Most of them lived in the north-eastern part (Dobruja)[dubious — see talk page], with some Aromanians in the southern part. They were divided into:

  • 69,080 Romanians
  • 5,324 Aromanians
  • 3,777 Kutsovlachs (the same as Aromanians)
  • 1,551 Tsintsars (the same as Aromanians)

According to the 1965 census, there were 6,000 Romanians. This result can be explained in the context of the population exchanges that took place in 1940. See Treaty of Craiova. According to the historian Kemal H. Karpat, during the Communist period of Bulgarian history, Macedonians, Turks, Greeks, and the Timoc Vlachs/Romanians in Bulgaria were unilaterally Bulgarised. (Karpat 2004)

The president of the Bulgarian Rudari Vlachs' (Romanian-speaking Roma people) Association claimed that at the last census (of 2001) they wanted to declare themselves Romanians, but the Bulgarian authorities forced them to choose either "Vlachs" or "Bulgarians", as the Bulgarian government counts Romanians and Vlachs separately. (Ziua, 5 May 2004)

[edit] Turks

During the Communist period of Bulgarian history, the Turkish minority in the south-east of the country was forced to change their names from Turkish to Bulgarian in 1984-1985, during the Todor Zhivkov regime. This violation of human rights met forceful resistance from large-scale protests, international pressure and cases of terrorism. Following the collapse of the Zhivkov regime, people were once again free to revert back to previous names or adopt new Islamic/Turkish names.

[edit] Greeks

During the second World War, the government of Bulgaria, allied with Nazi Germany pursued a policy of Bulgarisation in the areas of northern Greece and the region of Macedonia that it occupied. The use of the Bulgarian language was made compulsory, and in the words of Yiannis D. Stefanidis, "a reign of violence and terror was imposed in an effort to provoke a mass exodus of the inhabitants, those of refugee origin in particular". [1]

[edit] Gagauz

According to Centre National de la Recherche Scientifique, people from the Gagauz ethnic group remaining in Bulgaria were noted to have been Bulgarised at the end of the 19th Century.[2]
