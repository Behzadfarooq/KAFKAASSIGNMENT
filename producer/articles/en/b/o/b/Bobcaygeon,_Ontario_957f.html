Bobcaygeon is a community of approximately 2500 people, nestled along the Trent-Severn Waterway in the City of Kawartha Lakes, east-central Ontario, Canada.

Bobcaygeon was incorporated as village in 1876, and became known as the "Hub of the Kawarthas". Its name comes from the Mississaguga native word bob-ca-je-wan-unk, which means "shallow rapids". The first lock in the Trent-Severn Waterway was built in Bobcaygeon in 1833. [1]

[edit] History

By the early 1830s, the government of Upper Canada had completed its survey of the Township of Verulam and the area began to attract settlers. Thomas Need, who arrived in 1832 is recognized as one of the earliest settlers of Verulam Township and is the founder of Bobcaygeon. With his purchase of 3000 acres (12 kmÂ²) of land, Need built a sawmill, gristmill, and the first store.

Although the provincial government had reserved and surveyed a town site on the North Bank of Bobcaygeon River (Rokeby), Need laid out streets and plotted lots on the island, which became Bobcaygeon. Today, Bobcaygeon occupies both sides of the Bobcaygeon River, after the village of Bobcaygeon and the village of Rokeby were joined.

In 1844, Thomas Need sold his profitable business interests to Mossom Boyd. Boyd and his sons eventually built up a logging enterprise that was recognized as the third largest logging operation in Upper Canada.

Bobcaygeon, with a population of about 1,000, was incorporated as a village by a Victoria County by-law of 1876.

Today, Bobcaygeon's chief industry is tourism, particularly fishing-related tourism, and it is a service centre for farmers and cottagers (i.e., those who own recreational properties) in the area.

[edit] Facts and figures

Boyd Arch.
  • The town itself is situated on three islands, along with the main land.
  • The "Boyd Arch" still stands, after being transfered stone by stone to its new home in front of the Bobcaygeon Library.
  • In 1998, the Canadian rock and roll band The Tragically Hip immortalized Bobcaygeon in their Juno Award-winning hit single that takes its name from the small village.
  • The Bobcaygeon Independent was the local newspaper, established in 1869. Its last issue was printed in December 2004.
  • The Promoter (another local publication) published its first issue on March 15, 1991.
  • The village's Canada Day festivities are held at Tommy Anderson Park, and its fireworks display is held at Beach Park.
