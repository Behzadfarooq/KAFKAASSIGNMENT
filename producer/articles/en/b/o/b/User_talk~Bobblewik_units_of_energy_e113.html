I am not aware that any astronomers use attojoules rather than electron volts as a unit of energy, and I'm not convinced that the layperson would find the aJ a particularly useful unit either. Thanks for the pointer on my talk page (I was watching here too). My comment was really to point out that an astronomer would use eV (or, rarely, nm/pm) not aJ (or frequency). To convert, you need to use E = hν and c = λν, so E = hc / λ.
Thus, a photon with energy of 1 eV would have a wavelength of around  m (1 µm) and a frequency of  Hz. (I hope my maths and calculator are working today!)
Wavelength is used instead of frequency because it involves fewer powers of 10: a photon of 1 keV would be around 1017 Hz but 10 − 9 m (1 nm) ).
Hope this helps! -- ALoan (Talk) 15:20, 3 Sep 2004 (UTC)

(I much prefer to express in <math> than <sup> because TeX-style mark-up is much easier to deal with; I also use &nbsp; between numbers and units to prevent unfortunate linebreaks, but perhaps it is just me.)
For astronomy, the units depend on the context: radio astronomers will use wavelengths as it is handy to talk about cm or m, but they also use frequencies because they are in MHz and kHz. Once you get above GHz, frequencies start to look a bit odd, but wavelengths are still OK down past nm. Particle physicists also use eV (or, rather, eV/c²) as then they can use E = mc2 and use natural units where mass and energy are measured in the same equivalent.
