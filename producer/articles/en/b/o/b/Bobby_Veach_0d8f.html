Bobby Veach

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Bobby Veach

Robert Hayes Veach (June 29, 1888 - August 7, 1945) was an American left fielder in Major League Baseball who played fourteen seasons for the Detroit Tigers (1912-23), Boston Red Sox (1924-25), New York Yankees (1925) and Washington Senators (1925).

Veach was born in St. Charles, Kentucky. He made his major league debut on September 6, 1912 and played in each of the remaining 23 games of the season, batting .342. He became the Tigers' fulltime left fielder the following season when Davy Jones left the team; Veach played in an outfield with future Hall of Famers Sam Crawford and Ty Cobb. A left-handed batter, Veach led the American League in doubles (40) and RBI (112) in the 1915 season, in which the Tigers won 100 games and finished second behind the Red Sox.

By 1917, Harry Heilmann replaced Crawford as the newest outfielder among Veach and Cobb. That season Veach led all American Leaguers in RBI (103) and did so again in 1918 (78). He had the most hits (191), doubles (45) and triples (17) in 1919 while also posting a career-high batting average of .355, which was second only to teammate Cobb's .384 average. On September 17, 1920, he had six hits and hit for the cycle during a 12 inning game. In 1921 he had career-highs in RBI (126) and home runs (16) during Cobb's first year as manager.

After twelve seasons with the Tigers, Veach joined the Red Sox in 1924 and achieved 2,000 career hits. In his last season in 1925, he played for the Red Sox and Yankees before finishing with the Senators, whom he helped win the AL pennant. He played in two games of the 1925 World Series, totaling two pinch hit plate appearances and one sacrifice fly-RBI.

In 1,821 career games, Veach batted .310 with 2,063 hits, 393 doubles, 147 triples, 64 home runs, 1,166 RBI, 195 stolen bases, 571 walks, .370 on-base percentage, .442 slugging percentage, 2,942 total bases and 271 sacrifice hits.

He died in Detroit, Michigan at the age of 57.

[edit] Career Highlights

  • Had 2 200+ Hit seasons (1921 and 1922)
  • Had 6 100+ RBI seasons (1915, 1917 and 1919-22)
  • Led American League in Doubles (40) and RBI (112) in 1915.
  • Led American League in RBI (103) and Hit by Pitch (9) in 1917.
  • Led American League in RBI (78) in 1918.
  • Led American League in Hits (191), Doubles (45) and Triples (17) in 1919.
  • Led American League in Games (154) in 1920.
  • Led American League in Games (155) in 1922.
  • Ranks 56th on MLB All-Time Triples List (147).
  • Ranks 24th on MLB All-Time Sacrifice Hits List (271).
