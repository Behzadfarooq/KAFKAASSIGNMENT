--Lagarvelho 19:50, 2 February 2006 (UTC)--Lagarvelho 19:50, 2 February 2006 (UTC)I think this article should be kept in Wikipedia. Why? Fink may not be a "well-known" person, but he is well known among musicians and musicologists, and has done important work in the field of the origins of music. Whether one agrees or disagrees with his conclusions about this subject is another question, and whatever his political views, they are more or less irrelevant to his views on the earliest music and instruments


With all due respect, I beg to differ. Fink's "contribution" to musicology is essentially a single paper which was never accepted in a peer-reviewed journal. This is not to say that peer-reviewed papers are the only valid way to distribute academic work, but anyone making "significant" contributions to an academic field could, justifiably, be expected to have a solid bibliography of journal articles under his belt. Compare Fink's work with respected 20th C. musicologists and theorists such as Babbitt, Lewin, Forte, Perle, Gilbert, Berry, Cook, or Lester (just to name a handful, hardly a comprehensive enumeration) and Fink is simply not in this league.

As has already been discussed, a computer program composing music in 1995 is also hardly significant. The earliest computer compostion dates to 1957 (and Lejaren Hiller currently only has a stub article!). By the mid-90s thousands of computer-based compositions had been realized around the world and a new computer program would be what I would expect as an undergraduate term project from any of the dozens of schools with active music-technology programs.

Fink's work as a political activist might, possibly, justify an article, or perhaps as archaeology. I would not presume to judge significance in either of those arenas. But including Fink in Wikipedia for his musical work is not justificable. Pcastine 17:43, 15 April 2006 (UTC)
