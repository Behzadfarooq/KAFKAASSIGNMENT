Bob Ufer

Bob Ufer (born April 1, 1920; died October 26, 1981) was the lead broadcaster for the University of Michigan football team for 37 years, starting in 1944. An outstanding track and field athlete at Michigan, Ufer set eight freshman records. At the Big Ten Conference in 1942, he set the world indoor record in the 440m.

Bob Ufer broadcasting on WPAG (now WTKA) radio in Ann Arbor

He is known for his broadcasting accomplishments for the most part, as Michigan was known as "Meeechigan", one of several so-called 'Uferisms'. He also had the 'George Patton horn', which he sounded every time the Wolverines scored. Bo Schembechler shared a birthday with Bob, but Bob was 9 years older.

Ufer loved nothing more than his Wolverines, as he would scream and lose his voice during several broadcasts. One of his most classic moments came in the 1979 homecoming game against Indiana, when Michigan's Anthony Carter caught a touchdown pass with no time left to win it. Ufer went completely ballistic, overpowering the sound of the 106,000 fans in Michigan Stadium that day. Nothing could quell Bob's insane spirit for the dramatic and his animalistic inclination to exploit the microphone to voice the dramatic as only he could do. Bob often referred Michigan Stadium as 'The House that Yost dug, Crisler paid for, Canham carpeted, and Schembechler filled on Saturday afternoons'.

Ufer lost a long battle with cancer October 26, 1981, nine days after his last broadcast; at Ufer's funeral, former U of M defensive coordinator Jim Herrmann said, "Bob Ufer was Michigan football. That's what he lived and died for. I think he would have liked being described that way."

Ufer's son, also named Bob Ufer, was later commissioner of the International Hockey League.
