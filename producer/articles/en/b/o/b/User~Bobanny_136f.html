[edit] Bibliography

The following is a list of books I own relating to the history of British Columbia and Vancouver specifically. I thought I'd post them here as reference material in case other editors need a fact checked or a citation for an article, or a quote, and I'd be happy to look it up.

[edit] General BC histories

  • British Columbia: A History of the Province -- George Woodcock (1990) - haven't read most of it, but he imposes this weird Gandhianism on the bit on the depression
  • British Columbia: A History -- Margaret Ormsby (1958) - the classic history of the province, still holds up for the most part, IMO
  • The Rush for Spoils: The Company Province, 1871-1933 -- Martin Robin (1972) - political scientist, irreverent history of BC politics, fun to read
  • The West Beyond the West: A History of British Columbia -- Jean Barman (1991) - ask Skookum about this one

[edit] Specific BC histories

  • Historical Essays on British Columbia -- eds. J. Friesen and H. K. Ralston (1980) - Essays written at different periods of the province's history on a wide variety of subjects
  • Four Walls in the West: The Story of the British Columbia Penitentiary -- Jack David Scott (1984) - history of the institution, there's another one on Oakalla but I don't have it
  • White Canada Forever: Popular Attitudes and Public Policy toward Orientals in British Columbia -- W. Peter Ward (2002) - divided between anti-Chinese riots, Komagata Maru incident, and Japanese evacuation from the coast.

[edit] General Vancouver histories

  • Vancouver: Milltown to Metropolis -- Alan Morley (1974) - the first standard civic biography, I think this is the 3rd edition. Morley was a jounalist for the Province.
  • Vancouver -- Eric Nicol (1970) - more of a humorist than a historian, but still a useful book and fun to read, possibly a bit of a rejoinder to Morley's more reverent history

[edit] Vancouver specific

  • L. D.: Mayor Louis Taylor and the Rise of Vancouver -- Daniel Francis (2004) - excellent read, bio of the Mayor and the city
  • Mayor Gerry: The Remarkable Gerald Gratten McGeer -- David Ricardo Williams (1986) - piece of crud, but packed full of info
  • Vancouver's Chinatown: Racial Discourse in Canada, 1875-1980 -- Kay Anderson (1991) - user friendly despite the word "discourse" in the title, some theory but much of it is straight narrative
  • Gladiator of the Courts: Angelo Branca -- Vincent Moore (1981) - East end lawyer/BC supreme court judge, very anecdotal, a little disjointed at times, but good complement to any Vancouver legal history from 1930s to 1970s, written by a court reporter friend of Branca's
  • A Century of Service: The Vancouver Police Department, 1886-1986 -- Joe Swan (1986) - VPD history by one of their own, good read for an official history
  • The Mulligan Affair: Top Cop on the Take -- Ian Macdonald and Betty O'Keefe (1997) - 1955 Vancouver Police corruption scandal, more journalists cum local historians
  • Canadian Holy War: A Story of Clans, Tongs, Murder, and Bigotry -- " (2000) - Janet Smith murder case in 1920s
  • False Creek -- Rober K. Burkinshaw (1984) - development of False Creek
  • Exploring Vancouver: Ten Tours of the City and its Buildings -- Harold Kalman (1974) - architect, UBC prof I think, tours the city

[edit] Labour and left histories

  • No Power Greater: A Century of Labour in British Columbia -- Paul Phillips (1967) - the standard overview of BC labour history, old but still useful, downplays radicalism
  • Red Flags and Red Tape: The Making of a Labour Bureaucracy -- Mark Leier (1995)
  • Where the Fraser River Flows: The Industrial Workers of the World in British Columbia (1990) "
  • Rebel Life: The Life and Times of Robert Gosden, Revolutionary, Mystic, Labour Spy (1999) "
  • The Compassionate Rebel: Ernest E. Winch and his Times -- Dorothy G. Steeves (1960) - CCF bio
  • Reformers, Rebels, and Revolutionaries: The Western Canadian Radical Movement, 1899-1919 -- A. Ross McCormick (1977) - classic "western exceptionalist" history of radicalism
  • The Struggle for Social Justice in British Columbia: Helena Gutteridge, the Unknown Reformer -- Irene Howard (1992) - CCFer biography
  • Labour, Work, and Working People: A Working Class and Labour History Walking Tour, Vancouver, BC -- Juanita Nolan - some day I'll get around to doing articles on some of the places in this book

[edit] 1930s labour unrest/On-to-Ottawa Trek

  • When Freedom was Lost: The Unemployed, the Agitator, and the State -- Lorne Brown (1987) - Relief camp strike in Vancouver, On-to-Ottawa, and Regina Riot; he much prefers the unemployed and agitators to the state
  • All Hell Can't Stop Us: The On-to-Ottawa Trek and Regina Riot -- Bill Waiser (2003) - same topic as above, more liberal take (or nuanced, depending on your POV), well written
  • Fighting Heritage: Highlights of the 1930s struggle for jobs and militant unionism in British Columbia -- ed. Sean Griffin (1985) - Communist Party published, primary and secondary articles, including Steve Brodie's take on the 1938 Post Office "sitdowners" strike, Battle of Ballantyne Pier and the relief camp strike in 1935, and some other stuff.
  • Recollections of the On to Ottawa Trek -- Ronald Liversedge, ed. Victor Hoar (1973) - the classic first-hand account of the Trek and relief camp strike in Vancouver, plus loads of additional documents
  • The Little Band: The Clashes between the Communists and the political and legal establishment in Canada, 1928-1932 -- Lita-Rose Betcherman (198?) - good section on unemployed/police battles in early depression-era vancouver

[edit] Other resources

For a period of about 3 years, the government was publishing pdf copies of all Canadian masters and phd theses online, until the authors started getting pissed that an American company was then able to sell them without paying royalties; the practice was stopped when they asserted their copyright rights. Quite a large number of theses are still available online, and are, I believe, an under-used source of info, that both have academic cred and can be checked online by other Wikipedia editors/doubters. The search page is here, and below are some that I've found that are related to BC and Vancouver history, of varying degrees of quality and user-friendliness. Ones that might seem overly jargon-laden, esoteric, or theoretical to be accessible can often be a source of information and straight facts that might be hard to find otherwise if you have the patience to try and separate out what you might want. Other's are quite readable, and scanning the table of contents should indicate which it is.

  • Renisa Mawani, The 'Savage Indian' and the 'Foreign Plague': Mapping Racial Categories and Legal Geographies of Race in British Columbia, 1871-1925
  • Susan Mather, One of Many Homes: Stories of Dispossession from "Stanley Park"
  • Catherine Carstairs, 'Hop Heads' and 'Hypes': Drug Use, Regulation and Resistance in Canada, 1920-1961
  • Robert A. Campbell, Hotel Beer Parlours: Regulating Public Drinking and Decency in Vnacouver, British Columbia, 1925-1954
  • Jeffry D. Sommers, The Place of the Poor: Poverty, Space and the Politics of Representation in Downtown Vancouver, 1950-1997
  • Susan Roy, Making History Visible: Culture and Politics in the Presentation of Musqueum History
  • Charleen P. Smith, Regulating Prostitution in British Columbia, 1895-1930
  • Allan Rowe, The Surveillance of the Chinese in Canada during the Great War
  • Robert William Beck, "What is Not Justice Is Not Law": Patterns of Crime and Law Enforcement in Victoria, British Columbia, 1922-1940
  • Brian T. Thorn, "The Hand that Rocks the Cradle Rocks the World": Women in Vancouver's Communist Movement, 1935-1945

Of course there are a lot more online on a wider variety of topics.

[edit] Public Domain Photos in Canada/Vancouver

The law is pretty straight forward about what counts as a public domain image. This is what it says on the City of Vancouver Archives website:
The revisions to the Copyright Act (Bill C-32 An Act to Amend the Copyright Act) came into effect January 1, 1999. Under the old law, photographs were protected for 50 years from the time the photography was taken. The new law is the life of the creator plus 50 years. Photographs already in the public domain prior to January 1, 1999 will remain in the public domain (i.e. photographs taken up to December 31, 1948).[1]
A photograph is therefore in the public domain according to the date it was taken, and copyright over the image cannot be claimed by government or anyone else once something is public domain (American law looks to be more complicated because it has to do with date of publication as well as creation). Most archives with digitized image collections online are not forthcoming with this information, and some give the impression that they hold the copyright, presumably because they don't want to discourage people from obtaining permission and paying a fee to use the image. BC Archives is blatantly misleading because their online photos are contained within a border that claims copyright like this one, for example, which should be cropped off before being uploaded to Wikipedia. The federal Library and Archives Canada website is the only one that is upfront in labeling images whose copyright has expired. VPL has this disclaimer: In some cases the Vancouver Public Library, Special Collections, owns the physical print only and may not own copyright or other intellectual property rights to materials in its collection. Any possible copyright infringement is your responsibility.[2] (my emphasis) In other words, when they are claiming to hold the copyright for an old photograph, it is the physical photograph or negative they own, not the image itself. If you order an actual photo from them, you agree to not reproduce the image without permission and other conditions of usage and pay whatever permission fee they ask and are bound by that agreement, but this is not the case with online images. Therefore, there are a lot of historical photos available that we can use from these online databases:
  • City of Vancouver Archives
  • Library and Archives Canada
  • Vancouver Public Library Special Collections
  • British Columbia Archives
  • Glenbow Archives (Alberta)
  • The McCord Museum of Canadian History also has Vancouver images, but they embed the copyright right on the image, so you'd have to alter it to make it usable on Wikipedia.
According to the above information, absolutely I would challenge misleading copyright claims to public domain images in lieu of a legal reason that I'm overlooking (so if there's any lawyers out there...). Photos add a lot to articles, and historical photos in particular often contain information or illustrate the subject beyond what words alone can do, so I encourage people to take advantage of these sources. When uploading a photo to Wikipedia, you should note in the image description section the url of the online image and credit the source and their catalogue number, as a courtesy and to satisfy Wikipedia guidelines. The most suitable copyright tag is {{PD-old-50}}. This tag is not on the drop down list on the image upload page, but covers Canada and the US for images published before 1923 (English Wikipedia must satisfy US laws because that's where the server is located). {{PD-Canada}} can also be used (and covers any Crown Copyright issues, as far as I can tell) but you must still explain on the description the use rationale under US law. Wikipedia has the template {{PD-Canada}} for tagging such images.(from here.) This one works too: {{PD-old-50}}. Hope this helps Bobanny 17:23, 3 December 2006 (UTC)
