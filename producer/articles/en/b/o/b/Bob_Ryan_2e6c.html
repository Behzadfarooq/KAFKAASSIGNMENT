Bob Ryan

For the Washington, DC meteorologist, see Bob Ryan (meteorologist).

Robert P. Ryan (born February 21, 1946 in Trenton, New Jersey) is a longtime[1] columnist for the Boston Globe. He has been described as a basketball guru[2] and is well known for his coverage of the sport including his famous stories covering the Boston Celtics in the 1970's. After graduating from Boston College, Ryan started as a sports intern for the Globe on the same day as Peter Gammons.[citation needed] He is well known for his stuttering voice and has said of it "I don't like my own voice â€“ in fact I hate it."[3]

In 1997, Ryan won the Curt Gowdy Award from the Basketball Hall of Fame. In 2000 he was the AP National Sportswriter of the Year. He is also a member of the College Basketball Writers and New England Basketball Halls of Fame. On the show Around the Horn of which he is a regular contributor he has--by far--the highest winning percentage of everyone on the show. In addition Bill Simmons has called him "the best basketball writer ever." Paul Silas joked on Cold Pizza while Ryan was a guest, that all Bob Ryan's success was due to him.[4]

[edit] Life & Work

In the fall of 1969 a vacancy on the Globe's Celtics beat was created, and Ryan would take that job. While covering the Celtics Ryan would have a close relationship with the Celtics organization. Ryan would even go out to dinner with the team.[5] Ryan would sit at the press table 8 seats from the Celtics' bench, where colleagues referred to him as the "Commissioner," not unlike Peter Gammons's nickname. Boston Sports Media critic Bruce Allen[6] has said, "his passion is not faked."[7]

One night Hue Hollins, the referee, went to the press table to explain a call to Ryan during a time-out even though he was not obligated to. Another time Ryan wrote a column about the Washington Bullets' Rick Mahorn and how he played dirty under the hoop. When Mahorn was called for a foul Gene Shue, the Bullets' coach, turned around and said, "that's your fault, Bob Ryan, your fault!" Dennis Johnson was often annoyed with Ryan and would go up to the press table and say, "hey, Bob, keep it down. We got a game going on here" when Ryan sideline coached.[8] From Ryan's first column on Larry Bird headlined "Celtics draft Bird for oh what a future" to his last "Larry! Larry! Larry!" Ryan was always a fan of his and eventually co-authored a book with him.[9]

In Tom (Tommy) Heinsohn's book Give 'em the Hook, Heinsohn is negative towards Ryan. Ryan, who began writing for the Globe in Heinsohn's rookie season as a coach, would make friends with the players and vent their feelings towards Heinsohn, their fans, and their teammates, claims Heinsohn. Heinsohn didn't like how he didn't feel in control of his team. Heinsohn believes that Ryan started to "think of himself as another member of the family" and that he even started coaching the team through his beat stories. Heinsohn goes on to talk about Ryan's bloated ego and the fact that he was then thinking of himself as a basketball guru. Heinsohn also says while noting disapproval of Ryan that at the time anyone who lived in Boston and even remotely followed basketball read Bob Ryan.[10] In recent years Ryan has been less critical of Celtics coaches, including Doc Rivers, of whom he said, "I'm a Doc guy,"[11] even though he is not well liked in Boston.[12]

In 1982 Ryan would hand the torch that is the Globe Celtics beat to then-not well known Dan Shaughnessy, and later Jackie MacMullan. He did this in order to go to WCVB for a couple of years. Ryan ended up hating it and moved back to the Celtics beat in 1984 for two more seasons before getting promoted to general sports columnist in 1989.[13]

In May 2003, Ryan appeared on Sports Final, a local sports talk show airing on WBZ-TV. At that time, Ryan said that Joumana Kidd, the wife of New Jersey Nets guard Jason Kidd needed someone to "smack" her for taking her son T.J., then 4 years old, to NBA play-off night games where they could be taunted.[14] He accused Joumana of being an exhibitionist and using the child as a prop to get television time. The show's host, Bob Lobel, asked Ryan to retract his statement, but Ryan refused, saying:

LOBEL: You just donâ€™t want to smack her. You donâ€™t mean to say that.
RYAN: Alright.
LOBEL: I mean. Do you? Really, do you? Tell me you donâ€™t.
RYAN: Why should I say anything different here than I said all last playoffs last year?[15]

The comments struck a chord because in 2001, Joumana Kidd had been the victim of domestic abuse by her husband, Jason Kidd.[16] Ryan would return to Boston to meet with executives at the Globe. Ryan later apologized, but the Globe still suspended him and barred him from television for one month. "Four weeks took my breath away. But Iâ€™ll abide by it," he later said.[15]

Gerry Callahan of the Boston Herald would defend Ryan while others such as Herald Ombudsman Jim Baker did not.[15] The Boston Phoenix called it a "moment of temporary insanity."[17] Ryan later said, "I'm angry with myself forgetting in the heat of battle that the woman had been hit." [3]

Ryan would cover 20 NBA finals, 20 Final Fours, 9 World Series, five Super Bowls, the last 7 Olympics and many other events. In recent times Ryan has become less basketball-oriented and more general sports-oriented. He has also written for the Basketball Times. Bob votes for the Baseball Hall of Fame.[18] Ryan is a regular guest on radio; some of his appearances on the radio include:

  • The Bob Ryan Report on Loren and Wally (WROR): Every Thursday morning at 7:50 AM he goes on to give his opinion about Boston sports.
  • Ryan contributes to Michael Felger's show. He used to contribute to Dennis & Callahan on WEEI.[19]
  • Roundtable (featuring Ryan) on NPR's On Point to talk about the decline in basketball viewership.
  • The Tony Kornheiser Show; Ryan has appeared on the first episode of most of Kornheiser's show incarnations. Kornheiser calls Ryan "the quintessential American sportswriter".

Ryan is also a frequent guest host on ESPN's Pardon The Interruption.

At 60, Ryan wants his retirement from the job to be graceful: "Iâ€™m not bitter. I enjoy my job and I still think I do it well, but they are chipping away, chipping away and they are making it far less pleasurable. I want to get out when I feel like getting out. If you stay around too long, there is no way you can dictate your terms," he said. Ryan also asked, "How do you explain to Stephen A. Smith that he has no idea of the game and how much fun it was? He thinks he knows everything, but he will never know what I know about the Celtics."[5]

Ryan is a history major from Boston College (class of 1968). He went to high school at The Lawrenceville School from 1960 to 1964. He and his wife Elaine have two children and are proud grandparents of triplets. They have been married since 1969. Today Ryan lives in Hingham, Massachusetts. [3] The dedication page in Forty Eight Minutes, one of Ryan's books, says, "To Elaine Ryan: In the next life, maybe you'll get a nine-to-five man who makes seven figures." Ryan has also done humanitarian fundraisers for years to help inner-city teenagers with their educations. Ryan "can't stand fellow Globe columnist Dan Shaughnessy."[20]

[edit] Trivia

  • Like fellow Globe sports columnist Dan Shaughnessy he had a run-in with Red Sox GM Theo Epstein. In November of 2006 he had a small unfriendly exchange with Epstein saying "on behalf of an eager constituency, I hope the rumor (of a JD Drew deal) isn't true."[21] Curt Schilling would call into Dennis and Callahan and call out Ryan,[22] but some like NBC Sports's Tom Curran sided with Ryan.
  • Some of Ryan's favorite writers are Selena Roberts of the New York Times, Mark Whicker of the Orange County Register, Mike Lupica of the New York Daily News, Frank Deford of Sports Illustrated, Mike Vaccaro of the New York Post, Harvey Araton of the New York Times, Dan LeBatard of the Miami Herald and Jon Saraceno of USA Today.[3]
  • Ryan is partial to Marriott hotels.[3]
  • Ryan is a Baseball Hall of Fame voter.
  • Ryan believes that the Foster's Daily Democrat of Dover, N.H., has the best name of any newspaper in the United States.
