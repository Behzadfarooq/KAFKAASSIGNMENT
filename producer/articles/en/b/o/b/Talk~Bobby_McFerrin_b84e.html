[edit] Technique

It would be nice to add something about the special techniques he's learnt and applied during his career. Once I saw and heard him do a entire phrase singing 2 notes at a time, tuned almost perfectly. I found that amazing!

Please sign your comments with four tildes, thus: ~~~~
Mlibby 17:16, 10 August 2006 (UTC)
Another thing to add to a "technique" section: the "circular breathing" that McFerrin does on the track "Ave Maria" on the album "Hush" (and in live performances of the same). He's basically inhaling while singing the higher notes, and the smoothness and tuning with which he does it is so phenomenal that you don't even notice he's doing it (until you realize that he hasn't stopped for a breath in two and a half minutes). This circular breathing technique was mentioned, in passing, in a (brief) album review in Rolling Stone (back around when the album was released, obviously, like c. 1991), so that might be a decent reference to use. Mlibby 17:27, 10 August 2006 (UTC)
Though I'm all for adding information about McFerrin's awesome technical skill as a singer, I don't think he's using the technique Rolling Stone described. For one thing, according to its standard definition circular breathing can't be used for singing; it's a technique that involves holding air in the cheeks, not producing sound in the vocal cords while inhaling. It's certainly possible to produce clear notes while inhaling, and I wouldn't be surprised if McFerrin were quite capable of singing this way. But the necessary adjustment in vocal tension when going from exhaled to inhaled sound production, even if you're Bobby McFerrin, probably takes more time than just taking a quick breath and resuming normal singing. No one can "blend" inhaling and exhaling; there just has to be a break. I suspect the album recording was simply edited to remove breaths. An engineer could seamlessly combine two different takes of McFerrin's vocal, one where he breathes in the odd measures and one in the even, for example. As for live recordings, there's an amazing video of him performing Ave Maria in which he's clearly taking a breath during every third or fourth arpeggio. Skotoseme 09:11, 8 January 2007 (UTC)
Anyway, I would definitely like to see us list some more stuff on McFerrin's various techniques (more than what's currently listed in the first paragraph); maybe something approaching an "index" of recordings in which he uses certain techniques. Cross-link it to stuff in the musical performance techniques category or something like that. Mlibby 17:28, 10 August 2006 (UTC)

[edit] Parody on The Simpsons' "I'm Worried - Need Money"

83.109.144.150 (2006-01-25): Can anyone confirm this? I recently saw a documentary on Bobby McFerrin. It said that "Don't Worry, Be Happy" was a result of Bobby McFerrin, in studio, making fun of a sign saying "stu dios" (instead of studios) using spanish accent. Thus the spanish/latin accent in the song. Bobby McFerrin himself also said that "Don't Worry, Be Happy" was written and recorded in just a couple of hours (total time from idea to recorded song).
