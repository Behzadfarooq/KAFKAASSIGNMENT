Bobble (knitting)

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

In knitting, a bobble is a localized set of stitches forming a raised bump. The bumps are usually arranged in a regular geometrical pattern (e.g., a hexagonal grid) or may be figurative, e.g., represent apples on a knitted tree.

The basic idea of a bobble is to increase into a single stitch, knit a few short rows, then decrease back to a single stitch. However, this leaves many choices: how to increase and how many stitches, how many short rows to work, and how to decrease.

[edit] Single-row bobbles

Examples of 1-row bobbles include:

  • dot stitch(correct name?) (2-stitch/1-row bobble)
knit two stitches into one stitch, return both stitches to left needle and knit together.
  • rosette stitch (a different 2-stitch/1-row bobble)
purl two stitches together, then knit the same two stitches together.
  • trinity stitch (3-stitch/1-row bobble)
knit three stitches into one stitch, return all three stitches to left needle and knit together
  • popcorn stitch (4-stitch/1-row bobble)
knit four stitches into one stitch, return all four stitches to left needle and knit together
  • Irish knot (5-stitch/1-row bobble)
knit five stitches into one stitch, return all five stitches to left needle and knit together

The "knit n stitches into one stitch" instruction can be done by knitting into the front of the loop, then the back of the loop, then the front, etc. (bar increases). Alternatively, one can knit, then purl, then knit, etc. into the same stitch (moss increases).. In another alternative, one can cast on the extra stitches, although this approach is rarely done in bobbles.

[edit] Multi-row bobbles

Examples of multi-row bobbles include

  • classic bobble (4-stitch/5-row bobble)
knit four stitches into one stitch, turn, p4, turn, k4, turn, p4, turn, k4, turn, purl all four stitches together
  • small bobble (3-stitch/3-row bobble)
knit three stitches into one stitch, turn, p3, turn, k3, turn, purl all three stitches together

A great variety of bobbles is possible, by changing the number of stitches, the number of rows, whether you gradually increase and then decrease, types of stitches, etc. Visually, the most important variables in a bobble are probably the total number of stitches (since it determines the size of the bobble) and whether the bobble includes ornaments such as beads that change the appearance of the yarn.

[edit] Tips and tricks when bobbling

  • On multi-row bobbles, slip the first stitch of every row. This forms a bobble that is pleasingly spherical.
