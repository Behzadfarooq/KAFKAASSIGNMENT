[edit] Manzai

Another important component of Bobobo is the Japanese comedy style known as "manzai", which uses a "boke" (idiot) who spouts non-sequiturs, puns, and various other jokes, while the "tsukkomi" (straight-man, sane person, etc) corrects him, scolds him, or simply draws the audiences attention to the boke's jokes.

In the case of Bobobo, Beauty and Heppoko-maru are the tsukkomi, while basically everyone else are the boke. "Boke" and "tsukkomi" also can mean the action the respective role performs - thus, when Bobobo says something ridiculous, this is his "boke", and when Beauty reacts to this, she is (for lack of a better explanation) "tsukkomi"-ing.

Characters actually refer to this outright later on in the series. At many points, Beauty and Heppoko-maru complain about there being too many places to "tsukkomi" in a particular scene. Later, the character "Gyourai Girl" appears claiming to be a "boke killer", flatly turning down and even punishing Bobobo and the others' "boke".

Bobobo even incorporates advanced Japanese comedy techniques like the "nori-tsukkomi", or "riding tsukkomi". In this case, the tsukkomi accepts what the boke says as nothing unusual, going along with it momentarily before suddenly yelling out in a sort of delayed tsukkomi.

EXAMPLE:

The villian traps Beauty in a giant bowl of rice. He announces that after being trapped in the rice for one hour, she will turn into a panda, and another extra is shown in the rice who has been turned into a panda.

After a while, Beauty begins to worry that the time is almost up. She then notices a box with a panda costume, with a sign that says "After one hour, please put this on. Thank you."

At this point, the show itself has presented a boke, to which Beauty would normally tsukkomi something like "What the hell?!? I have to do it myself!?"

However, she silently starts putting on the panda suit, posing cutely...

Before suddenly yelling out angrily "As if I'm gonna fall for that!"


As illustrated, the comedic catch of the nori-tsukkomi is the sudden 180 of the tsukkomi's attitude, especially the tsukkomi acts, at first, completely opposite to what the audience is expecting. Like most Japanese comedy, tsukkomi and particularly nori-tsukkomi relies very heavily on timing, making stand-up manzai a fine art and also a very difficult one to master.
