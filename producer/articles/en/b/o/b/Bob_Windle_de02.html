Robert ("Bob") Windle (born November 7, 1944 in Sydney) was an Australian sprint and long distance freestyle and butterfly swimmer of the 1960s, who won the gold medal in the 1500 m freestyle at the 1964 Summer Olympics in Tokyo. He also won a silver and two bronze medals, and represented Australia at all freestyle distances at the Olympics from 100 m to 1500 m, and set six world records in his career.

Growing up in the Sydney suburb of Waverley, he learnt to swim at Bronte Beach with his sister Norma. His family later relocated to Bexley North, where he swam for his school team, and was coached by Frank Guthrie at the Enfield pool at the age of 12.

A turning point was the 1960 Australian Championships, when he came second in the 1650yd freestyle behind John Konrads and was selected for the 1960 Summer Olympics in Rome. He dropped out of high school to attend a three-month training camp in Queensland. He did not swim in Rome, with team officials wanting him merely to learn from the routine of Olympic swimming. On his return to Australia, he switched to the tutelage of Don Talbot, who was also Konrad's coach.

Talbot, who referred to Windle as the greyhound, had great trouble in trying to increase Windle's frame. Windle won the 1500 m at the 1961 Australian Championships, before showing his versatility the foillowing year, when he won the 220yd, 440yd and 1650yd freestyle events at the national Championships. Selected for the 1962 Commonwealth Games, he won silver and bronze in the 1650yd and 440yd freestyle events respectively, both won by Murray Rose, who had returned from competition in the United States. He also won gold in the 4x220yd freestyle relay, along with Rose, Allan Wood and Anthony Strahan. After the Games, Rose returned to the United States, and at the 1963 Australian Championships, he won the 220yd, 440yd, 880yd and 1650yd freestyle events and was narrowly defected in the 110yd event by David Dickson. He set world recrds in the 200 m and 220yd freestyle that year. The results were repeated again at the 1964 Australian Championships, when Windle qualified for the 1964 Summer Olympics in Tokyo.

Windle's first event was the 4x100 m freestyle relay, where he combined with Dickson, Peter Doak, and Peter Ryan to win bronze in a time of 3 m 39.1s, in the which the United States won in a world record time of 3 m 33.2s. In the 400 m freestyle heats, he made a misjudgement, swimming four seconds slower than at the Australian Championships to conserve energy for the final, and missing the final completely. Wood, who he had beaten for the national crown, won bronze in the event. In the 1500 m, Talbot decided that Windle would attack from the outset and attempt to hang on towards the latter stages. He broke the olympic record in the heats to qualify fastest for the final, and repeated the strategy in the final, lowering his own Olympic record to 17 m 1.7s and holding off American John Nelson. Windle later combined with Wood, Ryan and Dickson in the 4x200 m freestyle relay, but they finished fourth, with the Americans setting another world record.

After the Olympics, Windle enrolled at the Indiana University on a swimming scholarship, coached by Doc Counsilman. He studied Business at the same time. There by switched his focus to shorter sprint distances. His performances in the United States were enough to earn him selection for the 1966 Commonwealth Games in Kingston, Jamaica. There he set a world record in the 440yd freestyle, but with his focus now on the shorter distances, only managed fourth in the 1650yd freestyle. He won golds in the 4x110yd freestyle relay with Ryan, Dickson and Michael Wenden and the 4x220yd freestyle with Wenden, Dickson and Peter Reynolds.

In 1968 he again returned to Australia for the National Championships. Although he did not win any events, he was selected for the 100 m and 200 m freestyle events and their respective relays. He was eliminated in the 100 m freestyle semifinals and came sixth in the 200 m freestyle, with Wenden winning both events. In the 4x100 m freestyle, Windle combined with Wenden, Robert Cusack and Greg Rogers to win the bronze medal behind the United States and the Soviet Union. In the 4x200 m freestyle relay, he won a silver along with Wenden, Rogers and Graham White just 1.4s behind the Americans.

He retired after the Games, saying "I have swum to that point when I could hardly lift myself out of the water. I have seen John Konrads to that point also. I would always give 110%. There is no substitute for hard and honest work". He stayed in the United States for a further few years, working for Allis-Chalmers, an agricultural equipment corporation. He later returned to Australia to continue working for them.
