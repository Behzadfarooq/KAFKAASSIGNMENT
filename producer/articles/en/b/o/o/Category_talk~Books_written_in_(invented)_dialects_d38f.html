[edit] Why I created this category, and the problems inherent in it

I am a fan of certain kinds of writing in which the language (usually of the first-person narrator) is "non-standard", such as in Riddley Walker, A Clockwork Orange and Vernon God Little, but I noticed there wasn't a category bringing these kinds of books together, and felt there should be. I fully recognise the difficulty of referring to all these written styles as "dialects", especially as the Wikipedia definition of dialect says:

The concept of dialects can be distinguished from:â€”
sociolects, which are a variety of a language spoken by a certain social class
languages, which are standardized for public performance (e.g. written standard)
jargons, which are characterized by differences in vocabulary (or lexicon according to linguist jargon), and
slang
patois
creoles or pidgins
argots.

The reason I've used the word "dialect" however is that the category obviously cannot be called "Books written in (invented) sociolects, jargons, slang, patois, creoles, pidgins and argots", and the word "dialect" seems to be the term most obviously (mis)used to cover all of these "mutations" of language.

I do also fully recognise that there is a distinction between a book like Finnegans Wake, which would more accurately be described as 800 pages of wordplay rather than being written in a dialect, and Vernon God Little, which has no such self-conscious wordplay in it but tries to represent the speech pattern of a teenager from a certain area of the United States. Equally it's difficult to reconcile The Catcher in the Rye, which does a similar thing to Vernon God Little, with Riddley Walker, which is written in a version of English created by the author and combines wordplay and "real" slang (i.e. fictional to us, but real to the narrator). Furthermore, A Clockwork Orange is written in recognisable English with slang words again created by the author while Nineteen Eighty-Four (not an ideologically unrelated book) is itself written in standard English but contains coinages created by the author.

So basically I accept the faults of this category but do think it, or something like it, should exist, and I'm open to suggestions about how it might be improved. --Thoughtcat 16:20, 22 July 2006 (UTC)
