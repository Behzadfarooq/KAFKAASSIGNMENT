The Society of Book and Snake is one of the oldest secret societies at Yale University. Book and Snake was founded at the Sheffield Scientific School in 1863 as a three-year society. As other "Sheff" societies, it was once residential and maintained a separate residential building (or "Hall") built in 1888 but that is no longer standing. Each year Book and Snake taps 16 new members, which is also in contrast to the others, most of which tap fifteen. Members are said to be leaders in different activities on campus and representative of many of Yale's academic, athletic, artistic and social scenes. Book and Snake is rumored to have a relationship with the newer Manuscript Society, though these may simply represent personal ties between their respective memberships.

Book & Snake's reputation is "progressive" in its membership selection, in contrast to reputed "conservative" criteria considered by other societies. B&S became a Yale senior society in 1933, and regards itself as the first senior secret society at Yale to accept minorities and women. (However, Manuscript also claims the first female tap, while another former "Sheff" secret society, St. Anthony Hall, as a three-year society, was able to tap female sophomores after Yale College became co-ed in 1969. Moreover, the Elihu Club tapped a Native American in 1909)

Book and Snake owns its own meeting hall, referred to as a "tomb" as are many other Yale societies' meeting places.

As is traditional with the meeting places of the societies, the building is windowless and can only regularly be accessed by the 16 current members and alumni, although it has also held semi-private parties that include friends of members. Inside the tomb, alumni members customarily leave their own pewter or glass tankard with their name inscribed, hung on hooks in their dining area for their use whenever they return, making tangible a display of the generations that have come before. Some believe that when a member is deceased, his or her tankard is symbolically destroyed by breaking or piercing the glass or metal bottom of the tankard. It is unknown whether this tradition continues with the current undergraduate members.

[edit] Architects of the Book & Snake Buildings

  • Louis R. Metcalfe. (1901, Greek Ionic. The front door is modeled after the Erechtheion Temple on the Acropolis in Athens. Passersby will notice wrought-iron snakes, or "cadeuces" adorning the iron fence surrounding the property. The white marble temple, startling in its Classical Greek verisimilitude, is deliberately situated with its back to the Yale campus; instead its orientation facing directly across the street to the massive Egyptian-revival gates of the Grove Street Cemetery, makes for an impressive display of ancient, mortuary-themed solemnity. Citation at [1].)
  • J. Edwards Ficken. (1888, residential hall known as "Cloister", no longer extant. Citation and picture at [2] and at [3].)

Architectural historian Scott Meacham cites both of Book & Snake's buildings in his study of Yale and Dartmouth society and fraternity architecture. [edit] Membership

Notable members include Bob Woodward of the Washington Post, former CIA Director Porter J. Goss, former Secretary of Defense Les Aspin, former Secretary of Treasury Nicholas Brady, Florida Senator Bill Nelson, former Congressman and owner of the New York Herald Tribune, Ogden R. Reid, and Henry Ford II.
