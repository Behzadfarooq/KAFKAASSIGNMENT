Boötes void

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

The Boötes void is a tremendously large, approximately spherically shaped nearly-empty region of space, devoid of galaxies. At nearly 250 million light-years in diameter, it is one of the largest known voids, and is referred to as a supervoid. It was discovered in 1981 by Robert Kirshner, Augustus Oemler, Jr., Paul Schechter and Stephen Shectman in a survey of galactic redshifts. It is located in the area of the constellation Boötes, for which it is named.

To give an idea of its scale, "If the Milky Way had been in the center of the Boötes void, we wouldn't have known there were other galaxies until the 1960s." (Greg Aldering, University of Minnesota).

[edit] Theories

It has been theorized that the Boötes void was formed from the merger of smaller voids, much as soap bubbles coalesce to form larger bubbles. This theory would account for the small number of galaxies that exist in a rough tube shape through the middle of the void.

It has also been theorized that the Boötes void isn't empty, and is instead filled with dark matter.

[edit] In fiction

  • The Boötes void is mentioned in the serial novel Accelerando by Charles Stross.
  • The Boötes void is mentioned in Martin Amis' Night Train.
