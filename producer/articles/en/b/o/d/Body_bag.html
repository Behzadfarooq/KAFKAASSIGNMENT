Body bag

A body bag is a non-porous bag designed to contain a human body, used for the storage and transport of corpses. Body bags are also sometimes used for the storage of corpses within morgues.

In modern wars, body bags have been used to contain the bodies of dead soldiers. Governments typically have reserves of body bags, both for anticipated wars and natural disasters. During the Cold War, vast reserves of body bags were built up in anticipation of millions of fatalities from nuclear war. This was the subject of Adrian Mitchell's haunting protest poem "Fifteen Million Plastic Bags".

With the addition of provision for breathing, specially adapted body bags are also used for the BDSM practice of mummification.

Body bags are often portrayed in films and television as being made of a heavy black plastic. While this was originally the case many years ago, lightweight white body bags are now predominantly used. This change was made because it is much easier to spot a piece of evidence that may have been jostled from the body in transit on a white background than on a black background.

[edit] Use in German-speaking countries

In German the term body bag is sometimes used for fashion or other bags worn on the body and this sense has no connection with either of the two above senses. This usage is caused by a misconception about the proper translation of the German term Rucksack (backpack) into English and the intention to coin a new, hip marketing compatible term for young costumers. Similar misapprehension about the English language lead to the creation of the term Denglish.
