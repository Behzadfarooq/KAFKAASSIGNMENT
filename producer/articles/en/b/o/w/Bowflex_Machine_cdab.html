The Bowflex Ultimate model

The Bowflex Machine is an exercise machine used for strength training marketed and sold under the Bowflex brand by Nautilus, Inc.. It is primarily sold through the use of infomercials. It is marketed as being a gravity-independent form of resistance that can be used in outer space.

[edit] Design

The Bowflex grew out of a now expired patent first conceived by Tessema Dosho Shifferaw. Bowflex of America Inc began marketing the first product, The Bowflex 2000X in 1986. Bowflex of America changed its name to Bowflex Inc, and became a public company on the Toronto Stock Exchange. In 1999 the company changed its name to Direct Focus, Inc. and initiated an IPO on Nasdaq.

On the success of Bowflex, the company bought the Nautilus Corporation, Schwinn Fitness, and Stairmaster Fitness. In 2002 the company moved to the NYSE and branded itself as The Nautilus Group and is now Nautilus, Inc. which owns and markets the Bowflex along with Nautilus, Schwinn Excersise, Stairmaster and Pearl Izumi lines among other brands.

[edit] Current Models

Ultimate 2
This is considered to be the top of the line model and includes a "No Cable Change Pulley System" that allows the user to switch between squat pulleys, cross bar pulleys, and lat tower pulleys without changing the cables. It comes with 310 pounds of Power Rods and can be upgraded to 410 pounds.

Ultimate
Similar to the Ultimate 2, this model also comes with 310 of Power Rods but does not have the "No Cable Change Pulley System"

Xtreme SE
This is the newest model, an upgrade of the original Xtreme with a two-tone bench and is the only model to feature an ab-crunch strap designed to focus on ab exercises. To save space, the Xtreme models all have an upright design and do not come with a bench. It comes with 210 pounds of Power Rods and can be upgraded to 410 pounds. It features 65 exercises.

Xtreme 2
Similar to the Xtreme SE but with the addition of the "No Cable Change Pulley System". It features 70 exercises.

Blaze
This is considered to be the entry-level model. It has a bench and looks similar to the Ultimate but with 60 exercises only.

Motivator 2
This is a model sold only in retail stores and not through Bowflex's infomercials. It comes with 210 lbs of Power Rods but cannot be upgraded and features 50 exercises.

Conquest
This model was created specifically for QVC. It's very similar to the Motivator 2.

[edit] 2004 recall

January 2004, about 420,000 Bowflex machines were recalled due to mechanical problems. In November 2004, there was a recall of nearly 800,000 Bowflex Power Pro model machines after reports that several models had broken unexpectedly. The Consumer Product Safety Commission said that the seats could unexpectedly break, and that the backboard bench could collapse when in the incline position.[1] This recall was voluntary and the company shipped every purchaser it could locate a free safety repair kit. Soon after the recall, the company discontinued the Power Pro model.

[edit] Other Bowflex Machines

Bowflex Revolution
In an attempt to improve on the previous models. A new model was introduced in 2006 called the Bowflex Revolution [1].

Two adjustable "Freedom Arms" have 10 locked positions over a range of 170 degrees. These are used instead of the pulleys in the previous Bowflexes. This makes changing exercises less complicated, as one can simply move the arms rather than hooking the cords through a series of complex confusing wheels. The new model does not use the former Power Rod technology, replacing it with a newly titled SpiraFlex. They are interlocking plastic plates modelled after standard weight plates, but are much lighter than the level of resistance they provide when used in the machine. They attach to a bar, and when the cam is turned, a series of elastic bands within the plates are stretched within the plate.

These are safer in that should the resistance break, the elastic rebound would be contained within the special plates, as opposed to the former power rods which could be dangerous during a break. The rods themselves could rebound and strike someone, as well as the metal hooks used to attach the cords to the rods. The only external apparatus of the Bowflex Revolution is the cord, and it is unlikely that would break before the elastics inside the plates.

The revolution comes with up to 220lbs, with an optional upgrade to 300lbs max resistance.. This differs from previous BowFlex products which come with up to 210, with upgrades for 310 or 410 pounds resistance. Those were attained as a total of both pulleys (205+205=410), so the Revolution may have more depending on whether the 300lbs refers to a total of both sides, or a per-side figure.

While this seems to imply the Revolution has less resistance for max upgrade, it is listed as having a 600lb leg press, which is greater than the numbers stated on the other bow flexes that incorporate a leg press. It is unknown how greater resistance would be attained on the leg press than can be attained on the pulley exercises. It is either an addition of 2 300lb maximums, or a 300lb maximum exerted over a greater distance which, incorporating the Nautilus cam wheel, requires twice the force to move.

Nautilus claims that the new system makes the machine easier to use than its predecessor. That it is smoother and less noisy than it's previous machines. Unlike previous versions, the Revolution does not have a squat bar attachment or a lat pulldown bar attachment, though the movements can be performed, the work the limbs do is independent.
