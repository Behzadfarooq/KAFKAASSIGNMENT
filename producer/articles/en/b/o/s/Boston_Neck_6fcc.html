Boston Neck

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

The trajectory of the Boston Neck along today's Washington Street. Land to the north and west, formerly a tidal marsh, has since been filled in. The much narrower and shorter Fort Point Channel remains to the southeast.

[edit] History

The Boston Neck or Roxbury Neck was an isthmus, a narrow strip of land connecting the peninsular Boston, Massachusetts to the mainland city of Roxbury (now a neighborhood of Boston). The surrounding land was gradually filled in as the city of Boston expanded in population. (See History of Boston, Massachusetts.)

Boston Neck was about 120 feet wide at normal high tide. The settlers built a wooden town gate and earthen wall on the neck about 1631 to prevent attacks from Indians and to keep out unwanted animals and people. The gate was constantly guarded and usually locked during certain times during the evening. No residents could enter or leave during that period. There was a wooden gallows just outside the town gate. Burglars and pickpockets were commonly executed in those days as well as murderers.

In colonial times, the Charles River marshes were north of the neck, and Gallow’s Bay was on the south side. It was named because of the nearby executions at the neck. It later became known as South Bay. The main road through the neck was called Orange Street on Capt. John Bonner’s map of 1722.

In 1710, additional fortifications were constructed. There were supposedly two wooden gates, one for carriages and one for foot travelers. In Sep. 1774, General Thomas Gage strengthened the old fortifications of brick, stone and earth with timber and additional earth. Gage ordered a ditch to be dug in front of the fortifications which filled with salt water during high tides—effectively cutting off Boston from the mainland. The neck had soft mud on both sides at low tide, so it was very difficult to enter Boston on foot except through the town gate.

On the night of April 18, 1775, Patriot leader Doctor Joseph Warren sent Paul Revere and William Dawes on horseback with identical written messages to warn John Hancock and Samuel Adams of the British expedition to capture them and to seize the powder in Concord. Dawes, a 30 year-old Boston tanner, was well known to the British sentries at the town gate on Boston Neck and was able to pass through the checkpoint that evening despite a lockdown. Dawes traveled a southern route by land while Revere took the northern route. Dr. Warren sent both men to be certain one of them would be able to evade the British patrols. Dawes left about 10 pm and rode 17 miles in three hours. He met with Revere shortly before 1 am at the Hancock-Clarke House in Lexington in the early morning of April 19, 1775—the first day of the American Revolution.

The residents started filling in along the neck in the late 1700s because the low-lying area was prone to erosion. Beginning in the 1830s, the Charles River tidal flats were filled in with train loads of gravel from the Needham area. This created the present Back Bay section of Boston. The remains of the fortifications at the town gate were still visible in 1822. About 1824 this section of Orange Street where the town gate once stood was renamed Washington Street.

The Washington Street Elevated (the “El”) ran subway trains above Washington Street from 1901 until 1987 when the Orange Line was relocated and the elevated tracks and stations were torn down.

The Dover Street station was located at the site of the old town gate at the intersection of Dover and Washington Street. Dover Street was renamed East Berkeley Street sometime after the subway station was demolished. Today, at the intersection of East Berkeley and Washington Streets, nothing of the town gate or the fortifications is left.
