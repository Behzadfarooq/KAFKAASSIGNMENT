Božidar Đelić

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Božidar "Boža" Đelić (Serbian: Божидар Ђелић), (born April 1, 1965 in Belgrade, Yugoslavia, now Serbia) is a Serbian economist and politician. He was the Serbian Minister of finance in the first post-Milošević government of Zoran Đinđić in 2001-2003. As of January 2007, he is the candidate for the Prime Minister of Serbia of the Democratic Party, which obtained most seats in the Parliament among so-called "democratic bloc" parties at the January 2007 elections.

[edit] Biography

[edit] Early life and education

Đelić was born in Belgrade in 1965, as a single child[verification needed]. His parents divorced soon after his birth, and went abroad in search of better prospects, while Božidar was raised by his mother-side grandparents. Đelić will later describe his grandmother Mileva as the strongest figure in his childhood. When his grandparents died in 1974, he moved to Paris to live with his mother and stepfather in Paris. When her small business collapsed, the family got into difficult situation, and Božidar had to help his, practically bankrupt learn to earn for his living by dish and window washing, and to study in parallel.[1]

He entered the Institut d'Etudes Politiques de Paris and later Ecole des Hautes Etudes Commerciales in Paris where he graduated as a top student. In 1987 he received a double master's degree in economics at the Ecole des Hautes Etudes en Sciences Sociales. He eventually moved to the United States, where he completed an Master of Business Administration at the Harvard Business School and specialized in macroeconomics and international relations at Harvard's Kennedy School of Government.[2]

[edit] Expertise

Đelić continued to work as an expert advisor for several East European transition governments on the issues of privatization, banking reform and macroeconomic reform : 1991-1992 in Poland (where he also assisted the establishment of the Warsaw Stock Exchange[2]), 1992-1993 in Russia as an advisor to Anatoly Chubais, and 1996 in Romania[3].

In 1991, he become a partner in McKinsey & Company, a leading consulting firm.[3]

[edit] 2000: return to Serbia

After the 5th October Overthrow of Slobodan Milošević, his personal friend and leader of the expert group G17 Plus Miroljub Labus invited him to help reviving country's devastated economy[4]. On 13 November 2000, Đelić took an unpaid leave from McKinsey and arrived to Belgrade. He served as the main negotiator with IMF, Paris Club and other financial insitutions. After the parliamentary elections, in January 2001 he became the Minister of Finance and Economy.[2] He got acquainted with Mlađan Dinkić, who held the position of the governor of the National Bank with whom he worked closely to clean up public finances and banking sector, stabilize the national currency, reduce budget deficit and level up the national economy. In just the first year, he managed to reduce the projected budget deficit from 4.5% to only 1.5%; in Serbian public, he got the joking nickname 'Boža Derikoža' (Boža the usurer)[5]. However, he was actually very popular in public: he was declared "person of the year" in 2001 by Vreme [1].
