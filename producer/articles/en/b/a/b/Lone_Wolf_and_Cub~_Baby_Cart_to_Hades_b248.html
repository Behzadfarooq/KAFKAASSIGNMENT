Lone Wolf and Cub: Baby Cart to Hades (Japanese: 子連れ狼　死に風に向う乳母車 or Kozure Ôkami: Shinikazeni mukau ubaguruma, literally Wolf with Child in Tow: Perambulator Against the Winds of Death), is the third in a series of six Japanese martial arts films based on the long-running Lone Wolf and Cub manga series about Ogami Itto, a wandering assassin for hire who is accompanied by his young son, Daigoro.

[edit] Plot

Spoiler warning: Plot and/or ending details follow.

Ogami Ittō, the disgraced former shogun's executioner, or Kogi Kaishakunin, is travelling by river on a boat with his young son Daigoro floating behind in the baby cart.

A young woman at the front of the boat, clearly distraught for some reason, accidentally drops a bundle into the water, which Daigoro retrieves.

Ittō, meanwhile, draws his sword part way and notices in the reflection on the blade that some bamboo reeds are also trailing the boat. Ittō is being followed by operatives of his mortal enemy, the Yagyū Clan – a constant threat that he can never ignore. Later, as Daigoro is relieving himself in a bamboo glade, Ittō slices some bamboo stalks, causing some ninja to fall from their perch.

This is a time when there are ronin, or at the very least a low-class of samurai termed watari-kashi – small bands of fighters who flit from job to job, working from one daimyo to the next, depending on who's hiring.

A group of four watari-kashi are idling along the road. Hot and bored, they spy an attractive young woman and her mother being escorted by one man. Three of them run off to take advantage, but one of the band – Kanbei, the more honorable of the four – remains. The three knock the escort unconscious and proceed to rape the two women. The escort comes to, but is slain by Kanbei, who then also slays the two women. He then makes his three colleagues draw straws. The one with the short straw shall be killed to take the blame for the rapes and murders.

Ittō happens along this grim scene just as Kanbei is slaying one of the watari-kashi. Kanbei then demands that Ittō engage him in a duel. Ittō goes as far as meeting Kanbei in a field to have the duel, but at the last second sheaths his sword and calls it draw, leaving Kanbei to ponder his fate alone. "You are a true warrior," Ittō says. "One I hope lives on."

Ogami Ittō fights off an entire army.

At an inn, the young woman from the boat it turns out is to be sold into prostitution. Her pimp tries to have his way with her, but she bites off his tongue, spitting the bloody apendage onto the floor and killing the man.

The girl seeks refuge with Ittō, who steps in to protect her from the police. But then the town's real authorities show up – the yakuza, led by a woman named Toizo. After some verbal sparring and defending himself against Toizo's pistol, Ittō agrees to act as a substitute for the young woman and undergo buri-buri, a form of torture that involves the subject being hogtied and hung in the air and repeatedly dunked headfirst into a tub of water. The subject is then beaten to unconsciousness by men wielding thick rattan canes and shouting "buri-buri". Ittō endures the torture with his typical stoicism. This frees the young woman from having work as a prostitute.

Ittō, still with a debt to pay, for the murder of the pimp, agrees to take on an assassination for Koizo and her father, a one-armed man that Ittō is acquainted with from his past life as the shogun's executioner – he accidentally cut the man's arm off during the botched execution of an insane daimyo who would not sit still.

The target is a corrupt district deputy. Initially Ittō is to face the deputy's personal bodyguards, one of whom is a sharpshooter and quick-draw artist who wields a pair of revolvers. Through cunning and guile (and the help of his young son Daigoro, who acts as a decoy), Ittō defeats the armed man and takes his guns.

Ittō's battle culminates in his facing the deputy's army – perhaps 200 men – singlehandedly.

For the first time, the true power of the baby cart is revealed as it proves to harbor an entire arsenal of weapons, including a small battery of guns, capable of taking out many enemy soldiers.

Word of the fight has been passed to neighboring districts, and the ronin Kanbei shows up after Ittō has slain the deputy and his army and makes his demand again for a duel.

Though battle weary, Ittō accepts. The fight is over in an instant. Ittō is sliced across his back, but Kanbei is mortally wounded, impaled on Ittō's Dotanuki battle sword. As Kanbei kneels, dying, he tells Ittō his story and why he became a ronin – a tale involving him running ahead to meet the enemy head on and prevail, but since he left his master's side, he was dishonored. Ittō replied that he would have done the same. "I am glad to hear that," Kanbei says, who then asks the former shogun's executioner to act as his "second" in the act of seppuku. This Ittō does with honor.

The true "Way of the Warrior", it seems, is to die.

As Ittō starts to walk away Toizo runs after him, as if she had fallen for Ittō, but she is stopped by her men. They tell her not to go to him, that he is not human, that he is a monster.

Spoilers end here.
