Babka

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Babka (бабка), also known as baba, is a sweet spongy yeast cake that is traditionally baked for Easter Sunday. Babka originated in Eastern Europe and was introduced to North America by early immigrants. Traditional babka has some type of fruit filling, especially raisins, and is glazed with a fruit-flavored icing, sometimes with rum added. Modern babka may be chocolate or have a cheese filling.

Despite its Christian associations, babka is also popular among Jews, particularly those with family origins in Eastern Europe.

[edit] Etymology

The name babka is a diminutive of baba 'old woman' or 'grandmother', and probably refers to the shape of the pastry, a tall cylinder, sometimes with corrugations resembling a skirt's pleats.[1]

[edit] Popular culture

Babka was a plot point in the Seinfeld episode "The Dinner Party", in which the main characters try to buy a chocolate babka for a dinner party but have to settle for a cinnamon one instead. They argue with the couple who purchased the last chocolate babka, before realizing they are all attending the same dinner party. Jerry and Elaine debate over purchasing a cinnamon babka; Elaine is concerned that they will be viewed by others as having a "lesser babka." However, Jerry convinces her that it is a better choice because everything that tastes good always has cinnamon in it.
