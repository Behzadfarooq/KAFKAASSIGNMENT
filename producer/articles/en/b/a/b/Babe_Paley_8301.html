Barbara Cushing Mortimer Paley (July 5, 1915 â€“ July 6, 1978) was an American socialite. Born Barbara Cushing in Boston, Massachusetts, she was a daughter of neurosurgeon Dr. Harvey Cushing, who was professor of surgery at Johns Hopkins, Harvard and Yale universities.

Nicknamed "Babe," Barbara was blessed with great beauty, and her style and grace were ensured by her training by an ambitious, social-climbing mother. Babe and her sisters were schooled in the art of marrying well by their mother; they were taught to pursue only men of wealth and social prominence. Her older sisters both married into money and/or prestige: Mary (Minnie) Cushing was the second wife of Vincent Astor, and Betsey Cushing married James Roosevelt and then later John Hay Whitney.

[edit] Career, marriages, social status

Babe Cushing worked as a fashion editor for Vogue in New York City when she met and married Standard Oil heir, Stanley Grafton Mortimer, Jr. (1913-1999) in 1940. They divorced a few years later, and she was left to raise their two children, Amanda and Stanley Grafton Mortimer 3rd. Many recent retrospectives claim that she neglected her children while in pursuit of social status and depended upon the wealth of her husbands to support her lavish lifestyle.[1] Her daughter Amanda Burden admits that their relationship was "virtually nonexistent" and that the distance "was her choice, not mine."[2] Mortimer married Kathleen Harriman, the daughter of Averell Harriman, and fathered three children.

In 1947, Babe Mortimer married the wealthy and influential William S. "Bill" Paley, founder of CBS, and had two more children, Kate and Bill. As Babe Paley, she became a leader of New York high society, and the couple was famous for their lavish entertaining. Frequently listed as one of the world's best-dressed women, Babe regularly bought entire haute couture collections from major fashion houses and was considered one of the 20th century's enduring fashion icons.

Her circle of friends included author Truman Capote and fellow socialite Slim Keith. Capote included Paley and Keith in his group of "swans" (glamorous New York socialite women) - other members of this group were Gloria Guinness and C.Z. Guest. Paley famously dropped Capote as a friend when excerpts of his much-touted work in progress, Answered Prayers, revealed confidences, with suggestions of more to come, of many of New York's elite, including Bill Paley.

Babe Paley died of lung cancer the day after her 63rd birthday and was interred in the Memorial Cemetery of St. John's Church, Cold Spring Harbor, New York. On his death in 1990, her husband was interred next to her.

Babe Paley was portrayed in the film Capote (2005) by Michelle Harrison and in Infamous (2006) by Sigourney Weaver.
