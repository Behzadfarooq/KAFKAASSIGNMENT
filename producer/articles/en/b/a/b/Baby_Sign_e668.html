Baby Sign is the practice of using sign language to communicate with infants and toddlers.

[edit] Purpose

According to proponents of Baby Sign, infants and toddlers have a desire to communicate their needs and wishes, but lack the ability to do so clearly because the production of speech, which requires coordinating the lips, tongue, breath, and vocal chords simultaneously, lags behind cognitive ability in the first months and years of life. This gap between desire to communicate and ability often leads to frustration and tantrums. With Baby Sign, since hand-eye coordination is possible in advance of the acquisition of verbal skills, infants can learn to express their needs using simple signs for common words such as "eat", "sleep", "more", "hug", "play", "cookie", "teddy bear", etc., before they are able to produce understandable speech. [1]

[edit] Popularity

Use of baby sign language is growing, but is still not widespread.

[edit] Commonly Used Vocabulary

Parents often begin teaching Baby Sign through use of simple vocabulary, either ASL or home-based signs. Two common categories of words used are “need-based” and “highly motivating”

[edit] Need-based

Need-based signs focuses on basic needs, such as signs for "drink", "food", "sleepy", "hot"/"cold", "change me", etc. "Drink" or "thirst" can be expressed by mimicking drinking out of a bottle. "Eating" could be expressed by making a similar motion, or by rubbing one's stomach.

[edit] Highly Motivating

"Highly Motivating" signs focuses on items of interest or entertainment to the child, such as signs for "doggy", "television", "toy", "friend", etc. A parent can encourage growth in the child's vocabulary by teaching them signs for items that the child desires and seeks out.

[edit] Systems

[edit] Sign Language-based

In sign-language based Baby Sign, vocabulary words which are part of the indigenous sign language of the region are used. For example, in Australia, the signs are adopted from Auslan (AUstralian Sign LANguage); in America ASL (American Sign Language) signs would be used, and so on. This provides a standard vocabulary which allows other baby signers to understand and communicate with the baby using these signs and can provide a basis for the continued study of the indigenous sign language as the child grows older.

[edit] Home-based

Home-based Baby Sign is a system of sign developed individually between infant and caregivers whose proponents argue that vocabulary from ASL and other formal sign languages can be too difficult for an infant to produce.

[edit] Controversy over use of “Home Signs” vs. ASL in the USA

Although initially, there was significant division between advocates of ASL-based Baby Signs and proponents of “home-based signs,” leading Baby Sign advocates Acredolo and Goodwyn have incorporated significantly more ASL-based signs in the most recent revision of their Baby Sign program. [2]

[edit] Development in the USA

The development of Baby Sign in the United States is often credited to research by Linda Acredolo , Ph.D., Professor Emeritus of Psychology at the University of California, Davis and Susan Goodwyn , Ph.D., Professor of Psychology at California State University, Stanislaus, whose research began in 1982 and produced a 1985 article in Human Development titled “Symbolic gesturing in language development: A case study.” [3]

Further research by Joseph Garcia, an ASL interpreter and a leading proponent of use of ASL as the basis for Baby Sign, began with his graduate thesis in 1986, an analysis of the role sign language could play early childhood language acquisition. His research indicated that babies who are exposed to signs regularly and consistently at six to seven months of age can begin using signs effectively by the eighth or ninth month. [4]

[edit] Criticism

Criticism of Baby Sign tends to rest primarily in the fear that use of sign will delay spoken language acquisition.

[edit] Research in Support

There has been significant research into the benefits of Baby Sign for children, including its successful use with children with development delays and special needs. Some of this research indicates that children who have been taught Baby Sign have increased and early spoken vocabularies and improved cognitive and communication skills throughout childhood.[5][6]

[edit] Baby Sign in Deaf families

Much of the original interest in Baby Sign began with research into Babies in Deaf families, who, immersed in a signing environment, begin to use simple signs from as early as 6 weeks.
