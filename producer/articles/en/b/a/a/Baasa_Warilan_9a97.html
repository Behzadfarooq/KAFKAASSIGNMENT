[edit] Introduction

The Warilan Language, called Baasa Warilan (IPA: [ˈba.ʔa.sa 'wa.ɾi.lan]) is a constructed language created by Hadag Dililuga in the year 2007. It is modeled on mainly Austronesian languages such as Tagalog, Indonesian, and Cebuano (The word "baasa"(the language) is closely modeled on the Indonesian word for language, "bahasa.") It has a slight European and Esperanto influence as well. Its goal is to encourage international communication with minimal bias to any nation or group of people.

[edit] Phonology

Warilan has a minimal phoneme inventory with just 17 consonants and 5 vowels. It is unique in that it contains no palatal consonants (not even [j]).

Stops p b t d k g ʔ1
Fricatives β s ʃ x h
Nasals m n ŋ

1The sound [ʔ] is never written, but is pronounced between all combinations of vowels (i.e. "ua", "aa," and "io" are pronounced [u.ʔa], [a.ʔa], [i.ʔo] respectively). The only exceptions are "ai" and "au," which are pronounced as diphthongs [aɪ] and [aʊ] respectively).

[edit] Vowels

Vowel Height Front Back
Close i u
Close-Mid e1 o1
Open-Mid (ɛ)2 (ɔ)2
Close a

1The three primary vowels are a, i, and u. The vowels "e" and "o" are only required to be written/pronounced before a nasal (m, n, or ng). The vowels "e" and "o" replace "i" and "u" in these situations. However, "i" can become "e" and "u" can become "o" before k, g, l, or r in a word. Whether it happens or not is not systematic and have to be memorized, but these words (ek "whole," hitogu, -og "a son (of)") are few and far between.

2The vowels [ɛ] and [ɔ] rarely occur anymore in Warilan, but they were a regular feature of Old Warilan. The are written as diphthongs "ew" and "ow" respectively. If they remain, they are used in transliterations, place names, and the word "owl" [ɔl], which means "according to." (Remember that t, d, n, l, r, and s are DENTAL, NOT ALVEOLAR).

[edit] Writing System

Baasa Warilan uses a modified version of the Latin alphabet. With the exception of "h" and "r," each letter corresponds to one phoneme, which makes learning the Warilan alphabet relatively easy.

[edit] Alphabet

A a [a]; B b [b]; D d [d̪]; E e [e]; G g [g]; H h [h]1;

Ḥ ḥ [x]; I i [i]; K k [k]; L l [l]; M m [m]; N n [n];

Ng ng [ŋ]; O o [o]; P p [p]; R r [r, ɾ]2 S s [s]; Ṣ ṣ [ʃ];

T t [t̪]; U u [u]; V v [β].

1When the letter "h" comes at the onset of a syllable and in between vowels (e.g. "ha" (I), "vulahi" (to die)), it is pronounced as [h]. However, if it comes as the coda (end) of a syllable before a consonant or as a final (e.g. "Kirihsita" (Christ), "biluh" (to a book)), it indicates stress in that syllable. Normally, stress falls on the first syllable OR the syllable preceding a glottal stop.

2The letter "r" is pronounced as the flap "ɾ", except as an initial ("Ruṣas" (Russia)), where it is pronounced as the trill [r].

[edit] Translations into Warilan

Any word phonetically translated into Warilan is to be capitalized or put in quotes, and preceded by the particle "ṣa" which indicates transliteration.

Examples:

ṣa Buda - Buddha

ṣa Muhsisa - Moses

ṣa Saitigaisit - Zeitgeist

ṣa Pirihsidehnit Buṣ - President Bush

[edit] Rules

Generally, consonant clusters in Warilan are limited to "nasal-voiceless stop" and "nasal-fricative." The letters "i" and "u" must become "e" and "o" before nasals (m, n, ng) and can become "e" and "o" before "k, g, l, r." The letter "i", when in an unstressed syllable, is usually elided in rapid or vernacular speech (e.g. "Kirihsita" (Christ) is pronounced ['kri.sta] instead of [ˌki.ˈɾi.si.ta]).

[edit] Morphology/Grammar

Warilan is a highly synthetic language, so words are made up of several morphological roots and affixes. A single root can have many forms.

[edit] Nouns

Nouns are declined in the nominative, accusative, genitive, and less frequently dative and vocative. They also show number and definiteness. Nouns (and pronouns) do NOT show gender. This is how a typical noun would be declined.

bil- (book)

Singular Plural
Nominative Indefinite bilu bilong
Nominative Definite bila bilang
Accusative Indefinite biluk bilonguk
Accusative Definite bilak bilanguk
Genitive Indefinite bilon bilongon
Genitive Definite bilan bilangon

To sum up what's going on in the declension table:

  • The suffix -u acts as the indefinite article (it morphs to "o" before nasals)
  • the suffix -a is the definite article
  • the suffix -ng marks plural ("-ong" is indefinite plural (u~o + ng) and "-ang" is definite (a + ng))
  • the suffix -uk marks the accusative case (it morphs to "-k" in the indefinite and definite singular)
  • "-on" marks the genitive case (becomes "-n" in the indefinite and definite singular)
  • the order of suffixation for a noun is ROOT-ARTICLE-NUMBER-CASE
  • The singular number and nominative case are "empty cases," marked with "-Ø" (i.e. no suffix)

[edit] Word Formation

The table above show how a noun can be altered to take on different roles in a sentence. The rules of word formation below change the meaning of the word.

We'll use a different sample root:

bat- (person)

Now we'll change its meaning with affixes

batu = a person

bati = to live (not in the sense of a place, but in health)

ambati = to bear/birth a child (literally "to give life")

batilu = a community

batamilu = a crowd (-am- is an ad hoc infix)

batubu = an inspired person

batubatu = an inspiring person

batutu = a male person (the word "utu" means "a (young) man")

batenu = a female person (the word "inu" means "a (young) woman, the suffix is borrowed from Esperanto)

ombatu = a holy/sacred person (the prefix is oN-, so the nasal can morph depending on the following letter. If the following letter is a vowel, the nasal is the default "n")

ombatubatilu = an inspiring community of holy men (this is is similar to the native root for "monastery," which is "ombatil-")

batipiḥu = a midget (derogatory, don't use talking to a stranger)

EXPLANATIONS:

  • as you know, the suffix -u is equivalent to the English "a," the indefinite article
  • "-i" marks the infinitive form of a verb ("gogi" to think, "bili" to read (remember bil- is book??))
  • "aN-" is a PREFIX (different from the ad hoc (arbitrary) infix -am-) used on verbs and means "give, make (for)," and comes from the verb "ani" (to give, make (for). An example of this is when "aN-" is added to "bili" (to read) and transforms it into "ambili" (to write)
  • "-il-" is the affix for a collective plural or a group (e.g. "bililu" (a group of books, a pile of books)
  • "-am-" is one of two ad hoc affixes (the other being the prefix "si-") which are added to differentiate between words with similar but slighty different meanings.
  • "-ub-" means "inspiring," something that creates an idea or causes something (e.g "gogubatu" (a muse) (THOUGHT-inspire-agent/actor-indefinite)
  • "-at-" means the agent/actor of an action, the equivalent of the English suffix "-er" (as in "teach, teacher"), another example being ("bilatu" - a reader)
  • "-it-" is the opposite of "-at-," it means the receiver/patient of an action (in English it's "one who/that is ROOT-ed). Theoretically, "bilitu" (one that is read) could be a poetic synonym for "bilu" (others: "ubitu" (an inspired one), "Ompalita" (The enlightened one, the native term for "Buddha").
  • "-ut-" or "-in-" (masculine + feminine). An old man or woman marked with the affixes "-ab-" and "-or-" respectively.
  • "-ip-" is the diminuitive affix, it means "little" ("bilipu" - magazine, booklet)
  • "-iḥ-" is the "weak pejorative" or derogatory suffix. Generally used in conversation with friends or when making a joke. A stronger, cruder version (the "strong pejorative") is "-aḥ-" which should NEVER be used in public with strangers (it has an association with a certain four-letter word in English).

Here's a list of other important affixes:

-an (conceptual, general suffix) used for concepts and abstract things (ap- "eat," apan "food;" isip- "happy", isipan "joy, happiness"). It doesn't take an article, case, or number (but acts as a singular noun with the conjugation of verbs)

-ar- (all, the body of) used for an entirety of something, the whole of (e.g. "Kirihsitara" (Chrisitianity))

-an- (a member) a member of an organization/society (NOT an ethnicity or country) (e.g. "Dudanu" (a Jew), "iharatanganu" (a member of the military)).

-al- (conceptual, general suffix) the equivalent of -an but can take number, an article, and case (apan "food," apalu "a food")

-as (country) marks a nation or a region (Angilas "England," Amehrikas "America")

-ak- (portion, part) a section, portion, part (a- (house), aaku (a room))

-ib- (a tool, instrumental affix) used as a pseudo instrumental case affix (ambili - to write, ambilibu (a pen))

di(l)- (from) refers to a place

-(u)h (to) a locative purpose only, basically shifts the stress to the final syllable ("bila" [ˈbi.la] (the book), "bilah" [bi.ˈla] (to the book))

di...(u)k a circumfix, used to mark the dative case (indirect object)

-ahm or -amahng (plural) (vocative case) rarely used anymore, used to call someone (now people just use the root) "batahm!" (Person! (used to get someone you don't know's attention, considered polite)

ni- (opposite) the opposite of something, usually used with verbs, adjectives, and adverbs, although it can be used with nouns. ("ḥahi - to praise, judge," "niḥahi - to condemn)

[edit] Adjectives

Adjectives are made from roots and can use many of the above affixes. However, to make a word an adjective, one must add one of the four following suffixes:

-al - used for the physical description of a person or object (color, number, shape, texture, size, etc...), examples being "ṣimal" (wooden, woody), "meral" (red), "vaḥengal" (long, stretched).

-at - used for mental descriptions, emotions, descriptions of concepts and abstract ideas, examples being "isipat" (happy), "vaḥigukat" (very weird), "nivudat" (fake, artificial)

-ik - used for the descriptions of places/regions, ethnicity/background

-it - means "-like, -ish." Examples: "bilit" (book-like), "utit" (man-like)

(the genitive case of a noun is used to indicate possession and some adjective-like qualities as well)

[edit] Colors

The names are colors are special kinds of adjectives that contain three roots for the three primary colors and each have an "affixal" form.

English meaning root form affixal form
red mer- -em-
yellow sib- -is-
blue hus- -uh-

The dominant color is listed first (red is dominant over yellow, which is dominant over blue) as a root, the other (or two) as affixes. The only time this is changed is if one color is stronger than the other (very yellowish red: "sibemal" NOT "merisal")

Here are the basic colors:

meral = red

sibal = yellow

husal = blue

merisal = orange

meruhal = purple

sibuhal = green

vaḥemal = white (literally "very bright)

vaḥibogal = black ("very dark")

[edit] Adverbs

Adverbs are very simple. They use the roots and affixes, and end in the adverbial suffix "-il"

Examples: "bogil" (darkly), "isipil" (happily), "aḥil" (very rudely, crudely)

[edit] Verbs

Verbs are relatively easy in Warilan compared to other languages. They have their own special set of affixes.

Take the root "bak-" (be). It can be modified in mood, aspect, tense, person, and number. Here's its conjugation in the present imperfect indicative (simple present tense):

Person Singular Plural
1st bakip bakap
2nd bakit bakat
3rd baki baka

The Warilan can then be modified after it has been conjugative for person and number with affixes for mood, aspect, voice, and tense. They are put in this order:

Voice-Mood-ROOT-Aspect(Time)-Tense-Number-Person

Here are the affixes:

Voice: - "bi-" (passive voice), "ha-" (reflexive), (active voice is an empty prefix)

Mood: - "ku-" (subjunctive mood) (jussive, indicative, have an empty prefix, optative and conditional behave as tenses)

Aspect(Time) - "-ad-" (definite/defined time, perfective aspect), (undefined time/imperfective aspect is an empty infix)

Tense - "-am-" (past tense), "-om-" (future tense), "-as-" (conditional), "-ug-" (optative), (present tense is an empty infix)

  • Number and person are explained in the above table

[edit] Jussive Mood (Commands)

Once used along with the vocative case, the Jussive mood is the only irregular verb mood in Baasa Warilan, and is relatively simple anyway. Here's the sample verb "vaki" (to go), conjugated for number and person (the mood, tense, aspect, voice are all empty)

Singular Plural
vakah vakahng
  • Add "-ib-" if you want to be formal ("Bu, vakibah!" - You (formal), Go!)

[edit] Other Verb Affixes

"-i" - the infinitive

"pa-" - prefixal form of the verb "paki" (to be able to), used like so: "bilip" - I read, "pabilip" - I can read/I am able to read

"ṣi-" - prefixal form of "ṣisi" (to want)

"wa-" - prefixal form of "waki" (to ought to, should)

"mi-" - prefixal form of "miki" (to have just)

"-aṣ-" - intransitivizer (api - to eat something, apaṣi - to eat/dine)

"-ah-" - a kind of "permanent" affix (vuli - to sleep, vulahi - to die)

"-a-" - transitivizer ("basi - to speak (intransitive), baasi - to speak (a language))

"ni-" - opposite

"-isin-" - infix form of "isinil" (quickly) (bilisinip - I read quickly)

"-ogad-" - infix form of "ogadil" (slowly) (bilogadip - I read slowly)

"aN-" - give, make for... (from "ani" - to give, make)(ambilip - to write)

"id-" - receive, get made (from "idi" - to receive)(idabaki - to be created, born)

[edit] Pronouns

The pronouns are as follows. They are decline for number and case.

Person Singular Plural
1st Ha Hang
2nd Gu, Bu1 Gong, Bong1
3rd A Ang

1Both "Gu" and "Bu" mean you, but "gu" is used in informal/normal everyday situations, so it is used much more often. Only use "Bu" if you're in a very formal setting, like a business meeting or a fancy dinner party.

For the accusative case, add -k to the singular and -uk to the plural. In the accusative, the pronoun goes BEFORE the verb instead of after like a noun. For the genitive, add -n to the singular and -on to the plural. (For the vocative, it's -m (singular) and -am (plural)).

The roots are (H-, G-, B-, and Ø-). They are used for the possessive singulars, which take the suffix "-ul." Add "-ul" to the normal plural forms for the possessive plurals.

Hang (we), Hangul (our - (ours is "Hangula")). A (he/she), Ul (his/her), Ula (his/hers).

[edit] Numbers

Numbers are constructed and pronounced much as they are in English (with native Warilan numbers in place, of course). Ordinal numbers take the suffix "-ul" added to the root, temporal numbers (once, twice...) take "-ir," and multiplicatives (double, triple) take "-ar"

Here are the cardinal numbers:

ṣona - zero (ṣ-)

ḥa - one (ḥ-)

di - two (d-)

tar - three (tar-)

kwa - four (kaw-)

kwi - five (kiw-)

seh - six (s-)

pah - seven (p-)

haṣ - eight (h-)

nem - nine (n-)

ki - ten (k-)

da - 100 (dah-)

ka - 100 (kah-)

arusa OR milohn - 1 million (arus-, milon-)

aruna OR bilohn - 1 billion (arun-, bilon-)

The other numbers are then constructed like so: ki tar (13), sehki nem (69), kwadapahki di (472), aruna pahdasehkikwaarusa haṣidatarikika dadiki kwi (1,764,832,125).

The first book: Hul bila

I told you twice - Dir guk sibaasamip

double time - dar iruh (lit. "double to a time").

[edit] Syntax

Word order in Warilan is relatively simple. There are a few irregularities that must be memorized, though.

[edit] General Word Order

The word order in Warilan is usually SVO (subject clause-verb-object clause (predicate)), but this can be changed around without loss of meaning.

[edit] Dative Constructions (Indirect Object)

Nouns in the dative case (form of "di-ROOT-(u)k") come directly BEFORE the subject (whereas the direct object comes after the verb)

Ha anip ṣaraka - I give the dog (away)

Diguk ha anip ṣaraka - I give the dog to you

[edit] Accusative Pronouns

The accusative form of a pronoun comes directly before the verb.

Ha guk mongip! - I love you (I-NOM you-ACC love-SING-1P)

[edit] Objects of Jussive Verbs (Commands)

In a jussive clause, the direct object comes directly before the verb.

Dihak ak anah! - Give him to me!

[edit] Adjectives and Adverbs

Adjectives and adverbs come directly before the noun/verb they are describing or their roots are attached with an epenthetic "a"

Meral bila baki hulak. - The red book is mine.

Duamila baki merak - The good cat is the red one
