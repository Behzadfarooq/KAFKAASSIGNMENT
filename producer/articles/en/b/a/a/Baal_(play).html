Baal (play)

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Baal is Bertolt Brecht's first full-length play. Set in Berlin's demimonde, it concerns a wastrel youth who is involved in several sexual affairs and at least one murder. Brecht wrote Baal before he had developed the self-conscious alienation effects, or Verfremdungseffekt, that would mark his later work. There are more conscious references to nature in Baal than in other Brecht plays. The play was begun in 1918 (when Brecht was 20 years old) and first produced in December 1923.

He wrote it while he was a university student as a reply to the play Der Einsame (The Loner) by the Nazi writer Hanns Johst. The story traces the decline of a drunken and dissolute poet, an anti-hero, who rejects the conventions and trappings of polite society. Baal roams the countryside, womanizing and brawling. He seduces Johanna, who subsequently drowns herself. He spurns his pregnant mistress, Sophie and abandons her. When he murders his friend Ekart, he becomes a fugitive from the police. Defiantly aloof from the consequences of his actions, Baal is nonetheless brought down by his debauchery, leaving in his wake the corpses of deflowered maidens and murdered friends.

Brecht's interpretation of Baal is that he is an animal. There are many references in the play of Baal as animalistic. For example, he is called "Elephant". Also, Ekhert calls him a "degenerate beast."

Setting: about 1911, in Augsburg, Germany and the surrounding region.

In 1982, musician/actor David Bowie played the title role for a BBC television production of Baal. Bowie's recordings of the play's five songs were released as an EP, David Bowie in Bertolt Brecht's BAAL.
