[edit] To add

"He did, also, go frequently to the YMCA, and its affiliate the Bantu Mens' Social Club, at No 1 Eloff Street." Source Is it affiliated with the YMCA from the start? Lsjzl 18:40, 17 June 2006 (UTC)

  • Good question; my sources indicate a link, but does not explain the nature of the affiliation. I will try and find out. DocDee 18:47, 17 June 2006 (UTC)
  • D.D.T. Jabavu wrote: "One solution is suggested by the activities of the Rev. Ray Phillips in establishing the Johannesburg Bantu Men's Social Centre that provides for the African progressive men just what the great Y.M.C.A. centres in America do for Coloured men and women." The Segregation Fallacy and Other Papers, 1928 DocDee 18:58, 17 June 2006 (UTC)
  • I have come across some possible links: a. the Jan H. Hofmeyr School of Social Work for black Africans was housed in the Social Centre building between 1941 and 1947; b. Max Yergan, and African-American who organized on behalf of the YMCA in South Africa, had envisioned such training for black Africans in 1932; c. a building constructed under Yergan's initiative at Fort Hare was sold and the funds used for the Jan H. Hofmeyr School of Social Work; d. the School was initially started to train social workers for the YMCA. See Alan Cobley's book in the reference section of the article for detail.
