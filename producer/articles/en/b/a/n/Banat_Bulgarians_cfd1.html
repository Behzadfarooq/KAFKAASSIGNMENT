The Banat Bulgarians (Banat Bulgarian: palćene or banátsći balgare; common Bulgarian: банатски българи banatski balgari) are a distinct Bulgarian minority group which settled in the historical region of the Banat in the 18th century. Unlike most other Bulgarians, they are Roman Catholic by confession and stem from groups of Paulicians and Roman Catholics from modern northern and northwestern Bulgaria. The Banat Bulgarians speak a distinctive codified form of the Eastern Bulgarian vernacular with considerable foreign lexical influences. Since the Liberation of Bulgaria in 1878, a large number of them have also returned to the country, where they have founded their separate villages.

The official Romanian census states that 6,468 people of Bulgarian origin[1] inhabit the Romanian part of the Banat and the Serbian census of 2002 recognized 1,658 Bulgarians in Vojvodina,[2] the autonomous province covering the Serbian part of the region. Bulgarian researchers estimate the Banat Bulgarians' number at 12,000 in Romania and 3,000 in Serbia.[3]

The earliest and most important centres of the Banat Bulgarian population are the villages of Dudeştii Vechi (Stár Bišnov) and Vinga in modern Romania,[4] while notable Banat Bulgarian communities also exist in the villages of Breştea (Brešćа), Colonia Bulgară (Telepa) and Denta, as well as the cities of Timişoara (Timišvár) and Sânnicolau Mare (Smikluš) in modern Romania, and Ivanovo, Konak (Kanak) Jaša Tomić (Modoš), Skorenovac (Gjurgevo) and Belo Blato in what is today Serbia.[5]

In Bulgaria, returning Banat Bulgarians populated the villages of Asenovo, Pleven Province, Bardarski Geran, Dragomirovo, Veliko Tarnovo Province, Gostilya and Bregare, among others, in some of which they coexist or coexisted with Banat Swabians and Eastern Orthodox Bulgarians.[6]

[edit] History

[edit] Origin and migration north of the Danube

The Bulgarian Roman Catholic community in the northwestern ore-mining town of Chiprovtsi and the surrounding villages, possibly established by "Saxon" miners in the Middle Ages[8] who later migrated elsewhere or were assimilated,[9] organized the unsuccessful Chiprovtsi Uprising against the Ottoman rule of Bulgaria in 1688.[8]

Following the uprising's suppression due to organizational flaws and the halt of the Austrian offensive against the Ottomans, around 300 families of the surviving Catholics fled north of the Danube to Oltenia, initially settling in Craiova, Râmnicu Vâlcea and other cities, where their existing rights were confirmed by Wallachian Prince Constantin Brâncoveanu. Some of them moved to southwestern Transylvania, founding privileged colonies in Vinţu de Jos (1700) and Deva (1714). After Oltenia was occupied by Austria in 1718, the status of the Bulgarians in the region improved even further, as an imperial decree of 1727 allowed them the same privileges as their colonies in Transylvania. The favourable conditions for the Bulgarians under Austrian rule attracted another wave of migration of Bulgarian Catholics. It comprised about 300 families from the formerly Paulician villages of central northern Bulgaria who settled in Craiova between 1276 and 1730, but without receiving the same rights as the colonists from Chiprovtsi.[10]

As the Habsburgs were forced to withdraw from Oltenia in 1737 in the wake of a new war with the Ottoman Empire, the Bulgarians, fleeing from another Ottoman occupation, settled in the Austrian-ruled Banat to the northwest, and were allowed by the Austrian authorities[11] to found the villages of Stár Bišnov in 1738 and Vinga (or Theresiopolis) in 1741. In 1744, a decree of Maria Theresa of Austria once again confirmed their privileges received in Oltenia.[12]

[edit] Under Austrian and Hungarian rule

The Roman Catholic church in Vinga, Romania

While around a hundred Paulicians from the region of Svishtov and Nikopol followed the migration wave to the Banat between 1753 and 1777,[13] the existing Bulgarian population quickly spread around the region from the late 18th to the second quarter of the 19th century, settling in around 20 villages and towns in search for better economic conditions, specifically the need for arable land. During this period, the Banat Bulgarians established colonies in Modoš (1779), Kanak and Stari Lec (1820), Belo Blato (1825), Brešćа, Denta and Banatski Dvor (1842), Telepa (1846), Gjurgevo (1866) and Ivanovo (1867).[14]

The Roman Catholic church in Ivanovo, Serbia

After their settlement, the Banat Bulgarians began to take care of education and religion in their colonies. The Neo-Romanesque church in Stár Bišnov dates to 1804 and the imposing Neo-Gothic church in Vinga was built in 1892. Until 1863, besides Latin, the liturgical language of the Banat Bulgarians was "Illyric", i.e. Croatian, which had been spread among them prior to their migration to the Banat. With the cultural revival of the Banat Bulgarians in the mid-19th century, which led to the release of their first printed book, Manachija kathehismus za katolicsanske Paulichiane, in 1851, the Banat Bulgarian vernacular was gradually introduced in the church. "Illyric" was also substituted with Banat Bulgarian in education in 1860 (officially in 1864). In 1866, Jozu Rill codified the dialect with his essay Bálgarskotu pravopisanj.[15]

Following the Ausgleich of 1867, the Hungarian authorities gradually intensified the Magyarization of the Banat, imposing Hungarian as the main language of education, a state that would not change until World War I.[16]

[edit] In the Romanian and the Serbian Banat

After the dissolution of Austria-Hungary following World War I, the Banat was divided between Romania and Serbia, with the larger part of the Banat Bulgarians becoming citizens of the Kingdom of Romania, but a significant community landing in the Kingdom of Yugoslavia.

In Greater Romania, the Banat Bulgarians' separate identity was distinguished in censuses and statistics.[17] The main language of education was changed to Romanian and the Bulgarian schools were nationalized. A Romanian geography book of 1931 describes the Bulgarians in the county of Timiş-Torontal as "foreigners", and their national dress as "not as beautiful" as the Romanian one,[18] but in general the Banat Bulgarians were more favourably treated than the larger Eastern Orthodox Bulgarian minority in interwar Romania.[19]

In the Kingdom of Yugoslavia, the existence of any Bulgarian minority, whether in the Vardar Banovina, the Western Outlands or the Banat, was denied, and the post-World War I official statistics provide no data regarding the number of the Banat Bulgarians.[20] In comparison with the Eastern Orthodox Bulgarians in Yugoslavia, the Banat Bulgarians were treated better by the Yugoslav authorities,[21] although Serbo-Croatian was the only language of education.[22]

In the 1930s, the Banat Bulgarians in Romania entered a period of cultural revival led by figures such as Ivan Fermendžin, Anton Lebanov and Karol Telbis (Telbizov). The new cultural leaders of the Banat Bulgarians emphasized the Bulgarian identity at the expense of the identification as Paulicians and Roman Catholics, establishing contacts with the Bulgarian government and other Bulgarian communities in Romania, particularly that in Dobruja. The organs of this cultural revival were the newspaper Banatsći balgarsći glasnić (Banat Bulgarian Voice), issued between 1935 and 1943, and the annual Banatsći balgarsći kalendar (Banat Bulgarian Calendar), issued from 1936 to 1940. Although the planned 200th anniversary of the settlement in the Banat, the Banat Bulgarians' most significant manifestation in that period, was partially spoiled by the Romanian authorities, it still managed to attract considerable attention among the intellectuals in Bulgaria.[23]

Despite the rapprochement between Bulgaria and Yugoslavia in the 1930s and the resulting indirect recognition of a Banat Bulgarian minority by the Yugoslav government, the Banat Bulgarian revival at the time was much less perceivable in the region's Serbian part, and the local Banat Bulgarian population was only partially affected by the work of the functionaries in Romania.[24]

[edit] Immigration to Hungary, the United States and Bulgaria

The secondary migration of the Banat Bulgarians was mainly directed to Hungary and the United States. According to likely overestimated Bulgarian data from 1942, 10,000 Banat Bulgarians lived in Hungary, mainly in the major cities.[25] Members of the Banat Bulgarian community in Hungary include several deputies to the National Assembly, e.g. Petar Dobroslav, whose son László Dobroslav (László Bolgár) was a diplomat, and Georgi Velčov.[26]

Location of the main Banat Bulgarian villages in Bulgaria

During the Interwar period, the Banat Bulgarian communities in Romania were among those experiencing the greatest immigration to the United States, particularly in the 1920s and 1930s.[27] An organized Bulgarian community was established in Lone Wolf, Oklahoma, where the Banat Bulgarians primarily engaged in farming.[28]

A significant number of Banat Bulgarian returned to their original homeland, beginning in the 1880s and 1890s, founding several villages in Pleven Province, Vratsa Province and Veliko Tarnovo Province and receiving privileges according to the Law for the settlement of the unpopulated lands of 1880. They introduced superior agricultural technologies to the country and fully applied their experience in the sphere. In Bulgaria, their religious life was to an extent determined by the clashes between the dominant Eastern Orthodoxy and the minority Catholicism, as well as cultural conflicts with other Roman Catholic communities which they lived with in several villages, such as the Banat Swabians and the Bulgarian Paulicians from Ilfov.[29]

[edit] Language

The vernacular of the Bulgarians of the Banat can be classified as belonging to the Eastern Bulgarian group. A typical feature is the "ы" (*y) vowel, which can either take an etymological place or replace "i".[3] Other characteristic phonological features are the "ê" (wide "e") reflex of the Old Church Slavonic yat and the reduction of "o" into "u" and more seldom of "e" into "i": puljé instead of pole ("field"), sélu instead of selo ("village"), žiná instead of žena ("woman").[3] Another characteristic feature is the palatalization of final consonants, which is typical for other Slavic languages, but found only in dialects in Bulgarian (Bulgarian den ("day") sounds like and is written as denj).[30]

Lexically, the language of the Banat Bulgarians has borrowed many words from languages such as German (such as drot from Draht, "wire"; gáng from Gang, "anteroom, corridor"), Hungarian (vilánj from villan, "electricity"; mozi, "cinema"), Serbo-Croatian (stvár from stvar, "item, matter"; ráčun from račun, "account") and Romanian (šedinca from şedinţă, "conference")[31] due to the close contacts with the other peoples of the multiethnical Banat and the religious ties with other Roman Catholic peoples. Banat Bulgarian also has an older layer of loanwords from Ottoman Turkish[32] and Greek, which it shares with other Bulgarian dialects (e.g. hirgjén from Turkish ergen, "unmarried man, bachelor"; trandáfer from Greek τριαντάφυλλο triantafyllo, "rose").[33] Loanwords constitute around 20% of the Banat Bulgarian vocabulary.[30][32] A Hungarian influence can also be seen in the names of some Banat Bulgarians, as the Hungarian (eastern) name order is sometimes used (the family name coming first and the given one last) and the female ending "–a" is often dropped from family names. Thus, Marija Velčova would become Velčov Marija.[34]

Besides loanwords, the lexis of Banat Bulgarian was also enriched by calques and neologisms such as sfetica ("icon", formerly used ikona and influenced by German Heiligenbild), zarno ("bullet", from the word meaning "grain"), oganbalváč ("volcano", literally "fire belcher") and predhurta ("foreword").[30]

The Banat Bulgarian language uses a script of its own, largely based on the Croatian version of the Latin script, and preserves many features that are archaic in the language spoken in Bulgaria. The language was codified as early as 1866 and is used in literature and press, which distinguishes it from plain dialects.[30]

[edit] Notable figures

  • Eusebius Fermendžin (1845–1897) — historian, high-ranking Franciscan cleric, theologian, polyglot, active member of the South Slavic Academy of Sciences
  • Leopold Kosilkov (1850–1940) — teacher and writer
  • Jozu Rill — 19th-century teacher and internationally acclaimed textbook writer, codified the Banat Bulgarian orthography and grammar in 1866
  • Carol Telbisz (1853–1914) — long-time mayor of Timişoara (1885–1914)
  • Anton Lebanov (b. 1912) — lawyer, journalist, poet
  • Karol Telbizov (1915–1994) — lawyer, journalist, scientist[35][36]
