Balneário Barra do Sul

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Balneário Barra do Sul (26°27′26″S, 48°36′41″W) is a city located in the north of the Brazilian state of Santa Catarina.

Despite possessing a very small population (around 10,000 habitants circa 2006), it is one of the oldest communities in the Santa Catarina state, and arguably in Brazil.

The main economical activities of the city are tourism, fishing, shrimp growing, agriculture (tree plantation for paper/wood), underground sand extraction, and one large chemical industry (magnesium extraction from sea water).

Tourism is directly related to presence of very clean beaches and the sea channel that is good for fishing and water sports. The city population increases to around 50,000 in summer time (mostly in Christmas time until the end of January, when collective vacations are usually granted for people in nearby industrial cities).

Interestingly, the biggest city party (Festa da Tainha) happens near the winter, when the cold waters bring huge schools of Tainha fish (a fish with fat-rich meat, vaguely similar to salmon but meat is white).

Most of the Barra do Sul natives have Portuguese+Indian heritage, and inherit from them the intimacy with the sea, the cuisine based on sea food etc. Beginning at 60s, a small but steady immigration flux of people from other towns increased the ethnical diversity, so almost all ethnical communities found in Santa Catarina have a share in Barra do Sul.

History

The proximity with São Francisco do Sul city (itself one of the three oldest cities in Brazil) and Florianópolis made Barra do Sul a natural route for transportation, as well as an alternative São Francisco's port entrance coming from South (hence the name, Sul is South in Portuguese). The first three non-indian inhabitants were a small military patrol that helped travellers to cross the river and protected them from indian attacks (except by Carijós, tribes in the area tended to react violently to white man presence).

In a very slow pace, Portuguese immigrants or people with Portuguese descent began to colonize Barra do Sul, living from cassava planting, fishing and/or providing services for travellers en route to the bigger cities. In the beginning of the XX century, extrativism of certain leaves from "mangues" (seawater swamps) for the leather industry was also important. Immigrants intemixed with Carijó indians, creating the ethnical mix found today.

For centuries, Barra do Sul had no roads to other cities. Navigation and walking by the seashore were the alternatives. The main two roads were built well in the XX century and they have an interesting story:

  • The first one (Salinas) was open incidentally by a palm extractivism business - as the palms were cut, a small railroad made from wood progressed in direction to the South, outlining the road as the palm reserves exhausted. This road was very primitive, allowing only animal-traction vehicles. In the 60's the military engineering troops improved the road to the point it can (barely) be used by regular cars.
  • The second one (Conquista), that nowadays is the main access to the city, was open around the 50's by a private business that possessed a big area near Barra do Sul and planned to sell it in relatively small chunks (and no one would buy them in absence of a public road leading to them). This road was asphalted in the 90's. It was also corrected to be more straight, so around 8km of the original, sinuous route are still there and serve the few local inhabitants, small leisure farms and tree plantations.

The sea channel blocking

In 1935, the sea channel was landfilled to build a railroad across the channel, about 15km upstream of the channel's connection to the sea (road can be seen at 26°21′44″S, 48°39′46″W). This action caused several ecological and economical impacts. First, the South sea entrance no longer could be used for navigation, since the landfill had cut the route to São Francisco's port. Several fishing, extractivism and boat maneuerving workers lost their jobs.

Along time, the reduced seatide and sea currents made the South sea entrance become shallower, reducing fishing output and stagnating waters. The only small positive effect was to increase the dry area in city center; several blocks of city center are built on land that did not exist in the first half of XX century.

In 1982, an emergential action was taken to avoid the sea channel to completely stagnate: the South channel exit borders were filled with stones and made narrower, to avoid sand and mud accumulation. Since then, the strong seatide current has been able to keep the channel open to the sea, improving a lot the whole channel's situation.

There are proposals to re-open the landfill, entirely or just a small gap to allow the current to pass, but it will be an expensive measure and there are concerns about increasing sea level reclaiming back the now-dry once-wet city areas.
