Balvi is a small town in the Latgale region of eastern Latvia. It is the administrative seat of the region (Latvian: rajons) of the same name since 1949; prior to the occupation of Latvia it was part of the Abrene district.

The first mention of Balvi (the name derives from the stream Bolupīte and the adjacent lake) was in 1224. A small wooden church and manor were constructed on the estate of a Polish noblewoman at the site ca. 1765. When Latgale came under Russian rule in 1772, the estate was granted to the Yelagin family by Catherine II. In 1806 it passed to the Horozhinsky family and in 1876 the estate was purchased by the Baltic German Transehe-Roseneck family. The village was separated from the estate in 1915, and Balvi received town rights in 1928.

Most of the town's Jews (ca. 19% of the population) perished in the Stahlecker phase of the Holocaust in August 1941. The retreating Germans set fire the Balvi in July 1944, and the town was rebuilt according to Soviet plans from 1945. Balvi was a center of the Singing Revolution and is vital to Latgalian culture today, its library the focus of many cultural events.
