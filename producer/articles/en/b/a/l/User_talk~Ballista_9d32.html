[edit] Dinosaur articles

Hi - now that you're an admin, I can bother you with this legitimately. How do we work out if there are any other dinos who do not have the word 'dinosaur' on their page? (just today found that Tuojiangosaurus was without). Finding those that do is easy enough, on AWB but finding those without ....? I suppose we could ask AWB for a list of dinos and compare that with the project's list? Bit laborious, though. - Ballista 17:20, 6 August 2006 (UTC)

Hey Ballista!
No, no bother at all. Here's what I'd do. You could use the 'What Links Here' tool, but it would be painfully long. Use the special settings on AWB and it will take a half hour to churn thru the entire list.
Here's what you do:
1st tab: Make List: Make from… “Links on Page” and in the white box, type: List of dinosaurs. This will make a list which contains every dinosaur on the List of dinosaurs page.
2nd tab: Set Options. Check the box marked “Skip if contains”, in the white box, type: dinosaur . This will cause AWB to skip any page that already contains the word dinosaur, and move on to the next article.
3rd tab: Click “Start the process”. This will start the process.
I'm here all day, so if you have any more questions, or it just doesn't work, let me know. :) --Firsfron of Ronchester 17:37, 6 August 2006 (UTC)

[edit] Image copyright problem with Image:Bambiraptor-head.1.png

Thanks for uploading Image:Bambiraptor-head.1.png. However, the image may soon be deleted unless we can determine the copyright holder and copyright status. The Wikimedia Foundation is very careful about the images included in Wikipedia because of copyright law (see Wikipedia's Copyright policy).

The copyright holder is usually the creator, the creator's employer, or the last person who was transferred ownership rights. Copyright information on images is signified using copyright templates. The three basic license types on Wikipedia are open content, public domain, and fair use. Find the appropriate template in Wikipedia:Image copyright tags and place it on the image page like this: {{TemplateName}}. If you have not already done so, please also include the source of the image. In many cases this will be the website where you found it.

Please specify the copyright information and source on any other images you have uploaded or will upload. Remember that images without this important information can be deleted by an administrator. If you have any questions, feel free to contact me, or ask them at the Media copyright questions page. Thank you. Awyong Jeffrey Mordecai Salleh 07:55, 11 September 2006 (UTC)

Well I changed what you wrote because "one fewer phalanges" is grammatically incorrect. But it was better than what I had written... apparently I forgot that phalanx is the singular form when I first wrote it... I think I had "one fewer phalange" which apparently was referring to some Lebanese resistance movement. A little off topic huh? I agree the wording is awkward and needs to be fixed... just not sure how yet. A couple problems with your suggestion... for one, it wouldn't be the number of phalanges in the fourth and fifth digits of the forelimbs was reduced from three to two... it would be the number of phalanges in the fourth digit of the forelimb was reduced from three to two and the number of phalanges in the fifth digit of the forelimb was reduced from two to one. Another problem with that sentence is that it makes it sound like Abrictosaurus is more derived than Heterodontosaurus. The problem is that there is really no baseline digit formula to compare it to. What is the primitive state for Ornithischia? In Lesothosaurus the digit formula is 2-3-4-3-1. In Hexinlusaurus it is 2-3-4-2-2. In Abrictosaurus it is 2-3-4-2-1 and in Heterodontosaurus it is 2-3-4-3-2. Those fourth and fifth digits are just highly variable and it isn't possible to say whether the number of phalanges is reduced in Abrictosaurus or if there are extra ones in Heterodontosaurus (or both).

What if we just used "bone" instead of "phalanx" in the original sentence? Like one fewer bone in both the fourth and fifth digits of the forelimb. Does that make it any simpler? Or can you rearrange your suggestion to make it more accurate? Or make an entirely new one? Sheep81 07:50, 27 September 2006 (UTC)
