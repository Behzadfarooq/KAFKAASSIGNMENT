Bay Area Holocaust Oral History Project

The mission of the Bay Area Holocaust Oral History Project is to gather oral life histories of Holocaust survivors, liberators, rescuers, and eyewitnesses. The project is developing and maintaining a catalogue database for public use and to be used on the internet. Their goal is to provide students[1] , scholars[2] , and the general public access to their archives and distribute sets of the videotaped oral histories to libraries [3], schools and resource centers around the world.[4] [5]

The Bay Area Holocaust Oral History Project seeks to counteract Holocaust revisionism, and sees the alarming upsurge of hate crimes and intolerance as making the need to record these stories all the more urgent. The Oral History Project sees videos as a powerful antidote for Holocaust revisionism.[6] .[7]

[edit] The Collection

Lani Silver founded the Bay Area Holocaust Oral History Project in l98l and was executive director from l98l to l997.[8] Currently, the project is headed by Anne Grenn Saldinger.[9]Today, the project has some 1700 oral and video recordings of survivors, witnesses and children of survivors.[10]

[edit] Interviews

The Bay Area Holocaust Oral History Project in San Mateo has agreed to record the memories of SPK, AK, and Warsaw Uprising veterans. The Polish community are delighted to be working with the Jewish community on this project.

Each participant is given unedited video-tape copies of his or her interview at the conclusion of the taping. The interviewer is has a doctorate in psychology and years of experience conducting "survivor" interviews.

(The Bay Area Holocaust Oral History Project keeps a copy of each tape and sends duplicate copies to the Holocaust Museum in Washington, D.C. in addition to supplying, free-of-charge, a copy of the recording to the interviewee).[11]

[edit] The Histories are given in Oral Testimonies

  • Helen Farkas was born in Romania, as Helen Safa. She remembers the Hungarian occupation and growth of anti-Semitism very clearly. She was part of the death march, yet made a heroic escape. Helen married her pre-war fiance and left Romania shortly before it came under communist rule. Helen now lives in the San Francisco Bay Area and spends much of her time educating about the Holocaust.[12].
  • Hurst Sommer is a Holocaust survivor.[4]A German Jew, he escaped Germany with his family in 1938 to Shanghai, China, where he spent World War II in a Japanese internment camp.[13]
  • Max Drimmer and Herman Shine were both born in Berlin and knew each other as small children. In 1939 they were both sent to Sachsenhausen, a concentration camp close to Berlin. Their lives remained intertwined as they survived and escaped the horrific realities of the Holocaust. [14].
  • Gloria was born in 1930 in Nagy Bereg, Czechoslovakia, where her family owned a store. In 1938 Jews were forced to close all businesses. Gloria spent time in a local ghetto before being deportation to Auschwitz. While there, she was selected for the gas chamber, but escaped by jumping out of the truck . After several camps she was finally liberated. Gloria resides in San Francisco and is an active Holocaust educator. [15].
  • Oskar Klausenstock was born in Ciesezin, a small town in Poland. He was 17 years old when the war started. His memories of the Holocaust are from a teenager's perspective. He tells of his experiences with such vividness and clarity. He is also a poet and has transformed many of his memories into beautiful poems. [16].
