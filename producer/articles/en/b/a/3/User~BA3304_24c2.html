DETAILS OF ANALYSIS

I- Situational Analysis

The purpose of this section is to make explicit your beliefs and assumptions regarding the nature of the purchase decision process for the goods or services under investigation. In case analysis, we are concerned primarily with developing your skills of analysis in identifying areas of problems and opportunities and in developing well-supported marketing program recommendations.

Possible Questions for Nature of Demand: 1. How do buyers currently go about buying existing products or services? Describe the main types of behavior patterns and attitudes. 2. Number of stores considered 3. Degree of brand awareness and loyalty 4. Location of product category decision 5. Sources of product information and current awareness and knowledge levels 6. Who makes the purchase decision? 7. Who influences the buyer? 8. Duration of the process 9. Risk or uncertainty of negative purchase outcome 10. Can the market be meaningfully segmented using criteria such as age, family, life cycle, geographic location, product usage, etc.?

Basically, the student is to determine who, what, where, when, why, and how of the purchase decision. Note: the key to using the above questions is to ask what the implications for marketing programs are and then how you would use the information to support a recommendation.

Possible questions for Extent of Demand 1. What is the size of the market (units and dollars) now and what will the future hold? 2. What are the current market shares? 3. Is it best to analyze the market on an aggregate or segmented basis?

Possible questions for Nature of Competition The purpose of this section is to evaluate the present and future structure of competition. The key is to understand how the buyer evaluates alternative products or services relative to his or her needs.

1. What is the present and future structure of competition? 2. Number of competitors 3. Market shares 4. Financial Resources 5. Marketing and Financial Resources 6. What are the current marketing programs of competitors? 7. Is there a threat of new competitors? 8. What are the anticipated retaliatory moves of competitors?

Possible questions for Environmental Climate: 1. What are the relevant social, political, economic, and technological trends? 2. How do you evaluate these trends? Do they represent opportunities or problems?

Product Life Cycle: The purpose of this section is to make explicit assumptions about where a product is in its life cycle. This is important because the effectiveness of particular marketing variables may vary by stage.

Skills of the Firm: The purpose of this section is to critically evaluate the organization making the decision. Here, we effectively place limits on what they are capable of accomplishing.

1. Do we have the skills and experience to perform the functions necessary to be? in this business? Such as marketing, production, management, financial and R&D. 2. How do our skills compare to our competitors?

Financial Resources of the Firm: 1. Do we have the funds to support an effective marketing program? 2. Where are the funds coming from?

The ultimate goal of all marketing activities is usually expressed in financial terms. Proposed marketing activities must be evaluated for their financial implications. Financial analysis can be complex. It is the assumption that some simple financial calculations (as those listed below) can be useful in case analysis, however more sophisticated financial techniques are left to courses in financial management.

a) Costs (fixed and variable) b) Break-even c) Market share d) Margins


Distribution structure: 1. What channels exist and can we gain access? 2. Cost vs. revenue from different channels 3. Feasibility of using multiple channels 4. Nature of channel competition 5. Trends in channel structure

II- Problems and Opportunities In this section the students prepare a definite listing of KEY problems and opportunities identified from the situational analysis, which relate to the specific issues or decision questions faced by management. At this point, the critical issue is whether a profitable marketing program can be formulated or whether a current marketing program needs to be changed in order to overcome the problem areas and/or take advantage of opportunities.

III- Generation and Evaluation of Alternative Marketing Programs A marketing program consists of a series of marketing-mix decisions which represent an integrated and consistent “action plan” for achieving predetermined goals. Different marketing programs may be required for various target segments.

Objectives Defined 1. Target market segments identified 2. Volume to be sold (dollars or units) 3. Profit Analysis

Marketing mix/program decisions 1. Product decisions a. Develop new product? b. Change current product? c. Add or drop product from line? d. Product positioning e. Branding 2. Distribution decisions a. Intensity of distribution (intensive to exclusive) b. Multiple channels c. Types of wholesalers or retailers d. Degree of channel directness 3. Promotion decisions a. Mix of personal selling, advertising and sales promotion b. Branding c. Budget d. Message e. Media 4. Price decisions a. Price level (above, same, or below) b. Price variation (discount, geographical...) c. Margins d. Administration of price level e. Price leadership

The key to effective marketing decisions is to evaluate alternative marketing programs using information from the situation analysis. The pros and cons of each alternative should be presented and discussed.

Decision The outcome of the evaluation of alternatives is a decision. The student must make a decision. Case analysis is designed to develop your skills in making well-informed and supported marketing decisions. The quality of your reasoning is much more important than reaching any particular decision .
