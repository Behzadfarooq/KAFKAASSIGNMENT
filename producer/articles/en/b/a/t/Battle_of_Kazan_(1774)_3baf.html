The Battle of Kazan (1774) was a major battle during Pugachev Rebellion. It took place at July 12-15, 1774 in Kazan, Russian Empire, and neighborhoods. In the morning July 12, at the first stage rebels under Yemelyan Pugachev defeated governmental troops and seized them in Kazan Kremlin. During the battle some governmental forces came to the rebels' side. However, in the evening 12 July, governmental forces under Michelson reached Kazan and they defeated rebels in two battles 13 and 15 July, forcing Pugachev to retreat to Tsaryovokokshaysk and the to cross the Volga. From 25000 and 15000 rebels, participated in the first and last stages of the battle correspondingly, only 500 had escaped.[1]

[edit] Supposition

Kazan was endanged by Pugachev since the autumn of 1773. Many nobles escaped to Moscow, inspiring fear there. The defense plan was approved personally by Catherine the Great.

[edit] Kazan defenders

There were 3 sectors of defense in Kazan. The first under general Banner was from Kazanka and Arsk Field to Qaban lakes, there governmental troops were reinforced by gymnasium pupils under headmaster Kanitsa and armed officials. The second was from Pleteny to Yamskoy quarter under general-mayor Larionov, the third from Yamskoy to Kazanka, under colonel Svechin. The area north from the citadel was defended by Admiralty troops under Shchelin, the citadel itself was reinforced and garrison under Letskoy was ready to fight. Zilantov Monastery and the Admiralty were turned to strongholds. Redoubts were installed at Kazanka's bank and Arsk Field, as system of knife-rests were installed around the city. 9 cannons were placed behind the knife-rests. The suburb settlements should defend without governmental assistance.

There were 1500 regular troops in Kazan, along with policemen, Admiralty troops and firemen defenders numbered 2000. 2 July the governor von Brandt announced a coming siege.

[edit] Rebels

10 mullahs sent by Catherine's offer to agitate against Pugachev, were killed by rebels. Orthodox bishop Veniamin appealed to Christians to refuse from joining Pugachev army. However, many peasants from all modern Tatarstan joint the rebellion and marched to Kazan.

10-11 July Pugachev troops encamped to the north-east from Kazan, betveen Troitskaya Noksa and Tsaritsyno. The army numbered 25000, the most of them were Cossacks, ethnically Tatar and Udmurt peasants, Bashkir cavalry. Only Cossacks had fire-arms, Bashkirs were armed with bows, peasants were mostly armed with clubs and stakes. 11 July Pugachev with interpreter approached Kazan borders and sent bid for disarmament to governor von Brandt and city's population. Tatar community was invited to support the rebellion. Von Brandt refused to disarm, however, Tatar quarters sent 70 go-betweens with presents to Pugachev. They also disclosed weak places in Kazan defense.

12 July four o'clock of the morning Pugachev convened a council of war, dividing army into 4 groups. The first two under Beloborodov and Mineyev should attack Arsk Field, the main group under Pugachev himself attacked Sukonny (Broadcloth Manufactory) quarter. Group under Ovchinnikov and Bakhmutov should attack Tatar quarters.The storm started six o'clock after Pugachev's prayer.

[edit] Battle

[edit] Storm of Kazan

Beloborodov took Neyelova grove, the modern Gorky Park and approached citadel by the modern Karl Marx street. Pugachev's cannons neutralized governmental artillery. Pugachev shelled self-defense of the cloth fabric under manufacturer Dryablov. Workers joined the rebel army. The fourth group of rebels reached Mokry quarter and Admiralty.

Noble army was forced to retreat and was seized in the citadel. Nearby half of defenders, mostly ethnic Tatars go over rebel side. The central stone part of Kazan, mostly settled by nobles and merchants was set on fire. Sukonny and Tatar quarters, however, stayed safe.

Pugachev decreed to safe lives of civilians and captives. They were convoyed to the nearby village of Savinovo. Moreover, Pugachev invited them to join his troops. Believed, that the captive Lutheran priest was appointed colonel in Pugachev's army. However, nobles and resisters were massacred.

[edit] Pugachev's family

Pugachev's family, the wife Sofia Dmitrievna, 11-years old son Trofim, daughters Khristina and Agrafena and his brother, were imprisoned in Kazan during the rebellion. Sofia Pugacheva should confirm, that Yemelyan isn't Peter III, but a Cossack. She could visit markets to spread this information. When Pugachev's group marched they occasionally met his family. Trofim called him, but Pugachev said that he is simply a friend of this family. So, he took them to unit transport.[2]

[edit] Michelson's arrival

Kremlin was surrounded, rebel cannons were installed near Spasskaya Tower and started shelling. After noon, Pugachev stopped the storm and retreated to Arsk Field. There he participated in thanksgiving service. Then he had a dinner at Tatar merchant Musa Apanayev's house.

At the evening governmental troops from Ufa under Ivan Ivanych Michelson reached Kazan. A harsh battle took place at the modern Gogol street[3], but no side wined. Pugachev retreated to Savonovo and Sukhaya Reka villages. 13 July he tried to prevent unblocking of the citadel, but under pressure of Michelson and Potyomkin rebels were defeated were forced to retreat. 15 July Pugachyov's 15000 numbered army was defeated in the battle of Arsk Field. Pugachev with 500 men escaped to Tsaryovokokshaysk, to cross the Volga 18 July and to continue his struggle then. Michelson didn't follow him, as governmental forses hadn't horses.

The battle of Kazan had a significant role in Tatarstan's history. It should be noted that the Tatar participation in the rebellion numbered up to 85000. Nevertheless the participants of the rebellion were harshly punished, Tsar's government continued policy of the support of Muslim nobility and clergy, to prevent similar as a result of religious oppression in future. Feudal oppression, however, stayed for a long time. [4]
