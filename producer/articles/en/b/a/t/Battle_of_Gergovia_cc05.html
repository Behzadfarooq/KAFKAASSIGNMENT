The Battle of Gergovia took place in 52 BC at Gergovia (modern Gergovie), France. The battle was fought between a Roman Republic army, led by proconsul Julius Caesar, and Gallic forces led by Vercingetorix. The Gauls won the battle.

[edit] Background

Gergovia was the chief town of the Arverni, and was situated on a hill in Auvergne, about eight miles from the Puy de Dome. Some walls and earthworks believed to be from this period still survive. Later, when Gaul had been subdued, the place was dismantled and its Gaulish inhabitants resettled four miles away in the plain at the new Roman city of Augustonemetum (modern Clermont-Ferrand).

The Battle was the result of Caesar's attempt to force Vercingetorix into open warfare rather than his successful 'scorched earth' tactics, which left Caesar and his commanders baffled. The 'scorched earth' tactic involved burning farms in the area and therefore starving Caesar's army. Gergovia was where Vercingetorix chose to stand.

[edit] Siege and battle

Caesar faced Vercingetorix with 6 legions accompanied by Aedui auxilia and Germanic cavalry. Rather than risk a full frontal assault by the excellent Arverni Guard, Caesar relied on his superior siege tactics. He ordered a double trench, 12 feet wide, to be constructed between a captured hill and his main camp. Intending to completely encircle Gergovia and starve the Gauls inside, Caesar was interrupted by trouble with his Gallic allies the Aedui, whose force of ten thousand under Litavicus threatened rebellion. To deal with this threat Caesar took 4 legions and his cavalry and marched 25 miles to the Aedui camp, leaving the remaining 2 legions to guard the siege works at Gergovia under Gaius Fabius. This swift response pacified the Aedui who surrendered without giving battle. Caesar then returned by a forced march overnight to rejoin the two legions hard pressed at Gergovia.

Caesar now decided his idea for encirclement would fail. His only chance of victory was to get Vercingetorix off the high ground. He used a legion as a decoy and moved onto the better ground. He then ordered a general retreat to fool Vercingetorix and pull him off the high ground, but only the 10th legion responded to this order, the others being led by overeager tribunes or simply not hearing the trumpets signaling the retreat, being separated from them by a wide valley. These troops continued advancing on the town, entering the deserted Gallic camp and stopping just short of the walls. Alerted of the threat to the town, Vercingetorix abandoned his defensive maneuvers against the decoy legion and returned with the main body of his force.

Vercingetorix, at the head of his elite Arverni Guard, counterattacked from two sides, driving the Romans down the hill. Caesar was able to prevent a total rout, however, by taking the 10th legion, along with a few cohorts of the 13th that had been left to guard the camp, and drawing them up on level ground in support of those fleeing. Vercingetorix was successfully dissuaded and broke off his pursuit, returning to his fortifications around the town. The next day Caesar attempted to offer battle again but could not induce the Gauls to descend to level ground. Having failed to accomplish his objectives or improve his position Caesar then withdrew his forces toward the Aedui having suffered his first and only military defeat.

The Battle of Gergovia was a major victory for Vercingetorix, who prevailed over the Roman army despite being outnumbered 2:1.

An overconfident attack on Gergovia's defenses by Julius Caesar led to the loss of 46 centurions and 700 men along with over 6,000 wounded on the Roman side, compared to the several hundred Gauls killed and wounded.
