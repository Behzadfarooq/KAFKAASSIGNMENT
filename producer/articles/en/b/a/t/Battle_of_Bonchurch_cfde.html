The Battle of Bonchurch, although never having been specifically cited as the Battle of Bonchurch, was a confrontation between the military forces of France; and a local militia raised, and composed of residents of the Isle of Wight, in July 1545; which occured during the aborted French invasion of the Isle of Wight.

[edit] Wider Context

In revenge for a joint English-Spanish invasion of Boulogne, which the English would retain after the war had been concluded, and Soissons, Francis I of France decided to take on Henry VIII by launching an invasion of England. French ships were shored off of Portsmouth Harbour, waiting to invade the British Isles. A English force, under the command of the Duke of Suffolk, and comprising men from Kent, Berkshire, Sussex and Surrey; assembled at Southsea Common to prepare for the expected attack, with additional reinforcements making their way to their position. It is likely, therefore, that the French intended to capture the Isle of Wight to serve as a logistical base for wider operations on the mainland. The short-term cause of the conflict, aforementioned, was the invasion of mainland France. However, that too was a response to an attempt by France and the Ottoman Empire to try and seize parts of northern Italy, at that time a collection of city-states as opposed to a unified country. England and Spain swore to fight for its soverignty. In this way, the conflict at Bonchurch, whilst small in its scale, briefly became the focal point of an entire European conflict.

[edit] The French Plan

It has been presumed that the French, after landing at Bonchurch, would move rapidly inland, burning Wroxall and Appuldurcombe. It is likely that they would consolidate their forces on the heights of St. Boniface, before making their final push towards Sandown to link up with an additional landing on the north coast.

[edit] The English Plan

The local militia was under the command of Captain Robert Fyssher and, owing to the fact that the English had found out where the French were going to land; began to make defensive arrangements. He planned to attack the French as they advanced from Monks Bay to the face of St. Boniface Down.

[edit] The Battle

Having been exhausted by the climb up the cliffs of St Boniface Down, the French did enter the battle with a disadvantage, whereas the English had prepared themselves in their positions; thus satisfying the age old axiom that the conqueror is invariably the one who dictates where battle will commence. In the ensuing fight, which continued from dawn to midday, the English pulled off a victory. There are some accounts that local women fired arrows into the French line. The French were forced to abandon their advance into the Isle of Wight.

[edit] Aftermath

In what came down to be known as the Battle of the Solent, in which the famous English ship Mary Rose was sunk through the neglect of her crew to close off the lower gunports and prevent water from rushing in, on the 18th and the 19th July, the French were forced to retreat. The battle itself appeared to be inconclusive. However, in would appear the French commander on the spot, Claude d'Annebault, lost his nerve, and ordered a withdrawal. Preparing for their journey back to France, the French fleet landed a small band of men at Bonchurch to find fresh sources of water to replenish stocks for the return voyage. However, another battle ensued, in which a senior French commander, Chevalier D'Aux, was killed. When the war ended in 1548, his body was exhumed from the local cemetery, and taken back to France.
