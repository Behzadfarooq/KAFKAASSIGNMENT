Battle of Barrosa

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Peninsular War, 1810–1814
Fuengirola – Barrosa – Fuentes de Onoro – Albuera – Badajoz – Salamanca – Vitoria – Maya – Roncesvalles – Sorauren – Nivelle – Nive – Orthez – Toulouse

The Battle of Barrosa (March 5, 1811) was a minor rearguard action near Cádiz, Spain in which a British battalion drove back two French regiments and captured a regimental eagle.

[edit] Background

In the spring of 1811, Cádiz was besieged by the French under the command of Nicolas Jean de Dieu Soult, but Soult reduced his force in order to detach a force of 20,000 men to attack Badajoz. In an attempt to raise the siege of Cádiz, a mixed Anglo-Spanish force landed at Tarifa and marched up the coast to attack the French siege lines from the rear. The relieving army of approximately 15,000 was under the command of the Spanish General Manuel La Peña; the British contingent of 5,000 men was commanded by Lieutenant-General Thomas Graham.

At the approaches to Cádiz, the French had taken up a position at the place now known as "Pinar de los Franceses", a marshy area about 15 km (10 miles) east of the Barrosa beach in Chiclana de la Frontera, where the road to Cádiz and San Fernando passes between a marshy area and a pine forest. This position blocked an attack on the siege lines, but it left open a road nearer to the coast that led directly to Cádiz. La Peña decided to march onward to reinforce Cádiz rather than attempt to confront the French position directly and attack the siege lines.

[edit] Battle

To cover La Peña's move skirting the enemy right, a mixed English and Spanish force acted as a rearguard. Only the British component of the rearguard (a single battalion of 470 men drawn from the Gibraltar garrison) took up a position on a low hill inland from Barrosa. This battalion retreated when threatened by a superior French forces (of about 7,000 men). Graham's division, protecting La Peña's flank, came under long-range artillery fire, so he counter-marched his division to retake the hill that had been abandoned by his outmanned compatriots. The British infantry advanced in line as the French marched down the hill in columns to attack them. After an exchange of musketry at close range, the two French regiments broke ranks and fled. In the melee, the British captured a French regimental eagle.

[edit] Consequences

Tactically, and in terms of casualties inflicted, the battle was a victory for the British troops. They had already marched a considerable distance during that day (and the preceding night), and were fighting a French force approaching twice their numbers. (On La Peña's instructions, the Spanish stood and watched from a distance while their allies were assaulted). Strategically, it had no real effect; after the battle, the Spanish force resumed its march toward Cádiz without any further attempt to attack the French siege lines.

[edit] Notes

The Battle of Barrosa gives its name (despite the different spelling) to the Barossa wine-growing region of Australia.

A certain man named Keogh of the 87th Regiment of Foot (also known as The Prince of Wales' Irish Regiment of Foot) made an attempt at grabbing the eagle of the 8th Ligne Regiment in this battle. Keogh was killed in the process, but Sergeant Patrick Masterson (or Masterman, the records conflict) finally secured the eagle with the words, 'Bejabbers, boys, I've got the cuckoo!' He was later given a battlefield promotion to captain for this feat. A twist of history is that a golden wreath of laurel was placed upon this same eagle by the hand of Napoleon himself, in celebration of the 8th Regiment's distinction: forcing this same 87th Regiment to retreat with considerable loss at the earlier Battle of Talavera.
