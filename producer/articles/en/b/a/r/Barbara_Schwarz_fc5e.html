Barbara Schwarz is a German national living in the United States who has gained notoriety for making a record number of requests under the United States' Freedom of Information Act (FOIA). The Lexington Herald-Leader referred to Schwarz as "history's most prolific filer" of Freedom of Information Act requests.[1] Since 1989, she has issued thousands of FOIA requests and dozens of lawsuits to numerous federal and state governmental departments and agencies, naming up to 3,087 defendants at a time. In addition to lawsuits filed against the National Security Agency (NSA)[2] and the Central Intelligence Agency, (CIA)[3] Schwarz has filed suits against the Internal Revenue Service (IRS)[4] and the United States Department of the Treasury.[5] To date, she has not prevailed in any of these lawsuits.

Schwarz is a former member of the Church of Scientology. Her campaign is based on her stated belief that she is the daughter of Church of Scientology founder L. Ron Hubbard.[1] She also believes that L. Ron Hubbard is the son of President Dwight Eisenhower[3] and that the Federal government is concealing the whereabouts of Church official Mark Rathbun, who she claims is her husband, and that he was falsely imprisoned for her own murder.[6][7][2]

[edit] Biographical background

According to Schwarz, she was born circa 1956 in a submarine village beneath the Great Salt Lake in Utah, where she lived until she was kidnapped and taken into Germany at the age of four by Nazi agents. She says that she later married a prominent Scientologist, Mark Rathbun, who she claims was imprisoned on the false charge of having killed Schwarz. Schwarz joined the German Church of Scientology and served as its President in the early 1980s.[8]

In 1987 Schwarz was subjected to an attempted involuntary deprogramming by Cyril Vosper, a former Scientologist. A German court later convicted him of kidnapping and causing bodily harm during this incident.[9][10] According to Schwarz, she moved to the United States from Germany in 1989. She now lives in Salt Lake City, Utah.[6] In 2004, she posted a ninety-two-part account of her life on the Usenet newsgroup alt.religion.scientology,[11] to which she has been a regular and controversial contributor.[12]

[edit] FOIA requests and government litigation

Schwarz's FOIA campaign, which has been compared to carpet bombing for its scale and indiscriminate nature,[6] focuses mainly on requests for information about herself, Mark Rathbun, President Dwight Eisenhower, L. Ron Hubbard, and the Church of Scientology.[7] Schwarz claims to be the daughter of L. Ron Hubbard and the paternal granddaughter of President Eisenhower. She contends that the Federal government is withholding information about her origins, and the whereabouts of Rathbun, who she believes is secretly imprisoned in the United States. When shown a recent photo of Mark Rathbun provided by the Church of Scientology, she said that it is not the same man.[6]

[edit] Litigation

When the responses to Schwarz' FOIA requests failed to verify her claims, she responded with litigation, in which she has consistently acted in pro per. According to the Salt Lake Tribune, "at least one of Schwarz's lawsuits has been considered by a U.S. District or U.S. Circuit Court of Appeals somewhere in the nation every year since 1993." A 2005 article in the Asbury Park Press referred to Schwarz as "notorious for her nationwide quest for documents".[13] The same article quoted the New Jersey Government Records Council chairman as suggesting that: "lawmakers should consider amending the law to keep libraries from being forced to undertake private research projects, like Schwarz's."[13]

In 1997, a Schwarz lawsuit against President Clinton reached the United States Supreme Court.[14] United States federal agencies involved as defendants in other litigation by Schwarz included the Internal Revenue Service[4] in 1998, the Central Intelligence Agency[3], in 1999, and United States Supreme Court case against the National Security Agency[2], also in 1999, and the United States Department of the Treasury[5] in 2000.

In the 2001 case Schwarz v. United States Dep't of Agriculture, Civil Action No. 01-1464,[15] she named 3,087 defendants in a complaint totalling 2,370 pages.[6] Most of her lawsuits have been dismissed outright as frivolous[2] and she hasn't prevailed in any of them.[6] Schwarz is unable to pay for detailed information requests due to poverty, which has formed the basis of government refusals to perform voluminous searches.[7] As of 2005, the United States Department of Justice had authorized government employees to refuse to respond to any of Schwarz's FOIA requests until she pays outstanding fees.[16] and "two U.S. Courts of Appeals ruled that her requests and lawsuits are frivolous."[17] In 2004, The Oregonian reported that a federal court limited Schwarz's future FOIA requests to new subjects, "not to exceed 10 single-spaced pages."[16]

In 2005, the Utah Court of Appeals ruled that The Salt Lake Tribune did not libel Schwarz in its 2003 article "S.L. Woman’s Quest Strains Public Records System.", when The Tribune used terms such as "FOIA terrorist" and the phrase "Have you been Schwarzed today?", in reference to "her unending request letters."[18] In an email interview with the First Amendment Center, Schwarz responded: "We all depend on the media to keep us up to date with events, but we need true and responsible reporting and not horrible insults such as calling somebody a ‘FOIA terrorist."[18]

[edit] Supreme Court of the United States

Ruling of Supreme Court of the United States, 1999, Schwarz v. N.S.A., directing the Clerk of the Court not to acccept any further noncriminal petitions from Schwarz without a docketing fee.

After Schwarz filed 35 separate certiorari petitions to the Supreme Court of the United States, all of which were dismissed as frivolous, the Court issued a ruling on March 9, 1999, directing the Clerk of the Court "not to accept any further petitions for certiorari from Schwarz in noncriminal matters unless she pays the docketing fee required by Rule 38 ..." The ruling states, "Schwarz has repeatedly abused this Court's certiorari process."[19]

[edit] Schwarz and the Church of Scientology

Schwarz is no longer a member of the Church of Scientology (she states that she was ejected from the Church in the mid-1980s), though she describes herself as a "nonorganized" Scientologist.[6] She is strongly critical of the Church's leadership, claiming that it has been infiltrated by agents of what she refers to as "SEGNPMSS" ("Still Existing German Nazi Psychiatrists' Mindcontroller Secret Service"). She blames Nazi infiltrators of the Church of Scientology for her involuntary expulsion as the president of the Church of Scientology in Germany. She continues to consider herself a Scientologist and defends the Church, "in its true form."[20]

In a 1994 attempt to locate Mark Rathbun, Schwarz sued the Church of Scientology to force the church to divulge his whereabouts to her. The judge granted the Church of Scientology's request to dismiss the case and ruled that "Ms. Schwarz had presented no legally cognizable claim against the Church that entitled her to relief in federal court".[21] This case was eventually appealed to the United States Court of Appeals for the 10th Circuit where the court ruled that "the district court correctly dismissed the motions."[22] Linda Simmons Hight, a director of media relations for the Church of Scientology at the time, said about Schwarz, "We're clueless about this person and obviously she is delusional about Mr. Rathbun and she needs help. We're sorry for her".[6]
