Bas Jan Ader

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Bas Jan Ader (born Apr 19, 1942 in Winschoten, the Netherlands, lost at sea in 1975 between Cape Cod, Massachusetts and Ireland) was a Dutch conceptual artist, performance artist, photographer and filmmaker. He lived in Los Angeles for the last 10 years of his life. Ader's work was in many instances presented as photographs and film of his performances. He also made performative installations, including Please Don't Leave Me (1969). His work began to experience a surge in popularity in the early 1990s.

[edit] Education

During adolescence Ader took art classes at the Rietveld Academy in the Netherlands, and later in the United States during a study abroad program. Ader graduated from the Otis College of Art and Design in 1965 with a BFA, and from the Claremont Graduate School in 1967. After graduating, Ader taught at various institutions, including Mount San Antonio College, Immaculate Heart College, and the University of California, Irvine.

[edit] Death

Ader was lost at sea while attempting a single-handed west-east crossing of the Atlantic in a 13ft pocket cruiser, a modified Guppy 13 named "Ocean Wave". The passage was part of an art performance titled "In Search of the Miraculous". Radio contact broke off three weeks into the voyage, and Ader was presumed lost at sea. The boat was found after 10 months, floating partially submerged 150 miles West-Southwest of the coast of Ireland. His body was never found. [edit] Famous works

Ader's most popular work is his 1970 short film piece entitled "I'm too sad to tell you" that consists of the artist making himself cry in front of a camera. [1] While much has been said about the linguistic reversals involved in the aesthetic and allegorical/ironical framework of "I'm too sad to tell you," i.e. the emotive or theatrical content, the slapstick comedy present in much of his work also plays an important role. WIthin this duality Ader readily employs in much of his work and performances, it could be argued, a contrived theatricality that creates comedic space in its meta-awarness. This places not only content and aesthetics to the fore, but the idea of reception as well. The interests and concerns ripe within Ader's oeuvre locate him in similar art historical tropes of conceptual and performance artists of the 1970s, such as Chris Burden or Bruce Nauman. Like many conceptual art works of the 1970s, his works were recorded in descriptive notes and statements destined to have flexible and repeated incarnations, forever unfolding with the perception of history itself. Many myths have spread out about Ader's disappearance at sea, leading to speculations about supposedly lost works resurfacing.

[edit] Authenticity

There has also been many concerns amongst the art community about the legitimacy of some of Ader's work that has been posthumously produced by Patrick Painter, Inc. One example is the 'Thoughts Said, Then Forgotten (1973/2003)' installation, which exists as an edition of three sold for $100,000 by Patrick Painter Editions. Some art-scholars believe this to be a contemporary fake.[citation needed] The installation, which Ader intended as a one-time installation/performance, was reproduced in 2003 based on a polaroid documentation taken at the Nova Scotia School of Art and design where it was exhibited in 1973. The new version of the work disregards Ader's actual intentions on how it should be installed and performed (which exists in the school's files).[citation needed] According to Damien Moppet, the production manager who formerly handled Ader's estate, the instructions for installing the new version of the work were not written by the artist, but by those who currently represent the estate - people who never knew Ader personally.[citation needed] In the instructions in the school's archives, Ader specifically explains how the piece is to be installed and performed - he wanted the flowers to wilt and the text to be fully painted over by the end of the exhibition. The new $100,000 version of the piece doesn't take any of Ader's words into account, but it is stamped with authenticity of the estate.[citation needed] Concerns of what is and is not a 'work by Ader' now becomes mixed up with what is and is not 'stamped with the estate's authenticity.'

[edit] Revival

The work of Bas Jan Ader, while long considered influential in the art community, is now starting to gain an ever-increasing public interest. There have been a number of recent exhibitions of his work, including a European traveling retrospective in 2006-2007 with stops at the Camden Art Center, London, the Boijmans van Beuningen Museum, Rotterdam, and the Kunshalle Basel. A catalogue documenting all of Ader's artworks has been published on the occasion of the retrospective.
