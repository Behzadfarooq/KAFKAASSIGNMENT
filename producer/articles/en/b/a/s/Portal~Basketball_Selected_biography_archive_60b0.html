[edit] July 4 to July 23, 2006

Image:Sheryl Swoopes.jpg
Sheryl Denise Swoopes (March 25, 1971) is a professional basketball small forward for the Houston Comets of the Women's National Basketball Association, best known for conflating offensive and defensive skills to earn, in professional and international play, three Summer Olympics gold medals, four WNBA championships, five selections as a WNBA All-Star game starter, three WNBA Most Valuable Player Awards, and three WNBA Defensive Player of the Year Awards, and, in collegiate play, the Naismith College Player of the Year Award and a National Collegiate Athletic Association Division I championship.

Born in Brownfield, Texas, Swoopes began playing competitive basketball, having been taught the game by her older brothers, aged seven years, and excelled early, eventually leading her high school to the 1988 Texas state championship. Swoopes matriculated at Texas Tech University, where she earned first-team All-America honors in her junior and senior seasons, eventually leaving the school as the NCAA record holder for most career points scored and most points scored in an NCAA tournament; the latter record of 177 points, recorded in 1993, when she led the Red Raiders to the national championship, remains. For her senior year contributions, Swoopes won the Naismith and Women's Basketball Coaches' Association player of the year awards and was said by many to be a women's Michael Jordan; Swoopes' jersey number was also retired upon her graduation.

Swoopes rose to international prominence with her play for the United States national team at the Games of the XXVI Olympiad, contested in 1996, in Atlanta, Georgia, United States, in which the American side, for which Swoopes averaged 13 points, four rebounds, and four assists, recorded a perfect record en route to winning the women's basketball gold medal.

When the WNBA began play in 1997, Swoopes was allocated to the Houston Comets, for whom, after having missed two-thirds of the season while pregnant, Swoopes started as the team won the Western Conference championship and then defeated the New York Liberty for the league title. The championship would be the first of four consecutive titles for the dynastic Comets, as Swoopes teamed with point guard Cynthia Cooper and power forward Tina Thompson to dominate the league through the 2000 season.

[edit] May 28 to July 4, 2006

Peter Press "Pete" Maravich (June 22, 1947 – January 5, 1988) was an American basketball guard, best known for his incredible shooting abilities, creative passing, and dazzling ballhandling. Also known as Pistol Pete he starred collegiately at Louisiana State, winning, in 1970, the Naismith Award, and professionally, making the National Basketball Association All-Star game five times. Maravich holds the National Collegiate Athletic Association career record for points, having scored 3,667 in just three seasons, averaging 44.2 points per game prior to the introduction of the three-point shot.

Born in the Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania, suburb of Aliquippa, and of Serbian descent, Maravich amazed his family and friends with his basketball abilities from an early age. His father, Press Maravich, a former professional player turned coach, showed Pete the fundamentals, beginning when the younger was aged seven years. Maravich spent hourspracticing ball control tricks, blind passes, head fakes and long-range shots.

The practice paid off when he joined the Daniel High School (Clemson, South Carolina) varsity basketball team as an eighth grader. Maravich later played at Broughton High School (Raleigh, North Carolina) and Edward Military Institute (Salemburg, North Carolina) before matriculating at Louisiana State, where his father had become head basketball coach.
