In Canada, both the backcountry enthusiast and the urban explorer are prominent forms of travel that have gained popularity in recent years.

Urban explorers hover between the trendy hostels and clubs in Vancouver, Toronto and Montreal. Those seeking to explore more rural and extreme terrains, including Crown land, the Rockies and Atlantic Canada may be best suited to the nation's vast system of provincial and national parks offered through Parks Canada.

[edit] Hostels

Many hostels offer low-cost, safe and friendly accommodation for those looking for their first taste of life in Canada. Students, extreme travellers and backpackers have come to rely on such facilities to exchange information and experience about global travel.

As Canada continues to host international travellers from all across the globe, the hostelling and bed and breakfast industries have adapted to accommodate the needs of tourists from a wide array of different socioeconomic and geopolitical classes.

The 2006 election of the Conservative Party of Canada into power in Canada has led some to suggest that an economic boom, largely based on investment in the energy industry, will increase the availability of alternative hospitality services and dormitories across Canada. Central destinations like Fort McMurray (north of Edmonton and close to the Athabasca Oil Sands) have attracted a young migrating population.

[edit] Preparation & Equipment

Some styles of Canadian backpacking require intense physical and psychological conditioning. Those wishing to prepare for a long journey through the backcountry can expect to visit retailers such as Mark's Work Warehouse, Mountain Equipment Co-op, Coast Mountain Sports, Army-Navy store and Bass Pro Shops for equipment supplies. Gear reviews can be found at Outdoor Adventure Canada.

[edit] Working Temporarily in Canada

The Canadian government offers guidelines for those who wish to work temporarily in Canada during their travels.

"If you wish to work temporarily in Canada, you will likely require a work permit, which provides evidence that you have been authorized to work while in Canada. It is usually valid only for the specific job, employer and length of time stated on the permit. An immigration officer may issue a work permit after Human Resources and Skills Development Canada (HRSDC) confirms your job offer."[1]
