Talk:British Proganda in Ireland in the Early 20th Century

Throughout history there is evidence of Ireland being persecuted by the British. Consistant with this evidence of persecution is propaganda from the late [16th Century]]that depicts priests being tortured and executed as a result of practicing the Catholic religion, which was against British law. [www.bl.uk/learning/histcitizen/uk/ireland/source1/irishpriests.html]

Despite this propaganda, it was not until the beginning of the 20th Century that the British established an organization that was called an official report system. The official report system was one that allowed British propaganda, that depicted the Irish in a negative manner, to be accepted as fact, when in reality it was merely an attempt to boast British moral and to diminish the ethos of the Irish Republican Army also known as the IRA.[www.indymedia.ie/article/75117]

This propaganda, believed to be fact, stated that members of the IRA killed landowners of Protestant background because of their faith. Reports similar to this one came from the official report of army intelligence on the rebellion. The reports fail to indicate that the IRA killed Protestant landowners because they went against their fellow countrymen by informing the British of the details of the rebellion. The reports also signal the British Army as being racist toward the Irish, yet these details were conveniently left out. It should be noted that the British portrayal of these events has proved to inaccurate in current history books.

One of the most recognizable events of this conflict, known as Bloody Sunday, occurred on November 20th 1920. The IRA, headed under Michael Collins, received a tip that provided them with the location of several British agents. On the morning of Bloody Sunday, twelve British were found dead. Later that afternoon at a Dublin football game, British Police opened fire on Irish civilians. When the smoke had cleared, fourteen Irish civilians had been killed. The British claim that the IRA had fired on them first, which was accepted by the public, despite other first hand accounts that state the opposite. Later that day, two ranking IRA officials and a clerk, who was mistaken for another IRA volunteer, were tortured and killed. The story that the British provided was one that stated that Irish men were trying to escape British custody. Later, fake photos of them doing so were sent out to the public. Basil Clarke, a British public relations agent, created the photos to validate the reason for killing the three captives.[www.spinwatch.org/content/view/3194/29

In 1920, the new propaganda department produced a 'Survey of the Week's Activities' with the sole purpose of countering the negative publicity the British were receiving as a result of the Irish Bulletin, an Irish based publication. The simplicity of the department was remarkable considering the impact it had. A card index system was used, full of possible headlines and themes that would be chosen at random each week.

The British used propaganda as a weapon in their effort to control the Irish and to protect their reputation. The conflict between the two nations has gone on for centuries and it is evident that propaganda has been an important part in allowing the British to have control over Ireland for such a long period of time.
