Brian Glick (murder victim)

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Brian Glick was a homicide victim murdered at the age of 21 on October 25, 1993 by Dennis Tate and Michael Olson in Bloomington, Minnesota. Glick’s murder was a shock to the Twin Cities community and his death and the subsequent trials of his murders received widespread attention in the Twin Cities press.

[edit] Brian Glick’s life

Brian Glick was born on April 25, 1972 to a middle class family in Minneapolis. His family moved to Bloomington and in 1990 Glick graduated from Bloomington Kennedy High School. In 1992 he began working at a Subway store on E. Old Shakopee Road, a block away from where he lived with his mother, stepfather and teenage sister. At the time of his death Glick had been working to save enough money to marry his fiancée Katie Long.[1] Glick was interested in aviation, but his plans to become an aircraft mechanic were thwarted when it was discovered that he was color-blind. Instead, Glick returned to school and completed the necessary course work to become a paramedic.[1]

In the year before his death, it was discovered that Glick had a large benign brain tumor, and in May 1992 he underwent surgery to have it removed. [2] At the time of his death Glick was still recovering, but his prognosis was positive. Ironically, the operation that saved his life would contribute to his death. Shortly after surgery Glick returned to work at Subway. On the last day his boss saw him he commented "You're really bouncing around here tonight. You really look good." Glick responded that that he was feeling the best he had since the surgery and he was going to make it. [3]

[edit] The murder

On the night of Monday October 25, 1993, Glick was working alone at the Subway shop. He spoke with his fiancée by telephone around 11 p.m., and fifteen minutes later, at around 11:15 p.m., went out back to empty the trash. Glick was unaware that Dennis Tate, 16, and Michael Olson, 19, were waiting for him behind the store. Olson was a former employee at the store, but had been fired, and knew both Glick and the outlay of the store. [4] As Glick headed back into the shop after emptying the trash, Tate and Olson jumped him and brutally beat him with a piece of wood and choked him until he was unrecognizable. [5] The two then entered the store, vandalized surveillance cameras, and stole a cashbox containing $152. Glick's fiancée went to the store about 11:30 p.m. to pick him up. When she found the store empty she called police, who arrived shortly before midnight. Glick was found behind the store and immediately taken by ambulance to Hennepin County Medical Center. [6] Glick died at 4:50 a.m. Tuesday, October 26, 1993, with his mother at his bedside. Doctors noted that a piece of skull that was healing from the recent brain tumor operation had been pushed up and back into his brain, contributing to Glick’s death. [2]

[edit] The Trials and Convictions

Michael Olson was arrested on October 27 after police found a bloody shirt at his home. [5] Dennis Tate was apprehended the following day. The two confessed to killing Glick and told the police that they had intended to double the money stolen with the purchase and sale of drugs - a plan that went awry when they were ripped off by a drug dealer. [7]

Olsen and Tate were tried in the Hennepin County District Court. Olsen was found guilty of murder by a jury and sentenced in March 1994 to 27 years and two months in prison for second-degree murder. [3] Glick’s father, Stephen; mother; and fiancée testified at the trials of his murderers. At Olson’s sentencing Glick’s father said:

“ "His [Olson's] prison is now measured by walls. Mine is measured in sadness, loneliness and anger. For the rest of my life, my relationship with my son has been dictated by him. I hate him for making this prison for me. I hate him for taking it upon himself to decide how I will relate to my son."[3] ”

Tate stood trial as an adult and was convicted by a jury of second-degree murder. In January 1995 Tate was sentenced to 25 1/2 years in prison. [8]
