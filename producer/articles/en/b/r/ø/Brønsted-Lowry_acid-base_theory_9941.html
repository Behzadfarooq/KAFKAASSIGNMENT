Brønsted-Lowry acid-base theory

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

In chemistry, the Brønsted-Lowry acid-base theory or Brønsted-Lowry concept is an acid-base theory describing the reaction mechanism between acids and bases, and was independently proposed by Johannes Nicolaus Brønsted and Thomas Martin Lowry in 1923. In this system, a Brønsted-Lowry acid is defined as any species which has the tendency to lose, or "donate" a hydrogen ion, and a Brønsted-Lowry base as a substance with a tendency to gain or "accept" a hydrogen ion. Thus, other substances such as water molecules can have behaviours not defined in the common definition of an acid or base.

In the forward reaction, CH3COOH acts as an acid to donate a proton, and water as a base to receive it. H2O and H3O+ are therefore considered one acid-base conjugate pair, and CH3COOH and CH3COO- as another conjugate pair.
