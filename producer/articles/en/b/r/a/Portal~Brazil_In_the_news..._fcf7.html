  • March 13: On this day, Brazil marks the traditional 90th birthday of one of the country's trademarks: Samba. However, this date marks the anniversary of the first known recording of a Samba song: "Pelo Telefone" ("On the Telephone"), recorded in 1917 by singer and songwriter Donga, who would also become the very first Brazilian artist to campaign in favor of the protection of copyrights in the country. In the 1930s, Donga helped persuade then-president Getúlio Vargas to pass the very first legislation protecting copyrights in the country.
  • March 8: The former mayor of São Paulo and current Congressman Paulo Maluf was indicted by the District Attorney's office of the city of New York for failing to declare money transported in and out of the country. Maluf's son was indicted as well on the same charges.
  • March 8: The President of the United States, George W. Bush, arrived in São Paulo for a day-long visit to the country under a heavy security scheme. Bush's presence in the country sparked heated protests across the country, and in São Paulo, where Bush landed and was to remain, a protest escalated into an open confrontation with the police, which resulted in many being injured or arrested. In Rio de Janeiro the US general consulate was vandalized with paint and rocks.
  • February 23: The Vatican confirmed the canonization of friar Galvão. The official ceremony will take place on May 11, during Pope Benedict XVI's first visit to Brazil. Friar Galvão had been beatified in 1998, by Pope John Paul II, and now becomes the first Brazilian-born saint of the Roman Catholic Church.
  • February 21: In Rio de Janeiro, samba school G.R.E.S. Beija-Flor de Nilópolis was declared winner of the city's 2007 Samba School Parade, with G.R.E.S. Acadêmicos do Grande Rio placing second. This was Beija-Flor's 4th title in the last 5 years. A day earlier, in São Paulo, local samba school Mocidade Alegre was declared winner of the local Parade, second in importance only to Rio de Janeiro's.
