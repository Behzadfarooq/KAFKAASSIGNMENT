[edit] What about a "Cease and Desist" template to avoid future confusion of this kind?

Another user (apparently I shouldn't say whom) has offered (apparently I shouldn't say where) an adequate explanation on your behalf of what you wrote at Talk:Water Fuel Cell#No Legal Threats, but what about creating a boilerplate template with wording carefully chosen to avoid confusion in future cases of this kind (although I hope there won't be many)? I humbly suggest something like this (to be placed in the talk pages of articles A,B,C):

The WikiMedia Foundation has received a cease and desist letter from Company K. Please avoid mentioning topics X, Y, Z in this article until further notice. This request applies only to articles A,B,C. Your cooperation will speed the process of resolving this matter in a manner consistent with the goals of the Wikipedia.
--Brad Patrick, (date and time) General Counsel, WikiMedia Foundation

Just an idea.---CH 08:46, 1 October 2006 (UTC)

[edit] Editor's individual legal responsibility for their edits

Hello there, and thanks for all the work you do for us! I was wondering if you could weigh your legal opinion in over here: there's a discussion regarding blanked pages and the legal responsibility of reverting editors to make sure that the article that they're reverting the blanking to is free of libel. As none of us are lawyers, some clarification could be helpful if you have a free minute to put your thoughts down (the idea of being held liable for reverting vandalism is something that has some ramifications for us, so I know that I at least would love some "official" guidance on the issue). Thanks very much for your time, Snoutwood (talk) 19:22, 20 October 2006 (UTC)

Your participation would be appreciated. There are statements to the effect that editors and admins who revert articles to versions that may contain unknown libelous content could be held responsible for that content as if they were the original author of it. That standard would have a chilling effect on the patrollers who revert obvious vandalism, and have the paradoxial effect of leaving more defamatory content in articles. -Will Beback 04:32, 21 October 2006 (UTC)
Whoops... sorry, I missed the banner on the top of this page. Sending you an e-mail. Snoutwood (talk) 06:43, 21 October 2006 (UTC)

[edit] Fishing expeditions

I posted the following to Wikipedia talk:Requests for checkuser#Fishing expeditions

I know that we generally don't just run checkuser to see who posted certain comments, but came across this death threat and thought I would bring up the issue. Should checkuser be used to identify and block vandals that perform specific eggregious acts. For example:

  • posting user's name address phone number and children names (in the edit summary as well)
  • death threats
  • threatening lawsuits against Wikipedia

My view is that we should in some cases - i.e. we shouldn't have to identify potential socks if there are multiple instances of any of the above. I know a "I'm going to kill you" said in haste (or as an excited utterance) is probably not a true threat. But wikistalking followed by a threat to kill - we should treat as a potential threat and deal with the user more harshly. --Trödel 20:37, 21 November 2006 (UTC)

User:Essjay suggested we get your opinion on when specific egregious acts could justify a checkuser request without pre-identification of suspected sockpuppets. Addionally, if the threat involves minors, there may be an additional duty imposed on editors from child protection acts to report such violations (especially those editors who may meet the definition of a professional in their local jurisdiction). (cross posting to the Juriwiki-l mailing list) --Trödel 19:07, 23 November 2006 (UTC)

My suggestion to consult you was based on this comment

Death threats are legally considered assault (not to be confused with battery), which could be a felony in some jurisdictions. List them in the IP check section and ask for the results to be forwarded to the appropriate law enforcement agency. Failure to do this could be considered misprision of felony. Jesse Viviano 20:21, 22 November 2006 (UTC)

It occurs to me that before we begin having scores of requests to run checkusers and pester the FBI, we should have a decision from the Foundation counsel on our legal responsibilities and Wikipedia's best practice in such a situation. Essjay (Talk) 03:23, 24 November 2006 (UTC)
I replied. Thanks.--Brad Patrick 23:39, 24 November 2006 (UTC)
Thank you Brad. :) Essjay (Talk) 00:23, 25 November 2006 (UTC)
Yes thank you --Trödel 01:05, 25 November 2006 (UTC)

[edit] Indefinite blocking of User:Fordmadoxfraud

When sources such as Reuters are reporting on the Iranian sex tape scandal, I am unclear on how NPV reporting of said event here on Wikipedia is a blockable offense? If the idea that Iran might stone (or, well, actually, lash) a woman to death (or, well, 99 times, but then, stoned, as in killed makes for better condemnation) for a sex-tape offends you, that might be better taken up with Iran than the person who wrote a researched & annotated article about the incident? How does a discussion about the posting of link (a link originally removed by Ford MF, but reinstated as per his understanding of Wikipedia:Pornography) result in an indefinate block? Even if the link bears removal per Wikipedia:Biographies of living persons, blocking a user for good faith & NPOV contributions on a controversial subject is hardly appropriate. Well, I suppose he can always appeal his block by writing to...I guess that would be you. I guess my point is simply that rewarding a user with close to a thousand edits who has tried to follow WP policy while keeping a NPOV (along side being flamed in non-Wikipedia context) with a block does not seem to me to be the best course of action. --mordicai. 21:26, 15 December 2006 (UTC)

Two things - I spoke with someone by telephone today who is deeply concerned that the effect of this posting will be (a) Iran banning Wikipedia outright (b) the jeopardy of the life of the woman involved. This person has already been in contact with Jimbo, who I have asked to do what he thinks is right. I responded to a real-life threatening situation and popped Ford MF pending further investigation. This doesn't need to be decided until Jimbo takes a look at what happened and weighs in. Further, Ford MF's swooning over Jimmy commenting on his "work", plus his own pride in making an "international incident" on his blog [4] make me uninterested in encouraging him to contribute to this article. I am at pains to say, what is the point here? Having a link, so the rest of the non-Iranian world can see video, but thereby condemning a woman in Iran to death? Count me in the group that would first seek to do no harm, *then* address the broad and controversial thicket of issues around the topic as a whole. People in jeopardy for their lives is serious indeed. I don't see that the benefit outweighs the risk at all.--Brad Patrick 21:55, 15 December 2006 (UTC)
Thanks for the quick reply, Brad. Firstly, as to (a) on Iranian Wikipedia banning. Censorship in Iran (an article on FMF's created/heavily contributed list, by the way) seems to state that Iran has already banned Wikipedia. As to (b), Reuters & the The Guardian UK have both reported on the story. While the woman is in danger of 99 lashes, I don't think a media black-out of the event is likely to serve her interests best. As to Ford's swooning over Jimbo's edit, I hardly think that the recognition of internet celebrity or mention of the event in his diary are cause for your disdain. What you interpret as pride for making an international incident seems more to me like good-natured chagrin. His Livejournal was flamed on a number of occasions for his work on the article, from a number of different IPs in seperate nations. It is a misrepresentation of his contribution to focus on the link to the video, especially since he did not add it originally, & in fact removed it initially until reverting his removal as per his understanding of Wikipedia's pornography policies. Not only that, he created a forum on the talk page to discuss the validity of the link. Considering that most of the other content on the page is his, a ban for a link, especially one already removed by another editor (in this case, Jimbo) seems like a false move. The argument about whether the link is fit content is superfluous to the discussion of FMF's block. Making a judgement on an editor due to the controversial nature of a subject would seem to encourage putting blinders on, rather than careful dealing with the subject. Whether or not the link is fitting, Ford MF consulted policy & edited accordingly. Punishing him because Iran has capital punishment for adultery is, I believe, a mis-step, as would ignoring the subject of the "sex-tape incident." An act more in-line with care & caution on the subject would be to lock the article (already semi-protected, on FMF's suggestion), rather than to ban a user who's edit history has no evidence of bad faith. I understand that you have passed the authority on to Jimbo, but I wanted to make my point known. --mordicai. 22:29, 15 December 2006 (UTC)
Thanks for taking the time.--Brad Patrick 21:42, 18 December 2006 (UTC)
