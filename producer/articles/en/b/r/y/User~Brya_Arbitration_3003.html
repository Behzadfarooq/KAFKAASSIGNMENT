[edit] Comments on Arbitratrion

Although the focus now is on me it should be clear that what is happening here is actually part of what effectively an ongoing war, which had been underway for quite a time at the moment I made my first edit. It never really went away although there were occasionally lulls. At the heart of this war against basic policies and guidelines of wikipedia are some highly idiosynchratic habits of user:MPF. Maybe it is only a single habit with various aspects, but it manifests in what has been called "pushing PoVs" and an huge urge to standardise things across all ToL articles. As good an example of his PoV-orientation is Cephalotaxaceae. Based on a few publications (see Taxaceae) he has decided to favor a particular taxonomy, which is presented as universally accepted in both the page on Cephalotaxaceae and Taxaceae). However, the world at large assumes one genus in the family (see gymnosperm database, Flora of China , GRIN), but what widely accepted in the real world does not even rate a mention by MPF.

Well beyond PoV is Callitropsis, which is almost (but not quite a taxon) that exists in wikipedia only. There may be case to be made in favor of adopting this name (not that I see how) but the article does not make such a case. Instead it states that: "The name Xanthocyparis has now been proposed for conservation (Mill & Farjon 2006), but until that is decided on at the 2011 International Botanical Congress, it is correctly classified as Callitropsis. " which is well beyond a possible interpretation of the process of conservation. See the NPoV-item wiped here and here by MPF: For the position taken by the real world see: GRIN and gymnosperm database

This pushing may not really be because of a PoV he believes in, but rather enforcing a uniformity that he feel should exist, as in inserting British spelling for everything that does not happen on the same side of the Atlantic Ocean as the US) as here and subsequent edits.

From my very first edit on Wikipedia I found that no page on wikipedia treating a plant genus or species can be edited without an automatic edit war ensuing, with the end result being a page worse than before the edit. I have long since given up touching any such page, but a recent event showed this is still in full force. The picture in Koehler of Viscum album was so good that I could not help creating a page for this highly interesting and fun species. MPF pounced immediately to #$% the #$% out of it. As this was even more brutal than usually (with MPF featuring a picture which had no other appeal than that he personally had cleverly identified the tree in the background) and even at first glance was both PoV and original research (that is, MPF publishing his own original internal taxonomy of the species, and the elevating it to the only one possible) I tried to effect at least some improvement. The page history shows that it took two months of unpleasantness for MPF to even move to a somewhat neutral position. Also note that he did not hesitate to back his edits by providing a reference which, when I went to check, proved to contain nothing of what he claimed it contained.

In his editing he is also handicapped by (apparently) being a British English speaker only. He does not take into account the many ways a sentence can be misread, by people from different backgrounds. In many cases his "copyedits" are to sentences that (I assume) sound good in British English, without regard to how this may be read. In all to many cases his copyedits are oblivious to what the sentence means.

So in practice, all the plant pages at the level of species and genus are off-limits for any edits (any such page falling prey to this fate of a quick Google and a comparison to a popular handbook, with all the errors this entails), unless somebody moves firmly into ownership. However, botany in wikipedia is something like 90% empty. And anything that improves the ratio of real information to bogus information should be worthwhile, in view of the high exposure wikipedia gets.

Therefore, for a long while, I have restricted my contributions to a quite minimalistic style, not venturing beyond basic facts, and almost always backed by a link to an authoritatve source, available on the web.

I have certainly had my own disagreements with MPF; he will have to defend his own edits but aside from British spellings like "colour" his English is not an issue. As a native speaker of American English I find MPF's text to be quite readable and understandable, even when I disagree with what he is saying. For Brya to criticize MPF's English leaves me literally speechless. MrDarwin 16:57, 21 October 2006 (UTC)

[edit] Botanical nomenclature

MrDarwin commented that my contributions on the topic of botanical nomenclature are made from a narrow point of view, restricted to what is formally laid down in the ICBN. This comment is as true as it is irrelevant. Not only are nomenclature and taxonomy two entirely different things, but there is a big difference between the body of rules in nomenclature, as a body, and how they are applied in taxonomic practice. No doubt it would be possible to write a page "History and usage of the type concept in botany as practiced in the Eastern United Stated" or a page "History and usage of the type concept in botany as practiced in the France". As I understand it, in zoology it is something of a cult to disregard at least some of the rules in the ICZN. However, it is quite difficult to document such informal usage (just because it is informal), and to place it in its proper context. I have not seen anybody volunteer to write such pages. However it will be clear that if everybody is trying to force his local informal usage into the formal description chaos will ensue. These things should be kept separate.

As to being overly formal, well, there are between 3 to 8 different Codes of Nomenclature, partly independent, partly complementary, partly competing. Each with a different field of application (but a few that have the same field of application, depending on how you count). All these use concepts that are somewhat comparable to rather different, with different names, the same word having different meanings in different Codes. Without being clear on context and very precise about the concepts under discussion, a hopeless mess will ensue. Writing about this means knowing what words and phrases are to be avoided as being ambiguous.

I suppose I should qualify that a bit: Brya has a very narrow interpretion when it suits her. Unfortunately there was much that she introduced in the Type (botany) article that goes beyond a narrow interpretation. My primary concern with this and other botanical nomenclature articles is that they were (and thanks to Brya larely remain) more or less incomprehensible to anybody who is not already familiar with the subject. MrDarwin 16:57, 21 October 2006 (UTC)

[edit] Taxonomy

I made quite a few pages of families, orders and clades. Any such page I made will be minimalistic and mostly restricted to establishing a NPoV framework, and in making links to authoritative sites (there actually is quite a lot of information on plants and their taxonomy on the web, much more than referenced by wikipedia). There are very good reasons for this:

  1. A NPoV means that any page of a higher taxon, by its very nature should (also) be a disambiguation page. Such a taxon is a box and it may contain any of a number of things. It is not possible to describe an order or family without first establishing what belongs in it. Cases are known where a taxonomist adds or subtracts a single extra species, with as a result that the description changes completely.
  2. Writing descriptions for higher taxa is not as easy as it sounds. In practice some pages are the result of a quick Google of the more popular pages on the net, with some elements being lifted from each, and being melded into a composite of different concepts. A good description should be made with a monograph in hand, complemented by recent articles. Just dashing of quick descriptions is not very helpful, not when there are authoritative sites available, giving meticulous descriptons with authors and context being listed.

Note that I went to considerable trouble to establish a NPoV framework of reference for plant taxonomy, so as to be able to follow different placements of taxa (I should mention here that Berton pitched in enthousiastically). Pages such as APG system and APG II system, as wel as classics like Bentham & Hooker system (note the carefully preserved original typography).
