Bronkhorst

Bronkhorst is a village in the municipality of Bronckhorst, the Netherlands. Technically, it is a city (see below) and with only 171 inhabitants (2006), it is one of the smallest cities in the Netherlands.

[edit] History

[edit] Early history

The early history of Bronkhorst is, as is common for Dutch towns, largely unknown. It is possible that in the 7th century farmers settled on and around the hill on which a castle would be built later. The hill formed a refuge amidst the low-lying, fertile clay in the area, which is close to the river IJssel.

[edit] Lords of Bronkhorst

Bronkhorst used to be a "heerlijkheid" (domain) governed by a "heer" (lord). The earliest known "Heren van Bronkhost" (Lords of Bronkhorst) was Gijsbert of Bronkhorst, first mentioned in 1140. He and some of his descendants played an important role in the politics of the time. The last Bronkhorst, Joost, died in 1553, after which the domain fell to the widow of Georg of Limburg.

[edit] Castle

In the Middle Ages, a castle was erected on the aforementioned hill. It is first mentioned in a 14th century document. It consisted of a keep and associated buildings and was surrounded by a thick wall and a wide moat. It was sieged several times, most notably in 1582, when after nine months Dutch troops captured the castle, which was occupied by the Spanish. (See Eighty Years War.) The castle changed hands many times, until its last owner, a merchant, had it demolished in 1828.

[edit] Town

In a document dated March 13 1482 Gijsbert VII granted city rights to the inhabitants of the village Bronkhorst, which was right next to the castle. Concretely this meant, among other things, that they were given limited self-government and were allowed to administer the law to some extent.

[edit] Culture

Bronkhorst is known for its picturesque buildings, and houses a museum dedicated to Charles Dickens which a.o. contains items purchased from the former Dickens museum at Rochester.
