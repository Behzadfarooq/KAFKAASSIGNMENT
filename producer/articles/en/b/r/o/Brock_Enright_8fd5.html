Brock Enright is a young New York based artist who came to the attention of mainstream media in 2002, when he created a "designer kidnapping" service called VIDEOGAMES Adventure Services[1]. Thrill-seekers pay him thousands of dollars a time to be violently abducted, in a quest to live out fantasies and/or face their fears.

After the abuctions are carried out, filmed, and edited for viewing, they are presented in galleries along with props involved in the abductions. The props themselves have a chaotic but ordered set-up to them, reflecting the characteristics of an artificial abduction.

These tailor-made adventures represent the consumerism in our society. People are always looking for the next big thrill, products that promise to provide us with the experience we're looking for to satisfy our desires, and will pay almost anything to get it.

Aside from his services as a professional kidnapper/fantasy designer, Enright also directed a stage version of the famous 1970's pornography Debbie Does Dallas in 2001. Though the play does not portray much of the blatant eroticism as the movie (meaning that the porn is lacking), the story element is much more emphasized.

In 2005 he worked with Ivan Hürzeler to produce Forest, an hour long film that clouds the distinction between fiction and reality. The film documents a 5 day trip into the woods with a group of hormone-driven young men and women dressed as jocks and cheerleaders. The trip was real and so is much of what you see in the film, however, those in the group also played a part to go along with the scenario created by Enright and Hürzeler.

Enright's art is often controversial and much of it shocking. Human nature is dark and disturbing, and Enright is not afraid to explore the depths of it, nor is he afraid to present it.
