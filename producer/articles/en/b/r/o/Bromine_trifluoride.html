Bromine trifluoride is a highly toxic and corrosive fluoride of bromine with chemical formula BrF3. It was discovered by P Lebau in 1906.[1] It occurs as a colorless, yellow, or gray fuming liquid with an irritating odor. It is soluble in sulfuric acid but may explode on contact with water.

[edit] Synthesis

Bromine trifluoride can be obtained by the reaction of bromine with fluorine at 20 °C.

Br2 + 3F2 → 2BrF3

The disproportionation of bromine monofluoride is also a source for bromine trifluoride.

3BrF → BrF3 + Br2[2]

[edit] Structure

Like ClF3 and IF3 the BrF3 molecule is T-shaped. With the two electron pairs the coordination sphere is 5. The distance from the bromine each axial fluorine is 1.81 Å and to the equatorial fluorine is 1.72 Å. The angle between an axial fluorine and the equatorial fluorine is slightly smaller than 90° — the 86.2° angle observed is due to the replusion generated by the electron pairs being greater than that of the Br-F bonds.[3][4]
