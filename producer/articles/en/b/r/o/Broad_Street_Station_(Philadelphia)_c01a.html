Broad Street Station (Philadelphia)

Broad Street Station was built in 1881 and served as Philadelphia, Pennsylvania's primary rail hub until the Pennsylvania Railroad completed 30th Street Station in 1933. Originally designed by the Wilson Brothers, it was dramatically expanded according to a design by Frank Furness in 1891.

The Broad Street station dominated the center of the city - running right up to City Hall - and had a "stub" design whereby trains would both enter and exit the station through what is now John F. Kennedy Boulevard. The access tracks - mounted on a "chinese wall" - bisected the city from North to South from City Hall to the Schuylkill River. The station was renowned for its architecture but cursed for inundating the heart of the city with the smoke and noise pollution of the day's steam-powered locomotives.

In the 1920's and 1930's, the Pennsylvania Railroad constructed two new stations - 30th Street Station, which is now the main intercity hub for Philadelphia rail travel, and Suburban Station, an underground stub line that went from 30th Street Station to a tunnel that ended just west of City Hall (it was later extended east as part of the Center City Commuter Connection tunnel in the SEPTA era.). Closed in 1952 and razed in 1953, the land which was once occupied by Broad Street Station and its access tracks is now the home to commercial heart of the city, also known as Penn Center, including buildings such as the 54-story Mellon Bank Center. A bas-relief murial, located in the northwest corner of the main waiting area, was originally located in Broad Street Station.

The station also served as the Pennsylvania Railroad's headquarters before moving to Suburban Station in the 1930's.
