Aulakh

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Aulakh (Hindi: औलख) or Ola (ओला) or Aula (औला) is a clan or gotra of Jats found in Rajasthan, Punjab, Haryana and Uttar Pradesh. Aulakh are also found among Punjabi Muslims in Pakistan Punjab .


The inhabitants of Uluka state and the descendants of Raja Uluka during Mahabharata period were known as Aulakh or Aula or Ola.

[edit] Origin

Aulakhs claim Nagavanshi or Chandravanshi descent. Ayu was a Chandravansi ruler. Nine generations after him was born Alarva. Alarva had two sons namely Kaiyan and Nagas. Descendants of Nagas were called Nagavanshis. The Nagavansh had eight branches:

  1. Vasati or Vais
  2. Taxak or Takshak
  3. Aulakh
  4. Kalkal
  5. Kala, Kali, Dhaman, Kalkhande
  6. Meetha
  7. Bharshiv
  8. Bharhaich.

In the Dwapar Yuga, one descendant of the Nagavanshi was Uluka who ruled in state of Uluka. He was son of Kitava. He was king of a country and people of the same name. He was an ally of the Kauravas, and acted as their envoy to the Pandavas. The arrival of King Uluka into the Sabha ("Assembly") of Yudhishtra, has been mentioned in the Sabhaparva of the Mahabharata. The inhabitants of Uluka state and the descendants of Raja Uluka were known as Aulakh or Aula or Ola.

[edit] Geographic distribution

The clan is spread all over Northern India. There are many villages of this gotra in the Ambala district of Haryana. Two well-known Aulakh villages Shahbazpur and Dyal Rajputtan are located in the Majha district outside of Taran Tarn.

[edit] Religion

In Punjab, most Aulakhs are Sikhs. In Haryana, Rajasthan, Uttar Pradesh and Rajasthan, Aulakhs are mostly Hindus.

[edit] The name 'Aulakh'

Sikhs mostly spell the name as Aulakh. Hindu Jats of this gotra spell their name as Ola or Aula mostly in Rajasthan.

[edit] Trivia

Aula is also the Latin root of the English 'hall'. Ula is one of the main Manchu clans.

Aulakhs were well respected during the British Raj for their loyalty, physical strength and honesty

In 1922 Sepoy Jassa Singh Aulakh Was Shot Dead By Henry Templar A British Officer for marrying one his youngest sister. Jassa Singh's wife Margaret later committed suicide. There were many folk songs devoted to these lovers during the 1930’s and 40’s.
