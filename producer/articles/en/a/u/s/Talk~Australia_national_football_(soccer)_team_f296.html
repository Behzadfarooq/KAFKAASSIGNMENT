[edit] Football is more than one game/code

In the English language the word "football" has always referred to a number of different codes. Soccer dates back only to 1863, which makes it a more recent development than either rugby or Australian rules. Even in the U.K., use of "football" to refer exclusively to soccer is a 20th century phenomenon and rugby union and league supporters there still refer to their games as "football". As does virtually every single supporter of American football or Canadian football. The "How-dare-you-call-it-soccer" fundamentalists, wherever they are found, are wilfully ignoring both history and use of the English language in other countries. [edit] Let's keep this civil, please

To summarise arguments from the talk page archive, the present article title may be defended as follows:

  1. Articles on a topic specific to a particular English-speaking country are to be written in the form of English used in that country (see Wikipedia:Manual of Style#National varieties of English). Obviously, for this article, that's Australian English.
  2. On Wikipedia, popular usage trumps official usage for defining "correctness". For example, the Ukrainian government spell the name of the nation's capital "Kyiv" in English, which better reflects the correct Ukrainian spelling of the word, but on Wikipedia its article is found at "Kiev", based on the more common English usage.
  3. Clarity matters.
  4. "Football" is an ambiguous term in Australian English; sometimes it refers to soccer (including in most of the soccer "establishent"'s use of the term) but more often to Aussie rules, rugby league or rugby union.
  5. Therefore, the advantages of titling the article "Australian national soccer team" — clarity and following majority local usage — outweigh the disadvantages, which boil down to annoying some (but not all — I observe that Tancred's suburban club is, presumably, affiliated to Soccer New South Wales) of the players and followers of the game. Note that a redirect from Australia national football team here is used to ensure links put in the usual Wikipedia format for international soccer teams work.

Any reasonable additions or rebuttals are, of course, welcome. ～J.K. 08:00, 16 February 2006 (UTC)

[edit] Regarding the mistranslation of "Aussies playing dirty"

I apologize in advance if this is too trivial a matter to consider (which it is), but I have been in search of the truth in this field for a while.

It was widely reported during the World Cup 2006 that the head of the JFA accused Australian NT of playing "dirty". This is nothing but a bad translation, and even then you would need to make some effort to achieve such misinterpretation. The key officials in the JFA do not use such language for a start; this is obvious when you actually know them. This lead many of us to believe that this so-called "misinterpretation" was deliberate. The question is by whom.

There are many of us genuine football fans who looked into this in detail by tracing various media bodies as well as contacting the JFA. Possibly one of the most notable research into this subject was done by the editor, Matsu, of Rising Sun News. At any rate we are none the wiser. The end result is the same - the trail goes cold once you enter the west. It is plausible that a media in the west "planted an interesting story" by doing this. The tragedy is that in some ways this seems to have also resulted in some bad blood between the Australians and the Japanese. It seems there are enough Australian fans who genuinely believe in this story. From Japan point of view this is fabricated and bad taste, especially when a good friendship was desired from Japan end in general terms.

I am not trying to bring focus upon this subject, as far as this Wiki article goes. I have also added what has so far been found about this matter and edited the main article accordingly. However, I am wondering if any of you out there know any truth behind this - especially regarding who started it and to what end. While I do not expect any answers (as it is too trivial and obscured), I felt that it is worth pointing this out, in hope that maybe somewhere under somewhat bizarre set of circumstances someone may find the culprit.Jgrey 03:34, 2 January 2007 (UTC)
