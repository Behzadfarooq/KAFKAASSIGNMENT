Augustine Tolton

Augustine Tolton (April 1, 1854 - July 9, 1897) was the first Roman Catholic priest in the United States to proclaim himself African-American instead of passing as white. He was born the son of slaves, Peter Paul Tolton and Martha Jane Tolton, and was baptised in St. Peter Church in Brush Creek, Missouri. Augustine's father was a casualty of the Civil War fighting on the Union side. His mother seized the opportunity and ran away from their masters with Augustine by crossing the Mississippi River in Illinois. [1]

After arriving in Quincy, Illinois Augustine, Martha, and his brother Charley began working at the Herris Tobacco Company making cigars. After Charley's death, Augustine met Father Peter McGirr who gave him the chance to attend St. Peter School during the winter months when the tobacco factory was closed. Others were upset that Tolton was being taught with white children. He was rejected by seminaries for several years, though he was allowed to study abroad in Rome. He was there ordained into the priesthood in 1886 at age 31. [1] Tolton hoped to become an African missionary, but he was assigned to black churches in Quincy, where he met a great deal of racial hostility. [1] He later was the pastor of St. Augustine's, which met in the basement of St. Mary's, and later aided in the construction and administration of the negro national parish of St. Monica's, located on the south side of Chicago, where he was credited with unifying black Catholics. [1]The parish was made a mission of St. Elizabeth's upon the death of Fr. Tolton, and was eventually shuttered in 1924.

"Good Father Gus", as he was called by many who knew him befored his ordination, was known for his "eloquent sermons, his beautiful singing voice and his gift for playing the accordion." [1]

Hemesath wrote that Tolton was beginning to be plagued by "spells of illness" in 1893. [1] He collapsed and died in a heat wave in Chicago in 1897 at age 43. [1]He is now buried in Quincy, IL, as he had expressed the wish to be so.

Tolton was the subject of the 1973 book From Slave to Priest by Sister Caroline Hemesath. [1] The book was reissued in 2007.
