Shells of this family are typically shaped like slender augers or screws, and share similarities with Turritellidae, or the turret shell. A characteristic that distinguishes Terebridae and Turritellidae apart from each other is the short anterior canal or notch in the aperture. Terebridae tend to have characteristically flattened versus convex whorls; more twisted than spiral in shape; and, one or two folds on the columella. Species of this family are grouped as either Terebra or the [[Hastula]] genus, with a minority of species remaining in two other genera.

The Eastern auger (Terebra dislocata) measures on average up to 2 1/4 inches in length, with a pointed spire. Colors vary with exterior bands of pale gray, pinkish brown or orange-brown. This species lives in sounds and offshore on shallow sand flats. It is commonly found on sound and ocean beaches with a range from Virginia to Brazil. The Atlantic auger is a carnivore, but lacks the radula and poison gland found in most other augers.

There are about 300 known species worldwide. These species are sand-dwelling carnivores dwelling in warmer waters. Projection of a venomous barb like that of the cone shelled mollusk (e.g., see Conidae), stun their prey, which typically consists of various marine worms.
