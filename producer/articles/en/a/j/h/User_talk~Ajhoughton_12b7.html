[edit] Currencies in Scandinavia

Hi and welcome to Wikipedia. Sorry to say it, but I've removed your changes to the articles on the Danish, Norwegian, and Swedish currencies. I'd suggest you find a better source, because the page you quote contain obsolete information. The page you quote is not the best source. E.g. Swedish plural of öra is öre (not "ören"). Dkr and Nkr are simply obsolete ISO codes for DKK and NOK, but were replaced more than 10 years ago, so I don't understand why this page still uses them. The Swedish krona uses the ISO SEK, not Sk or similar, and I'm 99% sure that too is an obsolete ISO code. Regards and happy editing. Valentinian (talk) 20:19, 26 July 2006 (UTC)

Hi again. I can't say what the man on the street would do, but I don't think his first choice would be to abbreviate the currencies. I remember Dkr, Nkr and Sk / Skr as the old codes used on the official list of currency exchange rates issued by the National Bank of Denmark, but back in the very late eighties or early nineties, this system was replaced with the current ISOs. I remember because many people, myself included, preferred DKR to the new DKK. Danish banks only use the ISO system, which is also used on the currency lists printed in the newspapers. The average Danish company would probably also use them since the accounting software dominating the market (Microsoft Navision) uses the ISO codes. I've looked a bit further, and the Norwegian and Swedish Wikipedias don't mention them. However, the German Wikipedia uses Skr, Dkr and Nkr. This is speculation, but I wonder if German material plays a role somewhere? I did a search on the website of the Bank of Norway, and it gave no hits for Nkr. On the Swedish, I found a few documents from the late 1990s using Sk, but its search function isn't very good. It is also a complication that in Swedish, Sk means "so-called". A few places, I've found "Sk krona" so in this context, Sk means "Svensk", i.e. Swedish. Regards. Valentinian (talk) 09:58, 27 July 2006 (UTC)
I saw the DKR / SKR / NKR forms many years ago in a maths book, but it was printed before the current ISO system was implemented. It is very possible that a lot of people use these codes, but they have no official status. I've dug a bit deeper, and I've found that a number of Danish websites use both DKK and Dkr on the same webpages e.g. [[1]] - although most of them only targeted for the Danish market, but the text on these pages reminds so much of each other, that I think many of them have been generated by the same software.
As I see it, the article on Danish krone should list the official abbrevations only, and they are "kr." and DKK. Dkr has no official status. My information is based on being a numismatic, and I've checked with one of my friends who's studying to become a banker. He told me that the use of Dkr has no official status, especially since DKR would become the new ISO code should Denmark ever implement a currency reform. When I did a search on http://www.nationalbanken.dk I got results for kr and DKK, but not for Dkr. Regards. Valentinian (talk) 19:20, 28 July 2006 (UTC)
