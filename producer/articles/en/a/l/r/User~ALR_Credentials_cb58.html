< User:ALR

Does an installed Master who can prove his credentials to WP authority have a greater authority on the subject of the craft than someone else? Whilst I may disagree profoundly with the concept of the credentials debate, it may have some mileage. Not sure how things are in the US but in the UK we have provincial yearbooks which list the Masters of each Lodge. I'm not prepared to list my name and lodge on my user page but can provide the reference to the yearbook from my year in the chair, and indeed subsequently because it lists past masters of and in the lodge.

ALR 22:51, 9 March 2007 (UTC)

Given that (even with Jimbo's proposal to create a method to verify any credentials that are claimed) we are not supposed to rely on our credentials in content disputes, but rather on the strength of argument backed by sources... I would say No.
If (and it is a big if), Wikipedia were to shift it's stance, and admit that some people are more informed (and are thus more "authoritive") than others by virtue of their background and credentials then, perhaps, you would have a basis for arguing that you have greater authority on the subject of Freemasonry. I don't think it will happen. Blueboar 13:31, 10 March 2007 (UTC)
I'm not at all impressed with the proposal at the moment, as it flies in the face of the content policies and essentially sanctions the argument of edits based on knowledge. But it might create an interesting opportunity, in some areas.ALR 14:15, 10 March 2007 (UTC)
My opinion is this: ANY information that gets put into the encyclopedia needs to be referenced with reliable/attributable sources. Relying on expert evidence from individuals runs too close to original research, IMO.--Vidkun 18:38, 10 March 2007 (UTC)
Isn't any possible set of established credentials undermined by the fact that no one man speaks for Masonry as a whole? I think whatever we're doing will have to come from a published source, so I don't think it would make much of a difference. MSJapan 21:19, 11 March 2007 (UTC)
