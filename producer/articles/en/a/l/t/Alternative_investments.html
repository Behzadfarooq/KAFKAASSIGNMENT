The alternative investment class includes real estate, private equity, and hedge funds. One of the most important developments of the last two decades in the intuitional marketplace has been the growing acceptance of, and focus on, alternative investments.

Real estate led the way in the early 1980s and has since become a stable component of most institutional portfolios, worldwide. During the same time, private equity gained acceptance among institutions, and continues to gain broader acceptance across the globe today. Hedge funds acted on the periphery to the mainstream until the mid-1990s.

Since, hedge funds have been adopted as a viable and rapidly growing institutional asset class. According to a published report by Institutional Investor News, hedge fund assets hit $1.89 US trillion in 2006. To put this into context, Pensions&Investments Magazine reported on March 20, 2007 that worldwide indexed assets reached $5 trillion US for the first time at year-end 2006.

In terms of institutional sponsorship, according to an October 2006 White Paper issued by The Bank of New York and Casey, Quirk & Associates, estimates are global institutional investor capital in hedge funds will increase from around $360 billion US today to more than $1 trillion US by 2010. Therefore, institutional investors will account for more than 50% of the total flows into hedge funds through 2010.
