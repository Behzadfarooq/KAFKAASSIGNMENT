The altar (throns-syr) is the throne of the Holy Trinity and the alar canopy is the most expressive symbol of its awe and majesty. Three forms of altar canopies are popular. These are:

1. Ciborium or Ciborium magnum (Gr.) 2. Baldachin (Syr.) 3. Tester

[edit] Ciborium or Ciborium magnum

The Ciborium or Ciborium magnum is a solid structure of metal, stone, marble or wood erected over the altar, supported on four or more columns, with a roof which is either domed, spherical or flat.

[edit] Baldachin

The Baldachin is a smaller and lighter structure in metal or wood or merely light frame covered with silk. It is hung over the altar by chains attached to brackets fixed on the wall. The word Baldachinis derived from the Italian equivalent for Baghdad, where the textile material made of silk, wool and metal thread warp used for the canopies was first made.

[edit] Tester

The Tester is a rectangular, square, round or conical shape canopy made of rich fabric and stretched over metal or wood frame, with a deep valence on the lower edge. It is suspended from the ceiling (Anson,1948; Webb, 1949)

The use of the altar canopy became widespread in the 10th Century AD. It is normally found today only in old churches.
