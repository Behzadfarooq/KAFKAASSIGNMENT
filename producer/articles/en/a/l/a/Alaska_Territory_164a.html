[edit] Background

In 1724 the Russian czar, Peter the Great, ordered Capt. Vitus Bering to explore the North Pacific and landing in Alaska he claimed the land for Russia. The wild land was explored by Russian fur traders and pioneers, however in 1799 the passing of a charter for the Russian-America Company destroyed settler rights and gave all power to the government.1 Alaska remained under Russian control for the next seventy years however its vast resources were never tapped. On March 30, 1867 Russia sold Alaska to the United States for $7,200,000 mostly to prevent it from becoming an English colony.2

General Jefferson C. Davis was sent to set up a military occupation until a civil government could be established.3 To encourage economic growth a government subsidized, profit-sharing monopoly was formed called The Alaskan Commercial Company. The A.C.C. organized the fur trade and created transportation, however it hurt private commerce and population growth.4 The military rule lasted for an incredible seventeen years until 1884 when the Organic Act was passed. This law provided Alaska with a governer, John Kinkead, and judicial courts, however these governments were largely unsuccessful and it still did not have a legislative representative in Congress. Life prior to the Organic Act was chaos; with no civil law there was no marrying, collecting of debts, or acquiring of personal peoperty.5


[edit] Gold Rush

In 1897 gold was struck in the Klondike and life in Alaska was forever changed.6 The gold rush pushed Alaska to the forefront of national attention, people flocked to the Klondike and Yukon valleys to get rich. As a result of this influx of people all the land north of the 61st parellel was put under military control. Though the land was harsh and many of the "miners" perished, the gold rush helped boost the Alaskan economy and induce the building of the its first railroad. The rails connected Skagway with the lower 48 states and was owned by the English White Pass Railroad Company.7


[edit] Territory and Statehood Status

The passing of the Criminal Code, a tax on liquor among other things, in 1899 heightened the cry for Alaskan representation in Congress8 and the debate finaly ended on August 24, 1912, when the Alaska Territory became an organized, incorporated territory of the United States.

Alaskan participation in World War II was of great importance. From June 1942 until August 1943 the Japanese tried to invade the U.S. by way of the Aleutian island chain. This marked the first time since the War of 1812 that American soil was occupied by an enemy. The Japanese were eventually repelled by a force of 34,000 troops.9

Eventually the U.S. government came to realize the vast potential of this land, and on January 3, 1959, Alaska became the 49th state. To this day Alaska is a huge supplier of natural resources such as coal, timber, oil, and natural gases.10
