Alan Hull (James Alan Hull, 20 February 1945, Benwell, Newcastle-upon-Tyne - 17 November 1995) was an English singer-songwriter and founding member of the Tyneside folk-rock band, Lindisfarne.

[edit] Career

Hull took up the guitar as a boy, and became a member of the band, The Chosen Few, alongside keyboard player (and future Ian Dury alumnus) Mickey Gallagher, in 1962. He supported himself one year by working as a nurse at a mental hospital, before Lindisfarne came together, Hull came to be regarded as the de facto leader of the group, which may have contributed to its splintering in 1973. He recorded solo albums periodically, beginning with 1973's Pipedream on the Charisma record label, which included the services of second-generation Lindisfarne guitarist/keyboardist Ken Craddock, as well as original members Ray Jackson and Ray Laidlaw.

Lindisfarne reformed in 1976 after a well received Christmas show at the Newcastle City Hall which was broadcast on local radio.

In 1994, he recorded Back to Basics, a live all-acoustic survey of the best of his songwriting from 1970 onwards. On November 17, 1995, while working on a new album, Statues & Liberties, Hull died suddenly of a heart thrombosis.

His most famous songs include: Lady Eleanor, Fog on the Tyne and "Run for Home".
