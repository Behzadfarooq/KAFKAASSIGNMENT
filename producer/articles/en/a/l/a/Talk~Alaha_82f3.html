If I write French or German, I use the appropriate diacritics, so the fact that English uses virtually no diacritics makes no difference: the orthography of the language itself is what's important. Of course, ܐܠܗܐ is written in the Syriac alphabet rather than the Roman alphabet. However, it is useful to readers to be able to transliterate Syriac words into the Roman alphabet. There is no standard of transliteration for Syriac, but we should use a system that makes sense. Unfortunately, there are two different pronunciation of Classical Syriac — East and West. In West Syriac, the word ܐܠܗܐ is pronounced /alɔhɔ/. In the West Syriac vowel system, the first vowel is called pṯoḥo (/pθɔħɔ/; modern pronunciation ftoḥo, /ftɔħɔ/), and it is considered to be an a-vowel (/a/). The second and third vowels are each zqofo (/zqɔfɔ/), which is considered to be an o-vowel (/ɔ/). In the East Syriac vowel system, ܐܠܗܐ is pronounced /alɑhɑ/, and the vowels have similar names pronounced differently. The first vowel of ܐܠܗܐ is pṯaḥa or ptaḥa (variously pronounced /pθɑxɑ/ or /ptɑxɑ/), and second is zqapa (variously pronounced /zqɑpɑ/ or /zqɑwɑ/). Although classical East Syriac pronunciation and a number of dialects distinguish these two a-vowels (as shown here), many dialects do not distinguish between them. Therefore, the same word, ܐܠܗܐ, is pronounced /alɔhɔ/ in the West Syriac variety and /alɑhɑ/ in the East Syriac variety. We need to consider ways to transliterate ܐܠܗܐ. A transliteration based on the West Syriac system would simply be Aloho. Most people who use the East Syriac system would probably write Alaha for simplicity's sake, but many would appreciate that this does not do justice to the difference between ptaḥa and zqapa. In scholarly publications, it is important to use a transliteration scheme that distinguishes between these two vowels, and so the letter a is used for the letter pṯoḥo/pṯaḥa, and, variously, ā, â or å are used for zqofo/zqapa. Therefore, spelling ܐܠܗܐ as Alaha makes no sense to those who use the West Syriac system, and it oversimplifies the East Syriac system. Marking the vowel zqāpā with a diacritic is the usual way to avoid these problems. Is this clear. — Gareth Hughes 22:00, 12 June 2006 (UTC)

Ask someone who can read and write Classical Syriac or literary Neo-Aramaic how many vowels there are in the language. The answer is Pthakha (short a), Zqapa (long a), Zlami zogi/pshuqi (i/e mixture), Khwaṣa (i), Rwaṣa (u) and Rwakha (o). I've given you this list with the East Syriac names so that you can understand it. You see that there are two a-vowels. The Pthakha is written as two dots, one above and one below the letter. The Zqapa is written as two dots above the letter. In Alaha, although in many dialects the three vowels are pronounced the same, the first vowel is Pthakha and the other two are Zqapa. This might not mean much to you if your dialect does not differentiate between them (I known that Alqoshi and Zakho do differentiate). Many dialects especially Urmežnaya and Iraqi Koine change Pthakha to Zqapa regularly. Remember that in written Syriac, the two vowels are written differently. Therefore, is it such a strange thing to transliterate them into English differently. Now, why I have mentioned West Syriac before is not because I am telling you to follow the West Syriac way (my points above are from East Syriac), but because West Syriacs make a big distinction between these two vowels. That's why they say Aloho. As Wikipedia uses redirects, we could have redirects on both Alaha and Aloho leading to the article at Alāhā, Alāhâ or Alåhå (I would favour the first or second of these). People speaking any dialect can understand what these transliterations mean, and they are reflective of the Syriac, which marks two different types of vowel in this word, not one vowel repeated three times. — Gareth Hughes 12:05, 15 June 2006 (UTC)

Alaha (ܐܠܗܐ in Syriac script) means the One God transliterated from the Aramaic language. It is understood that the Arabic الله‎ Allah and the Syriac term for God have common Semitic roots. Both terms apply to the God of Abraham (ܐܒܪܗܡ in Syriac script) and of the Prophets (ܐܢܒܝܐ in Syriac script) that followed.

[edit] From the ancient Babylonian religion to Judaism and Beyond

The metamorphosis from henotheism to monotheism - for what may have developed into a "Jewish" subculture - happened in the city of Ur according to biblical sources. However, the original form of monotheistic religion in Babylon and/or the Near East may have already existed and may have evolved from worship of one specific god; Ashur of the Assyrians for instance became their main deity. Yahweh, or Yah for short; is the name of the god of Israel. That deity had preexisted as a god in that region millennia before the existence of the Tanakh.

Ironically in the city of Ur, the main deity was Sin, the moon god. Iah, pronounced the same as Yah, happened to be an ancient Egyptian moon god. Egypt, a powerhouse at the time, was influencial to the Near East. See Book of Abraham. Another irony is that Abraham is considered by Muslims to be the patriarch of Islam; which has the crescent moon as the symbol of the religion; which was actually the insignia of the Ottoman Empire borrowed from the flag of Byzantium. Also, the Islamic, Jewish, and Babylonian calendars were lunar. The word for years in Aramaic is Moons (Šenê). It is possible to say that originally the moon was seen as a heavenly celestial body which may have been later deified just as heroes and kings had been (see Gilgamesh). The ancient people, particularly the Egyptians, would often attribute stars and/or heavenly objects to fallen relatives and nobles, forming many legends that may still exist in some form today.
