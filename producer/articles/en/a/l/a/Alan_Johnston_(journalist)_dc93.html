Alan Graham Johnston (born May 17, 1962) is a journalist working for the BBC. He has been the BBC's correspondent in Uzbekistan, Afghanistan and Gaza. He went missing in March 2007, prompting fears that he had been kidnapped. On March 26, his captivity became the longest ever of a foreigner in Gaza in two years, having been missing two weeks. His captivity has led to many protests in the Palestinian territories, as well as the British government meeting a Hamas member for the first time.

[edit] Biography

Johnston was born in Lindi, Tanzania, on May 17, 1962,[1] but is a British citizen.[2] He was educated at Dundee University in Scotland[3], where he graduated with an MA in English and politics. Johnston also has a diploma in Journalism Studies from the University of Wales in Cardiff.[1]

[edit] Journalism

Johnston joined the BBC in 1991 and has spent eight years as a correspondent for the BBC, including in Tashkent, Uzbekistan as well as Kabul, Afghanistan.[1] He was in Kabul when Afghanistan was still under the control of the Taliban.[3]

He is currently the BBC's full-time correspondent in Gaza, and the only foreign reporter with a major Western media organisation to be based in the city.[1][4][5]

Johnston has covered many major stories in Gaza for the BBC, including Israel's unilateral disengagement plan in 2005, Hamas winning the 2006 legislative elections, the 2006 Israel-Gaza conflict and the Palestinian factional violence of late 2006 to 2007.[3]

Johnston is widely regarded as a respected, experienced journalist,[6] and due to his local knowledge, he was someone other journalists would turn to for information when in Gaza.[5] Johnston's BBC colleague Paul Adams noted: "It is his job to bring us day after day reports of the Palestinian predicament in the Gaza Strip."[1]

[edit] Kidnapping

On March 12, 2007, Johnston's car was found abandoned on a street, shortly after he left his office to drive home.[1] He had entered Gaza from Jerusalem earlier in the day,[4] where he had been for a dental appointment.[6] A business card belonging to Johnston was found at the scene, identifying him as having been in the car.[7] The BBC was alerted to his disappearance when he did not make a pre-arranged telephone call.[6]

According to Palestinian police, four armed men were spotted near Johnston's car,[8] and Johnston is believed to have been abducted at gunpoint.[2] A state of emergency was declared with checkpoints set up to find Johnston, who was in the final weeks of his posting to Gaza, where he had been stationed for three years.[8]

There were some reports that negotiations had begun to try to secure Johnston's release, although the BBC strenuously emphasised that it could not independently verify reports that Johnston had been kidnapped.[9] A week after his disappearance, the BBC admitted that it seemed certain now that he had been kidnapped.[10]

On March 21, Israeli sources reported that Johnston may have been taken by the same groups that captured Gilad Shalit in June 2006. However, this was strongly denied by both the Popular Resistance Committees and the Palestinian Army of Islam.[11]

March 26 marked the fact that his kidnapping was now the longest-ever of a foreigner in Gaza since abductions began happening in the Gaza Strip, which led to renewed calls for his release.[12][13]

In the midst of his third week in captivity, news agencies began reporting on speculation that Johnston had been kidnapped by a powerful Gaza family with criminal connections, and which was willing to switch support to the other faction in the Palestinian Territories should one displease them.[14][15][16] It then emerged that the family might be holding Johnston as a bargaining chip who would be released in return for ten Hamas gunmen who killed members of the family.[17]

[edit] Reactions, appeals and protests

The Palestinian government condemned the kidnapping, and vowed to "bring the criminals to justice",[4] calling the abduction "despicable".[2] The Foreign Press Association issued an appeal for Johnston's release, while both Hamas and Fatah also called for Johnston to be freed.[8] Reporters Without Borders (RSF) also voiced their concern at the apparent abduction, blaming it on the "impunity" that no-one involved with prior kidnappings had been convicted that "[encouraged]" his kidnappers to act.[18]

On March 15, the BBC's Middle East bureau chief Simon Wilson issued a statement in Gaza thanking Ismail Haniya, Mahmoud Abbas and the Palestinian government for trying to help resolve the situation. He also issued a new plea for information on Johnston's whereabouts.[9]

In London, the Muslim Council of Britain also put forward an appeal for the release of Johnston, calling on Abbas and Haniya to do their utmost to secure Johnston's freedom,[19] while over 20 Palestinian journalists held a rally on March 17 outside parliament in Gaza in support of Johnston. The rally was also attended by Information Minister Mustafa Barghouti and Wilson.[20] Barghouti said: "We are opposed to the kidnapping of foreign journalists who serve the Palestinian cause."[21]

A week after Johnston went missing, his father made a televised appeal for his release. Speaking from Argyll, Graham Johnston called on his son's abductors to "let my son go, now, today".[10] Wilson said that the fact that there had been no information on Johnston for over a week had been "disappointing", adding that efforts made to find Johnston would have to be redoubled. He also noted that the BBC's only request was to have "some firm information" on Johnston.[22] Deputy Director General of the BBC Mark Byford also called for people with influence to secure Johnston's release,[10] while BBC staff in London held a rally in support of Johnston.[23][24]

RSF invited Arabic-language news media and bloggers to post banners on their websites that called for Johnston to be freed. RSF also noted that the "silence" surrounding Johnston's abduction was "particularly worrying".[25]

The Palestinian Journalists Union in Gaza observed a 24-hour strike on March 20 to protest against Johnston's abduction, and threatened to "escalate" its protests until Johnston was released.[26] Foreign and local journalists in Ramallah, West Bank, had held a sit-in a day earlier, at which Barghouti again condemned the kidnapping.[27]

The BBC said that it had received "assurances" about the well-being of Johnston, but repeated that it had "no firm knowledge" of his condition. It also thanked journalists who demonstrated in a show of support both in the Middle East and back in the UK.[28]

European Union foreign policy representative Javier Solana told the BBC on March 20 that the EU was doing all it could to try to establish Johnston's whereabouts. Solana added that the EU had been involved since the day of the kidnapping.[29] British Foreign Secretary Margaret Beckett told Parliament that London was also doing everything it could, and had brought the issue up with Mahmoud Abbas,[30] saying that Abbas had given her assurances that finding Johnston was "very much" a goal of the Palestinian authorities.[31] Journalists also protested in front of Abbas's office to demand that more be done to deal with the situation.[32]

Protests continued on March 22, demanding more protection of journalists and more be done to prevent violation of press freedoms. Ahmed Abdel Rahman, advisor to Abbas for the PLO, told protesting journalists in Ramallah that there were "indications of an imminent release".[33] Twelve days into Johnston's abduction, the Bishop of Lichfield Jonathan Gledhill asked churchgoers to pray for Johnston's release, saying that people were grateful for "brave journalists" like Johnston.[34]

Over 100 people held a rally for Johnston's release thirteen days into his captivity. Simon Wilson again urged those with influence to "work tirelessly" to obtain Johnston's freedom.[35] On the day marking the second week since Johnston went missing, Gaza reporters held another strike in solidarity with Johnston.[35][36] The beginning of Johnston's third week in captivity also lead RSF to press the Arab League to issue a new appeal for Johnston's release at an upcoming summit.[13]

In response to the RSF appeal, at the end of the two-day summit Saudi Foreign Minister Prince Saud al-Faisal condemned the kidnapping, adding that he hoped the kidnappers would soon release Johnston, saying that this was "certainly ... not something that anybody, anybody would approve of" and that Johnston was just "doing his job".[37]

Amnesty International[38] and Johnston's former school Cardiff University[39] also both issued calls for Johnston's release. The deputy director of the university's journalism centre noted that the kidnapping "deprived Palestine of an objective reporter relaying its news to the West."[39]

A new three-day strike was planned by journalists in the lead-up to the fourth week of Johnston's kidnapping.[17][40] The strike included a ban on covering all government activities and the Palestinian Authority in general.[41] Simultaneous demonstrations and protests were also planned for April 2 in both Gaza and Ramallah.[40] On the day itself, over three hundred journalists held a demonstration in Gaza with their mouths tied and gagged.[42] They then marched to the city's government area. Another such protest was held in Ramallah in front of Abbas' office.[43]

The three-day strike meant that a meeting between United States Speaker of the House Nancy Pelosi (D-CA) and Abbas was boycotted by the local media.[44] That same day, UNESCO Director-General Koïchiro Matsuura added his voice to those calling for Johnston's release. He noted that the situation was "increasingly disturbing", and asked authorities to "do their utmost to obtain his release as quickly as possible".[45]

On April 4, the protesting journalists forced the cancellation of a rare meeting of Parliament in Gaza after the entrance to the Parliament building was blocked by the protesters. Some of the lawmakers then stood and listened to the protest.[46]

The next day, Britain's Consul-General in Jerusalem Richard Makepeace met with Prime Minister Haniya, breaking a ban by the European Union on contacts with Hamas. British diplomats stressed that the meeting was only to discuss Johnston's kidnapping and did not "represent a change of policy".[47] Makepeace's office emphasised that the meeting was "strictly for humanitarian reasons", while Reuters quoted some diplomats as saying that it was generally agreed that the boycott of Hamas could be relaxed in emergencies like kidnappings.[48]

Palestinian children took part in a demonstration on April 6 to call for Johnston's release. The children held banners and carried his picture when demonstrating in the streets. Johnston was also spoken of at Friday prayers.[49] Fresh protests were held in Ramallah, Nablus, Jenin and Gaza City the next day by Palestinian journalists, who held banners condemning Johnston's abduction.[50]

[edit] Guardian advertisement

To mark the twenty-first day of Johnston's kidnapping, three hundred British media personalities signed an advertisement, organised by the BBC, that was published in the Guardian. The advertisement states that the signatories "demand the immediate release of BBC Gaza correspondent, Alan Johnston".[51] The advertisement "[asks] again that everyone with influence on this situation increase their efforts, to ensure that Alan is freed quickly and unharmed."[52]

It was signed by most editors of British national newspapers, including Alan Rusbridger, Robert Thomson, John Witherow, Patience Wheatcroft, Will Lewis, Paul Dacre and Richard Wallace.[51] Other signatories included David Dimbleby, Sir David Frost, Jon Snow and Christiane Amanpour.[42] al-Jazeera's Wadah Khanfar also signed the appeal.[42]

That same day, the BBC news website created an online version of the petition to allow people from across the world to sign it. Over 8,000 people responded to the online appeal,[53] forcing the BBC to close it due to the overwhelming response.
