Alan Caruba is a public relations advisor, best known as a critic of environmentalism and Islam and, in 1990, founder of the National Anxiety Center, a think tank dedicated to debunking the idea that there is global warming and damage in the ozone layer. He is an adjunct scholar at the Center for the Defense of Free Enterprise,[1] and from 1994 to 2004 he was the Director of Communications for Tom DeWeese's American Policy Center. From 1984 to 2004 he ran the "Boring Institute," a media spoof that released annual lists of the year's most boring celebrities.

The website for Caruba's PR business states that his clients have included corporations, think tanks, trade associations and others. In the 70s he played role in the introduction of the carbamate insecticide bendiocarb,[2] and since the late 1980s, he has been the public relations counselor for the New Jersey Pest Management Association.[3]

[edit] Views

Caruba writes extensively on a wide variety of topics that include energy issues, education, the United Nations, and popular culture. He has claimed that global warming is a 'hoax', denied that CFCs damage the ozone layer, and criticised many other claims made by environmentalists. He has criticized Sourcewatch, who criticized him in return.[4]

Caruba initially supported the Bush administration's war with Iraq, but has since written to express a note of caution regarding the hoped-for outcome.

He believes that:

“ The whole of America, Europe and other nations that are the engines of the global economy, has been under attack by the environmentalists because an evil, corrupt United Nations wants to be an unelected global government and we stand in their way. This is why the worldwide environmental movement is directed from the United Nations. Behind the United Nations are those who subscribe still to the failed economic theories of Marxism and who hate the success of the United States and others who have embraced capitalism…They are the ones seeking to destroy the sovereignty of the United States by stealth, creating a North American Union to merge our nation with Canada and Mexico, sinking the individual protections afforded by our Constitution into a morass of regulations over which there will be no vote by Americans. Global warming is the mask, the charade, the Big Lie by which the destruction of the United States of America is being advanced. ”

—Alan Caruba, "Global Warming on Steriods," Warning Signs, Jan. 3, 2007

[edit] Publications

Caruba writes a weekly column, "Warning Signs", widely excerpted on conservative news and opinion websites. He is, for example, a regular contributor to CNSnews.com, the Free Market News Network, and AxcessNews.com. In 2003, a collection of these columns was published by Merril Press under the name Warning Signs In late 2006, a new collection titled Right Answers: Separating Fact from Fantasy was published.

Caruba is the author of several books dating back to the 1970s and has contributed opinion pieces to consumer and trade magazines, as well as to newspapers, including The Philadelphia Inquirer, The Providence Journal, and The Washington Times. He is a founding member of the National Book Critics Circle and maintains a website on new fiction and non-fiction called Bookviews. He is also a member of the Society of Professional Journalists, American Society of Journalists and Authors, and the National Association of Science Writers.

[edit] Criticism

Caruba's views on environmental issures are generally contrary to the mainstream scientific consensus, and critics have called him an "industry shill" and a "flack."[5][6][7] Some have accused him of blurring the line between his PR business activities and his writings as an "independent" journalist and commentator.[7] Indeed, the website for his PR business has a page that lists the websites that run his weekly column "Warning Signs,"[8] and he has published a series of pro-pesticide columns[9] while at the same time representing pesticide industry clients in his PR business.[3]

Sourcewatch criticized Caruba for defending columnist Michael Fumento when he was dropped by Scripps Howard News Service after Business Week revealed that Fumento had not properly disclosed his ties to biotech giant Monsanto. Howard Scripps carried several pro-biotech and pro-Monsanto columns by Fumento before his conflict of interest was uncovered. Caruba justified Fumento's actions writing, "Suffice it to say that even writers need to eat and pay the rent."[10]

The factual accuracy of some of Caruba's articles has also been called into question, but he has never withdrawn or altered any of his weekly commentaries. Examples include a January 2007 about energy independence[6] and an article from 2003 about mercury.[11]

[edit] National Anxiety Center

The National Anxiety Center identifies itself as "a clearinghouse for information about 'scare campaigns' designed to influence public policy and opinion." The Center maintains a website at www.anxietycenter.com.
