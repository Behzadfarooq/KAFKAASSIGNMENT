Alan Green (born Belfast, Northern Ireland 25 June 1952) is a BBC Radio Five Live sports broadcaster/commentator. He commentates mainly on football, but also covers golf and rowing.[1]


Green attended Methodist College Belfast. He then joined Queen's University, Belfast and worked on local newspapers before joining the BBC as a news trainee in 1975. His first match commentary on the radio was a 1982 World Cup qualifier between England and Northern Ireland, before he joined the BBC radio football team in 1982.

Following the death of Peter Jones in 1990, he joined Mike Ingham on the top BBC Radio commentary team in time for the World Cup. He is currently probably Radio Five Live's first choice football commentator, allocated most of the top matches. He is also one of the regular presenters of the BBC's main football phone in programme 6-0-6. He is noted for his strident opinions - he is quoted to not remember the last thing he 'didn't have an opinion about' in his autobiography - The Green Line. Since the beginning of the 2004-05 season he seems to have made a point of also praising referees occasionally, perhaps under instructions from station bosses to moderate his approach.

Alan Green is also well known for his "feud" with Sir Alex Ferguson to whom he has not spoken for years. He is frequently accused by fans of various Premiership clubs of having a vendetta against their club and/or supporting a rival team, but he denies having an affinity with any of them. Rival fans of Manchester United, Manchester City and Liverpool each accuse him of supporting the other team. A number of Manchester United fans base this resentment on Sir Alex's "feud" with Green. Ferguson has been reputed to have said that Green was 'a Liverpool fan... just like all the rest' when he felt that he was not getting enough credit for a comeback against Liverpool in the early 90s.

In 2005 on 6-0-6 Alan claimed that he is actually a Macclesfield Town fan, perhaps jokingly. In the same year Alan caused outrage within the blue half of Merseyside, with an article he wrote for the Irish Examiner entitled 'Wake up and smell the coffee, David! [2]. The article, a thinly veiled attack on Everton Football Club, was picked up by the Liverpool Echo and produced widespread disdain amongst supporters, in which Alan himself received personal death threats [3]

In his book, he states that the only team he has a particular affinity for is Linfield of Belfast, Northern Ireland. Green was banned from the Reebok Stadium (home of Bolton Wanderers) for making derogatory comments about the the team in February 2006. A lot of Green's statements are misinterpreted, but he has still developed a good relationship with a large number of fans and those involved in the game.

Again in January 2007, Green was in hot water on Merseyside over comments made on Five Live during the Everton v Reading match at Goodison Park. Film star Sylvester Stallone was paraded on the pitch, and Green quipped as to whether Stallone's limousine would still have wheels when he returned to it. This prompted an official complaint to the BBC by Liverpool City Council, outraged at his stereotypical views about the city being a hotbed of car crime. The story was reported by the Liverpool Daily Post and radio news website RadioToday[1].

Green also covers the Olympic Games, rowing and the Ryder Cup in golf.

Green was the commentator on the PlayStation football game Olympic Soccer.
