Alain Soral (1958-) is a French journalist, essayist, and politician, author of several polemical essays. A former member of the French Communist Party (PCF), he self defines him as Marxist and "National Republican" although he has joined the far-right National Front (FN) in 2005. According to him, "if Marx was alive today, he would call to vote for Jean-Marie Le Pen," the leader of the FN [1]. He is also the brother of actress Agnès Soral.

[edit] Biography

He lived in Annemasse in the department of Haute-Savoie during his childhood, and was beaten by his father. [2] When he came of age, he went to Paris where he spent two years doing menial jobs before being accepted to the École Nationale Supérieure des Beaux-Arts. He continued to the École des Hautes Études en Sciences Sociales, where he became interested in Marxism and the works of Georg Lukacs, Henri Wallon, Lucien Goldmann and Michel Clouscard.

Following his studies, he published two fairly successful books on sociology, including one on fashion, before becoming a widely-published writer.

Soral is married and lives in the Basque Country. In June 2004 he obtained a diploma that allows him to teach boxing.

His most recent novel, Chute : Éloge de la disgrâce, was published in France on April 6, 2006. In May 2006 he published an article in the far-right magazine Le Choc du mois.

[edit] Works and attitude

As a writer, he as self presented himself as a "national-Republican", a universalist and a Marxist; he was once, though is no longer, a member of the French Communist Party. He supported left-wing candidate Jean-Pierre Chevènement during the 2002 presidential election.

However, he has recently turned himself toward the far-right, supporting in 2005 Dieudonné M'bala M'bala and joining the National Front's campaign committee the same year, charged of the social issues and matters concerning the suburbs under the authority of Marine Le Pen. His personal itinerary has led some to compare him with Jacques Doriot, one of the neo-socialists in the early 1930s and Collaborationist under Pétain [3]. He supported the Bloc identitaire's distribution of popular soups including pork in January 2006 [3].

Soral gave an interview to Rébellion, a French review which self-describes itself as National Bolshevist, as well as to Radio Courtoisie, related to the Catholic fundamentalist movement. His last book was dedicated in the far-right library of Emmanuel Ratier (library Facta). Alain Soral has also signed the call for the liberation of far-right activist Michel Lajoye, imprisoned for having carried out a bomb attack on November 30, 1987, as a false flag strategy designed to incite racial hatred between Jews and Arabs. Others signatories included two members of the Bloc identitaire and Gilles Soulas, owner of the Librairie Nationale' (National Library) [4].

Along with Dieudonné, he traveled to Lebanon during the 2006 war in support of the Hizbollah, along with Thierry Meyssan, and Marc Robert, member of the National Front [3].

His books tend to attack aspects of communitarianism, and are especially aggressive towards homosexual and feminist movements, as well as the Jewish community. For example, he has criticised the "persécutions physiques de la milice communautaire Act Up" ("physical persecutions of the communitarian militia of ACT UP").

Notably, he has written: "En France, tous les communautarismes montants : gay, islamique... se créent et se renforcent par imitation, hostilité et opposition au communautarisme judéo-sioniste, dont le statut privilégié constitue la jurisprudence communautaire sur laquelle s'appuient leurs revendications face à la république" ("In France, all of the growing sectors of communitarianism: gay, Islamic... create and reinforce themselves with imitation, hostility and opposition to Judeo-Zionist communitarianism, whose privileged status constitutes the communitarianist jurisprudence on which their protests against the Republic rely"). [1] Soral believes that the rise of communitarianism in France is dangerous for the Republic; to him, it is an attack on the Republic's universalness.

His analysis of society dissects the mechanisms of what he terms "la société du désir" [5], praised by the media, feminist publications and the cult of celebrity. He has especially criticised monthly women's publications, which he believes alter the conscience and relegate women to the status of "femme-objet".

As part of the debate on 'laïcité' in French schools, Soral compares the Muslim veil to thong underwear [6] : the thong, a form of clothing that has arisen from this "société du désir", is a manifestation of a society which, despite its claims of female emancipation, is a society that relegates individuals to the same level as objects.

[edit] Cinema

Soral played the character 'Philippe' in Catherine Breillat's 1996 film Parfait Amour ('Perfect Love'). He also wrote and directed the 2001 film Confession d'un dragueur, which starred Saïd Taghmaoui et Thomas Dutronc; this film deals with class boundaries, and was panned by critics.

[edit] Controversy

The association ACT UP attacked Soral's publishers, Editions Blanches, for having published many authors (including Soral) who, they claim, spread prejudice against homosexuals, even to the extent of inciting "la haine des homosexuels" ("hate of homosexuals"). ACT UP requested that they cease publishing Soral's work. ACT UP have also taken protested outside the Editions Blanches buildings. [2]

Soral denounces the communitarianism of the gay community and the idea of 'gay pride', which he believes to be dangerous; indeed, he sees the term 'gay pride' solely as a promotion of handsome men, youth, parties and drag queens, something that hides the reality of homosexuality, especially that of older homosexuals, who he believes are deliberately kept from the forefront of 'gay communitarianism'.

[edit] France 2 incident

In a documentary on the French television channel France 2, Soral said: «Quand avec un Français, Juif Sioniste, tu commences à dire qu' il y a peut être des problèmes qui viennent de chez vous. Vous avez peut être fait quelques erreurs. Ce n'est pas systématiquement la faute de l'autre, totalement, si personne ne peut vous blairer partout où vous mettez les pieds. Parce qu'en gros c'est à peu près ça leur histoire tu vois. Ca fait quand même 2500 ans, où chaque fois où ils mettent les pieds quelque part, au bout de cinquante ans ils se font dérouiller. Il faut se dire, c'est bizarre ! C'est que tout le monde a toujours tort sauf eux. Le mec, il se met à aboyer, à hurler, à devenir dingue tu vois. Tu ne peux pas dialoguer. C'est à dire, je pense, c'est qu'il y a une psychopathologie, tu vois, du judaïsme sioniste qui confine à la maladie mentale…» ("When you start to say to a Frenchmen, a Zionist Jew, 'Maybe there are problems that come from you... you might have made a few mistakes... It's not systematically the other person's fault, totally, if no one can cope with you everywhere you go. (litt.: if no one can smell you wherever you put your feet)' Because that's their general history, you see. For 2500 years, every time they've settled somewhere, within fifty years they get themselves beat up. You've got to say, that's a bit weird! It's like everyone is wrong except them. And the guy'll start shouting, yelling, going nuts, you know? You can't carry on the conversation. Which tells you, I think, that there's a psychopathology that Zionist Jews have, that verges on a mental illness...") [3].

These comments sparked much controversy and have been judged anti-semitic by numerous observers, including representatives of the Euro-Palestine movement. Soral defended himself some days later on the website oumma.com, claiming that his words had been misunderstood.

Following this programme, on September 29, 2004 Soral and several other people were the victims of an attack, by about twenty people with baseball bats, while publicising his newest book Misères du désir in a shop in Paris's 3eme arrondissement. No group has claimed responsibility for the attacks, and the police inquest has found no leads. Soral himself believes the action to be the work of an extremist group such as Betar or the Jewish Defense League. Since (and, he, believes, due to) the attack, he has not generally been invited for interview on television or in magazines.

[edit] Soral and Dieudonné M'bala M'bala

In a 2005 interview given to the magazine VSD, Soral announced his intellectual support for the equally controversial Dieudonné M'bala M'bala, with whom he worked on the Euro-Palestine list for the European elections of 2004, before his withdrawal led M'bala M'bala to do likewise. The latter is visible in the background, listening to Soral, during the France 2 incident mentionned above.
