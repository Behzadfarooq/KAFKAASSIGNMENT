Alan Bashara is an important part of the development of the concert and entertainment scene in Western Michigan and specifically in Grand Rapids, Michigan according to The Grand Rapids Press.

He graduated from Ferris State University in 1967 with a Bachelor of Science degree in Commerce. In the 80s he produced over 300 concert events in the market at a time when there were no huge promotion companies like there are today. It could be said that the frequency and size of the events that he produced led to the building of the 12,000 seat Van Andel Arena which is one of the top 10 buildings of its size in the world.

Bashara began his career in show business in Los Angeles in the early 70s after returning from a tour of duty in Vietnam, where he met Larry Vallon, one of the most successful and highly respected talent buyers in the history of the touring talent industry. Through his relationship with Vallon, Bashara was able to establish his business a decade later in Grand Rapids.

But he started out as an actor and stuntplayer, having studied with Lee Strasberg and having worked with action director, the late Jack Starrett. He is a member of the Screen Actors Guild. His screen debut was in the television film "Nowhere to Hide" with Lee VAn Cleef and Tony Musante. His last film was the Johnny Cash/Bo Hopkins good ole' boys tale "Thaddeus Rose and Eddie". It was also Bashara's 70s relationship with Jim Wiatt, Chairman of the board of the William Morris Agency, that was instrumental in his later successes in Michigan.

A descendant of Lebanese immigrants, the often beleaguered Bashara spent a total of 8 years in county jails, state and federal prisons for a variety of drug related charges. His first stint, 6 months in the Ventura County Jail in 1978, interupted his acting career. His second bust, that led to a 16 month bit in the Michigan State Prison system, effectively ended it. Ironically, that conviction was overturned.

Following his release from state prison in 1980, and with backing from his cousin, John Ayoub, he started his concert business, Blue Suede Shows, in Grand Rapids. From 1981 to 1992 his productions grossed over $3,000,000 and entertained over 360,000 concert goers. At the time, the biggest venue was the 4200 seat Civic Auditorium. In 1986 the city was planning a larger venue. Bashara was a member of the exploratory committee and a major proponent of a new building. In another ironic twist, Bashara got busted by the feds in 1993 and spent the next 6 years in prison. The fruits of Bashara's labors, VanAndel Arena, opened in 1996; Bashara never produced a show there. He was released from Federal supervision in 2003.

It was finally determined through extensive Federal Bureau of Prisons and Veterans Adminstration counseling and treatment that his drug use was related to a Post Traumatic Stress Disorder from his experiences in Vietnam. It is probably what kept Bashara from reaching higher goals.

He lives today in Grand Rapids and works part time as a concert merchandise vendor. He still consults and functions on the fringes of the entertaiment business and from time to time produces events there. He is currently working on a series of shows at the new JW Marriott Hotel Ballroom which opens in the fall of 2007.
