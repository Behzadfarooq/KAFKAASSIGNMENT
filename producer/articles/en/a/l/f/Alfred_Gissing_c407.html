Alfred Charles Gissing (born at Epsom, Surrey on 20 January 1896, died 27 November 1975, Valais, Switzerland), was an English writer and headmaster, the son of George Gissing.

[edit] Childhood and education

After the early death of their father, the novelist George Gissing, his sons, Walter Leonard (born at Exeter in 1891) and Alfred Charles, benefited from a small government pension. The following report appeared in The Times newspaper for 24 June 1904:

"A pension of Â£74 a year has been granted to Mr. Walter Gissing and Mr. Alfred Gissing during the minority of either and in recognition of the literary merits of their late father, Mr. George Gissing and of their straitened circumstances."

At the time, Walter was a boarder at school in Norfolk, and Alfred had moved in 1902 to live with foster parents, a Mr and Mrs Smith who were farmers at Treverva Farm, Mabe, near Falmouth, Cornwall. Alfred remained with them until he left school.

Like his brother Walter, Alfred went as a boarder to Gresham's School, Holt. He was there between 1910 and 1914, becoming a prefect.

[edit] Military service

After leaving school, and with war in the air (Austria-Hungary declared war on Serbia on July 28) Alfred joined the British Army late in July, 1914. He was commissioned as a second lieutenant on 16 December 1915, and promoted to lieutenant on 7 July 1917. He was in India when the news reached him of his brother Walter's death at the Battle of the Somme on 1 July 1916.

After the war, Alfred Gissing remained in the army, serving in Iraq and then working for the Inter-Allied Censorship Committee between May 1919 and February 1920, when he left the service with the rank of Captain.

[edit] Writer

At about the time he left the army, Alfred Gissing took over his father's posthumous interests from Algernon Gissing, and he went to live at Fernleigh, St Mark's Avenue, Leeds, Yorkshire, which had been Margaret and Ellen Gissing's home. He began to pursue a collected edition of his father's works and to correspond with publishers and collectors as literary executor.

From 1924 until 1927, he lived in Richmond, Surrey. He wrote a preface for A Victim of Circumstances by George Gissing (Constable and Dutton, 1927), a collection of his father's short stories, and he edited a compendium of his father's work, Selections Autobiographical and Imaginative from the Works of George Gissing (Jonathan Cape, 1929).

In his own right, Alfred Gissing wrote biographies.

[edit] Marriage and children

On 26 July 1938, he married Frances Muriel Smith, a widow whose maiden name was Braham, in Broadway, Gloucestershire. They had three children, Michael, Charles William, and Jane, the last of whom became a British Olympic skier.

[edit] Teacher

After the Second World War, Alfred Gissing settled at Salvan in the Swiss canton of Valais, an area well known to George Gissing, where Alfred founded and ran an English school for children displaced by the war.
