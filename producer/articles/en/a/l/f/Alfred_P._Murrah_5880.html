Alfred Paul Murrah (October 27, 1904 - October 30, 1975) was an American attorney and judge. The Alfred P. Murrah Federal Building, which was named after him, was destroyed in the April 19, 1995, Oklahoma City bombing.

[edit] Childhood and education

Born in Tishomingo, Oklahoma, Murrah ran away from home at the age of 13, riding freight trains until he was dropped in the community of Tuttle, Oklahoma. A local family took him in and housed him, in exchange for labor on their farm. Murrah worked his way through high school and college, where he was a member of the Lambda Chi Alpha Fraternity. He ultimately earned a law degree from the University of Oklahoma.

[edit] Judicial appointment

In 1937, at the relatively young age of 32, Murrah was appointed to the U.S. District Court for Oklahoma, making him one of the youngest federal judges in American history. In 1940, he rose to the 10th Circuit Court of Appeals, where he served until his retirement in 1970. From 1969 to 1974, he directed the Federal Juidicial Center in Washington, D.C.

[edit] "One of the foremost figures in the American judiciary"

U.S. Supreme Court Chief Justice Warren Burger referred to Murrah as "one of the foremost figures in the American judiciary." He is noted for having pioneered the practices of pretrial conferences and of litigation panels with judges from multiple judicial districts.

[edit] Personal

Murrah identified politically with the Democratic Party. A Methodist, Murrah taught Sunday School classes at Oklahoma City's Crown Heights Methodist Church, of which he was a major financial supporter. Murrah was also a Freemason.
