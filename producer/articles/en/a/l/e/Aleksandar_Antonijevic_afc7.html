Aleksandar Antonijevic was born in Yugoslavia, Aleksandar Antonijevic attended the National Ballet School in Novi Sad where he studied under Ksenija Dinjaski and was coached by renowned Russian teachers Natalia Dudinskaya and Konstantin Sergeyev.

[edit] Career

In 1988 Mr. Antonijevic received a Certificate of Honour for reaching the final round in the 13th International Ballet Competition in Varna, Bulgaria. In 1989 Mr. Antonijevic joined the Zurich Ballet as a member of the corps de ballet under the direction of Uwe Scholz. Within 10 months he was promoted to Demi-Soloist.

In 1991 Mr. Antonijevic joined The National Ballet of Canada as a Second Soloist. He was promoted to First Soloist in 1993 and to Principal Dancer in 1995.

Since joining The National Ballet of Canada, Mr. Antonijevic has danced the roles of Albrecht in Giselle, Basilio in Don Quixote, Romeo in Romeo and Juliet, James in La Sylphide, Eugene Onegin in Onegin, Danilo and Camille in The Merry Widow, Prince Florimund in The Sleeping Beauty, Peter/ Nutcracker in The Nutcracker, Prince Charming in Cinderella, Colas in La Fille mal gardée, Oberon in The Dream, Pinkerton in Madame Butterfly, Beliaev in A Month in the Country, Des Grieux in Manon, Solor in La Bayadère, Act II, the Poet in Les Sylphides, the Young Gentleman in La Ronde and Oedipus in Sphinx. He has performed the lead in several Balanchine ballets, including the title role in Apollo, the lead role in Diamonds in Jewels, and the principal male roles in Theme and Variations, Mozartiana, Serenade, Stravinsky Violin Concerto and The Four Temperaments.

Mr. Antonijevic has created several lead roles including the role of the Father in The Contract (The Pied Piper) (2002), Prince Ivan in The Firebird (2000), Glenn 4 in A Disembodied Voice (1999), and the role of Siegfried in Swan Lake (1999), all choreographed by James Kudelka. Mr. Antonijevic has also created roles in Jean-Pierre Perreault’s The Comforts of Solitude (2001) and Matjash Mrozewski’s A Delicate Battle (2001). He has danced lead roles in the company premieres of Michel Fokine’s Ballet Russes classic Petrouchka (2006), Jerome Robbins’ Opus 19/ The Dreamer (2005), Kudelka’s There, below (2003), The Fairy’s Kiss (1999), Désir (1998) and Cruel World (1996).

Mr. Antonijevic has danced lead roles in Kudelka’s Musings, Pastarole and The Four Seasons as well as the as well as Makarova’s Paquita, Glen Tetley’s Voluntaries and the role of Lewis Carroll in Alice, Eliot Feld’s Intermezzo, Kenneth Macmillan’s Solitaire and Michel Fokine’s Le Spectre de la Rose.

Other ballets Mr. Antonijevic has performed include William Forsythe's the second detail and Herman Schmerman, Christopher House's Café Dances, John Alleyne’s Septet and Tristan and Isolde, Jiri Kylian’s Forgotten Land, Paul Taylor’s Company B and Glen Tetley's The Rite of Spring and Oracle. In 2006 Mr. Antonijevic performed the pas de deux from William Forsythe’s Herman Shmerman in The National Ballet of Canada’s inaugural performance in the Four Seasons Centre for the Performing Arts.

Mr. Antonijevic, along with Principal Dancer Sonia Rodriguez, was a special guest performer at the 2005 Skate Canada Achievement Awards Gala dinner. Mr. Antonijevic also performed at the fourth annual Gala of International Ballet Stars in Cincinnati in 2005.

In April 2000 Mr. Antonijevic performed Manon for Santiago Ballet in Chile in 2000. He has travelled to Tokyo Japan to perform with the Inoue Ballet in The Nutcracker in 1995, the role of Franz in Coppélia in 2000 and Siegfriend in Swan Lake (2006). In December 1999, Mr. Antonijevic received critical acclaim when he danced the opening night performance of Giselle with the Singapore Dance Theatre.

In January 1998, Mr. Antonijevic was a guest artist with English National Ballet. He performed the lead role in Derek Deane’s new production of The Nutcracker at the Coliseum in Covent Garden, London. In October 1998, Mr. Antonijevic performed in the Stars of the 21st Century gala performance in Paris, France.

In 1996, Mr. Antonijevic performed a pas deux from The Sleeping Beauty at the Dancers for Life Gala in Vancouver. He also performed the grand pas de deux from Don Quixote at Ballet British Columbia’s 10th Anniversary Gala.

In June 1993, Mr. Antonijevic performed in Johannesburg, South Africa where he danced in Balanchine's Agon and Ballet Imperial with PACT Ballet.

[edit] Media reviews of dances

  • The Four Temperaments:

“Veteran principal dancer Aleksandar Antonijevic continues to grow from strength to strength. His Melancholic Variations was mesmerizing – intense, passionate and beautifully performed. He moves in seamless patterns, making choreography a fluid flow of honey rather than abridge between individual steps.” The Globe and Mail, 2006

  • La Sylphide:

“The leads were captivating, with Antonijevic showing a particular affinity for Bournonville… was lyrical in his masculinity.” The Globe and Mail, 2005

  • Opus 19/ The Dreamer:

“…led magnificently by Aleksandar Antonijevic…he invests his role with the kind of physical intelligence and mature artistic understanding that is all too rare on ballet stages today.” The National Post, 2005

“…The Dreamer is a wonderful showcase for the ever-evolving partnership of Sonia Rodriguez and Aleksandar Antonijevic – a pairing that in a memorable opening night turn bought everything to it that the work demands and then added an indefinable something that made it soar.” The Toronto Sun, 2005

“Last night Aleksandar Antonijevic and Sonia Rodriguez danced the lead roles in Jerome Robbins’ Opus 19 / The Dreamer as if it has been created for them.” The Toronto Star, 2005

  • Alice:

“…a poet in motion if ever there was one…” The National Post, 2004

  • Onegin

“Antonijevic’s portrayal of Lensky’s bewildering despair was gut-wrenching.” The Globe and Mail, 2003

  • Herman Shmerman

“….expertly danced by Greta Hodgkinson and Aleksandar Antonijevic” The Toronto Sun, 2006

“Hodgkinson and Antonijevic showed off their technical expertise with brightness and humour.” The Toronto Star, 2006

  • Swan Lake

“Antonijevic was a gentle prince, an outsider living among marauding macho males. His ballon (or lightness) is legendary and his lyrical approach to Siegfried was poetry in motion. The dancer was able to mask his excellent technique within the meaning of the dance, rather than becoming a dancer who drops character when he has to perform virtuoso tricks.” The Globe and Mail, 2005

“Antonijevic flashes across the stage like a streak of moonlight illuminating the dark forest.” The Toronto Star, 1999

“Aleksandar Antonijevic, as Siegfried, was noble, with fine placement and a plush dynamic set off by his soft pliés”. The New York Times, 1999

  • The Sleeping Beauty

"Antonijevic's Florimund was one of those bolts of lightning that strikes only once or twice in a decade, announcing that there is magic in the air....Antonijevic must be one of the most exciting dancer-actors anywhere." The Globe and Mail, 1994

  • Romeo and Juliet

“Aleksandar Antonijevic and Sonia Rodriguez were magical together…Antonijevic just keeps getting better. His partnering is assured and unobtrusive; he has beautiful technique with gorgeous lines and phrasing, and a ballon lighter than air. Over the years he has also become a consummate actor, and that is the joy of his performance of Romeo – he is a complete dancer.” The Globe and Mail, 2006

“an extraordinary actor-dancer…filled with subtle personality nuance and character detail that fleshed out Cranko’s choreography beyond mere storytelling” The Globe and Mail, 2002

"His Romeo presented a perfect picture of Renaissance Italian youth, swept up by star crossed destiny. (Antonijevic is) an elegantly centred dancer able to toss off pirouettes with patrician ease...truly a Romeo in the making, blessed with impressive promise.” The Toronto Star, 2002

Giselle

“the most princely looking of the company’s present roster” The Toronto Star, 2004

“(Antonijevic) has a soft springy jump that a gazelle would envy – though this leaper lands in perfect fifth and deep plié each time. He possesses high extension and excellent lines, making an elegant and supportive partner.” Dance Magazine, 1999
