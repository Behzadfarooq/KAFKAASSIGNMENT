Algebraic cycle

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

In mathematics, an algebraic cycle on an algebraic variety V is, roughly speaking, a homology class on V that is represented by a linear combination of subvarieties of V. Therefore the algebraic cycles on V are the part of the algebraic topology of V that is directly accessible in algebraic geometry. With the formulation of some fundamental conjectures in the 1950s and 1960s, the study of algebraic cycles became one of the main objectives of the algebraic geometry of general varieties.

The major conjectures on algebraic cycles include the Hodge conjecture and the Tate conjecture. In the search for a proof of the Weil conjectures, Alexander Grothendieck and Enrico Bombieri formulated what are now known as the standard conjectures of algebraic cycle theory. There are two of these, known as A and B ‘of Lefschetz type’, and ‘of Hodge type’. As of 2007, these conjectures are all still open in the general case.

Algebraic cycles have also been shown to be closely connected with algebraic K-theory.

[edit] Equivalence relations

Starting from the geometric point of view, which is the Chow ring, geometers are interested in constructing equivalence relations that produce spaces more like homology groups (with rational coefficients). Fundamental relations are algebraic equivalence, numerical equivalence, and homological equivalence.

[edit] Cycle maps

Intuitively a subvariety W inscribed on a variety V represents a cycle in the sense of homology. In more formal terms this must be expressed as a cycle map, giving W a class [W] lying in H2d(V), where W has dimension d. The dimension 2d is the correct real dimension, in case V is defined over the complex numbers. For geometry over a general field, the same should apply, but the assumption will be that H means the homology theory attached to étale cohomology. To convert by duality between homology and cohomology one needs an equivalent to Poincaré duality. The construction usually comes from a Gysin map.

[edit] Standard conjectures

The standard conjecture of Lefschetz type, related to the hard Lefschetz theorem, states that the Lefschetz operator Λ, relative to an ample line bundle, is algebraic (this is also called conjecture B). The conjecture of Hodge type is modelled on the Hodge index theorem, and states that a certain quadratic form on the primitive cohomology is positive definite. Using these conjectures, one gets a conditional proof that homological equivalence and numerical equivalence for algebraic cycles are the same.

The original application envisaged by Grothendieck was to prove that his construction of motives give an abelian category, that is semisimple.

A further conjecture in this area is concerned with the Künneth decomposition of the diagonal in a product X × X, and is known as Murre's conjecture.
