[edit] Looking for another perspective...

I thought I'd ask you about this, since you must also have seen "Killing All the Right People" a decent number of times. I was checking Google for more information about the episode when I found this site. I know it's not a big deal, but I was really surprised that some people looked at the episode that way. Let me quote the part I'm talking about:

"In 1987, as part of its second season, CBS's Southern woman-centered sitcom Designing Women explicitly drew the connection between heterosexual problem and homosexual culpability. [...]
Mary Jo argued for condoms only as a means of protecting children, not as a way to stop the spread of AIDS, which the episode tacitly connected with promiscuous homosexual behavior. Thus, as Emile Netzhamme and Scott Shamp observe, even though Kendall is portrayed as sweet, well adjusted, and sympathetic, the episode subtly blames his homosexuality for his fatal condition."

I guess I just wanted to ask you if I'm missing something here? It seemed obvious to me that the entire point of the episode was to disprove the reasoning behind the bigotry directed at homosexuals with AIDS. Mary Jo did argue for condoms as a means to prevent AIDS; that was her biggest motivation for participating in the debate. Of course, she was arguing for it as protection for children, not adults, but that was the point of the debate—condoms being supplied in schools. Mary Jo also remarks at the debate that AIDS is a disease Kendall got before he knew what it was or how to prevent it. It's stated that anyone can get AIDS and that no one knew how it got started. I felt the episode showed great sympathy for everyone who died from AIDS or was living with it. I just don't see this "explicit connection between heterosexual problem and homosexual culpability." Do you see it? Thanks for your time. :) --Flummery 18:34, 28 October 2006 (UTC)

[edit] Lance Miller is NOT a pharmacist!

Apparently someone seems to think that the character of Lance Miller is a pharmacist, because this is about the fourth time, I have had to remove a line saying that he was the world's best pharmacist.

Let me clear the air here. For one; Lance Miller is a fictional character on a telenovela called Fashion House! Secondly, even on the show, he wasn't a pharmacist, he was an accountant, and a crooked one at that; and third, he was killed on the show.

So, I am kindly asking, whoever is putting in that article that he is a pharmacist to stop, because he was not a pharmacist, and never would be; because he is merely a fictional character and a dead one at that. —The preceding unsigned comment was added by AlbertGray (talk • contribs) 12:00, 9 December 2006 (UTC).
