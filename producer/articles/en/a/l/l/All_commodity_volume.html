ACV, or All Commodity Volume (Value), represents the total annual dollar sales volume of the selected defined retailer marketplace. This measure is typically in million dollars ($MM) in the United States, but may be represented in other currencies as appropriate.

The total dollar sales that go into ACV include the entire store inventory, rather than sales for a specific category of products - Hence the term "All Commodity Volume".

ACV is best related to the key marketing concept of placement (Distribution).

[edit] Usage Discussion of ACV

When dividing total sales into million dollar blocks, it allows for equivalization across uneven market or retailers in size.

For example, there are two retailers. One of the retailers could theoretically be a big-box format that boasts weekly ACV of 15$MM, and it has 5 total stores. Another retailer may have 5 stores, but they tend to be smaller towards mom and pop size, with average weekly ACV at 5$MM per.

If your product is distributed in the smaller-store chain with more total outlets, you are in half the stores (50%). However, you would only be in a quarter of the total market ACV (25%). The converse situation would have distribution in half the stores similarly, but ina stronger three quarters of the total market value.

This is the principal that not all retail outlets are the same, and hence you would rather have distribution in higher ACV volume stores given the choice for greater sales potential. Generally speaking in marketing and sales circles, what percentage of stores you may sell within is less relevant than the share of the store ACV value.

[edit] Examples of Application

Based on the above logic, common applications are as follows:

  • The overall importance of one retailer vs. another in terms of sales volume, or even geographical locations (e.g. New York Metro vs. Omaha)
  • An item (e.g. mayonnaise), or even grouping of items (e.g. Grocery dry goods) has an average share of every million dollars of sales in a sales outlet, representing its overall importance to that outlet.
  • As the example above, an item or aggregated grouping of items can have its distribution measured in equivalized fashion - what percentage of the total market ACV does a product actually sell in? i.e. appear on the shelf?
  • Related to share above, an item or category of items can have a sales rate per million dollars of ACV. This allows for comparison of how well an item sells in one outlet vs. another regardless of its over all distribution. This is often referred to as Weighted Sales Velocity
  • What percentage of ACV$ in a market is a product or grouping benefiting from trade merchandising such as feature advertisements, in-store point of sale display, coupons, etc. One can then deduce how much one time period has extra merchandising support versus another.
