Alliance Defense Fund

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

The Alliance Defense Fund ("ADF") is a conservative Christian non-profit organization with the stated goal of "defending the right to hear and speak the Truth through strategy, training, funding, and direct litigation."

According to its website, ADF was launched in 1994 by the leaders of more than 35 Christian ministries in order to preserve and protect America’s religious freedom. ADF aggressively promotes its worldview by empowering its allies through strategy, training, funding, and direct litigation through its legal team. ADF has been involved in such landmark cases as Rosenberger v. University of Virginia, Schenck v. Pro-Choice Network of Western New York, Boy Scouts of America v. Dale, and Good News Club v. Milford Central School.

The ADF claims to have been victorious in nearly three out of four cases litigated to a conclusion. They have also participated in 30 wins before the United States Supreme Court.[1]

[edit] Organization

In addition to ADF's principal office in Scottsdale, Arizona, it has six Regional Service Centers, located in Sacramento, California; Lawrenceville, Georgia, Shreveport, Louisiana; Memphis, Tennessee; Washington, DC; and Olathe, Kansas. In addition, ADF recently opened its Center for Academic Freedom, located in Nashville, Tennessee.

ADF's President, CEO and General Counsel is Alan Sears. Sears held several positions in the Department of Justice during the Reagan administration, including serving as the Executive Director of the Attorney General’s Commission on Pornography, and has co-authored two books with Craig Osten: The Homosexual Agenda: Exposing the Principal Threat to Religious Freedom Today, and The ACLU vs. America: Exposing the Agenda to Redefine Moral Values.[2]

The ADF Board of Directors is composed of individuals with backgrounds ranging from high-level military and government responsibility, to national ministry leadership, media and journalism, to business and entrepreneurial ventures.

[edit] Programs

ADF’s National Litigation Academy®[3] and Blackstone Legal Fellowship®[4] were created for the purpose of defending the Constitution and the intentions of America’s Founding Fathers.

Its National Litigation Academy brings together top law school professors, litigators, and constitutional experts for intensive courses of study. Volunteer and allied attorneys are trained in the areas of law as they relate to religious liberty, sanctity of human life, marriage, and the family. In return, each attorney pledges 450 hours of pro-bono time.

The Blackstone Legal Fellowship is a nine-week legal internship program that draws from the nation’s most respected law schools. Interns have the opportunity to work closely with leading legal experts, and are equipped with a distinctly Christian worldview.

[edit] Litigation

The Alliance Defense Fund, working with its allies, as well as experienced litigators, directly litigates some carefully chosen, strategic cases for a unique purpose: to prepare newly licensed attorneys for success in pursuing cases involving the protection of religious freedom, the sanctity of human life, the traditional marriage and its view of family values.

ADF has also created an internal mentoring program. Attornies selected for ADF’s three-year litigation mentoring program typically have excellent academic records, previous work experience with an allied organization, and many also are alumni of the Blackstone Legal Fellowship law student internship.

ADF’s senior attorneys guide the new attorneys through all aspects of their first court cases. The "apprenticeship" approach prepares the new attorneys to work in rarefied world of First Amendment law. Within months, they have developed the specialized skills and experience that would normally take years to develop outside the ADF program.

To expand this effect, ADF assignes one or more graduates of the National Litigation Academy (NLA) to almost every case that its litigation team directly litigates. This reinforces the NLA attorney’s previous training and results in a cadre of tested, tough litigators.

[edit] Day of Truth

The Alliance Defense Fund established the Day of Truth[5] to counter the promotion of same-sex marriage and express an opposing viewpoint from a conservative Christian perspective. It is held annually following the Day of Silence, which is organized by the Gay, Lesbian and Straight Education Network (GLSEN). According to the ADF, students who have attempted to speak against gay rights have been censored or, in some cases, punished for their beliefs.[6] ADF bills the Day of Truth as an opportunity for students to publicly exercise their free speech rights. The event aims to be student-led in the same model as the Day of Silence, with students forming small groups at their own school to organize and promote participation.

The Day of Truth was first organized in 2005. Over 1,100 students in 350 schools participated.[7]

The second Day of Truth was held on April 27, 2006, and nearly 3,000 students from more than 800 schools participated. In February, ADF reported that various unnamed bloggers opposed to the Day of Truth had attempted to undermine the event by swamping the Day of Truth web site with requests for brochures.[8]

ADF reports that it has made legal filings in 2006 on behalf of a student who was prevented by his school from participating in the Day of Truth, and has now filed an injunction request.[9][6]

[edit] ADF ethics questioned

The ADF has been accused of resorting to questionable ethical behavior.

In November 2004 the ADF sponsored a lawsuit of a Cupertino, California elementary school teacher against his school principal and school board members. On Wednesday November 24, the day before Thanksgiving, the ADF issued a press release entitled "Declaration of Independence Banned from Classroom", a title that caused considerable controversy, and some outrage, among listeners to various news outlets, predominantly broadcast by ones right-of-center in United States politics.[10]

Later in the ADF lawsuit, (Williams v. Vidmar) all parties (including the ADF) affirmed that the school policy in no way mentioned the Declaration of Independence, and no materials were "banned" from any classroom.[11]

The wording of the press release included text which asserted the school district sought to "erase God" from the classroom. Not surprisingly, this phrasing generated a substantial amount of press coverage in the conservative media over the holiday weekend. The story was picked up by Reuters and Fox News which rebroadcast the news of a ban on at least seven occasions and dispatched the Hannity and Colmes show to Cupertino for a special episode featuring the teacher. Bloggers, pundits, and especially talk radio shows across the nation picked up the story en mass, especially as it broke over the Thanksgiving holiday, a time traditionally associated with Pilgrims and patriotism. The timing of the press release had also ensured, intentionally or not, that the school district would be unable to respond for at least four days as it was shut down for the holiday.

The ADF has made no corrections to its original press release.[12] Threats against the elementary school and its principal caused the sheriff's department to put extra patrols on duty and the school had to hire additional security personnel for the remainder of the year.[13]

In August 2005, Williams and the ADF dropped the lawsuit, choosing to settle out of court. Per the official statement agreed to by both parties, no change was necessary to school policy and no payment required between the parties. Several days later Williams resigned from the school district and left the state.[14] Patricia Vidmar, a target in the original suit, continued in her position as Principal of Stevens Creek Elementary with strong support of parents, teachers, and the local community.[15] $50,000 of local taxpayers money was spent and the suit proved meritless, causing the parents organization to question if their school was just the unfortunate victim of a public relations exercise. The parents group questioned ADF's motives:

"The ADF is morally obligated to be proactive when issuing a correction of the record and they must give it the same wide publicity to ensure the same coverage given the original, factually inaccurate and devastating press release. Otherwise, they’re just paying lip service to their claim of concern for the safety of our children."[16]
