An alley oop in basketball is an offensive play in which one player throws the ball up near the basket to a teammate (or, much more rarely, to himself) who jumps, catches the ball in mid air and immediately scores a basket, usually with a slam dunk. It is an impressive way to score and often electrifies the spectators. The alley oop combines elements of teamwork, pin-point passing, timing, and dunking. The alley oop was first used and popularized during the early 1970s by North Carolina State University to take advantage of the 48-inch vertical leap of Wolfpack star David Thompson. Because dunking was illegal in college basketball at that time, upon catching the alley oop pass, Thompson would simply drop the ball through the hoop. During the 1990s NBA stars turned the alley oop into the game's ultimate quick strike weapon. N.C. State also won a national championship on probably the most famous alley oop play of all time. With time running out and the score tied in the 1983 championship game in Albuquerque, New Mexico, Dereck Whittenburg threw a high, arching pass to Lorenzo Charles, who caught the ball and stuffed it through the net to win the title.

The most common alley oop is done by having a player other than the dunker throw the ball into mid-air. The play can be difficult to execute during a game because it requires unspoken communication between the passer and the recipient of the pass. The passer must be able to anticipate the recipient's movement toward the basket and then time the pass appropriately. Outside of official basketball games, such as in a slam dunk contest, this is not an issue, and the thrower has more choices as to how he/she can throw the ball. For example, at the 2005 NBA Slam Dunk Contest, Steve Nash employed soccer techniques to get the ball to teammate Amaré Stoudemire.

A less common version of the alley oop is done by having the dunker himself throw the ball, often bouncing it off the floor or backboard. In official competition, this is incredibly difficult to do without either committing a traveling violation, or simply creating the needless risk of a turnover.

Alley oops are often combined with other tricks, such as a tomahawk, or a 360° spin. Many halftime shows feature trick slam dunks, which involve spins, flips and alley oops.

The term "alley oop" derives from French allez-oop, the cry of a circus acrobat about to leap. [1] The "Official History of the NFL" book says that the term "Alley Oop" was first used to describe what is now known as the Hail Mary pass in football, but that eventually the term became used in basketball. The NFL history book cites the origin of the term to the comic strip caveman, Alley Oop created by cartoonist V. T. Hamlin in the early 1930s, because sportscasters believed those throws looked like Alley Oop in action. Hamlin picked up the term "allez-oop" serving in the Army in France during WW I.
