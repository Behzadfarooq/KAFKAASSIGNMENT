Allan Williams

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Allan Williams was a former Liverpool businessman and promoter; original manager (or more accurately: booking agent) of The Beatles. He personally took the young band to Hamburg, Germany, where they gained the vital show business experience that led to their emergence on the world stage.

In 1957 Williams, an enterprising Liverpool Welshman, leased a former watch-repair shop at 21, Slater Street, Liverpool, which he converted into a coffee bar. At the suggestion of his friend Bill Coward, he named the venue after an exotic species of ornamental, flowering tree common to Central and South America and the West Indies and renowned for its beauty: jacaranda mimosifolia. The Jacaranda opened in September 1958. The Beatles played the Jac occasionally through the summer of 1960. Between May and August 1960, Williams secured a number of bookings for the group. (Scott Wheeler: Charlie Lennon: Uncle To A Beatle. Boulder, Colorado: Outskirts Press, 2005.)

In August of 1960, immediately after securing Pete Best as the group's new drummer, Williams and The Beatles left Liverpool in a small, crowded van which took John, Paul, George, Stu and Pete to Hamburg for the first time. He fell out with the Beatles in 1961 over the payment of his 10 per cent commission. He never had any further business dealings with the group, but he did remain on friendly terms with them. In 'The Beatles Anthology' video documentary, released in 1995, Paul McCartney characterises Williams as "a great guy."

In 1962, before Liverpool businessmanBrian Epstein became the Beatles' manager, he asked (Williams) what he thought of the group. Williams' forthright reply was: "Don't touch them with an ´effin bargepole!"[citation needed] (meaning that Epstein should stay as far away from them as possible.)

Williams released the album “The Beatles Live!” in the mid '70s, which was taped in December 1962 on the final night of the Beatles' final performance in the Star-Club in Hamburg. In 1975 he co-authored (with William Marshall) a largely fanciful biography titled "The Man Who Gave the Beatles Away" (New York: Ballantine Books, 1977). [1]

“The Man Who Gave The Beatles Away” is also a musical by Irish playwright Ronan Wilmot, which was performed at the New Theatre in Dublin in 2002.

Williams’s life was permanently transformed by his association with the Beatles. In the 1970s he played a crucial role in producing the first Beatles conventions to be staged in Liverpool, and he is a perennial VIP guest at the city's annual Beatle Week Festivals. Williams carries on today speaking at Beatles conventions from Liverpool to Singapore. The Jacaranda reopened under new management in the mid-1990s, and continues to thrive as a Liverpool hotspot with occasion live music. [2]
