If we're going to have this section at all, it needs to be much more complete and balanced; otherwise it affirmatively detracts from the article. Or so it seems to me. All biblical/religious quotes must be sourced. There are far too many versions of far too many books to leave floating quote. I do think this section is important, because the statement itself is religious. The founders appealed to a doctrine of natural rights, which itself eminates from their religious beliefs, and the section should not be removed.

Of course, we need support for those assertions (i.e., the assertions that the founders appealed to natural rights, and that natural rights emanate from religious beliefs) too. I still maintain that it's not enough—it is unbalanced and looks very odd and indeed amateurish—to include in a "religious views" section only the views of a few rabbis. I am afraid that I feel obliged to delete the section, unless someone is willing immediately to expand it, with proper sourcing. Respectfully, Hydriotaphia 21:37, 20 January 2006 (UTC)

[edit] Meaning of "Men"

I feel that this sentence is editorializing: "Indeed, at the time of the Declaration, "men" was understood to mean "white males" instead of "human beings"". For one thing, it seems to imply that the definition of "men" was different, which is ridiculous. It is basically a comment about who could vote at the time. The statement in the DoI is philosophical, not a statement of law or voting rights. I'm removing it. --JW1805 (Talk) 17:51, 15 April 2006 (UTC)

Dear user, you said "For one thing, it seems to imply that the definition of 'men' was different, which is ridiculous". I agree this sounds ridiculous to us now, but it is a historical fact that by referring to "men" in DoI, its great author meant "white males". This is not to say that the statement was not philosophical in nature, or that it was not advanced for its time. But it did has its drawbacks and they should not stay hidden just because the DoI is one of the proud cornerstones of US history. I nevertheless respect the fact that my sentence is considered editorializing and would not add it again, unless I get a positive feedback. Thank you! —The preceding unsigned comment was added by 71.198.134.122 (talk • contribs) 17:39, April 16, 2006.

How do you know what was in Jefferson's mind when he wrote "men"? I understand what you are trying to say: that the state of the laws at the time didn't match up with what Jefferson wrote. But the definition of "men" was not different them from now. --JW1805 (Talk) 19:12, 16 April 2006 (UTC)

We don't know that was in Jefferson's mind when he wrote it; but that's not what the sentence claims. The sentence claims that, contextually, at the time of the Declaration, "men" was usually understood that way. Jefferson's not adding a phrase redefining the term any other way, indicates, but does not prove, that he might have agreed with that then-common meaning. – Adhemar 08:48, 12 August 2006 (UTC)

[edit] Requested move

The following discussion is an archived debate of the proposal. Please do not modify it. Subsequent comments should be made in a new section on the talk page. No further edits should be made to this section.

The result of the debate was move to All men are created equal. Joelito (talk) 01:04, 14 August 2006 (UTC)

All people are created equal → All men are created equal. As per Liamdaly - Jefferson's phrase reads "all men," and gets 20 times as many Google hits as "all people." Articles about specific phrases should be titled exactly with that phrase. It's certainly valid to discuss modern reformulations and gender concerns in the article itself, but the article should be where people expect to find it.
