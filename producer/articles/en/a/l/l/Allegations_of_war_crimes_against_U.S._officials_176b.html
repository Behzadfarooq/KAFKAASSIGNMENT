Allegations of war crimes against U.S. officials

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

You have new messages (last change).

A number of prominent U.S. officials have been accused of war crimes and crimes against humanity by some antiwar and human rights groups. None of these officials, however, are indicted by any legitimate prosecuting domestic authority. Therefore legal proceedings were started against Rumsfeld, Attorney General Alberto Gonzales, former CIA director George Tenet and other senior U.S. civilian and military officers in Germany on November 14, 2006, invoking universal jurisdiction.[1]

[edit] Johnson administration

  • Robert McNamara, Secretary of Defense under John F. Kennedy and Lyndon B. Johnson, a prime architect of the Vietnam War. [2]

[edit] George W. Bush administration

  • John Conyers, Robert Parry and Marjorie Cohn[3] -professor at Thomas Jefferson School of Law, executive vice president of the National Lawyers Guild, and the U.S. representative to the executive committee of the American Association of Jurists- assert that the invasion of Iraq was not a war in self-defense but a war of aggression contrary to the U.N. Charter (a crime against peace) and therefore a war crime.[4]
  • On April 14, 2006, Human Rights Watch said that then Secretary Rumsfeld could be criminally liable for his alleged involvement in the abuse of Mohammad al-Qahtani.[5]
  • On November 14, 2006, invoking universal jurisdiction, legal proceedings were started in Germany against Rumsfeld, Attorney General Alberto Gonzales, former CIA director George Tenet and other senior U.S. civilian and military officers.[6]
  • The Military Commissions Act of 2006 is seen as an amnesty law for crimes committed in the War on Terror by retroactively rewriting the War Crimes Act[7] and by abolishing habeas corpus, effectively making it impossible for detainees to challenge crimes committed against them.[8]
