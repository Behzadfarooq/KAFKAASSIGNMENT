[edit] Brian Wilson

from http://www.magonia.demon.co.uk/arc/90/satanupdate.html :

former Beach Boy Brian Wilson tells a story [of being abused by Satanists as a child], denied by other family members, that only emerged after counselling by a dubious therapist

hence the inclusion on the See Also list, I suppose. Not quite within the article's purview, though, since it's actually "allegations of Satanism by a generator of popular culture", but then again, it is the "See Also" list. I'll leave it to others to decide whether this means it should return or stay out. -- Antaeus Feldspar 23:13, 3 August 2005 (UTC)

  • When the anon added it, I assumed there were people alleging that Brian Wilson left Satanic messages in Beach Boys songs (Hey, I've heard odder things...). That he claims he is a victim of Satanic ritual abuse is another matter, and should be mentioned in that article rather than this one.--Pharos 23:51, 3 August 2005 (UTC)

Listen to the song "Our Prayer" on Smile and you'll pretty much figure out the man was not a satanist. Out of all the musicians in the world, AC/DC, Led Zepelin, Marylin Manson, why Brian Wilson? Do you have any real evidence besides a crackjob's webpage? —The preceding unsigned comment was added by 66.215.101.71 (talk • contribs).

Well, if you had read what was written, you would have seen that that the "crackjob's webpage" says is not "Brian Wilson is or was a satanist", but that Brian Wilson alleged that he had been the victim of Satanists as a child. -- Antaeus Feldspar 16:45, 2 March 2006 (UTC)

[edit] More development, please

This article has the potential to explore a facet of modern culture. What it lacks is detail, a discussion on why some people/companies/products are accused of Satanism, examples of how some people who have been falsely accused made light of the thing (e.g., the whole "Paul is Dead" thing from the Beatles), and court cases revolving around the issue. I think there was a heavy metal band sometime in the early nineties that got sued because christian fundamentalists accused them of urging kids to commit suicide using backward masking. If I'm not mistaken, the band won the lawsuit. With a little more development, this could become a great exploration of Western cultural fears regarding satanism, and the tendency to use that label as a weapon. Wandering Star 03:08, 2 March 2006 (UTC)

I know that the SNL character of The Church Lady had a comedic schtick of suggesting Satan's responsibility for this and that or the other thing, frequently involving popular culture, but is the character worth mentioning here as demonstration that such allegations had become so frequent as to become a target for such parody? -- Antaeus Feldspar 17:09, 2 March 2006 (UTC)
