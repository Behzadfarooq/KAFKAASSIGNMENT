Allensworth is an unincorporated area in Tulare County, California. It was a town founded with the ideal that African Americans could own property, learn, thrive, and live the American Dream. It was also called the Tuskegee of the West (a reference to Tuskegee, Alabama).

[edit] History

In history, American law sometimes enforced conditions where African Americans were effectively prohibited from owning real property. Restrictive covenants on deeds, and the Right of Testimony Law, meant many were locked out of real property ownership (land and housing) and were not afforded benefits of the legal system. With Abolition and the westward movement of persons, there was an increasing pressure to accommodate African Americans in their aspiration for the normal human ambitions described in the United States Constitution.

“ There is much latent force both in men and women of which the world knows nothing, simply because the conditions of their lives are such that their strongest gifts remain dormant. ”

—Col. Allen Allensworth

In 1909, Civil War Union Army veteran Lt. Colonel Allen Allensworth sought to build a town where African Americans could own property, learn, and live the American Dream. With his friend William Payne, they chose a Tulare County site for a new town near the present location of Earlimart, California and about 45 driving miles north of Bakersfield. The site was chosen for fertile soil, adequate water, and its location along a railroad corridor. Allensworth reportedly had a strong moral compass, and one of the first buildings erected was the town church. It was the first California town to be governed by African Americans. In interviews, residents described the town as the ideal American small town: a thriving and positive place.

While traveling in Monrovia, California on September 16, 1914, Allensworth was killed in a collision with a motorcycle. This is cited as a factor in the decline of the town. Problems with the town's water supply, including shortages of water and the presence of naturally-occurring Arsenic in local groundwater, lead many residents to leave. While other communities nearby have naturally-occurring Arsenic in groundwater, at least one account suggests local farmers who lived near Allensworth poisoned the town's water source with arsenic.

[edit] Today

The Colonel Allensworth State Historic Park, the first California State Park dedicated to history of the state's African Americans, encompasses the town site. The park was purchased in 1976. The area is open to the public but the park is not always staffed. Burrowing owls, a species active in daylight hours, are sometimes present in the park.[1]. [edit] Location

The town is along State Route 43 between Kernell, California and Stoil. In degrees-minutes-seconds notation, the NAD83 coordinates for the town are 35°51′49″N, 119°23′20″W. From Kern County Airport, the town site is about 35 miles on a vector of 327.5 degrees off true north. The site is located along the Bakersfield Subdivision track of the present-day Burlington Northern and Santa Fe Railway at milepost 932.3.

[edit] Terrain

The terrain is typical of the southern San Joaquin Valley: flat when compared to San Francisco. Excepting the railroad roadbed, there is less than five feet elevation difference across town. Like most of the valley, there has been some subsidence probably related to historic overpumping of ground water for irrigation. While temperatures usually stay above freezing, there can be periods of heavy rain in winter and it is often in the 90-100°F range during summer. The valley was historically the site of vernal pools and seasonal lakes before Twentieth Century diversions of water and agriculture. A natural gas well field exists west of town. Pixley National Widllife Refuge, operated by the Department of the Interior, US Fish and Wildlife Service, is north of town. Much of the surrounding area is devoted to agriculture.
