All's Well That Ends Well

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

All's Well That Ends Well is a comedy by William Shakespeare, and is often considered one of his problem plays, so-called because they cannot be easily classified as tragedy or comedy. It was probably written in later middle part of Shakespeare's career, between 1601 and 1608. It is one of Shakespeare's least performed plays. The play is based on a tale (3.9) of Boccacio's Decameron.

Indeed, there are no recorded performances before the Restoration; the earliest occurred in 1741 at Goodman's Fields, with another the following year at Drury Lane. Sporadic performances followed in the ensuing decades, with an operatic version at Covent Garden in 1832.

The play was first published in 1623 in the First Folio.

A late revision of the play by Shakespeare has been suggested, as a way of explaining its inconsistencies of tone and style.[1]

[edit] Synopsis

At first, Helen, a lowborn beauty, is working for the Countess of Rossilion. Bertram, the Countess' son, is going to Paris to serve the King of France, because his father has recently died. Helen is madly in love with Bertram, even though Bertram is of higher class. The King is terminally ill, so Helen also goes to Paris to try to heal the King using medical knowledge acquired from her recently departed father. She saves the King's life, and the King lets her pick a husband from the eligible men at court. She picks the unwilling Bertram, and the King forces him to marry her. After the wedding, Bertram decides he would rather face death in battle than be married to Helen, who he believes is beneath him. While at war, he writes home to Helen:

"When thou canst get the ring upon my finger, which never shall come off, and show me a child begotten of thy body that I am father to, then call me husband." (III.ii.55-58)

Bertram thinks this is an impossible task. Helen, however, with the aid of a maiden who has taken Bertram's fancy, tricks him into giving her his family ring and sleeping with her as per the conditions in his letter. In the final act, Helen's cunning plot is revealed, and Bertram promises to be a faithful husband to her and "love her dearly, ever, ever dearly." (V.iii.354)

[edit] Reputation

There is no evidence that All's Well was popular in Shakespeare's own lifetime, and it has remained one of his lesser-known plays ever since, in part due to its odd mixture of fairy tale logic and cynical realism. The final scene in which Bertram suddenly switches from hatred to love in just one line is considered a particular problem for actors trained to admire psychological realism.

One character that has been admired is that of the old Countess, which is one of the few good roles for an older actress in the Shakespeare canon. Modern productions are often promoted as vehicles for great mature actresses; recent examples have starred Judi Dench and Peggy Ashcroft.

[edit] References in popular culture

The phrase "All's well that ends well" has become a popular adage in American culture. As a result, its frequent appearances in popular culture--from the animated series Futurama episode "Roswell That Ends Well" to Michigan Post-Hardcore band Chiodos's album All's Well That Ends Well--may or may not be conscious allusions to Shakespeare's play.

The main character of Shaenon Garrity's webcomic Narbonic, Helen B. Narbon, is named after the play's heroine, Helena de Narbon.
