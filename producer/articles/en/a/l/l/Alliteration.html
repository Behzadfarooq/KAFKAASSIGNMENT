Alliteration

Alliteration is a structuring device characterized by the reiteration of the initial consonant at the beginning of two consecutive or slightly separated words. It is generally found within poems. In one form or another, it is shared by all of the Germanic languages. In the English language, alliteration occurs in Old English poetry, of which it was a central component. Alliteration gained considerable popularity during the Romantic era, as the Romantics were interested in writing poetry with a musical quality, and in the ancient tradition of their native languages. Richard Wagner used alliteration extensively in his operatic texts. Common examples of alliteration include such tongue-twisters as "Round the rugged rock the ragged rascal ran," "Peter Piper picked a peck of pickled peppers," and "How much wood would a woodchuck chuck, if a woodchuck could chuck wood?"

[edit] Common examples

Like rhyme, alliteration can be used as a mnemonic device. As alliterative phrases are often memorable, they are frequently used in news headlines, corporate business names, literary titles, advertising, buzzwords, and nursery rhymes.

Sammy was a sailor, a sailor big and broad
He sailed aboard a whaler and tumbled overboard
He shouted "Someone save me!"
Someone said "Go 'head!
The sharks were circling wildly when the sailors said:

Swim Sam, swim Sam, swim Sam
Show them you're some swimmer

Peter Piper picked a peck of pickled peppers. Did Peter Piper pick a peck of pickled peppers? If Peter Piper picked a peck of pickled peppers, Where's the peck of pickled peppers Peter Piper picked?

  • Cartoon character Bugs Bunny, whose own name is alliterative, speaks this aside to the audience as he is about to deliver what will be the final pitch in the game depicted in Baseball Bugs: "Watch me paste this pathetic palooka with a powerful paralyzing perfect pachydermous percussion pitch!"
  • In Broadcast News (1987), Aaron Altman (Albert Brooks) mocked Tom Grunick's use of alliteration while reporting the news: "Alot of alliteration from anxious anchors placed in powerful posts!"
  • The 2006 film V for Vendetta depicts the protagonist V introducing himself to Evey Hammond with the following alliterative line:
Voilà! In view, a humble vaudevillian veteran, cast vicariously as both victim and villain by the vicissitudes of fate. This visage, no mere veneer of vanity, is a vestige of the vox populi, now vacant, vanished. However, this valorous visitation of a bygone vexation stands vivified, and has vowed to vanquish these venal and virulent vermin vanguarding vice and vouchsafing the violently vicious and voracious violation of volition. The only verdict is vengeance, a vendetta held as a votive, not in vain, for the value and veracity of such shall one day vindicate the vigilant and the virtuous. Verily, this vichyssoise of verbiage veers most verbose, so allow me to simply add that it is my very good honor to meet you, and you may call me V.
  • The 1999 film Mystery Men, characters argue about what their super hero group should be called:
Wait! Wait, that's it. We are the Super Squad.
No, no! Alliteration in these situations is corny.

Stan Lee, co-creator of many comic book super-heroes, has stated that he named many of his characters using alliteration as a mnemonic device as he created many characters, and easily forgets names.

Notable examples of computer games that use alliteration in their names include Leisure Suit Larry in the Land of the Lounge Lizards and Leisure Suit Larry 3: Passionate Patti in Pursuit of the Pulsating Pectorals. These games often feature alliteration for humorous names of fictional companies, magic spells, and the like.

DC Comics characters, specifically those centered around Superman typically carry LL names, especially the women in his life - all his major girlfriends (Lana Lang, Lori Lemaris, Lois Lane), babysitters (Lelita Lerner), relatives (Linda Lee) and also his arch nemeses (Lex and Lionel Luthor).
