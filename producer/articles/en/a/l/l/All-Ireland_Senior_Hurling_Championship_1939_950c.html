All-Ireland Senior Hurling Championship 1939

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

The All-Ireland Hurling Championship 1939 ran from May to September of that year. The All-Ireland Hurling Final was won by Kilkenny who beat Cork by 2-7 to 3-3. The "thunder and lightning final", as it has become known, is regarded as one of the greatest All-Ireland finals of all-time.

[edit] All-Ireland Senior Hurling Championship

[edit] Paths to the final

Cork were victorious in the Munster Championship for the first time since 1931, first taking on Waterford before defeating Limerick in the provincial decider at Thurles Sportsfield. A goal in the dying minutes earned them the right to advance directly to the All-Ireland final. In the Leinster Championship Kilkenny regained the provincial crown that they last held in 1937. They later defeated Galway in the only All-Ireland semi-final, to set up the 11th championship meeting between themselves and Cork.

[edit] The final

Sunday, September 3 was an important date for world affairs. France, the United Kingdom, New Zealand, and Australia all declared war on Germany, thus starting World War II. In spite of this, the battle for supremacy between Cork and Kilkenny at Croke Park, Dublin was foremost in the minds of the hurling public. Just over 39,000 people travelled to witness the first meeting of these two teams since 1931. Back then it took two replays to separate the sides, with Cork emerging victorious at the third time of asking. On the day of the final the players and supporters awoke to the sight of torrential rain. It continued to fall until early afternoon but eased off just before the game started. Little did the players know what a major role the weather would play in the result and the folklore associated with the game.

In the first-half Cork played with the wind at their backs, however, they started slowly. Within three minutes they were 1-1 to 0-0 behind, with Jimmy Phelan scoring the goal for Kilkenny. Jack Lynch opened the scoring account for Cork, however, they were to struggle until half-time when the scoreline read Kilkenny 2-4, Cork 1-1. The game had just restarted when a huge clap of thunder interrupted the play. What followed was a ferocious thunderstorm, with thunder and lightning accompanying a heavy downpour. The conditions became so bad that players and spectators failed to make out other players, let alone the sliothar.

The thunderstorm coincided with a fight-back by Cork. Their captain, Jack Lynch, was the hero again when he found the net with a goal for Cork. Paddy Phelan of Kilkenny was in top form however, repelling attack after attack by Cork. This was followed by another goal for Cork, scored by Willie Campbell and equalising the match. It looked like the game was heading for a draw and a replay, however, Terry Leahy latched onto a Paddy Phelan free and sent the sliothar over the bar for the winning score. Kilkenny had emerged victorious on a scoreline of Kilkennt 2-7, Cork 3-3.

The Cork and Kilkenny players could not have known it at the time but this game marked a dramatic change in fortunes for the two teams. While Cork would go on to become one of the most successful teams of all time, Kilkenny would have to endure eight barren years before their next All-Ireland title.
