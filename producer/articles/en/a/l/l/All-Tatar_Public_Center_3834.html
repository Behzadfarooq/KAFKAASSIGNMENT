All-Tatar Public Center, ATPC, BTİÜ, also known as Tatar Social Center (Tatar Latin: Bötentatar İctimağí Üzäge, BTİÜ; Cyrillic: Бөтентатар Иҗтимагый Үзәге, БТИҮ; Russian Всетата́рский Обще́ственный Центр, ВТОЦ) is a Tatar nationalistic organization. ATCP headquarters in Kazan, Tatarstan.

The first congress (qorıltay) of Tatar nationalists was held in February, 1989. At the fist time the formed organization was called Tatar Public Center (Tatar İctimağí Üzäge). At the second congress (February, 1991) there were passed ATCP charter and program, as well as name were switched to All-Union Tatar Public Center. The presidium of 35 member was elected. Subsequently, after the fall of the Soviet Union, the name was changed for current (1992). [1]

ATPC was established by M. Mölekev (the first chairman), İ Ämixanov, Fäwziä Bäyrämeva, Z. Zäynulin, R. Safin, F. Safiullin and some others. The most of them were intellectuals from Kazan State University.[2] Current chairman is Rafis Kashapov.

The main aims of ATPC: restoring of Tatar state (the last attempt was Idel-Ural State), stating diplomatic relations with Russian Federation and other states as equal, protection of Tatar language and culture, placing Tatar the only official language of Tatarstan or second official in all Federation. Another aims are revival of Islam in Tatarstan, supporting of Tatar diasporas and protection of their rights. [1]

ATPC meeting, 1988-89

Main publishing organs: Taşqın (Stream), Millät (Nation) and Russian language Izvestiya TOTs (Russian: Известия ТОЦ - Proceedings of ATPC). The governing bodies are Qorıltay (Congress), Ğäli Mäcles (Plenum), Presidium (works permanently), chairman. Congresses: 1st - 1989, 2nd - 1991, 3rd and 4th 1993, 5th - 1996, 6th 1999, 7th -2002. [1]

In late 1980s- early 1990s ATPC arranged many meetings of many thousands remonstrants, both young and adult, demanding of proclamation of independence from Tatarstan government. The only bloodshed was a meeting of October 15, 1991, when pro-Russia democrats arranged oppose demonstration and the confrontation came to blows. [2]

However, Shaymiev's Tatarstan government came to opposition against Yeltsin's Russia. ATPC lost it's unique role, it's popularity went down among the majority of population, as well as popularity of Shaymiev grew. Financial problems reduced nationalistic tendencies among Tatarstan people.[2]

Recent years ATPC was known by some pompous action in Kazan and Naberezhnye Chelny, arranged mostly by pensioners. However, Memorial Day (Xäter Köne), a day when Kazan had fallen is widely celebrated in Kazan, accompanied with funeral march and rock music concerts. Many nationalistic organizations of Idel-Ural use to come to Kazan to commemorate victims of Russian expansion.

In some regions local Tatar Public Centers collaborate with local officials, playing only cultural role in local Tatar diaspora. However, in Bashkortostan TPC is in strong opposition to Murtaza Rakhimov.

As it is written in ATPC charter, they struggle only in democratic way. However, some sources state that in 1990s some ATCP leaders had possible with Wahhabi Ichkeria and Taliban Afghanistan. Single volunteers were carried to Caucasus and Central Asia. Those sources also state that ATPC include militarized wings İdel and Altınzan.[3]

Recent years Tatar nationalism come look younger as a response to Putin's policy of centralization.
