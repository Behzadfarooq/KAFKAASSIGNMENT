To be technical, Naval Architecture did not begin until between WWI and WWII with the beginings of steel ships. Before that, the shipbuilders depended upon empirical data kept and supplied by insurance companies like Lloyds of London. (You could not build a commercial ship unless it could be insured. So Insurance companies held all the data.) Before the begining of Naval Architecture there was NO ENGINEERING INVOLVED. If you wanted to build a ship a certain size, you went to Lloyds, got measurment data and wood types of "successful" ships, then you just scaled up to the size you wanted, without any engineering or strengths of materials knowledge.
Because there was no data in the insurance companies about steel ships, steel shipbuilders built from the hip, modeling steel ships after wood ships. They soon discovered that that didn't work. They had to take a completely different approach to ship design using engineering. This is what has become known as Naval Architecture. And what we have learned from Naval Architecture has shown the fatal flaws of the previous wood ship design methods. It wasn't the materials -- i.e. the wood -- that was the problem, but the lack of engineering and dependence upon what was thought to be "successful".
