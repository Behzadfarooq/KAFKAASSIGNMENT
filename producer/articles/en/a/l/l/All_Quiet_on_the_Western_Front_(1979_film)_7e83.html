All Quiet on the Western Front (1979 film)

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

A 150 minute made for television version in full color of All Quiet on the Western Front was released on November 14, 1979, starring actors Richard Thomas from The Walton's fame, and Ernest Borgnine. Historical accuracy seems to have been strived for; the scene of Kaiser Wilhelm awarding decorations correctly shows him using only his right arm (in reality Kaiser Wilhelm's left arm was stunted).

The 1979 film was directed by Delbert Mann; though the acting of some of the performers was justly praised, the general opinion of most film enthusiasts is that it failed to equal the 1930 film directed by Lewis Milestone. In many respects, it offers a sanitised view of trench warfare - far from being destroyed by the conflict, 'John Boy' Thomas is relentlessly depicted as able to cope and adjust to his new surroundings.

It has been accused [1] of failing to capture the spirit of the original. Another commentator says that it is missing about 30 minutes of the original as shown on TV.[2] But both versions remain popular, with 8 and 7 stars respectively on the IMDb User Rating.

[edit] Trivia

  • In the TV remake of All Quiet on the Western Front, Paul is shot while drawing a bird. In the original film, he is shot while trying to grab a butterfly. In fact, neither scene is original to the book, but were inspired by an early scene showing a butterfly collection in Paul's home.
