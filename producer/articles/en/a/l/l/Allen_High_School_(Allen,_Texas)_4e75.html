Allen High School is the only high school in Allen, Texas, United States. It is part of the Allen Independent School District.

Allen High School serves most of the city of Allen. Until fall 2006, Allen High School took high school students zoned to Lovejoy Independent School District, which includes city of Lucas, most of Fairview, and a small portion of Allen. In fall 2006, Lovejoy High School opened [1].

[edit] Academics

Allen High School offers the International Baccalaureate program to its students.

The Allen Eagle Escadrille is the world’s largest high school marching band[2], with a membership of over 600 students. They play for the 5A football team on Fridays. The Escadrille attended in January 2006 the Rose Bowl Parade in Pasadena, California.

The Allen High School Student Council, led by President Christopher Wayne Tucker, will be seeking the office of Vice President of the [| Texas Association of Student Councils] (TASC) at the State Convention in Arlington, Texas during April of 2007.

The Allen Orchestra was invited to perform at The International Midwest Clinic and Convention in 2006.

Allen High School also has one of the nation’s top high school broadcast programs, KGLE 3 Teen News. From 1996 to 2006, the program has had five first places Best of Shows, two second places, one third place, one eighth place, and one not placed at the National Scholastic Press Association’s biannual competition. They have also been the Pacemaker finalists five times.

Allen High School's Eagle Angle newspaper has won several awards, and was a recipient of the John Jacob Award in Newspaper Excellence at the Journalism Education Association's award ceremony in Chicago in 2005.

Allen High School also has the top high school photography program in the state of Texas, winning ATPI's Top Program contest in 2006.

The school's German Folkdancing team won third place in state finals in 2006.

The Allen DECA association competed at the district competition and will be advancing to state competition 2006.

Allen uses an 8:15 a.m. to 3:35 p.m. schedule. The period schedule includes five standard periods per day, though students are only required to be present for four in tenth grade, and only three in eleventh or twelfth grade.

Students are required to remain on campus during their school hours, and off-campus lunches are not allowed unless the student has the period off.

[edit] Bomb threats

In the weeks following the Columbine High School massacre, the Allen Independent School District dealt with 12 bomb threats and eight school evacuations. The threats would lead to the shortening of the school year to keep the possible continuation of bomb threats to a minimum. These events would lead to the increased security of metal detectors and see-through backpacks throughout the campuses.

[edit] Metal detectors

In, what some say is an attempt to improve school security, metal detectors were placed at all entry points to the school and signs which state "WELCOME TO ALLEN HIGH SCHOOL. UPON ENTERING THESE PREMISES ALL CARRY-IN ITEMS ARE SUBJECT TO SEARCH" have been put up. [3] The metal detectors were removed and the required search program was ended after one school year due to cost considerations, privacy concerns, and general student and community dissatisfaction, though the signs remain in place. In 1999 Allen High School became one of the first to introduce cameras, ostensibly for security reasons. The cameras have, however, primarily been used to monitor students across the 177 acre (716,000 m²) campus and in particular to deal with those "who have made bad choices at the schools"(sic)[4]. Of Allen High School's permanent staff of 250, nine (almost 5%) are security staff who must bring their own weapons.

Allen High School at one point suppressed protest by its students against its policy of random searches. The students wanted to wear distinctive armbands to show their disapproval. According to Jennifer Boccia, the school ignored the ruling in Tinker vs. Des Moines and Jennifer was suspended for wearing the armband, after being warned of the intended consequence by an assistant principal. The ACLU took a case to court and launched a media campaign against the school. After the media campaign and negative publicity, the school relented and settled the case out of court [5] without an official court ruling.
