Allied occupation of Europe

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

For occupation by Nazi Germany, see Occupied Europe.

In the liberation of Europe at the end of World War II, the military forces of the Allies of WWII liberated most of the countries occupied in wars of aggression by Nazi Germany, liberated millions of slave laborers and European Jews from concentration and extermination camps, and liberated Germany from rule by the criminal leadership of the Nazi party.

The aftermath of World War II also resulted in the permanent Allied occupation of Europe. Although the formal (de jure) occupation, for the most part, ended by 1955, the presence of foreign troops on European soil continues to this day. Berlin remained under military occupation until 1991.

Checkpoint Charlie in Berlin became a symbol of the occupation and division of Europe

At the end of the war the Soviet Union occupied much of Eastern Europe. In all the Soviet-occupied countries, with the exception of Austria, the Soviet Union helped Communist regimes to power. At the same time the Anglo, French and American forces established their form of government, called "liberal democracy", in their respective occupation zones, resulting in the political division of Europe. This division continued for over forty years.

It the end of the Cold War in 1991, there were 250,000 American troops in Europe. In 2005 there were still 62,000. [1]

[edit] Persistence

Is is unclear why the two superpowers were unable to achieve superpower disengagement in Europe. Possible reasons include:

  1. As there was no German government, no peace agreement between the Allies and Germany could be signed. This enabled the occupation and division of Berlin to continue until 1991. Only in the context of German reunification could the Treaty on the Final Settlement with Respect to Germany be reached.
  2. The continued occupation of Berlin again enabled the two superpowers to maintain "supporting troops" in their client nations. Every tank facing each other at Checkpoint Charlie required hundreds of thousands of troops in the rear.
  3. Any superpower disengagement would have to be reciprocal, with both sides simultaneously reducing troops. Although several proposals were made by the Soviets, they were rejected by the USA. Western propaganda labled the Stalin Note of 1952 as dishonest. A similar agreement was however reached on Austria three years later, resulting in the end to the Allied occupation of Austria and to the unilateral withdrawal of Soviet troops in Romania.
  4. Leaders and governments of client states were dependent and relied on the protection of the superpower, and thus resisted any proposal for troop withdrawal. In West Germany the term Finnlandisierung was coined to describe the fear, that without the presence of strong American troops, Germany would politically slide under Soviet influence, even without any military interference by the Soviets.

It has also been argued that the powers were mainly interested in colonial domination of their spheres of influence.

[edit] Political consequences - the division of Europe

The presence of foreign troops had a profound effect on the political developement of the hosting nations. While helping sympathetic groups to power, the troops also prevented the interference and limited the influence of the opposing forces. In Eastern Europe the Soviet Union helped Communist regimes to power. In the west, representative democracy was established in nations under American influence. European nations not directly under American or Soviet influence and without a strong democratic tradition reverted to fascist dictatorships. The end result was the political division of Europe and the Cold War.

[edit] The end

Gorbachev's "kiss of death" at the 40th anniversary celebration of the GDR (October 7, 1989) ended the Brezhnev doctrine, when he anounced that the East German people were free to choose their "way to socialism".

[edit] Red Army Faction

In West Germany the Red Army Faction engaged in urban guerrilla warfare agains what they saw as an foreign army of occupation. On January 4, 1977 they launced an attack against the US 42nd Field Artillery Brigade at Gießen, in an attempt to capture tactical nuclear weapons secretly stored at the base.

[edit] The Baltic States

Of all the nations occupied during World War 2, the neutral Baltic states were in the unique position of being the only nations to have lost their independence as a result of the war. Prior to the Nazi occupation, the Soviet Union, in agreement with Nazi Germany via the Molotov Ribbentrop Pact, occupied and illegally annexed the Baltic states. The incorporation into the Soviet Union resulted in the arrest, deportation and death in captivity of thousands of Baltic citizens, including in the case of Estonia some 80% of all pre-war parliamentarians, 100% of the military general staff and other high ranking members of Estonian society.

Liberation of the Baltic states from the genocidal Nazi yoke therefore meant a return of the previous Soviet occupation regime and the resumption of the brutal political repressions that were begun before the Nazis arrived, such as further deportations in 1949, collectivisation and the wholesale destruction of all war memorials to those who fell in the 1920 Estonian War of Independence, for example.

See also: Occupation of Baltic states

[edit] Finland

Finland mostly escaped Soviet occupation at the end of the Continuation War. The Soviet fourth strategic offensive was stopped in the Battle of Tali-Ihantala, on the Soviet side of the 1940 border. An Allied Control Commission in Helsinki monitored the implementation of the Moscow Armistice until 1947. Finland was also required to lease Porkkala, located 30 kilometres west of Helsinki, as a naval base to the Soviet Union for fifty years. The Soviet military vacated the Soviet military garrison in 1955. In 1948 an Agreement of Friendship, Cooperation, and Mutual Assistance was signed between Finland and the Soviet Union, guaranteeing Finnish neutrality. [3]

[edit] Denmark

Following the Nazi Occupation of Denmark the Faroe Islands were occupied by British forces. Iceland was occupied on May 10, 1940 by the British, later to be replaced by Americans, who had also taken possesion of Greenland.

Most of Denmark was liberated from German rule in May 1945 by Field Marshal Bernard Montgomery; the easternmost island of Bornholm was liberated by Soviet forces, who remained there for more than a year.
