All Channels Act

The All Channels Act was passed by the United States Congress in 1961, to allow the Federal Communications Commission to require that all television set manufacturers must include UHF tuners, so that new UHF-band TV stations (then channels 14 to 83) could be received by the public. This was a problem at the time, since the major TV networks were well-established on VHF, while the local-only stations were starting up on the newly-allocated UHF band.

The law was later used to require that all AM receivers be able to pick up the new expanded band stations from 1620 to 1700 kilohertz. This requirement was enacted in the 1980s, in advance of new stations being allotted in the 1990s. Some have proposed this for FM broadcasting as well, taking TV channel 6 which will be largely unused after analog shutdown.

[edit] Digital television

The act has most recently been used in the 2000s to require TV manufacturers to include ATSC-T (terrestrial TV) tuners for digital television, in any TV set that includes an NTSC analog TV tuner. This requirement has been phased-in, starting with the largest TV sets. By early 2007, every device capable of receiving over-the-air TV (including VCRs) will be forced to include an ATSC tuner.

This final requirement is despite the fact that the fifty-dollar cost (including significant patent royalties, unlike DVB-T) will double (or more) the price of small devices, eliminating the affordability of these devices often used to receive emergency broadcasts while camping or during power outages during and after hurricanes, earthquakes, and other disasters. This is in direct contrast to other efforts to improve emergency preparedness, where the public is often advised to have a battery-operated portable TV for just such occasions.

Prior to 2006, TV manufacturers have been largely sidestepping this regulation by failing to include any tuners at all, instead selling large-screen video monitors that are HDTV "ready". These require an external set-top box that as of July 2006 is still not yet available, except in new HDTV DirecTV and Dish Network DBS systems, which pickup both DVB-S2 and ATSC-T. These however require a subscription, even to view the local free-to-air stations with an antenna.

Many new TV sets are finally now complying with the regulation, with many times more models offered by retailers than in 2005. The 2007 model year is expected to be fully compliant, and Motorola is expected to have a converter box as well.
