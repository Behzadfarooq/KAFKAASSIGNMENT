Alice Isabel Chisholm

Dame Alice Isabel Chisholm (July 3, 1856 - May 31, 1954), known as Mother Chisholm, was an Australian woman who provided canteen services for soldiers in Egypt and Palestine during World War I.

Alice Isabel Morphy was probably born near Goulburn, New South Wales, she was raised by her mothers parents and educated at home. In 1877 she married pastoralist William Alexander Chisholm, they had 5 children, 3 sons and 2 daughters. During the first World War her son Bertram was wounded on Gallipoli. She travelled to Egypt to be closer to him; when she arrived she noticed the lack of facilities for the troops and established a canteen in the Cairo suburb of Heliopolis largely at her own expense.[1]

She opened a second canteen in Egypt at Port Said, and a third in Kantara for troops fighting near the Suez Canal with fellow Australian Verania McPhillamy and a New Zealander known as Miss Rout. The Kantara canteen was very popular and expanded to include dormitories and dining-rooms and eventually had the capacity for handling thousands of men.[2] Profits from the canteens were used to provide the troops with comforts for their journey home.

She was appointed Officer of the Order of the British Empire in 1918 and Dame Commander of the Order of the British Empire in 1920. When she returned to Australia she continued working within the community, she helped found the RSL in Goulburn and she was active in the CWA and RSPCA. She died at age 97 in 1954.

A street in the Canberra suburb Cook is named in her honour.
