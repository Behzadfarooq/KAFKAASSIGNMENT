It was originally developed by Jeff Robbin and Bill Kincaid as an MP3 player called SoundJam MP,[1] and released by Casady & Greene in 1999. It was purchased by Apple in 2000, given a new user interface and the ability to burn CDs, had its recording feature and skin support removed, and on January 9, 2001 was released as iTunes.[2] Originally a Mac OS 9-only application.[3]

In October 2001, Apple announced version 2 of iTunes at the same time as they announced the iPod. The main feature of iTunes 2 was integration with the iPod, allowing users music libraries to be synced automatically across from their computers to their iPods. Version 2 also introduced support for Mac OS X, an equalizer and support for MP3 CD support, both reading and burning.[4] iTunes 2 was released on November 2, 2001, shortly before the iPod went on sale.[citation needed]

Smart Playlists and support for Audible.com audio bookes were the focus of Steve Jobs' keynote at the July 2002 MacWorld Expo in New York when he announced version 3 of iTunes.[5] Version 3, the first Mac OS X only release, also introduced the ability to rate tracks and record the number of times a track has been played.[6]

At a special Media Event on April 28, 2003, Jobs announced the iTunes Music Store and at the same time he announced iTunes 4 as the interface to the store. As such, through QuickTime 6.2, version 4 included support for the AAC audio codec and FairPlay digital rights management technology. iTunes 4 also included the ability to stream music between computers, support for album artwork and DVD burning to allow archiving.[7][8]

In October 2003, with the release of iTunes 4.1, Apple added support for Windows 2000 and Windows XP.[9]
