[edit] And now I have to punch you in the nose

I swore that the next person who invoked wp:iar would be tracked down and get punched in the nose by me, so now I have to.

I got nothing against the guy, and I loved Night Watch, but the seedling of "an article per cricket match ad infinitum" can next be "I have deleted the parts of your project page that didn't belong, undeleted all the deletes of the day because they were bad speedies, and I will not use VfU or RfC because those don't apply to administrators." There is another RFA there that I was highly, highly suspicious of. I'd almost like to see a form question for anyone who says "VfD" in their "why I wanna be an admin" thing: "The vote is 5D and 5K on Racism in Ireland. What do you do?" Then follow that with "What is your threshold for consensus on delete votes?" Anyone who says, "Remove the VfD tag and celebrate" for the former and "80%" for the latter is out, in my book, as I think there are some folks really, really wanting to overcome lack of consensus for their vision of the rules by acting out of process. Geogre 23:52, 24 August 2005 (UTC)

I agree that IAR is, in its authorship and intent, not bad. I read it as a variant on "Don't be a dick," as it basically says, "Don't lawyer or insist on a new policy for every disagreement." However, it is cited precisely in the context of "I do what I want because rules are the tether of lesser mortals." Thus, I don't want to punch the authors of wp:iar, but those who cite it once per quarter hour as they are being asked why they undeleted out of process or deleted out of process or violated the 3RR. That's why, once upon a session, I announced that it was the next person who'd get the sock in the nose.
The final dispensation of the cricket season doesn't bother me, either. It's fine. However, it seems to me that a match-by-match article is a monstrous thing, and that's where we were while the season was underway. If the intent from the start had been to get the documentation and then add into a big merge, then user space would have been the way to go, and I'd have had nothing to say, but the matter came up on two or three VfD's. By the second already, and then by the third, the "keep" rationale was, "There's a Project for the cricket season, so nothing can be VfD'd." Projects are not invisible armor, and there was a playing of "This is the WikiCricket Project (notice the official name), so be awed and cowed into silence" on the vote.
As for the answer you offered to the two hypotheticals, 5D 5K on Racism in Ireland (although the vote would probably actually be 65D 22K 9M with 35 suspect votes) should, in my opinion (and by former practice) result in sending the article to a Request for Comment or should get it tagged with a disputed tag. In other words, it shouldn't just be "kept" with nothing else happening, as it has proven to be an article that deeply divides the community, and the aggregate opinion (not "consensus") is that the article is suspicious. I.e. the result should not be "remove the tag or delete the article," in my opinion, but "address the issues that got the thing listed so it doesn't become a festering sore that will result in a renomination to VfD or a triumphalist campaign by its author to write 100 more just like it or worse and cite its passage as precedent." A divisive VfD is not a seal of approval, but a sign of something desperately needing work. As for the second question, I think deletion should take place at 2/3rd majority. I only have that because that's what US law has used for its second-level opinion threshold (overriding a veto, amending the constitution, etc.).
The reason I'd personally like to see more articles go is that I'm an elitist, but it has nothing to do with why I'd like to see people "close" VfD only if they plan to work their rears off on most keeps and why I'd like to see "consensus" eliminated as the word we use and set the bar at 66%. The reason for these latter positions is that we are a tertiary source of information. Encyclopedias are inherently conservative and don't have content that isn't derived from people who derive the information from scholars who derive it from primary sources. If our voters are proper voters and split over an article, we can't just say that article is fine for failing to reach some lofty standard of unanimity of votes. Similarly, a vote that has the community in knots is probably not so well established and clear to be well placed in an encyclopedia.
This isn't just philosophy, though. Folks have taken "OMG You deletionist troll" to heart and are now actually seeing the world only in terms of "absolute nonsense, which can be deleted, and everything else, which must not be deleted, no matter what." They're working on Everything2, IMO, and not any sort of encyclopedia. Geogre 12:23, 25 August 2005 (UTC)
