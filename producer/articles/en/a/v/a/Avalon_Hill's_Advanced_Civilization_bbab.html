Avalon Hill's Advanced Civilization is a computer edition of the Civilization board game that includes the original game and the expansion to that game called Advanced Civilization.

It was published in 1995, shortly before Avalon Hill was bought out by Hasbro in 1998. It is now listed as abandonware and can be downloaded for free at Abandonia.

This game came out about the same time that Sid Meier's Civilization II was released and did not do as well in the market.

Both the board and computer game portray the same basic concept: the players manage ancient Mediterranean civilizations in an effort to move them from the Early Bronze Age to beyond the Iron Age. The game itself is a complex and ingenious combination of strategy, empire management, negotiation, and trading.

[edit] Game play and strategy

The game play involves a map of the Mediterranean Sea and surrounding lands, where each player controls one civilization such as Africa, Thrace, or Crete. Players move population units across the board, taking over territory and competing with your opponents for the most sought after land. Well thought-out movement can allow your units to drive other players off certain land, or allow you to place your units strategically for a future foray.

Once your population reaches a sufficient size, you can congregate the units into cities. These cities can later be attacked for valuable plunder and to deal a blow to your opponents' chances of winning. Played well, you must move your population in order to attack your opponents' cities while defending your own.

Building cities is very important, as they allow your civilization to produce commodities such as iron, salt, wine, bronze, or spices. The more cities you have, the more commodites your civilization produces and the more valuable types of commodities you produce. Hidden within each type of commodity is also a calamity, such as earthquake, famine, Barbarian hordes, or civil war, each of which seriously damage your population and destroy your cities. Such is the risk of collecting valuable commodities.

Once your civilization starts collecting commodites, you can trade them with your opponents in order to corner the market on a particular commodity and increase its value. By creating sets of two or more of the same commodity (e.g. 4 units of salt) the value of the commodity increases exponentially. The trading works by offering your opponents three commodity cards in exchange for a3. You must tell your opponent truthfully what two of them are . As long as two of the cards are exactly what is stated the remaining can be a "bluff". The bluff can be a low-value commodity, or you can trick your opponent into taking a calamity off your hands. Of course, that 'silver' your opponent offers you just might be the feared epidemic, and you have to weigh the potential gains and losses before accepting.

Once you collect enough commodities, your civilization spends them to purchase tools, which range from pottery or astronomy to democracy or monotheism. By acquiring these tools, players' civilizations advance, and these tools also give each civilization particular advantages during the game play. For example, engineering makes your cities stronger from enemy attack and gives you an advantage in attacking other cities.

By collecting a large number of tools and maintaining a good number of cities on the board, your civilization advances through the ages, and with enough skill in strategy and trading, eventually your civilization advances far enough that you are the winner of the game.

[edit] Variants

The original game is for 2 to 7 players, but a "Western Expansion" map, which adds on Gaul, the Iberian Peninsula, northwest Africa, and southern Britain, creates a larger playing area and can allow up to 8 players at once. The game is fairly complex, and takes 4-8 hours to play, depending on the number of players.
