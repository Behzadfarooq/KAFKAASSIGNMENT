Ava Lowery is a 16-year-old homeschooled student [1] and peace activist from Alabama who has created over 70 Flash-based animations critical of the Iraq War, President Bush, policies of the Republican Party and several individual Republican politicians. She has actively demonstrated against the war, while in support of soldiers.[2]

Lowery is also the creator of the website Peace Takes Courage, and she and her work have received national media attention in 2005 and 2006, including interviews on CNN, MSNBC and Fox News. She was the object of conservative criticism in the Blogosphere and is reported to have received anonymous death threats [3] after her creation of an online animation, "WWJD" (which stands for "What Would Jesus Do?"). [4] This flash-based animation displays photographs of wounded and bloodied Iraqi children while a child sings "Jesus Loves Me."

Anti-war activist Cindy Sheehan, founder of the Gold Star Families for Peace, has praised Ava and her work and wrote an article in support of her "new friend... (who) is disgusted with the war and with the Bush regime."

Sheehan also said of Lowery:

“ Ava is one tough and compassionate cookie and she needs our support and love...our little sister in peace. Her type of behavior needs to be encouraged, emulated and rewarded and I can guarantee you, she will be one of the first recipients of a Camp Casey Peace Prize for young activists. ”
“ I honor Ava and I am proud to be her friend. She is a true American who wants to grow up in a country that is honorable and just. This is her right and she is properly claiming it. I hope she inspires you to do the same.[5] ”

In July of 2006 Lowery was awarded the BuzzFlash "Wings of Justice" award. [6] She is also a person of interest in the blog world, and was a featured speaker at the 2006 YearlyKos Convention.[7]

Ms. Lowery conceived and organized the "16 Candles for Soldiers" event. This rally in support of the troops was held on the steps of the Alabama state capital in Montgomery on October 21, 2006.[2]

Ava and her activism were profiled at length in the Jan. 28, 2007 edition of the Mobile Press-Register.[8]

[edit] Praise and criticism

Ms. Lowery's appearance of the 2006 Kos Convention was described by Adam Cohen of the New York Times, who noted that in spite of the many presidential candidates who addressed the crowd:

“ Perhaps the most notable presentation came from Ava Lowery, a 15-year-old from rural Alabama, whose homemade video was shown at the convention on jumbo television screens.

Ms. Lowery's video... contrasted the "liars" and "leakers" in the Bush administration with "those of us who choose to stand up for truth and justice." Her handiwork... is a bit over the top. But it shows that a 15-year-old with video software and Internet access can now create and disseminate a professional-quality political ad.[7]

 ”

Among several of the issues raised by CNN's Carol Lin in the BlogBuzz interview, Lin asked Ava:

“ These bloggers played your clips on the jumbo screen at their big annual convention -- I mean in some ways do you feel you were being used by them by them -- because would they have done that if you were a 25 year old or 35 year old?[4] ”

And in regards to 'WWJD':

“ Ava, taking a Christian hymn and putting it to pictures of suffering Iraqi children -- what is it you're hoping to accomplish -- because the disparity between the two -- it almost comes across as flip?"[4] ”
