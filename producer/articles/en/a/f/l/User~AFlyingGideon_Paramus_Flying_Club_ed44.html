The Paramus Flying Club (PFC) is a non-profit, owner-operated flying club. It has aircraft located at Caldwell and Linden Airports in northern New Jersey. It is the oldest such club still active in the northeastern United States, and one of the oldest in the entire country.

It currently has a fixed 45 members and 4 aircraft: 2 180HP Cessna 172 Superhawks, a Cessna 182 Skylane, and a Cessna 182 Skylane RG (retractible).

[edit] History

The club was formed in 1955 at Teterboro Airport. At that time, this airport was a lonely aerodrome in the middle of nowhere. It has since evolved to handle a huge volume of non-scheduled commercial and private jet traffic. It was this evolution that eventually caused the PFC to relocate to its current airports.

In 2006, the PFC became the only club to receive the Fred L. Wehran Award from the Aviation Hall of Fame and Museum of New Jersey for its contributions to aviation in New Jersey

[edit] Operation

The club operates completely upon the volunteer efforts of its members. It is organized into various working groups:

  • Financial
  • Maintenance
  • Administrative

with numerous volunteer positions in each group.

[edit] Finances

A key reason for the club's persistence, while many other aviation clubs have failed, is the care taken in the club's finances. For example, reserves are accumulated to all reasonably predictable future events. The cost of each hour of flight time includes monies paid into reserves for engine overhaul, repainting the exterior, and upgrading the interior. Thus, when the time arrives for any of these events, the funding is already in place.

[edit] Maintenance

A maintenance chief is responsible for the care of the entire fleet. Each aircraft also has a plane captain and lieutenant. This shares the workload over multiple people, and also offers opportunities to bring new people up to speed as the membership evolves.

Because the club's aircraft are owner operated, no 100 hour inspection is required by the FAA. However, the 50 hour oil changes have expanded over the years into almost that complete inspection. Along with compression checks, oil analysis, review of engine monitor data, the maintenance staff keeps close track of the state of each aircraft.

[edit] Training

While the PFC is not a school, and no formal courses are offered, there is a significant emphasis placed upon ongoing training.

The club maintains a diverse library of training material that is available to all members. The general meetings, held monthly, often involve training in at least some area of aircraft operation. Examples have included a presentation by Garmin in the use of their GPS, discussions of errors made during members' flights and flight operations in the Washington DC ADIZ.
