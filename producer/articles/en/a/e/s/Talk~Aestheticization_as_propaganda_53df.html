This is one of the most POV entries I've ever seen, especially down at the bottom where we equate "Shock and Awe" with doublespeak and goodthink. It's perfectly possible to describe this phenomenon without claiming that politicians merely use violence because of a lack of imagination, or claiming that people's natural aversion to violence is a "natural" "moral objection" that is artificially overcome, since arguably both aversion to violence *and* propensity toward violence are behavior patterns constructed by the society we live in.

You miss my point. The POV that comes across here is that aestheticization is always an evil, when there are large chunks of our society that would claim it to be if not a necessary evil perhaps a positive good. At the very least it's stretching it to say that a depiction of violence as horrifying and disgusting is depicting the "reality" of violence while a depiction of violence as glorious and exciting is "distracting" from the reality of violence -- both can be considered aspects of what violence is really like, and which one is more real is a matter of *point of view*.
An NPOV approach would be to look at the motivations people have for presenting violence as aesthetically desirable from more than one point of view, rather than constructing the situation simply as one of unscrupulous and lazy politicians manipulating the public.

I have redrafted to cover your particular point. I hope you feel that it is an improvement and that the page no longer assumes that the use of violence is always "evil". But we do have a minor disagreement. This topic is not about the motivations people may have for aestheticizing. It is about the process of aestheticizing. The perceptual problem arises once one gives an example because that immediately establishes a broader context. Thus, even though the example is given purely to demonstrate the methods, there is a temptation to gloss the analysis with consideration of other issues (hence, I am not discussing the causus belli, but simply three words). Suppose, for example, that I switched the example to one of the statements made by People for the Ethical Treatment of Animals. Whatever example I pick, the accusation can be made that it is serving a political agenda, whether for or against the cause adopted by the propagandist whereas my interest would simply be in deconstructing the particular phrase and pointing out how this operates to change the value of the words used. And there you see the problem. I have accused PETA of being a propagandist which has a pejorative connotation that many people might disagree with. It is a no-win situation. So should I retreat into history where the audience may be able to take a more dispassionate view of the material, or risk annoying people by appearing to criticise one of the implicit POVs? --David91 08:28, 21 Jun 2005 (UTC)
