Modern Times: A History of the World from the 1920s to the 1980s is a book by British journalist and writer Paul Johnson, which attempts to be an outline of world history during the twentieth century from the author´s perspective. The books consists of the following chapters:

1. A Relativist World 2. The First Despotic Utopias 3. Waiting for Hitler 4. Legitimacy in Decadence 5. An Infernal Theocracy, a Celestial Chaos 6. The Last Arcadia 7. Degringolade 8. The Devils 9. The High Noon of Agression 10.The End of Old Europe 11.The Watershed Years 12.Superpower and Genocide 13.Peace by Terror 14.The Bandung Generation 15.Caliban´s Kingdoms 16.Experimenting with Half Mankind 17.The European Lazarus 18.America´s Suicide Attempt 19.The Collectivist Seventies 20.The Recovery of Freedom

Description of the book

The book describes world history beginning with the aftermath of the First World War and ending with the collapse of Communism in most of Eastern Europe (however the book does not mention the collapse of the Soviet Union itself, since it was first published in 1983, and later the second edition appeared in 1991, a few months before the final Soviet downfall). As it is implicit in the span of time the in its title, the book deals mainly with the shaping of the Soviet Union in the first post-WWI decades, the collapse of democracy in Central Europe because of the rise of Fascism and National Socialism, the causes that led to World War Two and of course its development and outcome. It devotes a chapter (an Infernal Theocracy, a Celestial Chaos) to the development of Imperial Japan and the chaotic situation of China before its confrontation with its powerful neighbour due to the paramountcy of numerous warlords. Roughly the second half of the book deals with the post-WWII events: Cold War, the end of colonialism and the simultaneous birth of the Third World concept, the rise of the People´s Republic of China and of independent India (in Experimenting with half Mankind), the reconstruction and economic boom in post-war Europe, and the rise of the East Asian Tigers.

The Author´s Worldview

Modern Times, as most of Johnson´s works, is characterized by an elloquent prose, and a great narrative style. Johnson is a conservative Catholic, and as such, whenever he discusses secular (especially leftist) ideologies, he does so in a very hostile manner. Through out most of this book, in fact, he makes either subtle or strong criticism of all forms of radical political reform (which he usually calls "experiments in social engineering"), and several of its chapters are devoted to making strong criticism of extremist politicians (nazis and fascists included, but particularly the left-wing extremists, i.e figures such as Lenin, Stalin, Mao Tse-tung,etc, whom the author deems almost as vicious asHitler, if not worse). Modern Times is an enlightening piece of work, filled with a lot of facts and information. It can not be considered, however, as an unbiased account of the twentieth century. The author is pretty clear about the fact that he upholds the view that most of the evils of the twentieth century are a consequence of the replacement (in the Western World, of course) of the traditional Judeo-Christian values, for secular ideologies, whose influence he deems disastrous (Nazism, and especially Communism). Johnson is also a strong supporter of free market Capitalism, which explains his hostility towards Communism.He also portrays all famous Third World politicians, even the most revered like Gandhi,and Nehru (presumably for his friendlyness towards Socialism)as frivolous characters, in the best case, or as outright murderers and gangsters (Mao, Sukarno), in the worst. His work can be interestingly compared with a similar book by British historian, Eric Hobsbawm, "The Age of Extremes", which covers pretty much the same period of time, but discusses it under a very different (leftist) view.
