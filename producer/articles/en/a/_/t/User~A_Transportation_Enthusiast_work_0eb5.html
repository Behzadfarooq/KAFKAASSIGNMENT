[edit] PRT Capacity section

PRT is often compared to light rail and buses, so it is useful to consider system capacity relative to those systems. However, the complexity of a typical PRT network design makes it difficult to compare system capacity with line-based systems like buses and trains. This difficulty is compounded by the fact that most PRT system capacity calculations are based on analysis and simulation, which are viewed with skepticism by opponents.

A simpler measure of transit capacity is the maximum theoretical capacity of a single point on the track or guideway, measured in the number of passengers passing that point per hour. This can be calculated as:

maximum capacity = (maximum passengers per vehicle) * (maximum vehicles per hour)

Maximum passengers per vehicle is based on the vehicle design. Maximum vehicles per hour is calulated directly from the headway (nose-to-nose vehicle spacing, in seconds):

maximum vehicles per hour = 3600/headway

So maximum capacity can be written as:

maximum capacity = 3600 * (maximum passengers per vehicle) / headway

Trains achieve desired capacities mainly by maximizing passengers per vehicle. PRT, on the other hand, is based on very small vehicles, and therefore must minimize headway to achieve high capacities.

The minimum acceptable headway is the source heated debate. Systems have been extensively tested and received regulatory approval at 2 second headway (ULTra, Cabintaxi). Others (Cabintaxi, CVS) have been tested at 1 second headway, but did not receive regulatory approval. At least one system (Taxi 2000) has been engineered and simulated at 0.5 second headway, but has never undergone a full scale system test. There is some evidence that sub-second headways would be workable in an automated system: automobiles have been tested running at 0.25 second headway as part of an Automated Highway System (AHS) platooning demonstration.

Most current PRT designs target 0.5 to 2 second headways, although all proposals to date have assumed an initial headway of 2 seconds because of regulatory concerns. Proponents believe that regulatory agencies will be more accepting of shorter headways with more testing and operational experience.

Maximum capacity of PRT is further complicated by the fact that, unlike trains and buses, passengers would be unlikely to pack into PRT vehicles, because PRT vehicles travel point-to-point. Therefore, most analyses assume a smaller number for PRT passengers per vehicle. For commuter traffic, where most passengers travel alone, vehicles may carry an average of only 1.2 passengers. For a sporting event, where groups would be more common, the average would likely be higher.
