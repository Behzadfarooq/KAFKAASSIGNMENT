Rationale for retaining the 'Spin Off Publications' section

Certainly ACIM is a book that has no equal for many people. In that sense, some could reasonably say that there could be no spin-off books from it. Still, one cannot deny that several subsequent books have been written by students of ACIM that are intended to be primarily based on the teachings of ACIM, and also that some of these types of books have been extremely successful and popular. While the study of any of these books alone might be likened to trying to study the Bible by only reading commentaries on it, still these ACIM commentaries and other ACIM based books have clearly had an impact on how many see ACIM. It seems to me that to exclude this section on Spin Off Publications only forces anyone who would seek to study commentaries on ACIM and other ACIM based books to have to look outside of Wikipedia for a list of the most popular amongst these publications. Perhaps there is a better term, other than the title 'Spin Off Publications' that could be used for this section. Any suggestions?

Looking forward to any comments on the value of (or potential problems with) restoring this section, and/ or suggestions on a better name for this section.

Regarding symbolism and linguistics

I thought I might add something else to this discussion. I recently read a book called "Mathematics: Sience of Patterns." [3] This book said something that, after years of "not getting" math, helped me understand what it's all about. This book explained that the symbols used in mathematics -- "1" "2" "3" "=" "+" "-" etc. -- are completely constructs of the human mind. These symbols are very important to us. In many ways, they run our lives. But this book says that these symbols exist only in the human mind and nowhere in the physical world. Look out your window and see if you can find a big "5" staring you in the face. You won't. But you might find five geese flying overheard. That is, you might find the content of what "5" symbolizes; though you won't find the form of "5" out there. (The geese you see most likely have no concept of Arabic numerals and therefore most likely do not perceive themselves to be flying in a formation of five. It most likely was not their intention to form a group of five. They most likely cannot understand the concept. The symbols don't matter to them. But that doesn't change the property of "five-ness" in their formation.)

This book said that often this linguistic aspect of mathematics is overlooked and not taught in schools. Rather, what is focused on in schools is the “computational” and “procedural” aspect of math. In other words, math teachers say to the student, “This is what happens when you do this and that to these numbers….” The teachers don’t go further to explain, by the way, these numbers are only symbols and they themselves do not exist in the world out there. These symbols are constructs of the human mind.

As I had been corresponding with Noam Chomsky for some time by the point at which I read this book [4], and as Noam Chomsky is mentioned in this book, I wrote to Noam Chomsky and asked him his thoughts on this topic.

Chomsky told me that it is completely uncontroversial among mathematicians to say that the symbols used in math are man-made. However, he said that the laws expressed by these symbols are very real. It’s hard for me to explain, but basically the law that governs that 2 + 2 = 4 would govern the same even if we used different symbols to express this. The symbols are man-made; the laws they express are not.

I think that this is the same with “Jesus” in the Course. “Jesus” is a symbol that is completely man-made and, like numbers, is symbolic. But what “Jesus” stands for, love, is not. I don’t think we need to argue that love is real, because just about all of us have felt it at some point.

By the way, I asked Noam Chomsky why this linguistic aspect of math is not taught in schools. He told me that it is indeed taught in schools, but in the universities at advanced levels. The ripples of that thought of separation have spread out, creating a universe of myriad forms of separation. But God still is, as always. Outside of time was God’s instant answer to the thought of separation—forgiveness. Within time, the answer must be learned. Forgiveness is the recognition, the awakening if you will, to the reality that the separation never occurred in eternal reality. Forgiveness of another being is the recognition that whatever one perceives they or another may have done in time has had no effect upon eternal reality. All remain as God created—that is God’s will. ACIM teaches that forgiveness is the recognition (remembering) of God within others and than seeing that reflection in ourself and is in essence following God’s will of oneness.

Religious experience, formal religion and ACIM

Dear Speet,

I have not been able to find any reference in ACIM that the study of ACIM cannot provide one with a "religious experience", but I have been able to find many references in ACIM which make it clear that ACIM was not meant to be another "formal religion". Unfortunately, it seems that many people are more concerned with the formal aspects of religion than with the "religious experiences" that any given teaching may or may not provide one with.

The word: religion is derived from the Latin word: "religare" which means to reunite, bind or tie, presumably to reunite with God.

I agree with Wapnick that ACIM is not meant to be another "religion", but I do also believe that ACIM can provide one with a "religious experience". ACIM states that "Religion is experience; psychotherapy is experience. At the highest levels they become one." In that sense, it seems to me that it should be safe to call ACIM a "belief system that has many religious overtones, but that is not meant to be a formal religion in and of itself".

Some might say that Christ's teachings may have originally been intended to establish such a belief system. When judging by Christ's Gospel insistence that no one follower should be given any special authority over any other follower, it might seem that Christ may have originally intended to set up a very informal type of belief system himself, without any heirarchy. Please note that it was only after Christ's resurrection and physical departure that such a heirarchy later evolved under Paul as described in the Epistles.

Accordingly, it would seem to me that so long as it is clearly stated that while ACIM is a religious belief system, still it was not intended to be the basis for a new formal religion, that it should then be safe to count ACIM as a Christian teaching, or as a teaching whose students generally believe to be a teaching of Christ. Whether or not those who would detract from ACIM might consider ACIM to be a bonafide teaching of Christ seems to me not to be the question. The primary question here seems to me to be whether or not the majority of those who ascribe to this belief system consider these teachings to be from Christ, or Christian, which, to the best of my knowledge, they do. Perhaps the differences of opinion on this point could be "written around" in the article by simply stating something like "a majority of students of ACIM consider themselves to be Christian in some sense, and for this reason the ACIM teachings on forgiveness have been included within the Christian section of this article. (For more information about the relationship between ACIM and Christianity, please see Comparison of ACIM to Christianity)."

Personally it would seem a bit strange to me if you did not include the ACIM teachings on forgiveness under the Christian section of the article, as it would seem to me that by this you could somehow be implying that ACIM might in some way be un-Christian.
