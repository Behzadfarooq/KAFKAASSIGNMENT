A and Others v. Secretary of State for the Home Department

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

A and others v. Secretary of State for the Home Department, [2005] UKHL 71 was a unanimous decision of the House of Lords where it was held that evidence obtained by the government through torture is inadmissible in court. The decision overturned a Court of Appeal decision from 2002 where they held that such evidence could be used so long as it was obtained by those who were not agents of the British state. The ruling also overturned section 23 of the Anti-terrorism, Crime and Security Act 2001.[1]

[edit] Facts of the case

The case began with 10 men who challenged a decision of the Special Immigration Appeals Commission to eject them from the country on the basis that there was evidence of them being a threat to national security.

Of the 10 appellants, all except 2 were detained in December 2001; the others were detained in February and April 2002 respectively. All were detained under the Anti-terrorism, Crime and Security Act 2001.[2] Under section 25 of this Act, they had the legal right to appeal to the Special Immigration Appeals Commission against their detention.[3] The basis of their case was that the evidence against them had been obtained in foreign countries through the use of torture; they argued that, under the English common law, the use of evidence obtained through torture was unlawful.[4]

[edit] The ruling

The ruling, which was unanimous, held that the detention of the 10 appellants was illegal, for a number of reasons. Firstly, they held that the detention discriminated against non-British nationals and that the detention was "disproportionate and discriminatory", making it illegal under the European Convention on Human Rights.[5]
