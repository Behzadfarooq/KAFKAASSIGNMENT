A Jihad for Love, a documentary still in progress, is the world's first documentary film on the coexistence of Islam and homosexuality.[1] The film is directed by Parvez Sharma, produced by Sandi DuBowski (Trembling Before G-d).

[edit] Production

It is produced by Halal Films, in association with Channel 4 Television (UK), ZDF (Germany), Arte (France), MTV-Logo (US) and SBS (Australia).

The documentary was filmed in 12 different countries and in nine languages.[2] Sharma conducted interviews throughout North America, Europe, Asia and the Middle East. Countries included Saudi Arabia, Iran, Iraq, Pakistan, Egypt, Bangladesh, Turkey, France, India, South Africa, the United States and the United Kingdom.[2] He found many of his interviewees online, and received thousands of emails. [3]

It is projected to premiere in early 2007.[2]

[edit] Significance of the title

This film now has a finalized title: Jihad for Love, as of March 2007, but was originally called In the Name of Allah.[4] Among Muslims, the phrase (bismillah in Arabic) may be used before beginning actions, speech, or writing. Its most notable use in Al-Fatiha, the opening passage of the Qur'an, which begins "Bismillah ar-Rahman, ar-Rahim." All following surat of the Qur'an, with the exception of the ninth, begin with these words.

DuBowski's previous film, Trembling Before G-d, on Orthodox and Hasidic Jews, also included the name of God, written with a hyphen as in Jewish tradition. Allah is Arabic for God, and the Arabic word is often used among English-speaking Muslims.

[edit] Controversy and problems

Sharma's making of the film has not been without criticism.

“ About every two weeks I get an e-mail that berates me, condemns me to hell and, if they are nice, asks me to still seek forgiveness while there is still time.[3] ”

Another issue Sharma has been balancing Sharma's objective to portray homosexuality without shame, and the need to protect the safety of his sources, by filming them as silhouettes or with their faces covered. In one case, the family of an Afghan woman he interviewed "would undoubtedly kill her" if they found out she was lesbian. In another example, one of the associate producer, an Egyptian gay man, chose not to be listed in the credits for fear of possible consequences. [3]
