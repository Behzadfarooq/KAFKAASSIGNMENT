Here's where you seem to be getting your confusion from. The actual facts themselves cannot be copyrighted; but a particular expression of facts can. You can express facts in your own words without violating my copyright, but you cannot copy the words I used to express those facts; that particular phrasing is considered my work, to which I have copyright. Contrary to your claim that "it would require [a copyright holder] to actually file for copyright", under the Berne Convention it is actually already copyrighted from the moment it is in fixed form.
If you do not believe this, then consider this: the song "Yankee Doodle" is in the public domain. If Eric Clapton records a cover of "Yankee Doodle", is his playing of that song automatically in the public domain, just because the song is? No -- he may have started with the 'raw material' of a song in the public domain, but he added his own work, in the form of how he chose to play that song; the work he added is why the result is not in the public domain but rather under Clapton's copyright. In just the same way, the actual facts are the raw material, but the choices made about how to express those facts represent someone else's work, and that is what makes that expression of the facts copyrightable.
