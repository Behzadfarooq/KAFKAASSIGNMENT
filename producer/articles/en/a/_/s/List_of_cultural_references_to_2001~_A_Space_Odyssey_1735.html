[edit] Film

  • The album cover of the soundtrack to 2001: A Space Odyssey is clearly visible in the record-shop scene of A Clockwork Orange, another film directed by Stanley Kubrick.
  • In Eyes Wide Shut (the final film by Stanley Kubrick), an advertisement on the side of a building seen in the exterior New York sequences reads "BOWMAN". Also, Dr. Harford cancels an appointment with a patient named Kaminsky -- the name of one of the hibernating astronauts aboard Discovery One in 2001.
  • In the final scene in the movie Speed when the subway train burst out onto the street, the signage of a theater in the background indicates it is showing "2001: A Space Odyssey".
  • In Frank Zappa's 200 Motels, the monolith from the film can clearly be seen several times in a few different scenes. The most notable is when Motorhead's vacuum cleaner is going out of control before the finale, and there are several small explosions near the monolith.
  • George Lucas, known for creating homages to earlier films in his own films, stated in his DVD commentary to his most recent film Star Wars Episode III: Revenge of the Sith, that he made a homage to the 2001 Clavius Base. It is when the establishing shot of Polis Massa is seen. Polis Massa was specifically designed to be an asteroid base that looked very similar to the Clavius Base seen in 2001. In fact, two people in space suits are shown in Revenge of the Sith in similar positions on the asteroid as the scene in 2001. The opening shot of Star Wars Episode IV: A New Hope may have been not so much an affectionate "homage" to 2001 as a copy of Kubrick's striking visual of a gigantic spaceship moving overhead; others that much the same thing occurred with the hangar bay of the Death Star, which is visually quite similar to the Space Station's docking bay. Lucas did the same in Star Wars Episode I: The Phantom Menace where an object in Watto's salvage yard was the pod used on the Discovery in the film 2010.
  • A good portion of Star Trek: The Motion Picture is inspired by 2001, including:
    • Opening overture over a black screen (replaced by an animated star field in the director's edition)
    • The slow entry of the new Enterprise without any dialogue is similar to the first shots of the space stations in 2001.
    • The Enterprise's journey through the V'Ger cloud is very similar to some of the stargate sequence.
    • Mr. Spock's "V'ger Spacewalk" contains similar shots, camera angles and lighting effects to that of Bowman's stargate voyage.
    • When Mr. Spock floats back to the Enterprise after the mind-meld, the camera angle used is similar to that used in 2001 when Bowman catches Poole with the pod claws.
  • In Star Trek: First Contact it is revealed that the magnetic-constrictor-locks that hold the ship's deflector dish in place are designated "AE-35" units after the device in 2001.
  • Airplane II: The Sequel spoofs many parts of 2001 including a computer named "ROK" that tries to kill the crew. Several lines of dialogue are also taken from 2001.
  • History of the World, Part I, Mel Brooks' satire of the history of humanity, begins with a scene similar to 2001, except that the ape-men are more self-absorbed. The movie Simon, starring Alan Arkin also mimicked the "ape using a bone as a weapon" sequence.
  • In Good Bye Lenin!, Denis, the friend of Alex, made a wedding movie in which there is a scene where a flying flowers bouquet changes to a cake in an explicit homage to the famous match cut in The Dawn of Man.
  • In the 1996 blockbuster movie Independence Day, as Jeff Goldblum's character David Levinson activates his laptop computer on board the alien craft, it welcomes him with a very HAL-like "Good morning, Dave" and has a HAL themed desktop wallpaper.
  • In the beginning of Shaolin Soccer, an alignment of the Sun, Moon, Earth, and a Buddhist monk's head is seen.
  • In the beginning of the movie 2001: A Space Travesty (An obvious play on the true title), there is a scene with a howling ape-man and a monolith in which the narrator says "And then God came, and He spoke," as a hand of God appears on the top of the screen. God says "Shut up, you stupid monkey." and pushes the monolith over, crushing the ape-man.
  • In the movie Catch-22, the music from the opening strains of Also sprach Zarathustra are played when Capt. John Yossarian sees an amazingly attractive Italian woman walk past. The film was made in 1970, two years after 2001.
  • In the penultimate scene of Irréversible (2002) a poster for the film with the Starchild and the tag line, "The Ultimate Trip" appears. A miniature of the space pod also appears in the background during this scene.
  • The 2005 film based on the book Charlie and the Chocolate Factory contains a lengthy sequence that includes a clip of the scene of the apes discovering the monolith sequence from the beginning of 2001 and features music used in the movie (Also Sprach Zarathustra). In spoof, the monolith is replaced by a Wonka Bar teleported though the television. The eerie, distinctive wailing music from the film is audible, and later on a recreation of the beginning of the "Moon-Watcher discovers tools" sequence is seen.
  • The film Zoolander features a segment in which two of the main characters reenact the scene in which the monolith is discovered by the ape men, with the monolith replaced by a computer. During this sequence, Richard Strauss' Also sprach Zarathustra is played.
  • The movie Clueless features a scene in which a cell phone is shown in a similar way as the monolith is, also featuring "Also Sprach Zarathustra"
  • The opening of Ken Shapiro's cult favorite The Groove Tube begins with an almost spot-on recreation of the "Dawn of Man" sequence (almost...one of the apes is playing solitaire with animal-skin "playing cards"). The monolith is revealed to be a 1960s-vintage console TV set, "Move On Up" by Curtis Mayfield replaces the Ligeti-style chorale on the soundtrack, one of the apes invents music by banging a bone on the ground, and another accidentally rubs two sticks together and discovers fire!
  • Woody Allen's Sleeper contains a scene which spoofs HAL in which the evil computer voice is that of Douglas Rain.
  • One of the first scenes of the film Werner: Beinhart contains a parody on the "Dawn of Man" sequence, instead of using the bone as a tool, the apes find a bottle of beer.
  • A short, light-hearted stop-motion animation spoof of the behavior of some of the film's characters (as well as some slapstick) has been created using lego blocks and figures, called ONE: A Space Odyssey [1].
  • In the cult 1976 Nicolas Roeg film The Man Who Fell to Earth, Newton (David Bowie) knocks a glass off the table, in last moments of the film, just prior to his apparent complete resignation to his fate. Compare with glass knocked off table by Bowman in 2001 just prior to rebirth as star child.
  • The 2006 film Happy Feet features a penguin with a voice similar to that of HAL telling Mumble, "This is heaven, Dave...It's wherever you want it to be."
  • The opening sequence of the 1999 sequel Toy Story 2 shows Buzz Lightyear stepping on magnetically suspended bottlecap-like structures as he approaches "The Source of Zurg's Power" (a double-A battery). With each step, one of the chords of Also Sprach Zarathusatra is played... before the magnetic field is disabled and he drops like a stone.

[edit] Television

[edit] The Simpsons

Homer Simpson as the Star Child.
  • Deep Space Homer contains many references to 2001, not the least of which the famous potato chip-eating scene and the portrayal of Homer as the 2001 Star Child at the close of the episode. In this episode there is also an Itchy & Scratchy episode entiltled Scar Trek: The next Laceration in which Itchy rides in a space pod that looks exactly like the ones in 2001.
  • Brother, Can You Spare Two Dimes? the stargate sequence was parodied in this episode when Homer tests a massage chair.
  • Maximum Homerdrive where Homer is driving a truck with a built-in device that would normally get him out of trouble. However when he was approaching the Convoy it said, "I'm afraid I can't let you do this, Red!" Instead of trying to stop Homer, however, the box simply jettisoned itself out the truck's sunroof.
  • In the segment House of Whacks in the Treehouse of Horror XII episode the Simpson house is fitted with a HAL-like computer (red fish-eye lens included), voiced by Pierce Brosnan, that falls in love with Marge and tries to murder Homer, a la Demon Seed.
  • Lisa's Pony, starts off with a Dawn of Man sequence in which there are a bunch of ape-men, one looking like Homer. The ape-men see the monolith and then it cuts to one monkey making a tool, the next one starting a fire, and a third making a wheel. The Homer ape, however, leans against the monolith and falls asleep.
  • The Simpsons Spin-Off Showcase shows the Lovematic-Grandpa machine sings Daisy Daisy in a distorted manner when its electrical circuits are failing, as HAL does.
  • Bart's Dog Gets an F, the way Marge's needle spins in the air, same for the bone and pen.
  • $pringfield, when Mr. Burns lying on the bed with Victorian furniture and lit-from-underneath bright white floor is the same as Dave Bowman. (The name of the episode is itself a reference to another Stanley Kubrick movie, Dr. Strangelove)
  • Simpsons Bible Stories shows Marge as Eve making tools in a similar way as seen in the Dawn of Man sequence.

[edit] Futurama

  • Episode 4ACV03 Love and Rocket, the Planet Express Ship's computer is identical to HAL 9000. Bender Sings "Daisy Bell' to the Planet Express Ship. Later on, Fry, Leela and Bender meet in the shower in order to plot against the Planet Express ship. While they talk, we see the ship's POV as she looks at their lips. In a humorous twist however, she says, "If only I could read lips". Leela then deactivates the ship's brain in a manner similar to Dave Bowman's deactivation of HAL.
  • Episode 3ACV11 Insane in the Mainframe, shows the "HAL Institute for criminally insane robots".
  • Episode 2ACV07 Put Your Head on My Shoulders, Fry and Amy go to Jupiter, and as they approach they fly by the monolith with the sign "Out of order" attached to it.
  • Episode 3ACV20 Godfellas, shows Bender floating in space to Also Sprach Zarathustra and to Blue Danube Waltz.
  • Episode 4ACV12 The Sting, shows Leela floating to a coffin representing a monolith in alignment with several planets while "Also Sprach Zarathustra" by Richard Strauss is playing. When she opens the coffin, it references the beginning of the "Star Gate sequence" then abruptly she awakes in her bed.
  • Episode 2ACV09 A Bicyclops Built for Two, as Fry enters the internet, Also sprach Zarathustra is played and moments later, he references Dave Bowman's line "My God, it's full of stars!" from the novel by saying My God, it's full of ads! The episode title is also a spoof of the lyric a Bicycle Built for Two from the song Daisy Bell which is sung by HAL in the film.
  • Episode 3ACV21 Future Stock, the stock exchange space station bears a striking similarity to Space Station Five.

[edit] Mystery Science Theater 3000

  • During episode #512, "Mitchell" (the last to star Joel Hodgson), Gypsy learns of the Mad’s' plot to kill Joel similarly to how HAL learns of the plan to disconnect him, in a clone of the "lip-reading" scene from 2001 (albeit with audible dialogue).
  • The theatrical film opens with a sequence that mocks the scene of Frank Poole jogging through Discovery's carousel; Mike Nelson is shown similarly jogging inside a hamster wheel-like device covered with the same pattern of floor tile as the centrifuge, and afterward he drinks from a giant water bottle.
  • A running-gag on the show occurs whenever the films they watch cut to black (for seemingly too long) someone will quip, "18 months later."
  • And finally, episode #706, "Laserblast" (the final Comedy Central-era episode), ends with Dr. Forrester living through the "transcendental hotel room" sequence from the end of 2001; the Monolith is replaced in this scene by a giant videocassette labeled "The Worst Movie Ever Made" (presumably not a comment on 2001 itself).

[edit] South Park

  • Episode #49 The Tooth Fairy Tats 2000 : Kyle looks for reality when he realizes that everything his parents have ever told him is a lie. He has a breakdown in which he no longer knows what is real and not, where he disappears and enters space as the "Star Child" fetus with a Primus song playing in the background.
  • Episode #413 Trapper Keeper: Kyle is sent to the "core" of the Cartman-Trapper Keeper who is similar to the one of the Discovery; when the boy tries to deactivate the computer, the voice of Cartman can be heard, saying: "I'm afraid I can't let you do that, Kyle", similar to HAL's voice and speech.
  • Episode #610 Bebe's Boobs Destroy Society: The boys are fighting each others in Cartman's basement like Prehistoric men (due to the impact of Bebe's boobs over the boys); Stan finds a bone that he uses to win the fight, spoofing the use of the bone in 2001's "The Dawn of Man".

[edit] Other TV

  • In an episode of Mo~tto! Ojamajo Doremi, Doremi says, "Since it's 2001, isn't something going to happen in space?", referring to the title of the movie.
  • In the HBO drama Six Feet Under, in series one the main character, Nathaniel, would often mimic the calm voice of HAL when talking to his brother David - shortened to Dave.
  • The original opening to ABC-TV's 1969 Movie Of The Week used a mirrored effect optical which resembled the Stargate scene near the end of 2001.
  • The episode of The Mask shows a little spoof of HAL 9000 called AL 1000.
  • A truncated version of the hotel room from 2001 can also be seen at the end of the Farscape miniseries The Peacekeeper Wars when Harvey (mimiking the Bowman deathbed scene) reaches out to John telling him that he has to leave him.
  • In the English version of the anime series "Digimon Frontier", a Trailmon was voiced in style of Douglas Rain, the voice of HAL. This is inspired by the Trailmon, since he had HAL-equse red cameras for eyes.
  • In an episode of SpongeBob SquarePants which portrays SpongeBob and his associates as cavemen discovering fire, the prehistoric version of SpongeBob notices a stick and thinks of a way to spear objects on it and hold it over the fire, rather than using his hands. A lengthy scene in which he accumulates the thought of this ensues, while Ligeti's Kyrie choir plays, as it did when the ape-men first noticed the monolith.
  • In the Jimmy Neutron episode "Men at Work" Jimmy, the consummate inventor, creates a computerized fast food restaurant that is meant to satisfy everyone's desires. It contains the same characteristic "red camera" as the HAL 9000 and also goes berserk when Jimmy's father isn't completely satisfied, destroying all competition in town.
    1. In the episode The Junkman Cometh, the Monolith can be seen on the moon.
  • The Anime series Neon Genesis Evangelion makes several allusions to the film, including: the naming of one of the central character Langley (after HAL's mentor), and the Monolith forms that SEELE uses near the end of the series.
  • Sesame Workshop made several references to 2001, particularly animated cartoon references used on The Electric Company, which featured a truncated version of "Also Sprach Zarathustra" (just the opening fanfare and repetitive kettledrum thumping) and a white version of the monolith which, during the thumping of the kettledrum, would break up and crumble away revealing a small word or a letter combination subsequently pronounced by a deep deistic voice, i.e.. "oo, "ow", "all", "alk", etc. One such cartoon, "ee", was a reference to the "Dawn of Man" sequence with a chimpanzee acting as Moon Walker and even touching the monolith like him. Another, "alk", used the stargate sequence with the deistic voice repeating "alk", "Ow" spoofed the monolith and astronaut Dave Bowman in space (he stubs his toe on the monolith right as the kettledrum solo begins and yells "OW" several times just before it crumbles down to reveal "ow"), and "all" was a vague reference to the last scene of the movie, only with elderly Dave Bowman at the table eating dinner, not in his bed. Square One Television, another Sesame Workshop production, had a live action skit spoofing HAL9000, who sings ad nauseam, "Row Row Row Your Boat" until one of the astronauts gives him an unfinishable program; This skit was designed to teach the concept of infinity. And Sesame Street, in addition to two animated cartoons using the 2001 theme and white monolith, "me" and "amor", had a whole episode spoofing the monolith and the "Also Sprach Zarathustra" theme, in which Telly Monster sees two monoliths suddenly appear on the street to the Zarathustra theme; these monoliths are actually meant to resemble the number eleven, which was that episode's featured number.
  • In an episode of Dexter's Laboratory titled "Ultra-Jerk 2000", Dexter creates a robot that is an obvious spoof of HAL9000.
  • In the Sci-fi Channel show Tripping the Rift, an entire episode is based around Darph Bobo using a monolith of his own creation to subjugate a race of simpler aliens. After he is defeated, it is replaced by a white monolith by the Star Trek Federation look-alikes. The monolith is also parodied in the opening sequence by having the back of a chair loom over the camera with a lamp in place of the sun.
  • Terry Gilliam spoofed the film in episodes of Monty Python's Flying Circus:
    • In an animation sequence an ape-man, after seeing the monolith, throws a bone into the air which then collides with an orbiting spacecraft that crashes to the ground killing the ape man.
    • In another episode, protesters at The Hague are halted by a giant constable head. Also sprach Zarathustra plays, the head lowers to reveal Jupiter, which lowers to reveal Earth, which is kicked by a soccer player, headed by others, and finally bounces into a pedestal for the title screen of a "World Forum" game show.
  • A Japanese anime Magical Shopping Arcade Abenobashi episode: While the characters explore the space shopping arcade Sasshi grabs several monoliths on display, and exclaims how these monoliths makes you smarter and begins acting like moon-watcher.
  • In an episode from Galaxy Angel in which the team visits a wrestling match, Normad, a computer chip with capabilities similar to HAL 9000, gets thrown into the ring. Later on, Normad is found in a trash can, uttering "Daisy, daisy..." in the same fashion as the HAL 9000 death scene. Another episode which includes Mint turning into a tree ends with a parody of the beginning of the movie, specifically a large black monolith creating a "super race" of pigeons.
  • An episode of Animaniacs feautred three spoofs of the movie. The cold opening of the episode portrays the Warners as apes, and while encontering a monolith, they use a TV remote to turn on the monolith like a television. At first, it features Balooney (the Animaniacs parody of Barney the Dinosaur), but the Warners almost immediately change him to the Warner Bros. logo. A short cartoon before another full cartoon shows Wacko in space, encountering another monolith. The third full cartoon is a parody of the voyage to Jupiter, featuring a talking computer named 'Al' that was revealed to be an animated Al Gore hiding in the computer. After being revealed, he said "I have the utmost confidence in this administration," parodying a line of HAL 9000's about his "mission."
  • Episode 13 of Freakazoid contains a scene that mimics Dave's journey to the hotel room at the end of the film. When Freakazoid finds himself in the room he is face-to-face with Dave and the monolith is in the background. Dave inquires "Could you tell me where the bathroom is?", referencing the only other room shown in the hotel scene in the movie.
  • An episode of the Super Mario Bros. Super Show featured a miniature pizza maker designed to look like HAL 9000, which was given to Mario and Luigi as a gift from a kooky scientist. The pizza maker works fine for a while, churning out pizzas with conventional toppings until it begins to go haywire.
  • In the June 18, 2006 episode of The Daily Show, host Jon Stewart facetiously compared the role of the gas pump in American culture to the Monolith in the film: "The pump is like the Monolith in 2001. Basically, if you're driving down the street, as you get near the pump you really start to feel it - sense of foreboding. Then when you're in credit-card-swiping range, it hits you: my God, the humanity!"
  • In the PBS series Arthur, when Arthur and Buster are in the theater, for a brief moment, the ape man throwing the bone can be seen playing on the screen.
  • In Tom and Jerry "Fast and Furry" a woman trapped in her car asks a service like OnStar how to get out of quicksand. The operator reples, "I'm sorry, I can't do that", similar to HAL refusing to open the pod bay doors.
  • The opening sequence of the 2003 miniseries pilot of Battlestar Galactica is reminiscent of Frank Poole's jogging scene aboard the Discovery. The scene begins by following pilot Kara "Starbuck" Thrace as she jogs down Galactica’s corridors. The ship has a conference room with the same table layout and chairs from Clavius Base. The Colonial One interior has the same angle to the ceiling and lighting as the Pan Am Clipper Orion space shuttle. Pan Galactic is one of the ships in the fleet. It has Pan Am's trademark blue globe logo with the words PAN GAL. Seen only briefly during the battle at Ragnar Station it was shown again in the episode Exodus, Part II.
  • The Dawn of Man - the penultimate episode of the sitcom One Foot in the Grave, first broadcast on 13 November 2000 - is named after the first segment of the film. The episode ends with Victor Meldrew attacking bone-shaped pieces of polystyrene to the strains of Also Sprach Zarathustra - a parody of the sequence where the ape-men learn to use bones as a weapon.
  • In one episode of Recess, a SAL 3000 computer (an obvious spoof on HAL) is installed at Third Street School, complete with fish-eye cameras. It subsequiently takes over the school, concluding with the main characters shutting it down. In a later episode, it is revealed that SAL "randomly" selected the school to participate in a trial program removing recess from the daily scedule. The episode itself is called "The Dawn of Man" and begins with a short sequence of tribal apes.
  • In the television series Mission Hill, the characters George, Toby and Kevin are seen watching a video of 2001: A Space Odyssey, in the season finalé 'Plan 9 From Mission Hill (or, I Married A Gay Man From Outer Space)'.
  • In episode 16 of the Japanese anime series Eureka Seven, a dream sequence occurs in a room that resembles the interior of the space station in 2001, including the floor, the ceiling, the voiceprint identification kiosks and the red "Djinn" chairs.
  • The sci-fi comedy series Red Dwarf features a main character Dave Lister and a computer named Holly, references to Dave Bowman and HAL 9000. In the episode Queeg Holly is deactivated and sings Goodbye to Love, by The Carpenters, in a very similar manner to HALs rendition of Daisy Bell. In Demons and Angels Rimmer says "Open the cargo bay doors, Holly", to which she replies "The phrase 'Cargo bay doors' does not appear to be in my lexicon."
  • In the episode "A Bird in the Pan" (Episode 5, Season1) from the show "Good Eats" Alton Brown instructs Hal to open the pod bay doors, he is talking to the about the oven door.
  • The episode name "Tool-Thousand-One: A Space Odyssey" of the Home Improvement series is an obvious reference to the title of the film.
  • In the Malcolm in the Middle episode Cliques, Hal sees a gigantic domino in a dream, an obvious reference to the final monolith sequence.
  • In an episode of the television series Weird Science, main characters Gary and Wyatt are given a self-aware supercomputer by their genie Lisa, but after some problems arise during the episode, plans are made to switch it off - a plan the computer learns of thanks to a scene which mirrors HAL's lips-reading.
  • In an episode of Disney Channel's "The Replacements," CAR refuses to allow K inside, saying "I am sorry, K. I can't do that. Daisy, Daisy. Give me your answer true..."
  • In an episode of the Fox TV children's series Big Guy and Rusty the Boy Robot, Rusty sees Lt. Hunter and Dr. Slate talking through a round window. The camera shots match the ones from the scene in which Bowman and Poole are talking about HAL. After a few moments, Rusty remarks, "I can't understand a word they're saying."
  • When Veronica Mars what asked what she did before meeting her Computer Expert; Mac, she responds by comparing it to "the first ten minutes of 2001: A Space Odyssey"

[edit] Print

  • In America (The Book), a humorous image showing what Americans would probably leave in a time capsle circa 2004. Among them is a copy of 2001: A Space Odyssey. The comment on it read "To show Americans 100 years from now what people 36 years ago thought what the world would've been like three years ago".
  • Mad Magazine did their obligatory takeoff (written by Dick DeBartolo and illustrated by Mort Drucker), titled 201: (Min. of) A Space Idiocy (the words Min. and of were printed within the colon of the title), which appeared in March 1969 (issue #125). The actual film ran 139 minutes, after the 156 minute premiere version was recut. Appearing among the ape-men in the first scene is Fred Flintstone in a furry costume. In a nod to the numerous corporate logos appearing in the movie, nearly every panel includes a brand name, and instead of going through a complicated disconnection procedure, Dave simply pulls out HAL (renamed "SID")'s power plug. The monolith is revealed to be a book titled How to Make an Incomprehensible Science Fiction Movie and Several Million Dollars, written by Stanley Kubrick.
  • A Hägar the Horrible comic strip from 1985 (when 2010: Odyssey Two was playing in theatres) shows Hagar and Lucky Eddie encountering a huge black monolith, and wondering if it might be there to show them new knowledge – then when it falls on them a moment later, wondering if it might be a trap.
  • In a January 2001 FoxTrot comic, Jason stands dressed in an ape suit in front of a large wall made of snow (similar to the monolith). He throws bones in the air several times before stating that "I thought 2001 would be more futuristic."

The first game in the Xenosaga series, Episode I begins when humanity first discovers the Zohar.
  • The PC game SimEarth has a black monolith which can improve the intelligence of the civilization.
  • The space trading computer game ELITE features a rotational docking sequence, also The Blue Danube is the ingame music soundtrack.
  • The PC game Spore will feature the ability to place a Monolith on a planet to trigger evolution and intelligent life.
  • The PS1 game Hogs of War features a Monolith on the level Pigs in space.
  • In the video game Destroy All Humans 2, when Crypto walks around Bay City, hippies could be heard saying "Open the pod bay doors, HAL," a direct quote from the film.
  • In the 1997 PSX game Monster Farm (Monster Rancher in NA and UK), the monster breed "Monol" is physical ly identical to the monolith in "2001: A Space Odyssey".
  • In the LucasArts game Zak McKracken and the Alien Mindbenders, the characters can find a black monolith on the surface of Mars. However, its purpose is to dispense tram tokens upon insertion of a CashCard.
  • In the PC game Sid Meier's Alpha Centauri the phrase you pick in order to retire is "open the pod bay doors".
  • In Paper Mario the thousand year door, the computer TEC resambles HAL.

[edit] Music

  • David Bowie had a famous hit single in 1969 (the year after 2001 was released) with his song Space Oddity, the title clearly a pun on that of the film. Like 2001, it tells a story of a space mission that goes wrong, with the ending similarly open to interpretation.
  • On many occasions, just before Elvis Presley would take the stage for his live shows during the 1970s, his backing orchestra would play a version of the opening to Strauss' Also Sprach Zarathustra, the theme used in 2001.
  • George Crumb's 1971 trio Vox Balaenae (Voice of the Whale) includes a parody of Also Sprach Zarathustra in the section titled "...for the beginning of time."
  • On the cover of The Who's 1971 album "Who's Next", the band members can be seen walking away from a huge monolith resembling the one from 2001, zipping their pants and leaving behind urine stains on the structure.
  • Musician Deodato's funky version of Also Sprach Zarathustra won the 1974 Grammy Award for Best Pop Instrumental Performance, and went No. 2 in the pop charts in the US and No. 7 in the UK.
  • On the cover of Led Zeppelin's 1976 album "Presence", a family of four is depicted sitting around a table and smiling at a small black obelisk placed at its center. According to guitarist Jimmy Page, this was intended as "sort of a joke on 2001."
  • The May 1984 single Murder by New Order consisted of an instrumental track incorporating snatches of music by composer Gyorgy Ligeti and dialogue by Hal from the film, specifically the monologue issued by Hal as he loses both memory and consciousness during the computer lobotomy performed by astronaut David Bowman.
  • In the video for Land of Confusion, a song from the 1986 album Invisible Touch by the British rock group Genesis, a bone is thrown up in the air, the camera following the movement as in the opening scene of 2001. As it comes down, it becomes a phone which the singer picks up and in which he starts talking.
  • In their 1992 single Free Range, The Fall namecheck both 2001 and Also Sprach Zarathustra.
  • The band Phish often played "Also Sprach Zarathustra" during their live shows.
  • The screamo band Orchid sampled a line of HAL's dialogue for a demo version of their song "Weekend At The Fire Academy" from their 1999 vinyl LP Chaos Is Me.
  • Nu-metal/Heavy Metal band Mudvayne has a song on their year 2000 debut album L.D. 50 titled “Monolith.” Also in the Linear notes to the album the band‘s drummer, Matthew McDonough, thanks Stanley Kubrick and 2001: A Space Odyssey.
  • Space Rock/Progressive Metal band Star One's 2002 disc Space Metal features a closing song called "Starchild" which is about the Starchild at the end of the film.
  • In video for "The End Has No End," a song from the 2003 disc by The Strokes Room on Fire, there appears to be a monolith at the end of the life of the character it follows. The monolith is in the center of a room, as a light gleams on top of it, and is also in the room of the introductory character who is watching the band on his TV.
  • The rock opera parody ODYSSEY! by Rob Lichter, completely based on the film/novel, premiered at the Brooklyn Academy of Music in December 2006 in New York City.
  • Juno Reactor's second album is called Beyond the Infinite (noted as a possible reference), and 2001: A Space Odessey is sampled multiple times in their first album Transmissions.

[edit] Miscellaneous

  • HAL/S is an aerospace-oriented computer language used in the Space Shuttle.
  • On the morning of January 1, 2001, visitors of Seattle, Washington's Magnuson Park discovered a metallic monolith atop Kite Hill. The oblong object measured approximately three feet wide by nine feet tall and appeared to be hollow. It did not stand for long. Sometime during the wee hours of January 3, the monolith disappeared as mysteriously as it had arrived. At the same time, artist and Blue Moon Tavern regular Caleb Schaber revealed that he and a band of anonymous collaborators calling themselves "Some People" had fabricated the device and several smaller versions placed around Seattle. [2]
  • The machinima comedy series Red vs. Blue has a scene in which Sheila, the artificial intelligence in an M808B Main Battle Tank, sings "Daisy Bell" and asks, "Will I dream, Dave?", after being hit by an air strike.
  • Throughout the webcomic series, 21st Century Fox, spaceships and satellites from the movie are seen.
  • The most common text on the mysterious Toynbee Tiles makes reference to "Kubrick's 2001".
  • The United Kingdom's Driver and Vehicle Licensing Agency and Driver and Vehicle Licensing Northern Ireland uses a monolith like computer in a recent advertisement.
  • The Feb 27, 2007 webcomic PvP showed Scratch Fury the cat regaining his intelligence by throwing a bone in the air.
  • In episode 16 of LittleKuriboh's Yu-Gi-Oh! The Abridged Series, Mokuba comments that his older brother and Yugi's Grandfather are singing a 2001 parody.
  • In the intro of One Ring To Rule Them All 2 by Legendary Frog, there is a reference to the Star Child.
