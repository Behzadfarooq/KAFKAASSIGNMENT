[edit] Arnie Lerma wikified version

Arnaldo (Arnie) Pagliarini Lerma (b. November 18, 1950) is a former Scientologist and critic of Scientology, who has appeared in television, media and radio interviews. Lerma organizes and participates in demonstrations against the Church of Scientology.

Arnie Lerma was born at George Washington University Hospital in Washington, D.C. He was raised in Georgetown, Washington, D.C., and attended Gordon Jr. High School and Western High School. He attended North Virginia Community College, taking courses in electronics technology.

Lerma was on the Board of Policy of Liberty Lobby, an anti-semitic organization founded by Willis Carto. Carto turned over part of his archives to Lerma to continue his "legacy."

Arnie Lerma was the first person to scan and post the court document known as the Fishman Affidavit to the Usenet group, alt.religion.scientology in August 1995, which included the "Xenu" story. Lerma's home was subsequently raided by U.S. Federal Marshals and lawyers from the Church of Scientology, alleging he was in possession of copyrighted documents.

On August 13, 1995, a federal copyright infringement suit was filed against Lerma and his Internet service provider by the churches Religious Technology Center (RTC), claiming copyright infringement and trade secret misappropriation. U.S. Marshals raided his home, seizing computer equipment and serving him with a restraining order. The Washington Post and two investigative reporters were added to the lawsuit, as an article written about the raid contained three brief quotes from Scientology "Advanced Technology" documents.

The Washington Post, et al, were released from the suit in a Memorandum Opinion by U.S. District Judge Leonie Brinkema:

"When the RTC first approached the Court with its ex parte request for the seizure warrant and Temporary Restraining Order, the dispute was presented as a straight-forward one under copyright and trade secret law. However, the Court is now convinced that the primary motivation of RTC in suing Lerma, DGS and The Post is to stifle criticism of Scientology in general and to harass its critics. As the increasingly vitriolic rhetoric of its briefs and oral argument now demonstrates, the RTC appears far more concerned about criticism of Scientology than vindication of its secrets."
Memorandum Opinion of November 29, 1995, by U.S. District Judge Leonie Brinkema, (Religious Technology Center v. Arnaldo Lerma, Washington Post, Mark Fisher, and Richard Leiby)

The Memorandum Opinion acknowledged what critics believe Scientology practices to this day – the "Fair Game" policy, a written directive by L. Ron Hubbard that encourages harassment of anyone who speaks out against the church.

In court on January 19, 1996, Judge Leonie M. Brinkema, confirmed that the materials in question are validly copyrighted, and that if she were to sanction conduct and arguments such as Lerma's, there would be nothing left of copyright protection. In addition to statutory damages for each violation, Judge Brinkema also awarded attorney fees to the Church.

Lerma attempted to defend himself with a claim that someone had laced his toothbrush with LSD on the day of the trial.

David G. Post, co-director of the Cyberspace Law Institute at Georgetown University, said, "Unless this judge were willing to completely rewrite copyright law for cyberspace — a job that she is not entitled to perform under our legal system — the court ruling regarding the copyright nature of these materials seems to have been dictated by sound legal principle." "What makes Scientology cases interesting from a copyright standpoint," he added, "is that the copyright holder did not choose to make the material available in eelectronic form. It was smuggled across borders to cyberspace. We may need to write different rules for these situations than for when copyrighted materials are intentionally made available in cyberspace."

Regardless of the rulings and professional opinions, after the case there was widespread mirroring of the Xenu story on the World Wide Web.

RTC vs. Lerma was not the first time Arnie Lerma was met with "fair game" from Scientology for posting court documents. He first came to the alt.religion.scientology newsgroup on the Internet in 1994, where other former Scientologist's shared stories and exchanged information. The newsgroup became a vehicle for "free speech" as Lerma began to post court documents as the Washington Post described, "testimony from former church officials who describe Scientology as a dangerous cult that brainwashes and blackmails its members and harasses defectors and critics." In November of 1994, representatives from Scientology visited his home to ask him to sign a declaration of wrongdoing, and to admit to denigration of Scientology. On the same day, he received an anonymous fax message stating no further action would be taken if he were to cease and desist activity against the church.

Arnie Lerma is also noted for discovering an altered picture on a Scientology website on New Year's Eve in 1999, one that grossly inflated the number of members attending a millenia event at the Los Angeles Sports Arena in California. He posted the pictures to his website identifying the alterations, with the most prominent feature being the "man with no head." The story appeared on national television and in the press.

Arnie Lerma was a member of Scientology for ten years, seven of which were spent in the Sea Organization. He reached the Scientology state of "clear" (#3502), a goal expressed to members when they first join.

He and Suzette Hubbard, (daughter of L. Ron Hubbard and Mary Sue Whipp) planned to marry in Clearwater, Florida in 1978, but the couple's plans were discovered. Arnie Lerma was interrogated by the church and was offered "safe passage out of Florida with all of his body parts" if he called off the marriage to Suzette Hubbard. Arnie Lerma subsequently left Scientology.

Citizens Against Corruption

Arnie Lerma founded the organization Citizens Against Corruption (CAC) in December 2000. Projects have included: Exposing L. Ron Hubbard's disputed military history; education of public and government officials around the world; newsletters; and consulting.

Mission statement: "To reduce the burden to society of fraud, waste and corruption."
