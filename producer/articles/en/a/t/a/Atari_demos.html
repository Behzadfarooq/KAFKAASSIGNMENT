Atari demos

Atari demos are demos created for the Atari home computer.

Like on many other computers and consoles, the demo scene is a group of so called "sceners" who are trying to program outstanding effects for their given hardware. A demo group usually consists of programmers, graphic artists and musians who team up to work on a demo, which is usually a program which displays a sequence of realtime rendered effects accompanied by music and graphics. The aim in the early days of each scene was mostly to do things which seemed impossible with that particular hardware, while later on the focus went to a more design oriented approach. Nowadays there is a small demoscene for almost any device that you can program, even PDAs, iPods and mobile phones. Of course there is such a scene for the Atari range of personal computers as well.

The Atari Demo Scene can probably be traced back to a group called "The Exceptions" (TEX for short) who created a series of music demos (enhanced with a bit of scrolling text and some nice rasters) in 1987. In 1988 the Atari Demo scene gathered further momentum with the release of the B.I.G. Demo, which was a large collection of music ported from the Commodore 64 by Jochen Hippel onto the Atari ST.

Following the B.I.G. demo, various crews continued to push the limits of the Atari ST hardware with innovative and efficient coding techniques (pre-calculated sprites, table driven calculations etc), as well as discovering and exploiting undocumented hardware features and bugs (synchron coding, border busting, sync scrolling).

  • The Union Demo (1989, the first big multi-crew, multi-screen demo)
  • The Cuddly Demos (1989, The CareBears big splash in the Atari demo scene)
  • The Decade Demo (1990, by a UK based group called "Inner Circle")
  • The PHF Demo (1990, by a UK (Hull) based group called "PHF (Psycho Hacking Force)")
  • Ooh Crikey Wot a Scorcher (1991, the last by a group of Brits known as "The Lost Boys")

Besides the major contributors a number of unknown crews were active during this period, mainly preparing to become game developers - the ultimate ambition for many on the demo scene. Crews like X-Troll, for example, released relatively few demos before progressing to fully-fledged ST games development.

The Atari demo scene started to peter out around 1993 as the popularity of the 16-bit micros was overtaken by the relentless advance of the x86 based PC. Some nostalgic demos have been released since and several demos have enjoyed revivals running on ST emulators such as STonX and PacifiST or more recently on SainT or STEem.

A small but enthusiastic scene still exists in 2007, and an Atari presence can still often be found at numerous annual Demoscene parties like Outline, Paracon, Atari Connexion and others. Recently it seems the Atari scene has started to re-established contact to the general demo scene, which is indicated by Atari related releases at Evoke and other demo-parties.
