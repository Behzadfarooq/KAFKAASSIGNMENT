Ataúlfo Argenta

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Ataulfo Argenta Maza (November 19, 1913 - January 21, 1958) was a famous Spanish conductor.

Argenta was born in Castro Urdiales, Cantabria, on 19 November 1913. He entered the Real Conservatorio Superior de Música de Madrid (Madrid Conservatory) in 1927 at the age of thirteen. He was awarded a Premio Extraordinario award for piano in 1930 and the Kristina Nilsson Prize in 1931. He performed on the piano in dance-halls and bars, and also worked for the Spanish National Railroad as his father had. The Premio Extraordinario allowed him to escape conscription in the Spanish Civil War and to study both piano and conducting in Belgium and Germany. In Germany, he studied conducting with Carl Schuricht.

He returned to Spain at the end of the Spanish Civil War. During World War II he performed as piano and celesta keyboardist in the Orquesta Nacional de España (Spanish National Orchestra) in Madrid. In 1944, he became conductor of the Madrid Chamber Orchestra, and began conducting the Orquesta Nacional de España on 10 October 1945. He became director of that orchestra in 1947, a post he held until his death.

Argenta’s untimely death, in Los Molinos, Madrid on January 21, 1958, was caused by inhalation of carbon monoxide. He had turned the heat on in his study and then he got into his automobile and switched on the engine and heater with the garage doors down. A student in the car with him survived. His death came just as he was likely to achieve major international success.

Argenta recorded nearly 50 zarzuelas with Alhambra (Spanish Columbia) including famous opera singers in the lead roles. Many records in this series were reissued by Decca Records. He also signed a contract with Decca to record a series of records; mostly of Spanish, Russian and French music. A famous stereophonic demonstration record of its day was España!, issued as Decca SXL 2020. For this, Argenta led the London Symphony Orchestra at Kingsway Hall in January 1957 in “Spanish” music by mostly non-Spanish composers. The album included Emmanuel Chabrier’s rhapsody Espana as well as Nikolai Rimsky-Korsakov’s Capriccio espagnol, Enrique Granados’ Andalusia, and Moszkowski's Spanish Dances.

Argenta also recorded Granados’ opera Goyescas, Hector Berlioz’s Symphonie fantastique with the Paris Conservatoire Orchestra, and Claude Debussy's Images and Tchaikovsky’s Symphony No. 4 in F minor, with the Orchestre de la Suisse Romande. He had also been engaged to record the Brahms symphonies in Vienna when death intervened.

A statue of Ataúlfo Argenta by the sculptor Rafael Huerta was erected in the the garden of Castro Urdiales in 1961.
