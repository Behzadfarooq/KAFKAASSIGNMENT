Intro proposal #22b

Atheism is defined as either denial of the existence of God, or the absence of belief in deities. Self-proclaimed atheists tend to adhere to the second definition, and divide it further into strong and weak atheism. Modern dictionaries emphasize the first definition.
The subtle differences between these definitions can lead to miscommunication. For example, a person who does not believe in God due to ignorance or lack of concern would be called agnostic by someone who espouses the atheism-as-denial definition. A user of the other definition would call this person an atheist. These approaches are indicative of the underlying beliefs of the observers: since theists know that God exists, anyone who does not is best described as without knowledge—or agnostic. Those who argue that the default position, barring evidence to the contrary, is that no gods exist, find it absurd to discuss the knowledge, or lack thereof, of nonexistent entities. They find that it is more appropriate to state that the subject lacks what the theist has (i.e., theism), and therefore should be called an atheist.
