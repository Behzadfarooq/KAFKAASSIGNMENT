[edit] Opinion or fact?

The author wrote (or transcribed),

The US Army typically uses observation helicopters (such as the OH-58 and H-6) in support of attack helicopter. But it is starting to fall out of favor as the gunships are getting as sophisticated or more than the observation helicopters deployed to support them.

The US Army went to pure, single aircraft-type units in an attempt to streamline logistics lines and reduce the amount of money each unit had to spend on its parts requirements for maintenance. It has nothing to do with a supposed preference to work that way. Although I will admit there are those uneducated (no matter how educated) within the force who may see it that way. The pure MTO&E (I-series) occurred in the early 90's. All it did was create upheaval in doctrine, and unit operations. In addition, recent documents I've viewed at the Combined Arms Research Library[1] seem to point out that the U.S. Army actually drifted away from operating that way due to a focusing on what are now seen as the more traditional Cavalry or Attack missions, since the units no longer consist of a mix of reconnaissance and attack helicopters.

In the current conflict, this has changed, and once again, you see a pairing of the two different machines because one relies on the strengths of the other and vice versa, and the missions dictate the use of those combined strengths. (Born2flie 03:04, 24 July 2006 (UTC))

[edit] MH-60 vs. armed UH-1?

How has USSOCOM "developed" a new concept of Attack helicopter over the last 20 years? They've simply mirrored earlier U.S. Army, Soviet, British, etc. efforts to provide Attack support to ground troops by arming a utility-type airframe. It works for them because it streamlines their logistics tail (including pilot qualifications). (Born2flie 12:33, 28 September 2006 (UTC))

[edit] Vietnam and the Gunship

I flew Cobras in Vietnam for 18 months and I have no knowledge of a "flexible rapid fire 20mm cannon" I was just going to question this but I see it edited out. The only 20mm cannon I remember were wing mounted, although a 20mm turret may have been added later. Dan D. Ric 06:40, 4 October 2006 (UTC)

Nice work on the updates BTW. It might be worth noting that the interim Cobra, although much modified, remains in production 40 years later. Dan D. Ric 06:48, 4 October 2006 (UTC)
Dan, reading this article again, I think "The Modern Attack Helicopter" needs to be modified to include that information. Right now, it seems very U.S.-centric and very Apache-centric in its POV, when the fact is that the current conflict has thrown the Apache's apparent reign over all attack airframes into question. It reminds me of that laughable quote from The Military Channel's show Top Ten Helicopters where one commentator claims that, "Everybody wants to fly it, even if they won't admit it." (Born2flie 15:40, 9 October 2006 (UTC))
Some would argue that the Apache did exactly what it was designed to do survive under fire. To quote one of the product designers when I was given a tour (my Grandfather was a VP for McDonald Douglas) "For what it can't fly through, the pilot will likely survive the crash landing." Now since it's personal expirence, obviously I can't put such a quote in the article. PPGMD 18:19, 9 October 2006 (UTC)
Some would also argue that point in denial of fatalities from AH-64s being shot down. My point is that this article is about Attack Helicopters in general, not leading to an opinion that one particular helicopter is the preeminent specimen. The reader should be allowed to arrive at their own conclusions. (Born2flie 20:01, 9 October 2006 (UTC))
