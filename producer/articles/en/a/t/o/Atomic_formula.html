Atomic formula

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

In mathematical logic, an atomic formula or atom is a formula with no underlying propositional structure. The precise form of the atomic formula depends on the logic under consideration; for propositional logic, the atoms are the propositional variables, whereas for predicate logic, the atoms are of the form P (t1, …, tn), where the ti are terms and P is a predicate symbol.

The atomic formulas are the simplest propositions of the logic. More complex propositions are formed by combinging simpler propositions using the connectives of the logic. One thus obtains a recursively defined language of well-formed formulas of the logic. To illustrate, the well-formed propositions (A, B, …) of ordinary first-order logic have the following syntax (written in BNF):

A, B  ::= P(t1, …, tn) | A ∧ B | ⊤ | A ∨ B | ⊥ | A ⊃ B | ∀x. A | ∃x. A

where P(t1, …, tn) are the atomic formulas.

Any proposition, such as (∀x. P(x) ∧ ∃y. Q(y, f(x)) ⊃ ⊥) ∨ ∃z. R(z), is formed from the relevant atoms (P, Q and R in this case) and the syntax rules.

[edit] See also

  • In model theory, structures assign an interpretation to the atomic formulas.
  • In proof theory, polarity assignment for atomic formulas is an essential component of focusing.
