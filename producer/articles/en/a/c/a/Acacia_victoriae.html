Acacia victoriae is a species of tree in the family Fabaceae. It has numerous common names, including prickly acacia, bardi bush, bramble wattle, elegant wattle, prickly wattle, gundabluie, gundabluey and narran. It occurs throughout mainland Australia, being widely distributed in every mainland State except Victoria, where it occurs only in the north-west. It grows well in saline soil.

Prickly acacia grows as a spreading, straggly tree up to six metres high and ten metres wide, or as a shrub up to three metres high. Like most Acacia species, it has phyllodes rather than true leaves. These are flat, slightly curved, and may be up to three centimetres long and five millimetres wide. At the base of most leaves is a pair of straight spines about five millimetres long. The flowers are pale yellow, and held in spherical clusters. The pods are a golden brown colour, and papery, up to nine centimetres long and 1Â½ centimetres wide.

[edit] Food

Acacia victoriae is a traditional food of Aboriginal people, particularly for making seedcakes.

Since the mid 1980s is has been more widely used by non-indigenous Australian's. The seed is roasted and used as a coffee-substitute and flavoring, and is the standard ingredient in 'wattleseed'.
