The Academy of Arcadia or Academy of Arcadians (Italian official name: Pontificia Accademia degli Arcadi) was an Italian literary academy founded in Rome during 1690.

[edit] History

The beginnings of the "Arcadia" date back to February, 1656, when a literary circle arose under the auspices of the celebrated Queen Christina of Sweden, who, due to a combination of factors, had abdicated the Swedish crown in 1654 and converted to Catholicism, moving to Rome where she spent much of the rest of her life. There she became a significant patron of music and opera, with composers including Alessandro Scarlatti, Alessandro Stradella and Arcangelo Corelli dedicating works to her. After her death in 1689, the Academy of Arcadia was established in her memory, electing the late Queen Christina of Sweden as its symbolical head ("Basilissa", the Greek term for "Empress"). The Academy would last for the next two hundred years, becoming a leading cultural institution right up to the 20th century.

The Academy of Arcadia was so called because its chief aim and intention were to imitate in literature the simplicity of the ancient shepherds, who were supposed to have lived in Arcadia in the golden age, divinely inspired in poetry by the Muses, Apollo, Hermes and Pan. The Academy chose as its emblem the pipe of Pan with its seven unequal reeds. The fourteen founders selected as first "Custode di Arcadia", or president of the Academy, Giovanni Mario Crescimbeni, author of a history of Italian poetry and of various literary works. The Arcadians proposed to themselves to return to the fields of truth, always singing of subjects of pastoral simplicity, drawing their inspiration from the Greco-Roman bucolica poetry. The ideal parameters for the artistic work were simplicity and a sense of measure and beauty. Common to all the poets it was the desire to oppose the poetry of the Marinists, and return to classic poetry, embracing also the recent rationalist influences by Descartes. Norms and rituals of the Academy took cue from classic and pastorale mythology: between these there was the custom of the academics to assume classical names (Crescimbeni, for example, chose that of Carlo Alfesibeo).

The first solemn gathering of the "Arcadi" was held on the Gianicolo, in a wood belonging to the Reformed Minorites, on 5 October 1690. In 1692, the meetings were transferred to the Esquiline in the gardens of Duke Orsini; in 1696, to the Farnese gardens on the Palatine. Finally, the generosity of John V of Portugal, one of its members under the name of Arete Melleo, enabled the society to secure (1773) on the Gianicolo a site known as the "Bosco Parrasio". While the Academy was yet on the Palatine, its Statuto (constitution) was drawn up. This constitution (the work of co-founder Gian Vincenzo Gravina) was modelled on the ancient Roman laws of the "Twelve Tables", and was engraved on marble. The two main tendencies followed the path of the ideas of the two founders: that of Gravina stood in the tradition of Homer and Dante, while that of Crescimbeni was more influenced by Petrarca. Because of these differences Gravina left to found the Accademia dei Quinti in 1711. Despite this loss, "Arcadia" retained its vigour in the following years, and created "colonies" in many cities of Italy. Numerous noblemen, ecclesiastics and other artists held membership in it as an honour, and very soon it numbered 1,300: most of the members' production, however, was rather mediocre or pretentious (an example is Saverio Bettinelli's disparagement of Dante), even though the Academy received some endorsement for its attack of the redundant Rococo style, then ruling in art and literature.

A violent anti-Arcadian reaction soon developed, and, starting from the 19th century, Arcadianism began to be regarded as the last and unsuccessful tentative, literary and artistic, of the ancient regime. After the end of the French Revolution, the Academy strove to renew itself in accord with the spirit of the times, without sacrificing its traditional system of sylvan associations and pastoral names. The academy no longer represented a literary school, but merely a general tendency towards the classic style, and figures like Dante became to be greatly honoured by its members. Furthermore, the academic field was enlarged so as to include all branches of study, including history, archaeology and others. The new Arcadian revival was marked by the foundation (1819) of the Giornale Arcadico.

The Academy of Arcadia counted among its members some of the principal literary men of the time, including Menzini, Redi, Metastasio, Rolli, Guidi, and others. The famous composer George Frideric Handel is known to have often attended the meetings and symposia of the Arcadian Academics when studying in Italy.

The archives of the Academy are currently housed in the Biblioteca Angelica, next to the church of Sant'Agostino in Rome. The paintings are housed by Palazzo Braschi.
