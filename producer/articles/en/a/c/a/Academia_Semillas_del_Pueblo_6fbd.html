Academia Semillas del Pueblo Xinaxcalmecac (Spanish: Seeds of the People Academy) is a public charter school of the Los Angeles Unified School District (LAUSD). It offers instruction in grades Kindergarten through eighth, and is located in the East Los Angeles community of El Sereno. The school was founded by Marcos Aguilar, a former teacher at Garfield Senior High School, in the fall of 2001, and opened in 2002.

[edit] Funding

As a charter school, Academia is funded both by California taxpayers and from contributions from private organizations such as:

  • National Council of La Raza Charter School Development Initiative
  • Raza Development Fund, Inc.
  • Glendale Nissan/Infinity, Inc.
  • California State University, Los Angeles
  • Pasadena City College chapter of MEChA
  • The Latino Museum of History, Art and Culture

[edit] Curriculum

Like all California schools, Academia is required to adhere to statewide standards. However, administrators have autonomy determining the curriculum. The interior of the school has no walls separating classes, and multiple grades are taught the same material simultaneously. This structure is intended to replicate Pre-Columbian educational institutions, and to validate and foster the indigenous identity of the students. Students are taught English, Spanish, Mandarin Chinese, and the Aztec/Mexica Nahuatl language, spoken amoungst more then 2 million peoples in the western hemisphere. Including relatives of the attendees. Mathematic instruction is based on the Aztec ventigesimal (base-20) numeric system, and uses an Aztec abacus.

[edit] Controversy

In May of 2006, KABC morning radio host Doug McIntyre received an e-mail from a listener telling him that the school did not fly the flag of the United States on May 1st, 2006, presumably as part of the Great American Boycott, a nationwide protest against HR 4437, a bill passed by the United States House of Representatives that, if enacted, would have stiffened penalties for being in the United States without authorization. On his show, McIntyre claimed that the school was racist and had a "separatist agenda". Other critics said that statements made by Aguilar in an interview with an online journal of UCLA's Institute for Democracy, Education, and Access "support extremist views".

The allegations received attention in the local media and on the internet. District officials visited the school unannounced to investigate allegations of discrimination. Speaking on behalf of the investigators, LAUSD attorney Kevin Reed said, "We looked specifically for any indications of any overt discriminatory practices on campus, such as statements on bulletin boards that expressed racial animus, were kids learning English, was math being taught consistent with California standards, and my understanding is they left satisfied that nothing of great concern was going on." Investigation into the school's finances is also underway.

In the midst of the controversy, KABC sent reporter Sandy Wells to the school to interview Aguilar. The interview, which ASP has claimed was not scheduled beforehand, was denied. While at the school, Wells claimed that he was assaulted and his audio equipment seized. The Los Angeles Police Department is investigating the incident.

During the controversy, the school took their website offline without explanation. The mission statement which had appeared on the front page had been a source of the controversy. The original mission statement read,

"Academia Semillas del Pueblo Charter School is a kindergarten through eighth grade public school dedicated to providing urban children of immigrant native families an excellent education founded upon their own language, cultural values and global realities."

The site was later reactivated. The new version of the site bore a statement that the old website was removed "Due to the unsanctioned use of the images of our students." It also featured a modified mission statement, which read,

"Academia is a kindergartern through eighth grade public charter school dedicated to providing urban children of immigrant families an excellent education founded upon native and maternal languages, cultural values, and global realities. Our school's name in the Nahuatl native language means, "the house of higher learning for the seeds of our people. Academia has served the greater East Los Angeles community since 2002."

Both Academia and McIntyre claim to have received numerous threats during the controversy, the most noteworthy of which was a bomb threat phoned into the school in early June. The LAPD is investigating the threat, and McIntyre has offered $1,000 as a reward for anyone offering information leading to the arrest and conviction of whoever made the threat against the school.


Recently, Bob Sipchan, a noted pulizer prize winner and blogger of L.A. Times wrote an article regarding his findings and worries about the school. He concluded (in summary) that it was not a racially motivated school but a school fitting the community in which it was located. [the link to the article is located at the bottom of the page.]

[edit] Performance

In 2005, Academia posted an Academic Performance Index of 577, down 20 points from its 2004 score. The score was atypical of charter schools, which tend to perform better than their non-charter counterparts. The school's poor performance formed an important part of the criticisms that were levelled. Supporters of the school point to the socioeconomic background of its students, almost all of whom come from the families of Mexican immigrants living in one of the poorest parts of Southern California, as a reason for the low score. They also point out that many charter schools cater to higher-income brackets, whereas Academia's mission is to educate the indigent. However, even when compared to schools with the same socioeconomic conditions, the California Board of Education rates Academia a 1 out of 10.

Only eight percent of ASDP's teachers are fully credentialed. One-quarter of the teachers are in their first year teaching, and the average experience level is just four years.

As of March 29th the school's charter was renewed with a vote of 5-2.

[edit] Student body

Although chartered as a K-8 school, the Academia does not have any students enrolled in any grade beyond the 5th. 305 of the school's 318 students are Hispanic or Latino. The remaining students are Native American (9), African-American (2), Filipino (1), and White (1).

As of March 29th the school's charter was renewed with a vote of 5-2.
