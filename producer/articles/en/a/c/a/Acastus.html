Acastus

In Greek Mythology, Acastus was one of the men who sailed with Jason and the Argonauts.

[edit] Family

Acastus' father was Pelias, then king of Iolkos, and Anaxibia. He was later killed as the result of one of Medea's tricks. In revenge, Acastus drove Jason and Medea into exile, and so became king of the country.

[edit] Legend

Acastus purifed Peleus of the murder of King Eurytion of Phthia. Then, Peleus lost a wrestling match in the funeral games of Pelias to Atalanta. Astydameia, Acastus' wife, fell in love with Peleus but he scorned her. Bitter, she sent a messenger to Antigone, Peleus' wife and daughter of Eurytion, to tell her that Peleus was to marry Acastus' daughter, Steropes. Antigone hanged herself.

Astydameia then told Acastus that Peleus had tried to rape her. Acastus took Peleus on a hunting trip and hid his sword, then abandoned him right before a group of centaurs attacked. Chiron, the wise centaur, returned Peleus' sword and Peleus managed to escape. He pillaged Iolkos, helped by Jason and the Dioscuri, and dismembered Astydameia, then marched his army between the pieces. Acastus and Astydameia were dead, and the kingdom fell to Jason's son Thessalus.

[edit] Descendants

Acastus and Astydameia had two daughters: Sterope and Laodamia, and a number of sons. Another daughter, Sthenele, was given by Apollodorus of Athens as the wife of Menoetius and mother of Patroclus.
