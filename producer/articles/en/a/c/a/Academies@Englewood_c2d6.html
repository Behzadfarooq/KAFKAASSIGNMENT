The Academies@Englewood also known as A@E or Academies @ Englewood is a four-year comprehensive magnet public high school program started by Dr. John Greico (founder of the Bergen County Academies) in an effort to diversify the Dwight Morrow High School campus, to raise the standard of public education for Englewood residents, and to attract residents of Englewood and Englewood Cliffs back to the public school system. The school was established in 2002 with four academies: Finance, Information Systems, Law and Public Safety, and Pre-Engineering. A fifth academy, Biomedicine, was added in 2004. The school graduated its first class in 2006, with 91 students, about half of whom were from Englewood, the other half from several dozen North Jersey towns. The school is located in Englewood, in Bergen County, New Jersey, United States, as part of the Englewood Public School District.

[edit] Controversy

The primary controversy with the Academies is its location on the Dwight Morrow High School campus. The South building was taken away from Dwight Morrow High in 2002 to establish the Academies and the two schools also share a single auditorium and gynasium. This left Dwight Morrow High with only the North Building and fewer available classrooms. Residents in the City of Englewood have expressed feelings of anger in allowing the Academies to operate on the campus; newspapers such as the Bergen Record have quoted residents accusing the Academies of being a racist institution.

The Englewood Board of Education has repeatedly attempted in various ways to integrate the two schools, but that task has been proven difficult due to many issues. The original idea in bringing the Academies to Dwight Morrow High School campus was to diversify the student body of Dwight Morrow while setting a higher standing for education in the entire district. The campus itself has been diversified, but the two schools are kept almost completely separate. Until recently, the schools only shared classes such as electives, music, art, and gym, but from the school year of 2006/2007 onward, they now operate on the same day schedule and many students share core classes. However, if the Academies @ Englewood are not included as part of the Dwight Morrow High School student body the school still remains overwhelmingly minority, about 98% black and Hispanic. This concept of integration has angered many of the out-of-district students, many who or whose parents interpreted this as a reduction in the quality of education and have made the choice to leave the program.

A@E is known for its distrust in the Englewood Public School District and the state of New Jersey; the first inaugural class of 2006 were commonly referred to as "the guinea pigs" in the early, very experimental stages of the school and many State-sponsored budget issues have come close to causing the death of the program entirely. In 2006, several teachers from A@E left the school specifically on grounds of recent changes, finance and integration-related, that affected the program. In early 2007, two more teachers have left including founding administrator Patrick Bernardo who was responsible for the class scheduling. Many Dwight-Morrow students and staff feel that the program will eventually have to merge with the public high school in some way, shape, or form, though it is the feeling of many A@E students that this will lead to a severe lack of interest in out-of-district students and undo the original career and college-oriented philosophy of the program. The future of the Academies still remains as uncertain as when it was first conceived.
