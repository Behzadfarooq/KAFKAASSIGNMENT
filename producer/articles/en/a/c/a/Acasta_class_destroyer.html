The Acasta or K class was a class of destroyers built for the Royal Navy under the Naval Programme of 1911 - 1912 that saw service during World War I. They were the last class of Royal Navy destroyers to have mixed names with no systematic theme (see naming conventions for destroyers of the Royal Navy for more information.) The class saw extensive wartime service and seven were lost, including four at the Battle of Jutland.

[edit] Design

The Acastas were larger and heavier armed than the preceding H and I classes (Acorn and Acheron, respectively), displacing about 25 % more and with the mixed calibre armament replaced with a uniform fit of QF 4-inch guns, which the Acastas introduced. Previous 4-inch weapons had been of the breech-loading (BL) type. The guns were shipped one each on the forecastle and either side abreast the after torpedo tube (or amidships before and after the tube in some ships.) All ships had three funnels, the foremost being tall and narrow, the second short and wide and the third level with the second but narrower. The foremost torpedo tube was sited between the second and third funnels, a distinctive feature of this class. There were a number of builder's specials that, except for Garland, had a shorter, less beamy hull. Five were from Thornycroft with 22,500 shp, one by Parsons that made 31 knots on trials and a seventh from Fairfields.

[edit] Service

At the outbreak of World War I until mid-1916, the Acastas were serving in the Grand Fleet as the 4th Destroyer flotilla, with Swift as leader. By the time of Jutland the leader was the Faulknor class leader Tipperary, with Ardent, Fortune, Shark and Sparrowhawk lost in the course of the battle and Acasta was so badly damaged that she had to be practically rebuilt. After Jutland the remainder of the flotilla moved to the Humber and then to Portsmouth by the end of 1916, before dispersing, some ships to the 6th Destroyer Flotilla and the Dover Patrol and the remainder to Devonport. All survivors of the war were sold out of service for scrapping by 1921.

[edit] Ships

[edit] Admiralty K class

  • Acasta — built by Clydebank Shipyard, Clydebank, launched September 10, 1912, sold for scrapping 1921
  • Achates — built by Clydebank Shipyard, launched November 14, 1912, sold for scrapping 1921
  • Ambuscade — built by Clydebank Shipyard, launched January 25, 1913, sold for scrapping 1921
  • Ardent — built by William Denny & Brothers Limited, Dumbarton, sunk by secondary gunfire from German dreadnought SMS Westfalen at Battle of Jutland June 1, 1916
  • Christopher — built by Hawthorn Leslie & Company, Newcastle upon Tyne, launched August 29, 1912, sold for scrapping 1921
  • Cockatrice — built by Hawthorn Leslie, launched November 8, 1912, sold for scrapping 1921
  • Contest — built by Hawthorn Leslie, launched January 7, 1913, torpedoed and sunk gy German U-boat in the Western Approaches September 18, 1917
  • Lynx — built by Harland & Wolff, Govan, launched March 20, 1913, mined and sunk in Moray Firth by mine laid from German raider Meteor August 9, 1915
  • Midge — built by Harland & Wolff, Govan, launched May 22, 1913, sold for scrapping 1921
  • Owl — built by Harland & Wolff, Govan, launched July 7, 1913, sold for scrapping 1921
  • Shark — built by Swan, Hunter & Wigham Richardson, Wallsend, launched July 30, 1912, disabled by gunfire and torpedoed and sunk at Battle of Jutland May 31, 1916
  • Sparrowhawk — built by Swan Hunter, launched October 12, 1912, collided with Faulknor class leader Broke at Battle of Jutland and torpedoed by Marksman class destroyer Marksman June 1, 1916
  • Spitfire — built by Swan Hunter, launched December 23, 1912, sold for scrapping 1921

[edit] Builders' special K class

  • Fortune — built by Fairfield Shipbuilding and Engineering Company, Govan, launched March 17, 1913, sunk by secondary gunfire from German dreadnought SMS Westfalen at Battle of Jutland on night of May 31 / June 1, 1916
  • Garland — built by Parsons Marine Steam Turbine Company, Wallsend (hull sub-contracted to Cammell Laird & Company, Birkenhead), launched April 23, 1913, sold for scrapping 1921
  • Hardy — built by John I. Thornycroft & Company Limited, Woolston, launched October 10, 1913, sold for scrapping 1921
  • Paragon — built by Thornycroft, launched February 21, 1913, torpedoed and sunk by German destroyer in action in the Straits of Dover March 18, 1917
  • Porpoise — built by Thornycroft, launched July 7, 1913, sold for scrapping 1921
  • Unity — built by Thornycroft, launched September 13, 1913, sold for scrapping 1921
  • Victor — built by Thornycroft, launched November 28, 1913, sold for scrapping 1921
