The Academy of Art University (formerly Academy of Art College), a for-profit career college owned by the Stephens Institute, was founded in San Francisco, California in 1929 by Richard S. Stephens. In recent years, the Academy has expanded to occupy over 30 buildings in downtown San Francisco. With an enrollment of approximately 9,500 students, the Academy is reportedly the largest art and design school in the United States.[1]

[edit] History

In 1929, the Academy of Art University was established in San Francisco as the Academy of Art Advertising by Richard S. Stephens, a Creative Director for Sunset Magazine.[2] Assisted by his wife, Mrs. Clara Stephens, Richard S. Stephens opened the new school in a rented loft at 215 Kearny Street to teach advertising art. In 1933, the curriculum was expanded to include Fashion Illustration, and a Fine Art Department was added in 1936 to the course offerings. Many of the Academy's early instructors were professional artists who brought the experience of "real-world problems" to students.

Stephen's son, Dr. Richard A. Stephens, took over direction of the school after graduating from Stanford University in 1951. During Richard A. Stephen's tenure as the school's president, the Academy expanded its enrollment from 50 to 5,200 students. Richard A. Stephen oversaw continued expansion of department majors, starting with the addition of a Foundations Department to offer courses providing students with instruction in the basic principles of art and design and later additions of other Fine Art departments. In 1966, the school became the Academy of Art College, and the school was granted authority by the California Bureau for Private Postsecondary and Vocational Education to confer the Bachelor of Fine Arts degree.

By 1977, the Art of Academy College added the Master of Fine Arts program to its degree offerings, culminating the inauguration of the graduate school. The state of California approved the Masters program in 1983. After a few years, leadership of the Academy was turned over in 1992 to Dr. Elisa Stephens, granddaughter of the school's founder, who became the school's third president. The name of the school was changed from Academy of Art College to Academy of Art University in 2004 to reflect the growing scope of art and design disciplines.

[edit] General Information

[edit] Academic Programs

The Academy of Art University offers both on-campus (traditional instructor-led) and distance education (online) degree and Certificate programs in its fine arts programs. These programs include Associate of Arts (AA, 66 credit hours) and Bachelor of Fine Arts (BFA, 132 credit hours) for undergraduate degrees, Master of Fine Arts (MFA, 63 credit hours) and Master of Architecture (M.Arch, 102 credit hours) for graduate degrees, and 120-credit hour certificate programs for personal enrichment. The Academy offers degree and certificate programs in 13 majors: Advertising, Animation & Visual Effects, Architecture (MFA and M.Arch only), Computer Arts & New Media, Digital Arts & Communications (AA and BFA only), Fashion, Fine Art, Graphic Design, Illustration, Industrial Design, Interior Architecture & Design, Motion Pictures & Television and Photography.

[edit] Accreditation

The Academy of Art University is approved by the Bureau for Private Postsecondary and Vocational Education for the state of California,[3] and its degree programs (except the online Architecture program)[4] are accredited by the Accrediting Council for Independent Colleges and Schools (ACICS)[5] and National Association of Schools of Art and Design (NASAD)[6] for degree granting authority. The Academy is also an institutional member of the Career College Association (CCA) and the Council for Higher Education Accreditation (CHEA).[7] The Academy of Art University currently lacks regional accreditation by the Western Association of Schools and Colleges (WASC), and the evaluation for WASC accreditation is scheduled for February 26-28, 2007.[8]

The on-campus Bachelor of Fine Arts degree program offered by the Academy in Interior Architecture & Design is accredited as a Professional Level Program by the Council for Interior Design Accreditation (formerly "FIDER," the Foundation for Interior Design Education and Research).[9] In addition, the on-campus Master of Architecture program offered by the Academy's School of Architecture has been accredited as a Professional Level Program since 2006 by the National Architectural Accrediting Board (NAAB).[10] Although the Academy offers separate on-campus and online graduate degree programs with the term "Architecture" in their names (Master of Interior Architecture & Design, an interior design program, and the architecture degree programs for Master of Arts in Architecture and Master of Architecture), the NAAB accredited only the on-campus degree program for Master of Architecture.[10]

[edit] Campus Life

The Academy of Art University holds classes in a number of buildings, with most buildings located within a few blocks of each other in downtown San Francisco. Several of the buildings are significant historical structures — in some cases, bought by the Academy to preserve them from demolition or commercial redevelopment. The Academy also owns a series of art studios that were once owned by the non-profit San Francisco Art Institute. Residence facilities and all academic buildings are linked by an extensive school shuttle bus system, free to students, that runs both daytime and evening routes.[2]

The Academy owns many buildings that it has converted into dormitories, and some of these dormitories occupy structures built during the early 20th Century. Of these buildings, six of them are designated as student dormitories, with some designated as co-ed and others for male-only and female-only students. The school also offers a limited number of apartments for student housing. Although the Academy guarantees housing to all students, the rates charged for this housing are higher than most other colleges in the San Francisco Bay area.[2]

[edit] Financial Aid

The Academy of Art University is recognized a participating institution in the U.S. Department of Education Financial Aid Program.

[edit] Controversies

[edit] Purchase of St. Brigid Church

During Summer 2005, the Archdiocese of San Francisco sold St. Brigid Catholic Church, which first opened in 1864 and closed in 1994 by the Archdiocese as a cost-saving measure because of declining attendance, repair costs for damage caused by the Loma Prieta earthquake and the need to pay court judgments resulting from the Catholic Church sexual abuse scandal, to the Academy of Art University.[11] After a seismic restoration forecast showed that $7 million USD was needed to shore up the church building, the Academy petitioned the City of San Francisco to remove the church from the historic landmark registry. Several parishioners, who wanted to preserve St. Brigid Church as a historic landmark, petitioned the San Francisco Landmarks Preservation Advisory Board to prevent the Academy from demolishing the church's exterior and interior features and preserve the whole of the church as a historic landmark.[12] Although the Archdiocese of San Francisco declined to preserve landmark status for St. Brigid Church and despite the Academy's requests to remove the church from the historic registry, the San Francisco Board of Supervisors voted to grant partial landmark status to the church. In its October 3, 2006 ruling, the Board accorded St. Brigid Church historic landmark status for its exterior, leaving open the question about preserving the church's interior features for another time.[12][13]

[edit] Expulsion of Student Writer

In December 2003, a controversy erupted when the Academy expelled a student for writing a story in a creative writing class that featured a male protagonist, who was a serial killer who dismembered his female victims. Jan Richman, an award-winning poet and former recipient of a National Endowment for the Arts grant who was also the student's instructor, became concerned about the subject matter of the student's fictional story.[14]

Richman referred the matter to her department's coordinator to seek advice and guidance for how to handle the student's submission. The department coordinator suggested to Richman that she could have the student read the first chapter of The Lovely Bones by Alice Sebold as a way to initiate a discussion about how to introduce the power of violence in artful storytelling.[15] The content of the student's story later came to the attention of the Academy's administration office, and the Academy expelled the student and referred the student's graphic short story to the homicide division of the San Francisco Police Department for criminal profiling.[15] Richman lost her job at the Academy after the Academy learned she had earlier that semester required students to read Girl With Curious Hair, a short story by award-winning author David Foster Wallace.[14] The Academy claimed that Richman violated school policy by assigning a core text book that was not approved in advance for the course, although no policy existed officially at that time that prohibited instructors from using supplemental materials for the established curriculum.

Alan Kaufman, another faculty member at the Academy and author of the critically acclaimed Jew Boy and Matches,[16] took up the cause of the student's expulsion and Richman's firing by organizing protests against the Academy's response, which led to Kaufman's later dismissal from his job because of his role in leading protests about the controversy.[17] In support of Kaufman's protest against the student's expulsion, authors Stephen King and Salman Rushdie (at the time, Rushdie was President of the PEN American Center) wrote letters of protest concerning the Academy's handling of the matter.[18] The Academy defended its actions to expel the student and request criminal profiling amid concerns about the context of the student's work and safety of its other students in light of the Columbine and September 11 incidents.[18] In spite of protest letters penned by King and Rushdie concerning both academic and artistic freedom, the Academy proceeded with expelling the freshman student from the school and refusing to offer teaching assignments to both Richman and Kaufman.[15][19]

During the course of the protests, the Academy's student newspaper, Starving Artist, covered the story. After the student editor of Starving Artist approached the Academy about covering the protests, the Academy granted permission to the student newspaper to cover the protest in two parts, giving equal weight to both sides of the controversy. In its coverage of the story, the front page of Staving Artist featured the headline "Safety from What?" with a front page photo of a student with a taped over mouth wearing the sign "At the Academy of Art ... Students = Credit Cards," along with an editorial that compared the Academy's response to fears that erupted during the September 11, 2001 attacks.[17] In an attempt to quell the ongoing disruption and protests at the campus, the Academy confiscated half of the distributed newspaper copies featuring the provocative headline, rescinded permission to cover and print the remaining part of the story and shut down the newspaper.[17]

[edit] Battling the Faculty Union

During late 1970s-early 1980s, the faculty at the then Academy of Art College formed a union, certified by the National Labor Relations Board as the California Federation of Art Teachers.[20] After many attempts by the school and a protracted legal battle to prevent the union from becoming active, the National Labor Relations Board prevailed in federal court and secured back pay for many instructors who were fired illegally in violation of the National Labor Relations Act.[21]

[edit] Notable alumni

  • The design works of some of the Academy's current and former students – Natasha Shah, Malia McGlothlin Pawsey, Jessica Thompson, Jennifer Bolanos and Tianyu Li – have been featured on the Home and Garden Television (HGTV) show Designer Finals.[22]
  • Mari Matsumoto, a MFA School of Fashion graduate of the Academy of Art University, is one of Forbes' five future fashion designers to watch.[23]
  • Deanne Fitzmaurice, a BFA graduate of the Academy and a staff photographer for the San Francisco Chronicle, won the 2005 Pultizer Prize in feature photography[24] for her photo essay of efforts by Children's Hospital Oakland to treat an Iraqi boy for life threatening injuries sustained in an explosion.[25]
  • John Vasicek, a MFA graduate of the Academy's School of Motion Pictures & Television, wrote, produced and directed the feature film Voyeur, a film accepted by multiple film festivals, including the notable Sonoma Valley Film Festival.[26][27]
