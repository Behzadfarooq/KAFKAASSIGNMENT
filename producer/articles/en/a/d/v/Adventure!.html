This article is about the Adventure! role-playing game. For the primary use of the term adventure in role-playing games, see adventure (role-playing games).

Adventure! is a pulp action role-playing game originally printed by White Wolf Game Studio, the third and last book in the Trinity Universe line of games. The game, printed in black and white on pulp-like sepia paper to resemble a period piece, was conceived as a one-book game line, and was never supported by official supplements. The Trinity Universe line was discontinued shortly after the game's publication, despite a strong and vocal fanbase, but recently a d20 system version was released. In 2002, Adventure! won the Origins Award for Best Role-Playing Game of 2001.

[edit] Setting

The game is set in the 1920s and therefore at the origin of the Trinity Universe timeline. The release of unknown Telluric energy has led to the appearance of a number of particularly gifted characters, whose actions have now the potential for shaping the future history of the world. The Aeon Society for Gentlemen, founded by philanthropist Maxwell Mercer, is trying to bring together as many "Inspired" characters as possible, supporting and coordinating them in their effort for the betterment of all of humanity. But darkness lurks, and Mercer's champions will have to face it in the most unlikely locales. There are three archetypes ("character classes") for players to choose from:

  • Daredevils - whose only "powers" revolve around luck and incredible skill.
  • Mesmerists - the precursors to Trinity's Psions, masters of the powers of the mind.
  • Stalwarts - the precursors of Aberrant's Novas, capable of superhuman feats.

[edit] Game system

Set to emulate the exploits of Saturday matinees serials and pulp magazine action, Adventure! uses a very loose and flexible variant of the White Wolf proprietary Storyteller System. A number of game tweaks have been added to the system to allow both the over-the-top action and the plot twists of the pulps. In this sense, Adventure! has been the first game in which rules included extensive and consistent means through which the players could take control - if briefly - of the story narrated by the Storyteller.
