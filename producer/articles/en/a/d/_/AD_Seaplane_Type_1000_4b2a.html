The AD Seaplane Type 1000 also known as the Admiralty Type 1000 and the AD.1 (from Air Department) was a British seaplane of the First World War designed to attack German warships. When it first flew, was the largest British aircraft yet to take to the air.

[edit] Development

The design of the AD.1 was by Harris Booth of the Admiralty's Air Department just prior to World War I. It was the world's first aircraft designed from scratch as a torpedo bomber one of three versions of the plane planned. The other two were a bomber and a plane armed with a recoilless Davis 12-pounder gun (approximately 76 mm calibre.

The aircraft was a float-equipped biplane of pod-and-boom design, with engines mounted at the front of both booms, as well as at the rear of the crew pod. Development began in 1915; it was completed and flown for the first time during the summer of 1916. The Davis gun would project a blast rearwards so the weapon was changed for a conventional 12-pounder "Naval Landing Gun" though in practice a gun was never installed in the AD.1.[1]

Initially the plane was to use Sunbeam engines, the Nubian but this was delayed and a Hispano-Suiza selected instead, though eventually the Sunbeam Nubian II pf 150 hp was delivered in 1917. [2]

[edit] Service

Seven aircraft were ordered from J. Samuel White, but the performance of the first one delivered proved so poor; the machine was found to be too heavy, and its alighting gear too flimsy that the other machines were cancelled. The sole example is known to have survived until 1916, probably at the Royal Naval Air Service's Seaplane Experimental Station, Felixstowe base.
