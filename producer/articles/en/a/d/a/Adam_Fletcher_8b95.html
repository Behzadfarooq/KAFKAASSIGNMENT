Adam Fletcher (b. 3 March 1975) is a lecturer and author in the fields of education, democracy building, and youth development. As the founder and executive director of CommonAction, a U.S. nonprofit civic engagement organization, Fletcher is an outspoken advocate for youth voice and against adultism.

[edit] Biography

Fletcher was born in Calgary, Alberta and grew up there and in North Omaha, Nebraska. After graduating from Omaha North High School, Fletcher earned his B.A. at The Evergreen State College. Fletcher worked in youth-serving nonprofit organizations in Nebraska and Washington, and served in AmeriCorps programs in Nebraska, Washington, and New Mexico. He has worked for the Washington State Office of Superintendent of Public Instruction, the Points of Light Foundation, and the Corporation for National and Community Service. Fletcher started The Freechild Project in 2001. Currently Fletcher is the Executive Director of CommonAction, in addition to partnering with Youth On Board, and serving as a contributing editor to the academic journal Review of Education, Pedagogy and Cultural Studies and on the boards of the Generation Y Foundation and the National Youth Rights Association. His graduate studies are located at the University of Washington in educational leadership and policy studies[1].

[edit] Notability

Through his work with The Freechild Project, a CommonAction program focused on engaging young people in social change, and SoundOut, focused student voice in schools, Fletcher is a recognized international authority on youth. The United States Department of Education has cited Fletcher's work related to student voice[2], along with state education agencies[3] [4] and national education associations[5]. Additionally, Fletcher has been cited[6] [7] for his work with focused on youth engagement in social change. Fletcher has been featured in one newspaper[8], and is regularly refered to expert on a variety of youth issues by magazines[9] [10], newspapers across the country,[11] [12] [13] and youth themselves.[14]
