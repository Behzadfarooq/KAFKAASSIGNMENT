Adam Jacobs

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Adam Jacobs (born June 4, 1984, in Brooklyn, New York) is the co-founder and Editor-in-Chief of The Final Sprint. Jacobs is a graduate of Indiana University and a J.D. candidate at Rutgers University School of Law - Newark. He grew up in both Staten Island, NY and Manalapan, NJ, and currently lives in Jersey City, NJ. Jacobs also founded Seize the Day Fitness, a privately-owned personal training and nutritional counseling company, and executed the "Race to Protect Human Rights" philanthropy.

[edit] Race to Protect Human Rights

In the fall of 2005, Jacobs created and administered the Race to Protect Human Rights; a philanthropy that secured sponsorships for him to run the Philadelphia Marathon on November 20, 2005. The revenue raised through these sponsorships was donated to Human Rights Watch, a Nobel Peace Prize-winning human rights organization and Direct Relief International, a not-for-profit that works to improve the health of people living in developing countries and those who are victims of natural disasters, war, and civil unrest.

In an interview about the philanthropy, Jacobs explained[1] why running a marathon was so fitting for his cause:

“ I believe that it [marathon running] is an activity that is analogous to life. In a marathon there can be tough inclines and rough terrain and times when you don't think you'll be able to go any further. However, with some determination and a little help from the aid stations along the way, you can cross that finish line and realize your goal. The same can be said for life, which has both its extraordinary peaks and intense valleys. Regardless of one’s situations, I believe that with some inner strength, courage and encouragement from those whom you love ... people can achieve anything. ”

The Race to Protect Human Rights received sponsorships from individuals, families, corporations and celebrities; including substantial donations from Robert F. Kennedy, Jr.[2], comedian Dave Attell[3] , and musician Max Weinberg[3], despite suffering from intestinal damage[4], and raised approximately $8,000 (including some donations that came in weeks following the event) for the two benefit organizations.[5]
