Adam Cvijanovic (b.1960, Cambridge, Massachusetts) is a painter based in New York City.Personal life:Married to cabaret singer Peri Lyons from 1992-2007. Separated, no children.

Cvijanovic studied at the Philadelphia College of Art (1982) and Columbia University (1986), although his approach to painting is often described as "self taught". His practice stems from his early career as a professional mural painter.

Cvijanovic’s work has been shown widely internationally. His paintings have featured at several important galleries and museums including the PS1 Contemporary Art Center in New York, the UCLA Hammer Museum in Los Angeles, and the "USA Today" exhibition at The Royal Academy in London. He is represented by Bellwether Gallery in New York.

Borrowing from trompe l’oeil architectural decoration and the artificiality of movie backdrops, Cvijanovic reinvents the site-specific genre of mural art by working on Tyvek sheeting (a plastic used by FedEx), which is easily transported and reshaped to fit a variety of gallery spaces. Depicting vast desolate suburb-scapes, Cvijanovic’s images merge the high drama of Hollywood disaster films with the gravitas of historical religious painting.

Though working on a massive scale, Cvijanovic prizes artistic authenticity: he often doesn’t use assistants and seldom drafts from photographs. Allowing his internalised images to materialise with the subtlety of individual expression, Cvijanovic’s paintings are not only epic tableaux, but extravagant feats of skill.

Using the muted palette of antique frescos and complex multi-point perspective, Cvijanovic adopts the aesthetic of the Renaissance era, infusing his kitsch subjects with classical deportment.

Combining pop culture with art history, Cvijanovic’s practice amalgamates both European and US traditions, from the celestial aura of cathedral murals, the August landscapes of the Hudson River School to the forlorn solitude of Edward Hopper’s small towns.

His apocalyptic panoramas encapsulate the splendour and horror of a new American wilderness, literally engulfing the viewer in all its commercialised and plastic glory.
