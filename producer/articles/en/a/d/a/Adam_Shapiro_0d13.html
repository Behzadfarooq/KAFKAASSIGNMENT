Adam Shapiro (1972- ) born in Brooklyn, NY, is a co-founder of the International Solidarity Movement (ISM), a pro-Palestinian organization, the stated mission of which is to resist the Israeli occupation using nonviolent tactics, but which is regarded by some as condoning terrorism, a charge ISM vigorously denies. He became famous for staying with Yasser Arafat in his Mukataa compound in Ramallah while it was besieged by the Israeli Army.

[edit] Early life and family

Shapiro was brought up in a non-observant Jewish home, but he does not consider himself an adherent of Judaism.

His interest in the Palestinian cause started with his involvement with "Seeds of Peace," an organization that sought to foster dialogue between Jewish and Palestinian youth, where he met his wife, American-born of Palestinian Christian extraction, Huwaida Arraf. She is also a co-founder of the ISM.

Shapiro received his A.B. in political science and history from Washington University in St. Louis in 1993. He subsequently spent a year studying Arabic in Yemen after receiving his M.A. in Arabic studies at Georgetown University in Washington DC, he received his M.A. in political science from New York University. Shapiro is currently pursuing a doctorate at American University in Washington.

Both his parents are public school teachers, and his brother and family spokesman is an attorney.

[edit] 2002 stay with Arafat and media coverage

Shapiro was in Ramallah in March 2002 during Israeli action there. He was able to enter the compound where Yasser Arafat was, and remained for some 25 hours. He emerged, and gave extensive news conferences not only discussing his experiences (by his own statement, he had breakfast with Arafat), but vilifying the Israelis for their actions, equating their actions to those of the Nazis. A firestorm promptly erupted, with opinion as to Shapiro polarizing immediately.

One New York City paper, the New York Post, consistently refers to Shapiro as The Jewish Taliban, comparing him with John Walker Lindh. In another paper, the NY Newsday, writer Sheryl McCarthy stated on April 4, 2002, "In a season already long on heroes, this makes him another one in my book." [1]

It is not clear whether Shapiro intention in staying with Arafat was to act as a human shield, as some news media reported. His family in Brooklyn received death threats after this event, and even temporarily relocated out of fear despite NYPD officers guarding their house, according to the New York Times.[1]

[edit] Controversial statements

Shapiro has compared the demolition of homes of Palestinian families to Kristallnacht, the brutal, nationwide destruction of Jewish homes, businesses, and synagogues in Germany (including occupied Austria) in 1938, which left many Jews dead and many more deported to concentration camps.

Other statements made by Shapiro have encountered widespread controversy: according to a 2003 profile of ISM in The Star (Jordanian newspaper), Shapiro "justifies the Palestinian armed resistance against Israel as long as it is targeting Israeli soldiers and Jewish settlers in the West Bank and Gaza Strip." [2]

Shapiro wrote: “Palestinian resistance must take on a variety of characteristics, both violent and nonviolent.” and confirmed that he had written this in a CNN interview in which he also explained that it had been taken out of context. http://transcripts.cnn.com/TRANSCRIPTS/0205/10/ltm.08.html

According to The New Republic magazine, "Shapiro and his fiance (Huwaida Arraf) are indeed activists, just not for peace." http://www.tnr.com/doc.mhtml?i=20020429&s=notebook042902twp

In an article in they published in the Palestine Chronicle, Shapiro and Arraf wrote: " "[Nonviolent resistance] is no less noble than carrying out a suicide operation." home.comcast.net/~jat.action/ISM_essay_ref07.htm, "Why Nonviolent Resistance is Important for the Palestinian Intifada: A Response to Ramzy Baroud," Huwaida Arraf and Adam Shapiro appeared in the Palestine Chronicle on January 29, 2002, http://palestinechronicle.com/story-20020129050221695.htm

The article also said, "Let us reiterate, we accept that Palestinians have a right to resist with arms..."

[edit] Other activities

Shapiro has also worked extensively in other conflict zones, namely Iraq, Darfur, Afghanistan, and most recently, Lebanon. He has co-directed two critically acclaimed documentaries, "About Baghdad" and "Darfur Diaries", which expose the dire humanitarian situations in Iraq and Sudan and spotlight the victims' personal lives and stories. Shapiro says that he acts "as a human being, as an American who has grown up with freedom -- seeing what's happening, the injustice, and wanting to do something about it."[citation needed]
