Ada Blackjack, (1898-1983) was an Inuit woman who lived for two years as a castaway on uninhabited Wrangel Island in northern Siberia.

[edit] Biography

Ada Blackjack Johnson was born in Solomon, Alaska on May 10, 1898. In 1921 she joined an expedition to Russiaâ€™s Wrangel Island lead by Canadian Allan Crawford but financed, planned and encouraged by Vilhjalmur Stefansson. By that time, Ada was already a widow with a son, in desperate need for money.

On 16 September 1921 a team of five people was left on Wrangel Island north of Siberia, with the intention of claiming the island for Canada or Britain: Ada who had been hired as a cook and seamstress,three American men, one of whom had, in 1914, spent eight months on the Island after surviving the shipwreck of the Karluk and a Canadian man Allan Crawford.

The conditions soon turned bad for the four men and Ada. They were unable to kill enough game on the island to survive after rations ran out, so on 28 January 1923 three men made an attempt to 700 miles of pack ice to Siberia for help and food, leaving the fourth (sick) man and Ada behind. The sick man was afflicted with scurvy and was cared for by Ada until he died in April 1923. The other three men were never seen again. And so, Ada was utterly alone.

Ada learned how to survive in the icy world until she was rescued on 19 August 1923 by a former colleague of Stefansson's, Noice. In some newspapers she was called a real "female Robinson Crusoe". Ada used the money she saved to take her small son Bennett to Seattle to cure his tuberculosis and had another son Billy. Eventually Ada returned to the Arctic where she lived until the age of 85. She was quiet and hated the media circus that developed around her and the attempts by her rescuer Noise and Stefansson to exploit her story. Except for the salary that she made on the trip and a few hundred dollars for furs that she trapped while on Wrangel, Ada did not benefit from the subsequent publication of very successful books and articles concerning this disastrous voyage.
