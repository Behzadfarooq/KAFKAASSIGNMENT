Adam Rich (born on October 12, 1968) is an American actor born in Brooklyn, New York City. He is arguably best remembered for playing youngest son Nicholas Bradford on the 1970s television drama Eight is Enough, and his stint on the short-lived Lorne Greene vehicle Code Red. He also made appearances on The Love Boat, Fantasy Island, The Six Million Dollar Man and Silver Spoons.

In the years following Eight is Enough, Rich has faced substance abuse issues and run-ins with the law. He has also checked himself into rehab clinics such as the Betty Ford Center. In 2003, he appeared in the film Dickie Roberts: Former Child Star. During the closing credits of Dickie Roberts was a music video featuring many former child stars. Rich's lyrics were "Eight rehabs isn't enough, but I've done and seen the most killer stuff".

In 1996, Might Magazine published an article detailing Rich's robbery-related death at the hands of an "unemployed dinner theater stagehand" and meticulously reconstructed the final years of his life, including his work on an uncompleted independent film known as "The Squatter Project." The story was an elaborate hoax, concocted by a childhood friend of Rich's who worked at the magazine, and fleshed out with Rich's cooperation. The issue was intended as a satire of death issues published by the likes of Spin and Rolling Stone when popular rock stars die.

This event is chronicled in Dave Eggers's A Heartbreaking Work of Staggering Genius, with semi-factual explanation of the events and exposition on the significance behind such an event. Eggers was a founder of Might Magazine.

Adam's mother, Fran, helped Kirk Cameron (and his mother, Barbara) and is probably the reason for his success. This information is found in A Full House of Growing Pains, by Barbara Cameron.
