Adamou Ndam Njoya

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Adamou Ndam Njoya (b. 8 May 1942) is a Cameroon lawyer, author, professor, politician, and former presidential candidate.

[edit] Education

Ndam Njoya was born at Njika, Foumban, West Province, Cameroon, on 8 May 1942, and is currently married with five children. He received his early education at Foumban and Nkongsamba and his undergraduate at General Leclerc College in Yaoundé. He went to France for his advance education, receiving a MA and a Ph.D in public international law and political science at the University of Paris (Panthéon). Ndam Njoya studied diplomacy at the Institut International d'Administration Publique (IIAP), followed by three internships, with the French Ministry of Foreign Affairs, with the French Embassy in London, and with International Organizations at the United Nations European office in Geneva, before returning to Cameroon in 1969.

[edit] Government service

Ndam Njoya was briefly Secretary to the Minister of Foreign Affairs, 1969-1970. He joined the law faculty at the University of Yaoundé in 1970 (a post he still retains). Ndam Njoya then worked for the Carnegie Endowment for International Peace in Cameroon as director of the diplomatic training program, during which time he helped create and was then the first director of the International Relations Institute of Cameroon (IRIC) (1972-1975). In 1975 he was appointed Vice Minister of Foreign Affairs.[1]

In 1977 Ndam Njoya was appointed Minister of National Education. His attempts to introduce stringency and morality into the educational system met with resistance, especially from the wealthy Francophone families who were used to financial incentives determining the success of their children at primary and post-primary institutions. In 1980, under such pressure, President Ahmadou Ahidjo removed him as Minister of National Education and assigned him the post of Minister Delegate at the Presidency in Charge of General Inspection of State and Administrative Reforms, a relatively powerless post.[2] In January 1982, Ahidjo dismissed him from the government.

[edit] Writer

From 1982 to 1990 Ndam Njoya focused on his writing and teaching as well as donating his time to philanthropic endeavors. He has written in the fields of law, political science, history, international relations and political ideas, as well as theatrical pieces, poems, and fiction.[3]

[edit] Politician

In December 1990, President Paul Biya again permitted opposition political parties in Cameroon, and Ndam Njoya started gathering together reformist minded politicians. In July 1991, he was briefly arrested along with other opposition politicians, but was quickly released.[4] In September 1991, he announced the formation of the Democratic Union of Cameroon political party. In his party statements Ndam Njoya has stressed decentralisation of government and respect for the rights of minorities.[5]

During the Presidential Elections of 11 October 2004, all of the opposition parties agreed to support a single candidate against Biya. After Ndam Njoya was chosen by the coalition caucus, John Fru Ndi pulled the Social Democratic Front out of the coalition, and ran separately.[6] As a result Ndam Njoya representing a coalition of political parties, officially received only 4.5% of the vote, coming in third behind the Social Democratic Front candidate John Fru Ndi at 17.4%. President Biya received 70.9 %. As has become standard in Cameroon elections "massive fraud" was alleged. [7]

Since 2004, Ndam Njoya has remained chairman of the Democratic Union of Cameroon, speaking out against corruption in government and continuing to work for decentralisation.[8][9]
