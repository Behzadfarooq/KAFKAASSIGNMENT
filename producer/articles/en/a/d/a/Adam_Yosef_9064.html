Adam Yosef (b. 1981) is a British journalist and community activist.

[edit] Background

Yosef was born in Birmingham and is of Muslim background.

[edit] Community work

Yosef worked at the Birmingham Mosque Trust where duties included PR work for Dr Mohammad Naseem.

He is co-founder of the interfaith Saltley Gate Peace Group, Community & Interfaith Liaison Officer for the Birmingham Stop the War Coalition and was Press Officer for Salma Yaqoob, a Respect - The Unity Coalition candidate in the 2005 election.

[edit] Journalism

Yosef was senior journalist with The Asian Today and Desi Xpress, owned by Urban Media.

He also writes for BBC Online and works closely with BBC Birmingham, with work highlighting issues of socio-politics, diversity, culture, racism and religion.

In 2007, Yosef became the sub editor of Ikonz.


[edit] Controversy

[edit] LGBT Community

[edit] Pride

In June 2005 he published a report of the Pride parade that passed through Birmingham on the BBC Birmingham website. Yosef wrote "I didn't see it as just a celebration of gay identity. I certainly didn't see it as an attempt, acknowledgement or celebration of the acceptance of the gay and lesbian community". He went on to say "to have streets crammed with so many different people all enjoying themselves and accepting each other is, to me, what pride represents." [1]

[edit] Civil partnerships

In the December 2005 issue of the British Asian newspaper Desi Xpress (Issue 42), he made allegedly homophobic remarks about the UK's new civil partnership law for same-sex couples, including remarks such as "Hmmm... gay weddings... Gay people and commitment? I don't think so... They'll be shagging the neighbours before they even cut the cake. Bad idea I'm afraid. Great way of evading tax though...." He later apologised in the same newspaper, claiming his opinions were misinterpreted[2].

[edit] Peter Tatchell

In January 2006, he subsequently wrote another article for the same newspaper on bigotry, in which he named Peter Tatchell, leader of Outrage!, Nick Griffin, the leader of the British National Party, and Omar Bakri Mohammed, leader of Al-Muhajiroun, as the three top "hate filled bigots" in the United Kingdom. He also said that Tatchell "needs a good slap in the face" and that he and his "queer campaign army" should "pack their bent bags and head back to Australia." After complaints that he was homophobic and inciting violence, Yosef apologised [3], saying that the reference to Australia was intended as a reference to the Sydney riots.

Tribune magazine, allied to the left of the Labour Party, criticised Yosef and suggested his NUJ card should be withdrawn [4]. He was further described as "bigoted" by Progress magazine, which is allied to the modernising section of the Labour Party [5]and criticised by the Green Party [6], of which Tatchell is a member. Respect defended Yosef [7], saying he has "the right to voice his own opinions".
