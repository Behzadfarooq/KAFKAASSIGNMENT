Anahuac is an ancient name for a Mesoamerican, particularly Aztec, area or areas, usually identified as located within or even coterminous with the Valley of Mexico. It can also refer to the former lake basins around Mexico City, often including the Lerma and Pánuco river systems.

[edit] 1911 Encyclopedia Britannica article

According to the 1911 Encyclopedia Britannica, Anahuac is "limited by the traditional and vaguely defined boundaries of an ancient Indian empire or confederation of that name previous to the Spanish conquest.

"The word is said to signify 'country by the waters' in Nahuatl, the old Aztec language; hence the theory that Anahuac was located on the sea coast. One of the theories relating to the location of Anahuac describes it as all the plateau region of Mexico, with an area equal to three-fourths of the republic, and extending between the eastern and western coast ranges from Rio Grande to the isthmus of Tehuantepec. A more exact and more commonly used description, however, limits it to the great plateau valley in which the city of Mexico is located, between 18°40' and 20°30'N latitude, about 320 kilometers (200 miles) long by 120 kilometers (75 miles) wide, with an average elevation of 2300 meters (7500 feet), and a mean temperature of 17°C (62°F). The accepted meaning of the name fits this region as well as any on the sea coast, as the lakes of this valley formerly covered one-tenth of its area. The existence of the name in southern Utah, United States, and on the gulf coast of Mexico, has given rise to theories of other locations and wider bounds for the old Indian empire."

[edit] Other usage

Anahuac, as described by the Mexica Movement.


The U.S. based "Mexica Movement" uses the name "Anahuac" as the Nahuatl equivalent for the entire North American continent, including Central America as far as Costa Rica. The term means "Land between the waters." [1]

The group states that the continent of Anahuac "...is united by the Olmec origins of our civilizations, our corn culture, our one race, our linguistic ties, and our common world view that was spread throughout this continent." [2] The movement sees the historical existence of the Mississippian Moundbuilder civilization as justification for including the eastern United States into its definition.

There is an expansionist tone to their usage of this term, since the term "Anahuac" had historically enlarged along with the expansion of the Aztec Empire. It was first applied as a secondary title to the emerging island-city of Tenochtitlan in the 12th century, but by the 16th century, Anahuac referred to the entire imperial domain of the Aztecs (from coast to coast).

The group seeks to reconstruct a new super-nation called Anahuac, one completely free of European (White) presence and influence. The "lingua franca" of this new nation would be the Nahuatl language, while other indigenous languages would continue to exist as regional and sub-ethnic toungues. The citizens of this new nation would be Mexicans, Central Americans, Native Americans, an Canadian First Nations. The national identity would be the name Nican Tlaca, a Nahuatl-language term meaning "We people here." Existing indigenous identities would continue to exist as sub-ethnic groups within a larger national identity.

The group sees this futuristic nation as the reconstitution of the indigenous sovereignty that ended with the arrival of Europeans in 1492. The group sees the demographic expansion of Mexicans and Central Americans within the United States as critical to the achievement of their aims. The group supports the preservation of the US Constitution, which most likely will serve as a transitional legal framework to their new nation. Presumably, this future nation of Anahauc will be enacted through democratic processes in the hands of a Mexican and Central American majority population.
