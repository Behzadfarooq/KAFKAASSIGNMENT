An analog chip is a set of miniature electronic analog circuits formed on a single piece of semiconductor material.

The circuits in analog chips operate with voltage and current varying in a continuous fashion; in contrast, digital chips behave as though the voltages and currents occur only at discrete levels, with no recognition of the transition between levels. Analog chips often have passive elements (Inductor/Capacitors/Resistors) built into them, whereas the most popular, modern digital chips typically don't.

The death of analog chips has been proclaimed regularly, however the field continues to grow and prosper. Some examples of analog chips are the 741 op-amp and the 555 timer. The popular 555 timer was invented by Hans Camenzind in the 1970s.

Important basic building blocks of analog chip design include:

  1. current sources
  2. current mirrors
  3. the differential BJT pair
  4. bandgap references.

People who have specialized in this field include Bob Widlar, Bob Pease, Hans R. Camenzind, George Erdi, and Barry Gilbert among others.
