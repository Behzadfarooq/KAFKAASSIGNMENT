Anal sac adenocarcinoma

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Cytology of an anal sac adenocarcinoma

An anal sac adenocarcinoma is an uncommon and aggressive malignant tumor found in dogs that arises from the tissue of anal sac. At least one case has been reported in a cat.[1]

Anal sac adenocarcinomas first appear as a small lump associated with one of the anal sacs, but they can become quite large. Large tumors can cause pain and straining when the dog is defecating. Between 25 and 50 percent of dogs with these tumors will develop hypercalcaemia (high serum calcium)[2] through secretion of parathyroid hormone-related protein by the tumor.[3] Symptoms of hypercalcemia include increased drinking and urination, vomiting, loss of appetite, weight loss, and bradycardia (slow heart rate). Anal sac adenocarcinomas also tend to metastasize to the lymph nodes, spleen, and lungs.[4] The sublumbar (iliac) lymph nodes are the most common site of metastasis and can become larger than the original tumor.[5]

Treatment is with aggressive surgical removal of the tumor and any enlarged sublumbar lymph nodes. There is a high recurrence rate, although removal of lymph nodes with metastasis may improve survival time.[6] Radiation therapy and chemotherapy may be helpful in treatment. A poor prognosis is associated with large tumor size (greater than 10 cm), hypercalcemia, metastasis to regional lymph nodes, and generalized metastasis.[7]

Breeds that may be more commonly affected include the English Cocker Spaniel, German Shepherd Dog, Alaskan Malamute, Dachshund, and Springer Spaniel.[8]
