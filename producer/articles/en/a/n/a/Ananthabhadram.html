Ananthabhadram (Malayalam: അനന്തഭദ്രം) is a 2005 film about ghosts, spirits and black magic based on the award-winning novel of the same name by Sunil Parameswaran.[1] It is Indian director Santhosh Sivan's first Malayalam venture. Celebrated for his earlier directorial ventures Asoka, and The Terrorist (a.k.a. Malli), Sivan was also the cinematographer for the film. The film was originally going to be directed by Sabu Cyril with actress Meera Jasmine in the lead.[2][3] Ananthabhadram is notable for being the first Malayalam feature screened using satellite technology instead of conventional prints.[4] Aimed at an international market and released as a Ramadan special, it was also dubbed in Tamil, Telugu and English.[5][6] It was also the first Malayalam film for Bengali model-actress Riya Sen.[7]

[edit] Inspiration

Kathakali movements, magic spells and special effects set the tone when Manoj K Jayan abducts Riya Sen.

The film derives much inspiration from arts of India. Sivan says, "I have derived a great deal of inspiration from our arts. We have a rich visual culture and even in Ananthabhadram, I have used certain aspects from Theyyam dancers and Kathakali to create the wizard Digambaran's image. The colour, long nails, kohl-lined eyes and so on were inspired from Theyyam and Kathakali."[8] The sequence between Manoj K Jayan using Riya Sen as a channel for black magic, choreographed by Aparna, the dance director of the film, uses Kathakali movements in particular.[9][5]

Kathakali has become an inspiration for many Indian films, already full of song and dances.[10] In 1999, director Shaji Karun made Vanaprastham, a film themed on Kathakali.[11] In 2005, Adoor Gopalakrishnan made a documentary on the dance form called Vasanthi Sankaranarayan.[12]

Pinakkamano was inspired by Raja Ravi Varma's paintings, like Kavya Madhavan emulating Woman in thought.

The director used three paintings of Raja Ravi Varma - Damayanti and the swan, Lady in thought and Girl carrying milk tray - as inspiration to picturize the song Pinakkamano (acted by Prithviraj Sukumaran and Kavya Madhavan; sung by M. G. Sreekumar and Manjari).[13][14][15] Sivan says, "Yes, it is a tribute to Raja Ravi Varma, who is so intrinsically etched in every Malayali's mind."[16][17]

This interest in the work of Varma, a 19th-century Indian painter who brought Western influences to India and represnted Indian art in the West,[18][19] in popular culture began in the late 1990s, inspired by auctions at Christie's and Sotheby's. In 1998, Indian pop star Phalguni Pathak's music video for the song Meri Chunar Ud Ud Jaaye (acted by Trisha Krishnan) emulated Varma's Shakuntala.[20] In 2003, Kanaka Sabha, the institution devoted to the propagation of Bharatanatyam and Kuchipudi dance forms in Mumbai, presented a reportaire that emulated the artist's works.[21] In 2006, Shaji Karun declared a film to be made on the artist's life, in which Madhuri Dixit (actress of Gaja Gamini, a film by painter M.F. Hussain) is supposed to play the female lead.[22][23][24]

[edit] Plot

The story of Ananthabhadram is that of a fairy tale that happens in a rural Kerala setting dominated by black magicians, martial arts experts, sorcerers and seductresses. Sivan says, "For instance, it was the story of Ananthabhadram that captivated me. The stories my grandmother used to tell me used to take me to a world of mystery. It was the same feeling when I heard this story and I have tried to capture that in my film."[8] Parameswaran says, "The fables that my grandmother narrated to me when I was a kid was there at the back of mind and that has been a source of my stories."[1]

The movie opens with little Ananthan (Prithviraj Sukumaran) hearing a tale from his mother (Revathi). She tells him that his family in the ancient village of Shivapuram comes from a line of powerful magicians. The family is responsible for protecting nagamanikyam (a jewel on the serpent's head), which lies in a secret place in the house guarded by snakes, especially a tiny snake called Kunjootan.

The real story begins when Ananthan retuns to his ancestral village of Sivapuram from the US to light the lamps at the Shiva temple in deference to the long-standing wish of his mother. The local black magician Digambaran (Manoj K Jayan) in order to make his evil desings successful opposes it on the grounds of local superstitions. His plan is to own the nagamanikyam in Ananthan's ancestral house to become all-powerful. Chemban (Kalabhavan Mani), the local martial arts expert stands in Digambaran's way. Eventually the evil black magician manages to remove Cheman from his way by blinding him.

Digambaran lures Chemban's sister Bhama (Riya Sen) in a long sequence that features Kathakali movements, traditional magic spells and special effects. In the end his sinister conspiracy is unravelled by Ananthan and his cousin and lover Bhadra (Kavya Madhavan).

[edit] Reception

Ananthabhadram won five awards in the Kerala State Film Awards for 2005, including best cinematgraphy (Santosh Sivan), best music direction (MG Radhakrishnan), best editing (Sreekar Prasad), best art direction (Sunil Babu) and best make-up (Pattanam Rasheed).[25] It won five awards in the Kerala Film Critics Association Awards 2005, including best film, best director (Santhosh Sivan), best actor (Manoj K Jayan), best actress (Kavya Madhavan), and best cinematography (Santhosh Sivan).[26]
