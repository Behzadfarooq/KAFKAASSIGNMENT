Anablepidae is a family of freshwater and brackish water fishes living on river estuaries from southern Mexico to southern South America.[1] There are three genera with sixteen species: the four-eyed fishes (genus Anableps), the onesided livebearers (genus Jenynsia) and the white-eye, Oxyzygonectes dovii. Fish of this family eat mostly insects and other invertebrates.

Unlike the other Anablepids, the white-eye is oviparous and lacks a gonopodium. Fertilization is external. This fish inhabits Pacific drainages in Nicaragua, Costa Rica, and Panama; it occurs in estuaries, but breeds in freshwater.[1] This fish has been bred in captivity. This fish is not seasonal. White-eyes feeds on detritus, algae, and occasionally insects.[2]

[edit] One-Sided Reproduction

Fish in the subfamily Anablepinae are ovoviviparous. Curiously, they only mate on one side, right-"handed" males with left-"handed" females and vice versa.[3] The male of most species in the family has specialized anal rays which are greatly elongated and fused into a tube called a gonopodium associated with the sperm duct which he uses as an intromittent organ to deliver sperm to the female.
