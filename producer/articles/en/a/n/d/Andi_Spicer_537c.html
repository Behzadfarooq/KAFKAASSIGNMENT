Andi Spicer

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Andi Spicer in London, circa 2004

Andrew John Preston "Andi" Spicer (born 1959 in Birmingham, U.K.), is a British electroacoustic classical music composer who uses electronics (see electronic art music) in his compositions.

The composer is also a writer and journalist. He contributes to The Wall Street Journal and The Gramophone and has written for many international newspapers, magazines and news agencies, including Dow Jones Newswires and The Associated Press.

[edit] History and influences

He studied economics at Aston University in Birmingham and pursued a career in journalism, while composing and performing free form improvised music (see free improvision). He lived in Johannesburg, South Africa between 1996 and 2003, after which he moved back to England. Since then he has been a member of the New Music Brighton[1] and London Forum [2] collectives of composers in the UK. His compositions have been featured at the Brighton Festival, Huddersfield Contemporary Music Festival and at the All Ears Contemporary Music Festival[3] in London, as well as at the Grahamstown Festival in South Africa and performed in France, Mexico and the U.S.

He is largely self-taught, although he took private lessons in composition and music theory with South African composer Martin Watt at the University of the Witwatersrand and took lessons with Polish composer Witold Lutoslawski on aleatoric music. His music uses serialist techniques (see serialism), improvisation, graphic notation, electronics (see electronic art music) and emphasizes surface textures, but is also influenced by southern African and Asian world music.

He is associated with the Gallery III [4] group of artists, musicians and multi-media artists in Johannesburg, South Africa. Spicer is among a new generation of composers in post-apartheid (see apartheid) South Africa. Other examples are Dimitri Voudouris, Jürgen Bräninger, Cobi van Tonder, Hannes Taljaard,[5] Michael Blake (see Michael Blake - Composer)[6]Robert Fokkens and Spicer's teacher Martin Watt.

[edit] Compositions

In Anglo Boer War (1999) he explored cluster note (see tone clusters) and microtonal techniques. The piece is a strident anti-war composition written for the hundredth anniversary of the Anglo Boer War (see Second Boer War) and was a collaboration with the artist James de Villiers.

His 63 Moons (2003) composition was heavily influenced by Javanese gamelan music, Shona mbira music (see Shona music) and contemporary minimalist (see minimalist music) composers.

Click Language (2004) continued Spicer's African themes and uses sampled words from southern African click languages such as Xhosa (see Xhosa language), Zulu (see Zulu language) and Khoisan languages as a sound patina for four percussionists, comprising vibraphone, marimba, waterphone as other hand-held instruments. Baobab (2003) employs polyrhythms inspired by southern African drumming and features the vibraphone and marimba. There is a version of Baobab for harpsichord (2006). He is presently writing an opera for video based on Arno Schmidt's novel The Egghead Republic (Die Gelehrtenrepublik). This will be produced and directed by Peter Gold.

Recent works have explored live electronics and acoustic instrument blends, including midi instruments. Since the beginning of 2006, he has worked closely with woodwind and electronic music soloist Julien Feltrin.[7] Spicer has also worked with London-based percussion ensemble Brake Drum Assembly. [8] He formed the ensemble Caos Harmonia [9] to perform his music in 1997.

[edit] Film and video

Austrian video artist Peter Gold[10] produced a short film for three movements of Anglo Boer War for the 2006 All Ears Contemporary Music Festival in London. Antarctica (1995-1996) is an early work for electronics written for an unreleased video of Antarctic (see Antarctica) landscapes.

[edit] Art installations

Spicer collaborated with performance artist Paolo Giudici in the installation Thesis at the Hockney Gallery at the Royal College of Art in London in 2006. Painter/multimedia artist James de Villiers worked with Spicer in The Architecture of Air,[11] which toured the US, Mexico and South Africa in 2001-2003 with Transformations,[12] an exhibition of South African art. Inside, Outside (2001) is an electronic piece for a James de Villiers' installation of the same title shown at Carfax [13] in Johannesburg.
