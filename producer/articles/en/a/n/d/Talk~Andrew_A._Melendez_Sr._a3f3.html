Rev. Dr. Andrew A. Melendez Sr., born Dec. 29, 1902 - July 24, 1999 in Puerto Rico. Was a Lutheran minister for 69 years, speaker on the Spanish 'Lutheran Hour' (a radio ministry of the Lutheran Laymen's League)named editor of Spanish literature for the Luteheran Church.

 Dr. Melendez received his higher education at the University of Puerto Rico.  In 1930 he graduated from Concordia Theological Seminary in which necessitated his becoming proficient in the German language, the academic language used in the seminary at that time.  That same year he was ordained and commenced his ministry in San Antonio, Texas.  In 1941, because of his neutral Castilian Spanish, he was selected as the first speaker of the Spanish Lutheran Hour program entitled 'Cristo Para Todos Las Naciones' (Christ For All Nations').  This radio program was broadcast in Latin America, the West Indies, the United States, and parts of Spain and the Phillipines, and had an estimated audience of 14 million.  Also in 1941, during WWII, because of his fluency in the English, German and Spanish languages,, he was asked by the United States and Mexican govertments to serve in a special capacity at the U.S./Mexican border for the U.S. Department of Intelligence.  In 1944, he was asked to serve as the editor of Spanish literature for the Lutheran Church Missouri Synod headquarters and in this capacity translated theological books, pamphlets, tracts and Sunday materials from German and English into Spanish and also co-chaired the committee which prepared the first Lutheran Hymnal in Spanish.
 Upon his retirement in 1972. Dr. Melendez continued to serve on a committee to translate into Spanish the 'Book of Concord' the theological book outlining the doctrines of the Luteran Church was published in 1991.
 Throughout his ministry, Dr. Melendez was recognized for his immeasureable contributions as a pioneer in and for the continued expansion of mission work by the Lutheran Church to the Spanish-speaking population worldwide.  In 1953 he received the doctor of divinty degree from Concordia Seminary and 1977 the doctor of letters degree from Concordia Teachers College in recognition of his outstanding service.
