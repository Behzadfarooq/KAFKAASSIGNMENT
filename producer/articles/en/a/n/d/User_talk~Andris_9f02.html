[edit] Cold Fusion

Hello Andris,

I noticed you corrected my "correction" on the cold fusion page. As I understand it, a deuterium-tritium reaction results in the creation of the nuclear byproducts that you talked about (it results in the release of an extra neutron; it is the neutron that collides with the container walls to create the radioactivity.) (A side note: The tritium is already radioactive.) A pure deuterium-deuterium reaction would avoid this as no extra neutron is release, helium being the only byproduct. If I am incorrect in this, I am sorry, I will not revise the page again.

Regards, Kenton

Andris,

I have looked at the web page you referenced. It does indeed state that a D-D reaction can produce an extra neutron. If you read further down the same page, it goes on to say that the only radioactivity concern is actually the Tritium produced in the other reaction (reaction #4 on the site you referenced. Tritium has a relatively short half life (12 years) and is quite easy to control as containment of a reasonably short length of time is sufficient, compared to the products of fission (and the Tritium is now free to participate in a D-T reaction.) The extra neutron of reaction #3 does not seem to be of major concern. My main concern is that the original article gave the impression that some really nasty stuff is created through fusion, while the truth is that fusion would be infinitely cleaner and safer than the fission reactions our current power plants are based on. I am worried that a good idea may face unwarranted opposition.

I have referred the radioactivity question to Bjoern for his opinion. He is a PhD. in physics with some nuclear background and may be able to shed some light. (He certainly has experience beyond my meagre education.)

Kenton

Andris,

Bjoern has looked into the question for me and it turns out there is no way around the production of excess neutrons, and by extension radioactive isotopes. Too bad, I had high hopes. Perhaps the reactor containment could be constructed of a material that produces waste of a very short half life when it absorbs neutrons. This would help to make the situation manageable. I'm digressing, but I still have hope.

Thanks again for your input. It looks like my optimistic edit was premature and the page will remain as written.

Regards, kenton 17:17, 27 Aug 2004 (UTC)

[edit] Article Licensing

Hi, I've started a drive to get users to multi-license all of their contributions that they've made to either (1) all U.S. state, county, and city articles or (2) all articles, using the Creative Commons Attribution-Share Alike (CC-by-sa) v1.0 and v2.0 Licenses or into the public domain if they prefer. The CC-by-sa license is a true free documentation license that is similar to Wikipedia's license, the GFDL, but it allows other projects, such as WikiTravel, to use our articles. Since you are among the top 1000 Wikipedians by edits, I was wondering if you would be willing to multi-license all of your contributions or at minimum those on the geographic articles. Over 90% of people asked have agreed. For More Information:

  • Multi-Licensing FAQ - Lots of questions answered
  • Multi-Licensing Guide
  • Free the Rambot Articles Project

To allow us to track those users who muli-license their contributions, many users copy and paste the "{{DualLicenseWithCC-BySA-Dual}}" template into their user page, but there are other options at Template messages/User namespace. The following examples could also copied and pasted into your user page:

Option 1
I agree to [[Wikipedia:Multi-licensing|multi-license]] all my contributions, with the exception of my user pages, as described below:
{{DualLicenseWithCC-BySA-Dual}}

OR

Option 2
I agree to [[Wikipedia:Multi-licensing|multi-license]] all my contributions to any [[U.S. state]], county, or city article as described below:
{{DualLicenseWithCC-BySA-Dual}}

Or if you wanted to place your work into the public domain, you could replace "{{DualLicenseWithCC-BySA-Dual}}" with "{{MultiLicensePD}}". If you only prefer using the GFDL, I would like to know that too. Please let me know what you think at my talk page. It's important to know either way so no one keeps asking. – Ram-Man (comment) (talk)[[]] 15:37, Dec 9, 2004 (UTC)
