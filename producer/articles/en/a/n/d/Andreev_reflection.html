Andreev reflection is a special type of particle scattering which occurs at interfaces between superconductors or superconductor-normal metal interfaces. In such a reflection process an electronic excitation incident on the interface is retro-reflected and converted into a hole and vice versa. It is named after the Russian physicist Alexander F. Andreev, who discovered it as a graduate student.

[edit] Assumptions

To keep the discussion simple we focus in the following on an interface between a normal-metal and a s-wave/conventional superconductor (NS-interface). The interface is assumed to be transparent, that is, it does not include any tunneling barrier due to an oxide layer for instance.

[edit] Background

Superconductors have an energy gap Î” in the density of states at the Fermi energy. Hence, there are no single particle states at those energies. A normal metal, however, has all electron states filled up to the Fermi energy. Connecting these two materials there will be electrons moving from the normal metal towards the superconductor at subgap energies. The absence of states with the same energy in the superconductor forbids a transmission of such electrons. Single particle transmissions are therefore forbidden.

[edit] Andreev reflection

However, there is another, higher-order two-particle process possible. By transferring two electrons at the same time, the incident and another one, a Cooper pair can be formed in the superconductor. In view of a single particle picture, the second additional electron corresponds to a reflected hole in the normal metal. Note that electric charge is conserved in this process: the electron charge e becomes 2e for the Cooper pair and negative e for the reflected hole.

[edit] Properties

  • In this reflection process, all components of the velocity vector are reversed: the hole travels along the same line as the incident electron. (This is strictly true only for electrons at the Fermi energy. At different energies there is a slight mismatch, on the order of distance from the Fermi energy over Fermi velocity, between electron and hole momentum which causes a deviation from this trajectory.)
  • To form a Cooper pair which has zero total spin, the electron and the reflected hole are taken from opposite spin bands. For this reason, Andreev reflection is suppressed if the metal is a ferromagnet and only a single spin band is occupied.
  • With increasing barrier strength between the materials, the Andreev reflection is suppressed in favour of usual reflections electron to electron and hole to hole.
  • Time reversal symmetry requires that there exists also an Andreev reflection in which a hole is reflected into an electron. Its properties correspond to those of the above discussed Andreev reflection with a conversion from electron to hole.
  • The phase difference between the electron and hole is -Ï€/2 plus the phase of the superconducting order parameter.

Andreev reflection is the mechanism by which a superconductor influences an adjacent normal metal (an effect known as the superconducting proximity effect). The Andreev reflection between two opposite NS interfaces transfers a supercurrent from one superconductor to the other, an effect known as the Josephson effect.

Andreev reflection has an analogue in optics, known as optical phase conjugation.
