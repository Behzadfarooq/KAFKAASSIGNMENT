Android Nim

Android Nim was a game for the TRS-80 computer in which the player competed with the computer in removing a series of androids from the screen to be the one who removes the last one.

At the beginning of each game, the player is asked whether to play first or allow the computer to do so. Then eighteen animated androids are drawn on the screen in three rows, each with one "gunner" android, and seven, five, and three androids respectively. When the player's turn is reached, a row number is typed to begin, which is displayed to the left of that row's gunner android. Then the player types the number of androids to be removed from that row. The gunner android is then animated as if to look at the number, and then to look at its row of androids. If the play is not possible because there are not enough androids on that row, the gunner then shakes its head "no", and play must proceed with a viable number. When an appropriate number is played, the gunner then nods its head and raises a gun towards its androids. All other androids on the screen are then animated to look at the selected row, and that rows androids look at their gunner. Then a laser beam comes from the gun and dispatches that number of androids, one at a time. Then play returns to the computer while the same animations are used.

Play continues until the last android is removed from the screen, the player doing so being the winner. The game was programmed so that the computer was amusingly a poor sport, displaying a huge "I WIN!" graphic on the screen when the computer won, and displaying a series of randomly selected adjectives of astonishment when the player would happen to win. Also, when the computer detected imminent failure, it would pretend to seek in futility for ways to avoid losing, by selecting more androids than were available on a given row several times until "giving up".

The animation of this game was impressive for the 128 by 48 pixel display. Throughout the play of the game, or it was left idle, the androids were animated to gradually face in different directions, almost out of boredom or engaging in conversation with one another. The game itself was plain, but the animation was the game's most admired feature.
