Annette K. Ziegler (born 1964) is an American attorney and judge.

Since 1997, Ziegler has served as a Circuit Court Judge in the state of Wisconsin (for Washington County, based in West Bend.)

Ziegler is a candidate in a high-profile race for an open seat on Wisconsin's State Supreme Court.

[edit] Career

[edit] Legal career

Ziegler received a Bachelor of Arts degree in Business Administration and Psychology from Hope College, and a law degree from Marquette University.

She was admitted to the Wisconsin State Bar in 1989. While in law school she was a staff editor of the law review, as well as a recipient of the Dean's Award.

Before serving in the judiciary, Ziegler worked as a Federal Criminal Prosecutor, and an Assistant United States Attorney, in the Eastern District of Wisconsin. She was also a pro bono Special Assistant District Attorney in the Milwaukee County District Attorney’s Office. In private practice, she was a civil private practice attorney for several years at the law firm of O’Neil, Cannon, Hollman & DeJong, SC.

[edit] Philanthropy

Ziegler's campaign website lists a number of community and philanthropic activites for her, including:

  • President and Board of Trustees for Boys & Girls Club of Washington County
  • West Bend Noon Rotary Club, Paul Harris Fellow
  • St. Thomas More Sandwich Project for Homeless People
  • Past board member for Washington County Volunteer Center
  • Past committee chair for Law Explorers Boy Scouts of America
  • Cedar Lake Conservation Foundation
  • Past Volunteer Judge for Moot Court Competition
  • Sunday School Teacher, Trinity Lutheran Church

[edit] Wisconsin Supreme Court Candidacy

Ziegler and fellow candidate Linda Clifford won the two berths in the primary voting in February; the vote for the seat will occur on April 3, 2007.

The campaign has become a contentious one. Clifford contends Ziegler ruled in cases where she was unable to be objective. [1] Ziegler contends Clifford has never been a judge and would be ill-prepared to do so; she also raised concerns about two of Clifford's campaign workers misrepresenting themselves to law enforcement officials. [2]
