Ann Lambton

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

For the British actress, see Anne Lambton.

Professor Ann Katharine Swynford Lambton, OBE FBA (born 8 February 1912) is a retired British historian and leading figure on medieval and early modern Persian history, Persian language, Islamic political theory, Persian social organisation, a universal authority on land tenure and reform in Iran, Seljuq, Mongol, Safavid and Qajar administration and institutions, and local and tribal histories.

Lambton is the eldest daughter of Hon. George Lambton (himself a younger son of the 2nd Earl of Durham) and a cousin of Antony Lambton. She studied at SOAS under Sir Hamilton Gibb. From 1939–46, she was Press Attaché of the British Legation to Tehran and then Professor of Persian at the School of Oriental and African Studies, University of London from 1953–79 succeeding Vladimir Minorsky as holder of the chair. In 1943, she was awarded the OBE and holds honorary DLitt degrees from the University of Durham and the University of Cambridge. She is also an honorary fellow of New Hall, Cambridge, the School of Oriental and African Studies and the University of London.

Ann Lambton played a role in overthrowing the democratically elected government of Mohammed Mossadegh. After the decision to nationalize Iran's oil interests, she advised the British government to undermine the authority of Mossadegh's regime. She suggested Oxford University professor R. C. Zaehner to go to Iran and begin covert operations. With the help of the CIA, the regime of Mossadegh was overthrown and the Shah of Iran was in power.

A spinster in her ninety-fourth year, she currently lives at Kirknewton, Northumberland and is Professor Emeritus of the Diocese of Newcastle. Formerly Chairman of the Iran Diocesan Association, she served on the Middle East Committee and has advised Archbishops on inter-faith matters. She has delivered Lent lectures biannually to clergy and laity for many years. She was awarded the Cross of St. Augustine in 2004 by the Archbishop of Canterbury in acknowledgement of her work and commitment to Christianity and the Church of England. She is a honorary Life Member of the North American Middle East Studies Association, MESA. At the University of Durham, the Centre of Iranian Studies has instituted an annual Prof. A. K. S. Lambton honorary lectureship. Prof. Lambton delivered the inaugural lecture in this series in 2001.
