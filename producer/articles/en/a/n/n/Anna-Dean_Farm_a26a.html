The Anna Dean Farm of Ohio Columbus Barber

American industrialist and founder of Barberton, O. C. Barber first began to acquire land on the east side of Barberton with the idea of building a super farm, as early as 1900. Construction on his 3500 acre estate would not begin in earnest until 1909, however in 1907 he built a concrete poultry wall on the property near the old Kepler homestead.

With the construction of the poured concrete poultry building in 1907 it seemed to unleash something in the mind of Ohio C. Barber. Certainly in its own right the 22,000 square foot concrete poultry building was one of the biggest chicken facilities ever built, even if it was a bland rather non-descript structure with poured walls and a wooden roof.

Suddenly there was a major building boom unleashed on the Anna Dean Farm that ran from April of 1909 until the winter to 1912. Articles at the time mention that a $1000.00 per day was spent in construction, with over $7 million being spent in all to build and outfit the Anna Dean Farm. All in all, when completed, O. C. Barber would have built 35 buildings on the Anna Dean Farm, most of them in the French Colonial style with heavy Beaux-Arts influence. O. C. Barber's book, “Anna Dean Farm - The Story of An American Farm.” quotes there as being 102 buildings on the Anna Dean Farm. No doubt there were 102 buildings, but the remaining 67 of these buildings were pre-existing homes, barns, stables and sheds that were incorporated into the Anna Dean Farm from the previously existing farms, that now made up the collective Anna Dean Farm.

Most of the buildings Barber built were built of red brick, white concrete block and trim, with red French style tile roofs, and supported royal blue doors and window trim, making the buildings appear to be red, white and blue.

Considered garish by some, Barber dismissed any criticism of his buildings as being unpatriotic. Barber could trace his ancestry to the Mayflower, but he firmly believed that had he not been born in America, that he could never have accomplished the great things he did in his lifetime, because he was born as an American.

The crowning achievement of the Anna Dean Farm was no doubt the 52 room Beaux Arts style mansion built between April of 1909 and completed on Halloween in 1910. This imposing French style home when completed was dubbed by a New York Times reporter as the "finest mansion between New York and Chicago."

Today only 8 of the 35 original buildings on the Anna Dean Farm remain, of which 5 of the 8 are owned by the Barberton Historical Society, who has been dedicated to their preservation.

On the east side of Fifth Street was the show portion of the Anna Dean Farm. This portion of the Farm was bordered by a split rail concrete fence. The main buildings of the east side of the Farm were the Main Gatehouse, The Farm Managers Home, The Mansion, The Power House, The Apiery, The Machine Barn, Barn No. 2, and the Colt Barn. Unfortunately today, only the Colt Barn remains.

The west side of the Anna Dean Farm was more the working part of the Farm. The west side of the Anna Dean Farm was bounded by Fifth Street on the east, Snyder Ave. on the south, Robinson Ave on the north and the Ohio Canal on the west. This part of the Anna Dean Farm was bordered by a concrete post and steel picket fence. The main buildings on the west side of the Farm were Barn No. 1, Barn No. 3, Bull Barn No 1, the Creamery, the Heating House, the Green Houses, the Brooder Barn, The Poultry Department, and the Piggery. Today, Barn No 1, the Heating House, the Creamery, the Brooder Barn, parts of the Poultry Department, and the Piggery remain. You can see the west side of the Anna Dean Farm is more complete and much easier to explore.

A good place to start any visit to the Anna Dean Farm is to log on to the website of the Barberton Historical Society. You can visit the Anna Dean Farm at www.annadeanfarm.com. If you are in Barberton you can visit the Anna Dean Farm for their annual free walking tour. The Anna Dean Farm tour is always held on the third Sunday in May at 1:30 PM.
