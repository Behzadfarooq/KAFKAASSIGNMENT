[edit] DBL - Institute for Health Research and Development

[edit] History

DBL-Institute for Health Research and Development (DBL) was established in 1964 as the Danish Bilharziasis Laboratory. From an initial focus on control of schistosomiasis only, DBL’s profile has developed to an institute for health research and development.


[edit] Profile

DBL is a cross-disciplinary institution which through research, capacity building and technical and advisory services supplies relevant health and health related knowledge in support of sustainable development.

DBL’s aim is to contribute to improved public health in developing countries, through research and research related activities, with a focus on neglected tropical diseases.

DBL’s philosophy is that knowledge and skills are vital for health development, that research and research capacity are keys to sustainable development and that health development requires cross-disciplinary and inter-sectoral stakeholder collaboration at all levels. Contribution to poverty reduction and reaching some of the Millennium Development Goals is the ultimate goal.

DBL’s aim is achieved through:

Generation and dissemination of new knowledge, methods and tools for promotion of health and for the prevention and control of diseases

Building of research capacity and capacity for integrated disease control at institutions and networks, primarily in Danida programme countries, but also in Denmark


[edit] Research

DBL’s research aims at generating and disseminating new knowledge, methods and tools for promotion of health and for the prevention and control of diseases in developing countries. An integrated, cross-disciplinary and inter-sectoral approach is emphasized.

Special attention is given to: applied research, collaboration with Danida Sector Programme Support, dissemination of research findings to relevant stakeholders, research priority setting processes, to transformation of research findings into practical application.

Health research ethics is a cross-cutting issue. In support of implementation and dissemination, DBL supports several networks in the South and implements agenda setting, information sharing and dissemi¬nation workshops. DBL’s research addresses: Health interventions, Health determinants, Disease mechanisms.

Most DBL research contains research capacity building as an important element.


[edit] Capacity building

DBL’s capacity building programme aims at building research capacity and capacity for integrated disease control at institutions and networks in developing countries. Activities span from training individual students to building general institutional capacity.


[edit] Research capacity building

Courses and workshops, mainly held in developing countries, and support to individual PhD and Master students comprise the core activities. Courses and workshops held in developing countries are always jointly organized and implemented by DBL and the host institution.

DBL also contributes to research capacity building in Denmark. Danish PhD and Master students work with DBL and many teaching and censorship contributions are made at Danish universities.


[edit] Disease control capacity building

Many activities address capacity building for health promotion, disease prevention and control. Central among these is capacity building regarding evidence-based decision making (interface between research and development), control of neglected tropical diseases, health impact assessment (HIA), and health priority setting.

[edit] General capacity building

DBL supports development of institutional, managerial and social capital at key collaborating institutions. Support is provided to general infrastructure upgrading, to curriculum development and to strategy and work plan development.


[edit] Technical and advisory services

The purpose of DBL’s technical and advisory services, including service provision activities, is to deliver expert advice and research-based knowledge to Danida, international organizations and other recipients engaged in the field of sustainable health development in developing countries. The work scope is broad. Activities range from professional contributions to meetings, evaluation of new aid policies, strategies and guidelines and participation in service missions, to the implementation of short- and long-term consultancies on a full commercial basis. Research advisory activities are given high attention.


[edit] Network and partners

DBL has a network and partnership approach to programme and project implementation. DBL’s particular strength is its partnerships with collaborating southern institutions. DBL supports professionally and financially a number of networks anchored in the South, and implements its activities with reference to formal collaborative agreements with Danish universities.

DBL is an active member of a number of Danish networks established to increase partnerships, coordination and communication. DBL is also a member of many international partnerships, and hosts two very active WHO Collaborating Centres and the Mandahl-Barth Centre for Biodiversity and Health. The network approach is also very evident in a number of EU-funded projects anchored at DBL.


[edit] Financial resources

DBL receives core-funding from Danida. Additional funding is received from a number of sources, including among others, the EU-INCO-DEV programme, Bill and Melinda Gates Foundation through the Gates Malaria Partnership and the Schistosomiasis Research Programme, the Council for Development Research, the Knud Højgaard Foundation, the Villum Kann Rasmussen Foundation, the National Institutes of Health (USA) and Danida’s Health Sector Programme Support.

[edit] Human resources

Staff comprises of 15-17 researchers and advisers and 13-14 technical/administrative staff. Staff competencies cover broadly from molecular biology over biomedicine to medical anthropology and health systems.
