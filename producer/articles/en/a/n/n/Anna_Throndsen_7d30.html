Anna Throndsen (also known as Anna Throndssen, Anna Trondsen and Anna Rustung) (born somewhere around 1540; died 1607 or later) was a Norwegian noblewoman. Anna was a member of the distinguished Rustung family, which has links to the Norwegian Galtung family. In the English-language countries, she is famous for her affair with James Hepburn, 4th Earl of Bothwell.

[edit] Background

Anna was the eldest daughter of Kristofer Throndsen Rustung, a famous 16th century Norwegian admiral and nobleman. Their family is part of the larger Galtung clan, which can be traced back to the 10th century, as being very close to the Kings of Norway. After the war with Denmark, Kristofer was appointed to serve King Frederick II as Danish Royal Consul in Copenhagen. Kristofer took his Norwegian family (his wife, Karine was Swedish) to Copenhagen while working as Danish consul. After his death, they returned to Kvinnherrad, around the Bergen area. Anna also returned to Norway, though she had grown up in Denmark, at the end of her affair with Bothwell.

[edit] Affair with Bothwell

Anna was known to have been assisting her father in his work. It is possible that this is how she came to meet Bothwell, who found the young woman attractive and proceeded a relationship with her. Anna followed Bothwell, but in Flanders, he announced to her that he was out of money, presumably he had used it to pay debts. He asked Anna to sell all her possessions, which she did, and she left to return to her family in Denmark to ask for more money. Anna was unhappy and apparently given to complaining about Bothwell.

[edit] Revenge on Bothwell

Jean divorced him for adultery while Bothwell had their marriage annulled on grounds of consanguinity. He proceeded to marry Mary Stuart, Queen of Scots. After his escape from Carberry Hill, Bothwell sailed from Scotland and crashed on the coast of Scandinavia. He was discovered by the local authorities to lack proper travelling papers, and was transported to Bergen, then the capital of Norway. At this point, Anna had returned to Norway, where her entire family had returned upon the death of her father. Anna made a legal case against Bothwell for having taken her money and abandoning her. Bothwell managed to negotiate with Anna, who agreed to release him from his debt for the trade of one of his ships. Yet during the time of his capture, news of his activities in England as a possible murderer of the Queen's husband became known to the Danish King Frederick. The King decided to move Bothwell down to Dragsholm Castle, where he died under terrible conditions, after many years of imprisonment.

[edit] Casket Letters

Anna is rumoured to have authored part or all of the Casket Letters which were attributed to Mary Queen of Scots, and were used in the court case against Mary, to prove her complicity with Bothwell in the murder of her husband, Lord Darnley.
