[edit] About "polemics"

As a person cited on this "Anastasia" page, I object to my article on Franziska Schanzkowska being described as "[an attempt] to reconcile [my] belief that Anna Anderson was Anastasia with the 1994 DNA results." It is, in fact, an effort to demonstrate the impossibility of Anna Anderson and Franziska being the same person, a point on which no one who actually knew AA -- whether they believed she was Anastasia or not -- was in any doubt. It doesn't matter to me whether anyone believes in the Anastasia identity or not -- many do, many don't, and it was always so -- but the Schanzkowska "theory" is and was a pure invention. How they got the DNA to match I have no idea, but it is by no means accepted as final proof by anyone truly familiar with the case. Examples: blood samples of Anna Anderson that were tested at the same time as the hair and intestinal material demonstrated a completely different mtDNA profile than either the hair OR the intestine. It is demonstrated beyond doubt in contemporary records that AA and FS were of different height, different build, different hair color, different eye color, and in no way coincident when it came to bodily marks or, especially, wounds and scars on the body -- AA's body was "covered with scars" and FS's was not -- she was never wounded in the famous factory explosion that was put to forward to account for AA's multiple stab wounds. They can have all the DNA they want -- the Schanzkowska story is pure rubbish. Peter Kurth

The ruling that AA's claim had been "neither established nor refuted" far exceeded the original brief of the German courts to determine whether or not the claim was authentic, inasmuch as her opponents in the lawsuit (actually, the defendants, two members of the House of Hesse) had *countersued,* claiming that she was NOT Anastasia and that she in fact was Franziska Schanzkowska. The High Court at Hamburg, in 1967, declared in its verdict, among other things, that "the death of Grand Duchess Anastasia at Ekaterinburg cannot be accepted as a conclusively proven historical fact." The (then) West German Supreme Court at Karlsruhe, three years later, took it further by adding that the case was "non liquet, unsatisfactory to BOTH parties." For what it's worth, the judges at Karlsruhe actually called AA "Anastasia" while reading the verdict. The "transcript" of the case amounts to more than 60 volumes of evidence and testimony. The Hessian defendants, in the end, were Duchess Barbara of Mecklenburg (granddaughter of Alexandra's sister Irene -- whose own father, Prince Sigismund of Prussia, was a staunch AA supporter) and Prince Ludwig, who entered the case as "voluntary co-defendant." Neither of them ever laid eyes on AA. Peter Kurth

According to Byzantine Canon Law, cremation is not permitted. Sources state that the original ban arose out of consideration for the fact that within pagan and possibly gnostic circles creation was commonly practiced. Today there are those who would propose that the practice of cremation be explored anew, in light of the fact that the practice's association with paganism or gnosticism is no longer a reality. Proponents of this line of thinking may also assert that cremation is a less expensive way to dispose of the body than the increasingly expensive -- and often non-Christian -- burial practices common in many cultures and societies, such as in the United States. I have heard of a few cases in recent times where, in extreme cases and for good cause, cremation has taken place with the knowledge of the Church. In each case, however, I have heard that the actual cremation did not occur until after the funeral service at which the body of the deceased has been present. This does not represent standard practice, however, and in each instance there were extenuating circumstances which led the Church to apply the principle of economia.

I think we should point out that the Russian Church in Exile would have been the one that any living Anastasia would have favored, as it was free from Soviet/KGB/Patriarchate compromises to the faith and practice. Here a priest would give a more appropriate answer about cremation than I, but, even as a traditional Lutheran (as was Anastasia's mother originally) I was raised with the teaching that Cremation was wrong and problematic for one's soul. Church in Exile priests I have spoken to are adamant about the unacceptability of cremation on theological grounds. The body should go naturally back to "dust" as it will be Resurrected (curiously the meaning of ANASTASIA, as in CHRISTOS ANESTI!")at Christ's return.

Anna Anderson would have had no reason for "economia" and her desire for cremation connotes a lack of sensitivity to Orthodox teaching. That would be very unlikely for the true daughter of the Tsar, who was the protector of the Faith. Whatever her place in this mystery, Evgenia Smetisko, another claimant, desired to be buried in traditional Orthodox fashion in an Orthodox cemetery connected with an Orthodox parish in exile.
