Anne Chu (born 1959, New York, US) is an artist based in New York.

Chu graduated from Philadelphia College of Art [1] in 1982 and received her MFA from Columbia University [2] in 1985.

She has shown work internationally in many exhibitions including “Prop Fiction” at White Columns [3] in New York “New Young Artists” at the Saatchi Gallery [4] in London and “On Paper 1” at Galerie Friedrich [5] in Basel. She is represented by Victoria Miro [6] in London and 303 Gallery [7] in New York.

Anne Chu makes painted sculptures from a variety of materials resulting in work which embraces references from a huge selection of cultural standpoints. The skill in Chu’s art lies in her ability to blend Eastern and Western visual and mythical heritage without it seeming forced or inappropriate. Add to this various nods to classic painting and traditional storytelling, and it would be easy to assume that Chu has set herself too large a task of synthesis. However, the success of Chu’s art speaks for itself: known most widely for her paper-maché bears and chainsaw-ed wooden sculptures and marionettes, her output delivers a host of contradictions in perfect balance. She makes seemingly solid objects from delicate materials, unites Velasquez and the Tang Dynasty and her journeys into areas of highly skilled craft (puppet-making, for example) are often left unfinished. This eclectic mix of allusions and contexts gives Chu’s work an atmosphere of narrative-in-the-making, a snapshot of a carefully staged tale which the audience has to complete for themselves. Often curiously unbalanced due her choice of incongruous materials, Chu’s scenes consist of figures in equal part Eastern and Western, primitive and futuristic, whole yet incomplete. This scattered frame of references and genres resonates in a world where identities are fluid and personal histories are an assortment of cultures, geographies and relationships. Chu’s work straddles the fine line between the traditional and the contemporary, forgetting neither, while well aware that it is impossible to have one without the contrast of the other.
