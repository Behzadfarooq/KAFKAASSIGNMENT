What is "Byelorussian"? --rydel 15:54, 13 Apr 2005 (UTC)

The Byelorussian language, the language of Belarussia (White Russia), of course. Jordi·✆ 16:49, 13 Apr 2005 (UTC)
Wow! How very interesting. Are you from White Holland by any chance? --rydel 18:24, 13 Apr 2005 (UTC)
That's BELAholanda (formerly transcribed BYELOhollanda), thank you very much! And no, I'm from Yellow Germany. Jordi·✆ 22:14, 13 Apr 2005 (UTC)
Sehr witzig. On a more serious note, I recommend that you open the two articles: Belarus and White Russia, read it carefully and finally learn the difference. I know it's difficult for people from countries like Holland, White Holland and Small Holland, but no, Belarus does not really mean Whtie Russia. I'm sorry to disappoint you. And "Byelorussian" is not the correct form of that adjective in English. --rydel 01:15, 14 Apr 2005 (UTC)
You seem to not understand the importance of the combine name and the hyphen. White-Russia is something else completely than 'White Russia'. I ensure you White-Russia (Weiss-Rußland, Wit-Rusland) is seen by people as the name of a country. If it were seen as "White Russia", for "a part of Russia that is white", it would not be hyphenated. If the argument is taken the hyphen does not matter (which breaks language rules), is the Russian Federacy "Green-Russia" or something?
Additionally, Belarussia itself opts to use Weiss-Rußland for its German embassies, and Wit-Rusland for its Dutch embassies. I am not that good in modern Greek, but Λευκορωσία (the name Belarussia uses in Greece) would transcribe as Leukorosía, or White 'rosía' (Russia).
Take a look at Etymology of Rus and derivatives to see why Rus' became Russia in the west. Names like White Russia/Weiss-Rußland/Wit-Rusland/Λευκορωσία are not artifacts of the Russian Empire or Soviet Union, but natural names for the region and country. Just like the English call Deutschland Germany (land of the 'Germans', for a tribe which once lived there), the French call it Allemagne (land of the 'Allemans', another tribe), and you call it Niamieccyna (land of the mutes).
You won't succeed in changing the name of your country in other languages by yourself. First convince your government to start calling itself Belarus everywhere, and then over time it will change. Look at Myanmar/Burma: the name was changed in 1989, but today (16 years later) most people still refer to it as "Burma". It will take another few decades before the name change becomes final. Jordi·✆ 01:43, 14 Apr 2005 (UTC)
On the Myanmar/Burma thing, a lot of the reason why people, at least here in Norway, use Burma instead of Myanmar, is because of their undemocratical government, and their reasons for changing the name. Jon Harald Søby 14:38, 1 May 2005 (UTC)

[edit] Image:Saddamstatue.jpg

Let's please stick to a straightforward NPOV description for the caption of this image. Otherwise someone else could just as easily try to apply Category:Propaganda to say, Image:Anti war protesters melbourne.jpg, and claim this was a "staged" propaganda event too. Wartime photos of any kind could be susceptible to labels of "propaganda" and staging, such as the well-known photo of Russian soldiers raising the Soviet flag on the Reichstag building in Berlin or even McArthur wading ashore on his return to the Philippines.

There's a reason for the NPOV policy, and as a longtime contributor you are surely aware of this. Image captions in particular are mainly for the purpose of identification and showing credits and copyright status; the usual place for describing historical events (and any claims or counterclaims about them) is within some article. -- Curps 17:45, 23 Apr 2005 (UTC)
