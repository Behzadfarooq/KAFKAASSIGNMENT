[edit] History

Anomie Theory is based in large part on the works of French sociologist Émile Durkheim. Durkheim first coined the term anomie in his 1893 treatise, "The Division of Labour In Society". Anomie is a state of being where societal norms are unclear or not present. It can also be defined as a general state of lawlessness. Durkheim reasoned that as society grows more and more complex (from, as he puts it, the "mechanical" to the "complex"), people stop behaving in such a fashion that benefits the group, and they start behaving in their own interests. As people (and their jobs) become more complex and individualized, the interpersonal bonds between them begin to break down. Without these bonds, and without clear rules to guide them, these people cannot find their place in society, resulting in conflict and deviant behavior. Durkheim went on to conclude that anomie was present during periods of great social unrest. As the rules governing society broke down as a result of this unrest, one would then see anomie in the form of an according rise in episodes of crime, violence, etc.

[edit] Merton's Anomie Theory

Durkheim did not invent what we know today as Anomie Theory, however (in fact, Durkheim was most concerned with using his new term to explain suicide, rather than any legal application). The theory was first coined by Criminologist Robert Merton. Merton went on to redefine the term in regards to industrialized countries in general, America in particular. Merton defined Anomie as difference between what people want to do and what they must do to achieve them. Merton's Anomie is further broken down into macroside(when society fails to set clear rules on how to achieve certain goals, and cannot thusly regulate members of society), and microside, more commonly referred to as strain (the breakdown in society followed by the attendant rise in deviance among members of society as a result of perceived pressure to commit crimes). Examples of macroside include the Sudan, Iraq, or any country where the government has little or no power to regulate its citizens. Examples of microside include commonly cited instances in the "inner-city" where people cannot achieve their goals either through personal failure, or through external factors (and thus may resort to illegal means to accomplish these things).

[edit] Strain

Merton identified two kinds of Strain: Structural and Individual. Structural Strain is caused in large part by society, and results when society creates ideals and individuals then struggle to meet these ideals. Individual Strain, on the other hand, is caused by the person themselves, as they create their own ideals and then struggle to meet them. According to the theory, these can create Anomie when the Strain (as defined by having a goal to achieve something) increases, and the ability to meet that strain decreases. Merton went on to identify his five Modes of Adaption to further identify responses to these Strains.

[edit] Merton's Modes of Adaption

  • Conformity: The most common Mode. In this Mode, people will attempt to achieve goals set before them with the most socialy acceptable means they have at their disposal.
  • Innovation: People in this Mode have begun to perceive socialy acceptable means of goal-achievement as either being ineffectual or closed to them. As as result, they will begin to use illegal means to achieve their goals.
  • Rebellion: People who reach this Mode are generally disaffected with society and have rejected goal achievement and the socially acceptable means by which to achieve said goals as a viable route to success. These people can generally be categorized as mistrustful, paranoid, violent, etc. Often, they advocate the complete overthrow of the "system" altogether (this can and often does include whatever government happens to be representing society at the time).
  • Retreatism: An alternate route instead of Rebellion. This is often called the "escapist Mode". These people believe, just as the rebellious, that setting and achieving goals is impossible or at least unrealistic. However, instead of advocating the overthrow of the system, they simply drop out of it, effectively disappearing. Merton indicated that this is where the drug addicts, alcoholics, vagrants, and mentally ill eventually end up. Having failed so often in life, they simply give up.
  • Ritualism: The last Mode. These people feel they have no way of advancing in society, but actively seek to retain what they have managed to acquire. They do not harbor visions of becoming more than what they are, and have in fact given up advancing at all. They generally return to the first Mode of Conformity if only because it is the most stable and the best way for them to hang on to what little they may have.

[edit] Agnew's Three Sources of Strain

One of Merton's contemporaries, Robert Agnew, later added to this Theory with his three "Sources of Strain".

  • Failure to Achieve Positively Valued Goals: In this Source, the person is generally confronted with an inability to achieve desires and goals.
  • Removal of Positive Stimuli: This occurs after a significant trauma or negative life-altering event. The most common trigger is a death of a close friend or loved one.
  • Confrontation with Negative Stimuli: The person in this Source is forced to routinely deal with negative activity. Common examples include child abuse and peer pressure to do something illegal.

[edit] Modern Acceptance

Merton's Anomie Theory enjoyed widespread appeal throughout the first half of the 20th Century before falling out of favor for lack of enough evidence to support it. It has since enjoyed a resurgence, however, as in 1992 he connected his theory with high levels of delinquency. today, the Theory is a widely respected one in the annals of Criminology.
