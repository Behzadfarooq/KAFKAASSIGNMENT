Angela Dufresne (born 1969, Connecticut, US) is an artist based in Brooklyn.

Dufresne received a BFA in 1991 from the Kansas City Art Institute [1], and an MFA from the Tyler School of Art [2], Temple University, Philadelphia, in 1998.

She has shown work internationally in many exhibitions including “Greater New York 2005” at P.S. 1 [3] in New York, the 2005 ARCO Art Fair [4] for Galeria Marta Cervera [5] in Madrid, “Miracle on Franklin Street” at GV/AS [6] in Brooklyn and “The Triumph of Painting” at the Saatchi Gallery [7] in London. She is represented by Miller Block Gallery [8] in Boston and Monya Rowe Gallery [9] in New York.

Angela Dufresne most well-known take on painting involves her making images that depict locations and structures that do not really exist – unrealised architectural designs, ruined or mythical places, or entirely fictional scenes, for example. Frequently Dufresne’s imagined landscapes are built up through disparate selections of historical buildings and plans for buildings and her own outlandish predictions for future structures, all of which combine to create entirely impossible scenarios. The titles of Dufresne’s works are often straight-ahead descriptions of the united elements such as 2006’s ‘The Oum Kaltheum International Centre for Vocal and Intellectual Training built on the Remains of the Congress Building in Brasilia, Brazil. Designed by Brian Burton (aka Danger Mouse) c. 2052’. Dufresne’s evocations of unattainable architectural dreams invoke a nostalgic memory of the ambitions of Modernist architecture and its sadly unfulfilled desire to create a utopian environment via good design. In Dufresne’s universe this dream is not dead, each of her vibrantly coloured canvasses offers the opportunity to experience the world as if Frank Lloyd Wright or Le Corbusier had a hand in everything.
