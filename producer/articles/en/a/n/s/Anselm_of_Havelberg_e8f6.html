Anselm of Havelberg

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Anselm of Havelberg[1] (c.1100-1158, Milan) was a bishop and statesman, and a secular and religious ambassador to Constantinople. He was a Premonstratensian, a defender of his order[2][3] and a critic of the monastic life of his time, and a theorist of Christian history. According to Friedrich Heer The peculiar course of Anselm's life made this much-travelled man the theologian of development, of progress, of the right of novelty in the Church.[4]

[edit] Life

His birthplace is uncertain. He was a pupil of Norbert of Xanten at Laon, and then became bishop at Havelberg in Burgundy. There he was caught up and displaced in the Wendish Crusade, moving to Jerichow; on his return the cathedral construction was started.[5]

He was in 1136 sent by Emperor Lothair III to Constantinople.[6] in the hope of a Byzantine alliance. He held theological discussions with Nicetas of Nicomedia[7], an account of which he wrote later as his Dialogues, at the request of Pope Eugenius III. His account tended to play down the theological differences, including the filioque clause[8], but was more stark on the political issues. A later encounter with Basil of Achrida in 1154 proved fruitless.[9]

He was Archbishop of Ravenna 1155-8.[10]

[edit] Works

His works include De ordine canonicorum regularium, Apologeticum pro ordine canonicorum regularium, and the three Dialogi (Greek title Antikeimenon[11]), in the Patrologia Latina.
