Antoine Béchamp

Antoine Béchamp (October 16, 1816 in Bassing, a small village in East France, — April 15, 1908 in Paris). A French biologist.

[edit] Contribution to science

Béchamp worked on silkworm diseases, identifying one as due to a parasite — a separate, smaller organism which entered the body from outside, in this case causing disease. However in a period when considerable scientific interest and effort was applied to this problem, he did not correctly evaluate the role of bacteria in disease as is generally accepted today.[1]

He was a contemporary of Louis Pasteur, who developed the currently accepted germ theory, and of Robert Koch whose postulates are the basis of deciding whether a microbe is the cause of a disease. Bechamp however maintained a pleomorphic theory — essentially that bacteria change form and are not the cause of, but the result of, disease, arising from tissues rather than from a germ of constant form. This has also been called the cellular disease theory, in that scavenging bacteria are supposed to arise from what he called microzymas (microorganisms) he postulated to be normally present in matter (including tissues). These bacteria then feed on dead or decaying cells.

His theory was discarded by brewers, bakers and doctors in favor of Pasteur's repeatable demonstrations.

Other notable pleomorphists since then are Wilhelm Reich, Gaston Naessens, Alan Cantwell[citation needed], and Günther Enderlein (1872-1968).

Atoxyl was first synthesized in 1859 by Antoine Béchamp by chemically reacting aniline and arsenic acid. Béchamp chose the name Atoxyl referring to the reduced toxicity of the resulting compound, compared to arsenic.

  • Doctor of Science;
  • Doctor of Medicine;
  • Professor of Medical Chemistry and Pharmacy at Montpellier;
  • Fellow and Professor of Physics and Toxicology — Strasbourg Higher School of Pharmacy;
  • Professor of Chemistry at Strasbourg;
  • Professor of Biological Chemistry and Dean of Faculty of Medicine of Lille;
  • Chevalier of the Legion of Honour;
  • Commander of the Rose of Brazil
