Anthony DiLapi

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Anthony DiLapi (February 9, 1936-February 4, 1990) was a Teamsters union leader in New York City's garment district, as well as a soldier in the Lucchese crime family. DiLapi was an instrumental cog in family boss Anthony Corallo's illegal control of the garment district in conjunction with Thomas Gambino, who was the son of Carlo Gambino and the son-in-law of Thomas Lucchese. DiLapi was one of organized crime's biggest earners and most respected racketeers on Seventh Avenue. In May, 1980, DiLapi was convicted of conspiracy and obstruction of justice in a plot to squelch a union's attempt to organize workers at a garment-center trucking company. Judge Henry Bramwell sentenced DiLapi to the maximum possible term - five years for the conspiracy count and five for the obstruction count, with the terms to run consecutively for a 10-year total.

Upon DiLapi's release from prison he was summoned to a meeting with new family underboss Anthony Casso. DiLapi who had done his time like a wiseguy's supposed to knew that Casso and family boss Vittorio Amuso were clipping made guys for no reason whatsoever, and understands the two Brooklyn based leaders' distaste for members of the family's historically more powerful Bronx wing, DiLapi feared for his life and fleed New York City to leave the criminal life behind.

Casso used his two NYPD Detective moles Louis Eppolito and Stephen Caracappa to track down DiLapi who had left for California. The two detectives made calls to police stations and law enforcement agencies in California and were able to come up with a location for DiLapi. Casso and Lucchese captain Alphonse D'Arco organized a hit team to fly out to L.A. to find DiLapi. D'Arco proposed his own son Joseph D'Arco to take the job so that he could "make his bones". D'Arco and a crew of hitters shot DiLapi to death in L.A.
