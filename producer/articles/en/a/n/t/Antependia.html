Antependia

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

The altar in St. Mary Anglican Church, Redcliffe, Bristol, England. It is decorated with an elaborate frontal in green, a colour typically associated with the seasons after Epiphany and Pentecost.

Antependia (Latin: "to hang before"; singular: Antependium), more commonly known as Hangings, or, when speaking specifically of the hanging for the altar, Altar frontals, are decorative pieces of material that can adorn a Christian altar, lecturn, pulpit, or table (as opposed to the vestments worn by the minister or priest). Specifically, antependia hang down in front of whatever they cover, and must be distinguished from the altar linens which are used in the service of the Eucharist, and altar cloths which cover the top of the altar table (mensa).

Altar Frontals may come down only a few inches from the top of the altar table (a "frontlet")—especially if the front of the altar is elaborately carved or painted—or they may reach all the way to the floor (the "frontal", properly so called). They will usually cover the entire width of the altar. A "Jacobian frontal" will completely cover the entire altar, reaching down to the floor on all sides.

Antependia are normally of the same color and fabric as the vestments worn by the clergy. The fabric may vary from very simple material such as cotton or wool, to exquisitely wrought damasks and fine watermarked silk, velvet, and satin. Decoration is commonly by means of decorative bands of material (orphreys), embroidery (sometimes in gold or silver thread, or making use of pearls and semi-precious stones) or appliqués, fringes and tassels, all of a complementary color to the fabric. The most frequent emblem used on both vestments and hangings is the cross. The antependia are normally lined in a matching color, often of satin.

The colors used tend to be fixed by the liturgical tradition of the denomination. Most Western Christian churches that observe a developed liturgical tradition will use white, gold, red, green, violet, and black, all used only on specified occasions. Rose colour may be employed for certain Sundays during Lent and Advent. Blue is sometimes prescribed for feasts of the Blessed Virgin Mary (see the article on Liturgical colors for more information). Among Eastern Christians, there tend to be two types vestments used: somber ones and festal ones. Beyond that, no specific colors are officially required. Among some groups, such as the Russian Orthodox Church, a pattern of fixed colors has developed, somewhat similar to that used in the West, although not strictly required.

The term "antipendium" can also be used to describe front of the altar itself, especially if it is elaborately carved or gilded. The famous Pala d'Oro in St. Mark's Basilica in Venice originated as an antependium (though it is used as a reredos now).
