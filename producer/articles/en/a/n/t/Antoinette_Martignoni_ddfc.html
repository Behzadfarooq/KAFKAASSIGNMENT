Antoinette Martignoni

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Antoinette Martignoni, a.k.a. Toni Martignoni (b. December 8, 1918 - ), a pioneer artist in the personal growth and development movement, credited with the Inner Portrait process that uses art as a self-help means to see hidden personal potential.

[edit] Parentage and Family

Born Antoinette S. Walker in Brooklyn, New York. She is the great-granddaughter of James McCune Smith, the first African-American medical doctor with a university degree in the United States.

[edit] Education

Martignoni was the first graduate to receive the Applied Arts degree from the University of Pennsylvania in 1941. In addition, she received a Diploma in Illustration from the Pennsylvania Museum and School of Industrial Art, now known as the Philadelphia College of Art -- now called University of the Arts (Philadelphia).

[edit] Illustration Career

Creative Director and Partner at Koelling/Martignoni & Co., Westport, Connecticut, later founder of Martignoni & Co. a graphics and illustration firm in Fairfield, Connecticut. Martignoni created illustrations for large United States corporations (such as Avis Rent A Car System, Pepsi, H. J. Heinz Company, Nabisco, and Duracell). As an illustrator for some of the top engineering firms during the 1960’s through the 1980's, Martignoni rendered illustrations of futuristic engineering projects, such as the Boston Tunnel that would become known as the Big Dig and America’s first people mover that were built at the West Virginia University. In 1973 Martignoni illustrated an instructional woodworking book by author Carolyn Meyer, Saw, Hammer, and Paint: Woodworking and Finishing for Beginners.

[edit] Involved in Personal Growth & Development

Involved in the Pathwork process from it’s beginning in the 1960’s, Martignoni was present at many of the lectures channeled by Eva Broch Pierrakos. Martignoni explored EST which became Landmark Education in the 1980’s and was in charge of assistants in Connecticut. She was a member of the Albertson Memorial Church in Greenwich, Connecticut. As an artist Martignoni sought ways to unite human potential with art. She explored Zen drawing classes with therapist and poet Gunilla Norris and practiced a form of Painting from the Source, as created by Aviva Gold, with a group of women artists.

Influenced by writers Frederick Franck (The Zen of Seeing), Caroline Myss (Anatomy of Spirit), and Julia Cameron (The Artist’s Way), Martignoni began to use the power art to connect people with their potential. In 1993 she drew her first Inner Portrait at age 74.

[edit] Inner Portrait Artist

Martignoni has drawn more than 700 Inner Portraits since 1993. The portraits are in an illustrative style in which people and shapes are easily recognizable with a sub-text of hidden symbols and images. As a Self-help technique that incorporates art with personal development, Martignoni developed a process of “reading” the portrait to uncover a story about the subject of the portrait. Making the emotional, spirtual, or psychological connection with people is the keystone of the Innter Portrait process: “When you feel compassion and see other people as they are, it’s because you can truly experience who you really are.”

Martignoni was interviewed on TV by Heather Kovar for News 12 Connecticut in May 2006 and an Inner Portrait drawing was used as the cover art for Natural Awakenings Magazine in September 2006.
