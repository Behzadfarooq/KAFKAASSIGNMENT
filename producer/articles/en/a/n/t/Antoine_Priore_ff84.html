Antoine Priore (April 10, 1912–May 9, 1983) was born in Trieste, Italy. He received training in electrical engineering, and obtained special expertise in radar working as a technician for the Italian Navy. (Perisse, 1984) He was a prisoner of the Germans on a naval base in Bordeaux, France until he was rescued by the French resistance in 1944. (Bird, 1984) Priore worked for 25 years (between 1950 and 1975) in Bordeaux, and built a series of electromagnetic devices producing a strong magnetic field of 600 gauss (60 mT) or more. His last device was funded by the French government with the help of his friend and one-time Prime Minister of France Jacques Chaban-Delmas. Priore managed to build a large device for the purpose of treating people with cancer. Priore was reported to have successfully treated a number of animals with cancer with this device, but it broke down before it could be applied to human patients. (Perisse, 1984) After the incident, Priore later was reported to cure a number of terminal cancer patients with his earlier devices. (Bird, 1984) Priore's work reached the attention of the French media in 1965 who dubbed it "L'affaire Priore".

Priore was accused of manipulating his scientific data. (Rorvik, 1975) He was also accused by the French press of not understanding his own technique for treating cancer and disease. (Ibid.) Priore never exposed his exact method, believing others would only steal it for themselves. (Bird, 1984) The scientific establishment and the French media alike seriously questioned the legitimacy of Priore's research. The U.S. Office of Naval Research reported that Priore's patents seemed deliberately made unclear, leaving little information to ascertain the mechanical and electromagnetic workings of Priore's devices. (Bateman, 1978) It is possible Priore may not have understood his method, leading many to believe it was a complete and utter hoax.

In 1965, one event exposed possible foul play in Priore's experimental research involving 'English mice.' Mice with experimental cancers sent to Priore from the Chester Beatty Institute in England lead to controversy over his research. After Priore treated the mice with his device they were sent back to the Institute. Scientists from the Institute claimed the mice returned to them were not genuine because they rejected new cancer grafts. (Rorvik, 1975) One scientist with the Institute, Pierette Chateaurenaud-Duprat, retorted the claim stating the mice showed signs of immunity to the cancers they were originally grafted with. (Bird, 1975) Except for his colleagues, Priore's research was met with disbelief from the French Academy. The National Cancer Institute in the U.S. also doubted Priore's biological data and rejected a proposal for funding to replicate his methodology given by a scientist, John Carstoiu, who understood the Priore device as a magnetohydrodynamic (MHD) wave guide. (Carstoiu, 1973)

Priore, along with his colleagues, including renowned immunologist Raymond Pautrizel and Robert Courier, former secretary of the of the French Academy of Sciences, published a series of notes on Priore's research in the proceedings of the French Academy. Pautrizel also classified Priore's methodology as immunological in nature, since animals inoculated with deadly doses of parasites were also reportedly healed with Priore radiation. (Rorvik, 1975) Priore wrote a thesis for his medical doctorate describing his apparatus and experimentations in detail, but it was rejected.

According to Christopher Bird, and other investigators into Priore's methodology, Priore was capable of not only treating cancer in animals and humans, but boosting subjects' immune systems, allowing them to heal themselves of incurable diseases. (Bird, 1984) Antoine Prioré died on May 9, 1983 of a stroke. (Perisse, 1984) Jean-Michel Graille, a french journalist, met with Raymond Pautrizel and wrote a book on the Priore affair, Dossier Priore. The mystery of "L'affaire Priore" remains unsolved. According to available sources, no one has been capable of reproducing the effects of or reproducing Priore radiation. Only one known organization has attempted to replicate Priore's methodology, ARTEC Laboratory in Bordeaux, France. A number of other scientists and engineers have developed or theorized about electromagnetic methods for treating cancer and disease including: Georges Lachovsky, Royal Raymond Rife, Thomas Valone, Robert Becker, Tom Bearden and Nikola Tesla.
