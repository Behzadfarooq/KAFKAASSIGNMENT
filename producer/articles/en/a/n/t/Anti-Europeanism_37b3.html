Anti-Europeanism

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

You have new messages (last change).

Anti-Europeanism is opposition or hostility toward the governments, culture, or people of the countries of Europe. The term has no set definition and is used in reference to a variety of sentiments and opinions including: criticisim of the policies of European governments; general prejudices against Europeans; or a rejection of European culture. The term should be contrasted with unfavourable attitudes to the process of European integration by citizens of the member states of the European Union, a sentiment which is usually termed "Euroscepticism".

Critics of the term question whether Anti-Europeanism can be isolated as a coherent phenomenon and often claim the label primarily has a propagandistic or suppressive function over political discourse. In this sense it is similar to sentiments such as Anti-Americanism.

[edit] Examples of anti-Europeanism

[edit] Government Criticism in the United States

Perhaps the most obvious example of anti-European sentiment relates to political opinions. Disagreements in foreign policy concerning the 2003 invasion of Iraq, in which a variety of European governments witheld support for the action, prompted notable criticism from American politicians and political columnists.[1]

A more general example of this phenomenon in the United States is the criticism of certain domestic policies carried out by European governments: in particular publicly funded healthcare systems and the more generous social welfare provisions present in certain European countries. The American journalist Daniel Pipes for instance, has blamed European welfare systems for "feminizing males", stifling the urge to breed - leading to a declining European population - and promoting cowardice.[2] European healthcare systems on the other hand are often characterised as providing a poor standard of service in comparison to private systems such as that in the United States.[3]

Some critics of these perspectives argue that this form of anti-Europeanism is directed more inwardly at American domestic policy than outwardly at Europe. According to this argument criticism of European domestic policies serves as an example for what may happen in the U.S if similar policies are adopted and is less focused on contemporary issues in Europe itself. This, according to proponents of the argument, is particularly relevant given the questionable empirical basis of many of the accusations.[4]

[edit] Stereotyping

Stereotyping is another common form of anti-European-ism, with several derogatory stereotypes relating to the people of European countries having a wide usage. Many of these stereotypes, particularly those concerning the people of France, and less commonly, Germany are a seemingly accepted part of mainstream discourse in numerous countries.[5] A more general European stereotype not relating to any specific countries also exists, particularly in North America where Europeans are conceived of as, amongst other things: effeminate, cowardly, lazy and condescending towards other cultures.
