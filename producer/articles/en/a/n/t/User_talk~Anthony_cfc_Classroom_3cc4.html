Welcome to the Adopt-a-User Classroom of Anthony cfc. The aim of this page is to provide short, snappy lessons for my three adoptees on basic Wikipedia topics, on the week's selected topics. The page will also contain Tips of the Day, as well as a tip archive, and useful links.

The additional function of the page will be to provide a key site for the adopted users to request assistance, or just drop by with a quick question.

As with all areas of Wikipedia, this page should be community-run. That means anybody can participate - whether on the advice-giving or advice-receiving stance. All questions, queries or suggestions should be directed to Anthony's talk page; the discussion tab of this page redirects there.

 Lesson the First: Disputes, and How to Handle Them;

Links on this page are rendered in orange; be sure to hover over important phrases - chances are, there will be a link.
Disputes are a part of Wikipedia that will never leave: at the end of the day, humans differ from one another and, at some point or another, will disagree. Wikipedians are humans, too, and as such sooner or later a dispute will be brought to your front door. That's when you need to tread carefully; before you do anything, take the time to get your facts in order: check out links, or manually fetch the pages if links aren't given in the post. Make sure you've got the full story, and that you didn't miss anything first time. Then, leave it for a bit; make sure you're perfectly calm and focused - don't post when you're angry!.

That's when the DR begins: the first step of Dispute Resolution (DR) is to discuss, so do that. When you reply, start of civilly; ask how their doing, then thank them for bringing the matter to their attention. Remain civil. Then, you can take one of these steps:

1. -- if you are in the wrong, apologise. "Please accept my humblest apologies" should do.
2. -- if they are in the wrong, then you need to carefully bring it to their attention: "I respectfully beg to differ: (why you beg to differ; include links, and link to policy: use the links at the left)"
3. -- if both of you are wrong, or if one is incorrect but the other has conducted themselves wrong, etc... if it's you that's wrong, follow step 1. If they are wrong, step 2. If you have conducted poorly, then apologise! For example, "I would like to apologise for (link to diff* of comment that you are apologising about); I acted with incivility, and I beg you to accept my humblest apologies. If they have conducted themselves poorly, bring it to their attention softly, with links and policy links.

[edit] Minor edits

When should I use minor edits? And how are they different from non-minor edits? Capuchin 13:46, 9 March 2007 (UTC)

Basically? Minor edits are to be used for an edit that is extremely unlikely to be disputed by another edit; examples include typos, or link fixes. It's fundamentaly a common sense issue - if your edit might prod a fellow Wikipedian into saying "Hey, could you take a few seconds to provide your thoughts when you made the edit?", then don't check the minor edit box. anthonycfc [talk] 18:57, 9 March 2007 (UTC)

I've heard reverting vandalism should be considered a minor edit. What are your thoughts on this? Mkdwtalk 07:32, 13 March 2007 (UTC)

My advice is never, ever mark an anti-vandalism edit a minor edit - there is always a pretty decent chance you're reverting a perfectly legitamite edit, that will almost certainly bring a dispute to your front door - therefore, they shouldn't be marked minor. The best approach to such a dispute being brought to you is to make your apologies, and offer to tidy up your mistakes. You might want to see the above lesson for details on disputes. anthonycfc [talk] 19:49, 16 March 2007 (UTC)
