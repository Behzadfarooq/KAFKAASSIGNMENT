L'Abbaye de Mortemer is a Cistercian Abbey originally built in 1134 on land gifted to the order by Henry Beauclerc (Henry I of England).

[edit] History

The monks found that the land received from King Henry was water-logged. In old Norman, this was known as "Dead Water" or "Morte Mer", hence the name. It is believed to be the origin of the name of the noted Mortimer family.

After digging a large drainage lake which still exists today, they constructed what was then the largest Cistercian Abbey in the world.

Over the centuries, the Abbey fell into decline and disrepair. A newer Abbey, still standing intact, was built in the 17th Century; by 1790, only five monks remained.

By the time of the French Revolution, the original Abbey was already in a perilous state, and as locals looted its stone for local construction, it became little more than a ruin.

[edit] Features

The original 12th Century Abbey can still be visited, but is now little more than a shell, although the cloisters are relatively intact.

The 17th Century Abbey is well preserved and is open to visitors, with guided tours available most of the year. There are a number of legends and ghost stories relating to the Abbey, which make the visit particularly interesting.

The Abbey boasts a well-maintained 17th Century dovecote, which was also used as a gaol house in the 18th and 19th centuries.

[edit] Location

The Abbey is in the Forest of Lyons outside Lisors, some 34Km (21 miles) Southeast of Rouen.
