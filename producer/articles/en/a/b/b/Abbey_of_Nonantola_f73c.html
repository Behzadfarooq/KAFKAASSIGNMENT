Abbey of Nonantola

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

The tribune of the basilica in the abbey of Nonantola

The Abbey of Nonantola, entytled to Saint Sylvester, is a former Benedictine monastery and prelature nullius in the commune of Nonantola, six miles north-east of Modena, in the Emilia Romagna region of Italy

The abbey was founded in 752 by Saint Anselm, Duke of Friuli and brother-in-law of the Lombard king Aistulf. The latter richly endowed the new abbey, starting his role as one of the main land proprietors of northern Italy. Pope Stephen II appointed Anselm its first abbot, and presented the relics of St. Sylvester to the abbey, named in consequence S. Sylvester de Nonantula. After the death of Aistulph (756), Anselm was banished to Monte Cassino by the new king, Desiderius, but was restored by Charlemagne after seven years. In 813 the abbot Peter was chosen as Imperial ambassador to Constantinople. In 883 it was chosen as the place of a conference between Charles the Fat and Pope Marinus I.

Up to 11th century Nonantola was an imperial monastery, and its discipline often suffered severely on account of imperial interference in the election of abbots: Nonantola was in fact one of the most powerful abbeys of Europe and control over it was considererd a major issue by the emperors and popes. It had a famous scriptorium and the abbot Godeschalc had a new basilica built in 1058. In the beginning of the Conflict of Investitures it sided with the emperor, until forced to submit to the pope by Matilda of Tuscany in 1083. It finally declared itself openly for the pope's party in 1111. In that year the famous monk Placidus of Nonantola wrote his De honore Ecclesiæ, one of the most able and important defences of the papal position that were written during the Conflict of Investitures.

The decline of the monastery began in 1419, when it came under the jurisdiction of commendatory abbots. In 1514 abbot Gian Matteo Sertorio gave it to the Cistercians, but continued to decline until it was finally suppressed by Clement XIII in 1768. Pius VII restored it January 23, 1821, with the provision that the prelature nullius attached to it should belong to the Archbishop of Modena.

The Basilica' is a Romanesque edifice, which in the early 20th century had been restored to the restoration of abbot Damian (12th century). The abbey comprises an archive, considered “the most important archive of Mediaeval Europe”, and a museum. The church has a nave and two aisles, with the presbytery on the vast crypt that preserves Mediaeval testimonies from the 8th century.

Part of the buildings once belonging to the abbey now host the Town Hall of Nonantola. In one of these precious frescoes from 11th century have been discovered.
