Abbots Ripton rail disaster

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

The Abbots Ripton rail disaster occurred on 21 January 1876, when the Flying Scotsman express train was involved in a double collision during a blizzard [1]. Because many signals were frozen in the 'clear' position, a coal train which was running ahead of the express ran through 'danger' signals and was finally stopped by the Abbots Ripton signalman, who instructed the driver to set back into a siding. While the coal train was setting back, the express (also over-running signals) arrived and crashed into the coal train, which had only partly cleared the line.

A second collision occurred shortly afterward when a northbound express crashed into the wreckage of the first collision. Since the driver of the northbound express was warned of the obstruction by the crew of the coal train engine, he tried to stop his train, but with the inefficient brakes then available he could not stop in time and crashed into the wreckage from the first collision.

At the time of the accident, the semaphore signals were normally left in the 'clear' position (arm lowered), and they were only set to the 'danger' position (arm horizontal) while the block section was occupied by a train. A long gap between trains allowed the blizzard to freeze the signal arms in the lowered position, so that even when the signalman put his signal levers back to the 'normal' position, the signal arms did not follow suit. It is also likely that there were no electric indicators repeating the actual position of the signals. Because the signal arms at this time, and on this railway, disappeared into a slot in the signal post, it was also easier for the arms to be frozen by the snow.

[edit] Aftermath

Several things were changed as a result of this accident:

  • the normal position of the block instruments and semaphore signals was altered to be 'line blocked'/'danger', and signals were cleared only to permit the passing of a train. This is a very important principle.
  • slotted signals were outlawed - the design of signal arms was changed so that various failures (broken signal wires, for example) would cause the arm to fall back to the 'danger' position.
  • electric repeaters of signals that were out of sight of the signalman were introduced.
  • later the electric repeaters were interlinked with the block instruments as an extra precaution.

This accident is classified as a wrong side failure.
