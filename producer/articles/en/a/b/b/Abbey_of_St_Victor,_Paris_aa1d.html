Abbey of St Victor, Paris

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

The Royal Abbey and School of St. Victor had its origins when William of Champeaux, the Archdeacon of Paris retired to a small hermitage near Paris in 1108. He became one of the Canons Regular; an Augustinian, and his community followed the Augustinian Rule.

William was famed for his teaching, and was followed to his hermitage by many of his disciples, including Peter Abelard and was convinced by them to take up his lecturing again. William was made Bishop of Chalons in 1113, but was succeeded in his hermitage at St. Victor's by Gildwin, who promoted the canonical order and its new abbey vigorously.

Through generous gifts from popes, kings, queens, and nobles, the Abbey of St Victor was soon richly endowed. Many religious houses of canons regular came under its influence and reformed through its leadership including the Abbey and Church of Ste Geneviève (Paris), Wigmore in Wales, St. Augustine's (Bristol), St. Catherine's (Waterford), St. Thomas's (Dublin), and St. Peter's (Aram, Naples). King Louis VIII mentioned no less than forty abbeys of the order of St. Victor in his last will and testament, and he left all his jewels for the building of the abbey church and 4000 pounds to be equally divided among them. Before the abbey was 160 years old, several cardinals and at least eight significant abbotts had been produced from among its members.

The traditions of William of Champeaux were handed on, and St. Victor's became a centre of piety and learning, attracting famous students, scholars and intellectuals including Hugh of St. Victor, Peter Lombard and Thomas à Becket. The school, with the schools of Ste Geneviève and Notre-Dame de Paris, was the cradle of the University of Paris.

The Abbey later fell on hard times, being identified with the Jansenist movement, until the abbey was destroyed during the French Revolution.
