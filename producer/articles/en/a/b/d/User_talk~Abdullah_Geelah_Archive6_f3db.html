[edit] Baha'i Faith

Hello Abdullah, and thanks for your comment about the Baha'i Faith on my user page.

I agree with you that the Baha'i Faith can look similar to Islam at one level. This is because they are both divinely inspired and because the founders of the Baha'i Faith were mostly responding in their writings to Muslim mystics and clerics. Viewed this way, all the religions of God appear similar because they all promote the same spiritual values, including love, patience, generosity and so on.

At another level, the religions are different as they came for different stages of social evolution. Islam catered for the needs and social laws of the nation state, while the Baha'i Faith brings laws and standards for a global society.

But people become attached to their religions, believing that their religion can never be superceded by another, contrary to what is in their holy scriptures. However, the Baha'i Faith teaches that God loves humankind and will never stop sending his divine messengers, each of whom brings a more advanced message. This is similar to teachers in a school: subsequent teachers bring more advanced messages according to the needs and capabilities of the students. Likewise, the divine messengers of God are equally knowledgable and powerful, but they give their message according to the needs of the time.

I hope that this helps you in your search. --Occamy 19:34, 13 August 2006 (UTC)

[edit] Red Sea

If you read it more carefully you'll see that it says that it connects to the ocean through the Gulf of Aden (to which it is in turn connected by the Bab el Mandeb), not that the Gulf of Aden is a part of it. The two are distinct. Moreover, the official languages of Djibouti are Arabic and French, not Somali and Afar, and the part bordering the Red Sea (north of the Gulf of Tadjoura) is inhabited by Afar speakers, not Somalis (I just don't know how to say "Red sea" in Afar). — ዮም | (Yom) | Talk • contribs • Ethiopia 18:06, 19 August 2006 (UTC)
