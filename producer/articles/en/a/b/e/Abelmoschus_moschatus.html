Abelmoschus moschatus (Ambrette seeds, Annual hibiscus, Bamia Moschata, Galu Gasturi, Muskdana, Musk mallow, Musk okra, Musk seeds, Ornamental okra, Rose mallow seeds, Tropical jewel hibiscus, Yorka okra; syn. Hibiscus abelmoschus L.) is an aromatic and medicinal plant in the Malvaceae family, which is native to India. These seeds have a sweet, flowery, heavy fragrance quite similar to that of musk. Despite its tropical origin the plant is frost hardy.

[edit] Uses of the plant

Musk mallow oil was once used as a substitute for animal musk; however this use is now mostly discontinued as it can cause photosensitivity.

It has many culinary uses. The seeds are added to coffee; unripe pods ("musk okra"), leaves and new shoots are eaten as vegetables.

Different parts of the plant have uses in traditional and complementary medicine, not all of which have been scientifically proven. It is used externally to relieve spasms of the digestive tract, cramp, poor circulation and aching joints. It is also considered an insecticide and an aphrodisiac.

In industry the root mucilage provides sizing for paper; tobacco is sometimes flavoured with the flowers. Among the small farmers the popularity of medicinal crop Kasturi Bhindi (Abelmoschus moschatus) is increasing day by day. Due to susceptibility to insect infestation earlier the herb growers hesitated to try this crop but now they have learnt a lot from the past experiences. Like other medicinal crops its planting material business is over and now serious growers are engaged in quality production with an idea of extracting oil for international market.

Kasturi Bhindi is known for its aromatic seeds having peculiar smell of Musk. In trade it is known as Ambrette. Although its different plant parts are used as traditional medicine and the traditional healers use it in herbal formulations having odd smell but it is having demand at national and international markets. The farmers are not growing it for the traditional healers. The healers are fulfilling their demand from its wild population as well as from very small-scale cultivation.

It is raised as Kharif (Rainy season) crop. I have found it as hardy crop and gives good returns even under rainfed conditions. It gives good responses to inputs like fertilizers but for the seeds with good aroma its organic farming is recommended. It is good sign that most of the herb growers are raising it organically.

At international market there is fair demand of its seeds but high demand of its oil. Earlier the farmers were growing it for seeds and they are selling it through the small traders. At that time they were getting less returns but now they have found potential buyers interested in oil. Many farmers are in process of getting buy back arrangements. The farmers having certified farms are getting relatively higher prices for the oil. Initially the farmers prepared small extraction unit with local inputs. It was cheap but this unit failed to give the quality oil. Now with the help of co-operative societies they have purchased costly processing unit and established small lab to put check on the quality of oil.

For the fist time Kasturi Bhindi was raised in the year 1994. The growers especially organic growers have done a lot of experiments and developed many promising herbal solutions to manage the pest problem in this crop. The solutions are sprayed as preventive sprays in order to repel away the insects. You will find many rows of native plants like Kalmegh (Andrographis paniculata), Genda (Tagetes erecta) etc. around this crop. These plants act as guard and keep the enemies at bay. For seed treatment the farmers are using cow urine and extracts of local weeds. This combination is giving much promising results than the fungicides.

While studying the Allelopathic potential of Kasturi Bhindi plants I have found that its certain extracts are having strong Allelochemcials. This is the reason that during cultivation this crop doesn't requires much support for weed management. Its Allelochemcials suppress the weeds effectively. The traditional healers have confirmed this effect by observing its dominance in wild.

It is not wrong to say that Kasturi Bhindi is a promising crop for the small farmers having processing unit. Initially the research organizations promoted it but now they are discouraging the farmers by saying that there is no demand of its seeds. They are true to some extent. The seeds for new crop raising are not in demand but the final produce i.e. oil is having continuous demand at national as well as international markets. The innovative farmers can reap the profits from this demand.
