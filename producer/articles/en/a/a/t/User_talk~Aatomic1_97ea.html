[edit] Your category nominated for deletion

The Royal Prerogative is a body of customary authority… recognised … as belonging to the Crown alone. It is the means by which some of the executive powers of government are possessed by …a monarch.

Among the powers possessed by the monarch in the United Kingdom under the Royal Prerogative are:

  • Clemency and pardon;

The power to grant pardons and reprieves is a royal prerogative of mercy of the monarch of the United Kingdom. There are significant procedural differences in the royal pardon's use today, however. Today the monarch may only grant a pardon on the advice of the Home Secretary or the First Minister of Scotland. Aatomic1 08:30, 28 March 2007 (UTC)

Aatomic1, you misunderstand the nature of the royal prerogative -- it's a lot more complicated than that, and for ,most modern purposes it refers to a power exercised by the government in the name of the crown, and fotr which it does not need parliamentary authority. So far as I can determine the power of clemency was effectively vested in the Home Secretary in 1837: see for example http://www.richard.clark32.btinternet.co.uk/reprieve.html
After that date, therefore, the role of the monarch in executions was entirely ceremonial, by lending her name to a decision made by the Home Secretary. Before that date, though, the monarch had long since had only "royal prerogative]] of mercy", a power to reprieve people from the death penalty, but not to impose it. Considering that the death penalty was imposed elsewhere, the monarchy is a largely irrelevant basis for categorising executions since the 17th century. There might be a better case for categorising by monarchy the pardons granted between 1660 and 1837, but not the executions. --BrownHairedGirl (talk) • (contribs) 13:07, 28 March 2007 (UTC)
