Aaron Lafette Brooks (born March 24, 1976 in Newport News, Virginia) is an American football quarterback in the NFL, who currently is a free agent. Brooks attended the University of Virginia. He is also the second cousin of Atlanta Falcons quarterback Michael Vick and Miami Dolphins quarterback/wide receiver/return specialist Marcus Vick. He formerly played for the Green Bay Packers; the New Orleans Saints, where he was the starting quarterback for six years; and the Oakland Raiders.

[edit] College career

After high school, Brooks decided to play at the University of Virginia. redshirted his true freshman year and received no meaningful playing time in 1995. He competed with fifth year senior Tim Sherman, whose father was wide receivers coach at Virginia, for the starting quarterback job in 1996. The Cavaliers were returning defensive standouts such as Jamie Sharper, James Farrior, Ronde Barber . The offense was led by running back Tiki Barber, Ronde's identical twin brother. The coaching staff made Sherman, who had played well against Virginia Tech in 1995 after starter Mike Groh went down to injury, the primary quarterback instead of the inexperienced Brooks. Nonetheless, Brooks received playing time in nearly all games and was the primary quarterback in a few. Inconsistent quarterback play by both Brooks and Sherman led to an underachieving season, and Virginia fans to this day still debate the 1996 quarterback controversy.

Brooks was the unquestioned starter in 1997. Both Brooks and the team struggled in the early part of the season but surged late to finish 7-4. The Cavaliers, however, were not invited to a bowl game. In 1998, the team got off to a strong start and was briefly in the top ten. The highlight of the year, and Brooks' career, was the season finale at Virginia Tech. The Cavaliers were down 29-7 at halftime, but Brooks led the biggest comeback in school history in the 36-32 victory. The final regular season record was 9-2. The Cavaliers narrowly lost 35-33 to Georgia in a thriller at the Peach Bowl.

[edit] NFL career

First drafted by the Green Bay Packers in 1999, Brooks began the beginning of his career as a backup to Brett Favre, taking no snaps during the regular season. In New Orleans, Brooks became one of the NFL's leaders in game-winning drives in the fourth quarter or overtime with 16 (behind only Tom Brady), including five in 2004. Brooks took over as the starter in 2000 in relief of the injured Jeff Blake in Week 11 (vs. Oakland, 2000-11-19), started the following week and took every snap until the last month of the 2005 campaign, when he was benched. Brooks played in 72 regular-season games with the Saints, including starting the last 69 contests. His 441 passing yards vs. Denver (2000-12-03) was the highest single-game total in franchise history until Drew Brees surpassed it with throwing for 510 yards on November 19, 2006, and Brooks is also the only Saints quarterback to rush for over 100 yards in a contest (108 vs. San Francisco, 2000-12-10). In 2004, Brooks set club marks for attempts (60) and completions (34) in a contest.

Unfortunately for Brooks, the high point in his career with the Saints came very early. Brooks led the turnaround of the Saints in the 2000 season, his first with the franchise. In the previous season, the Saints were 3-13. In 2000, the Saints not only made the playoffs with a 10-6 record, but defeated the defending Super Bowl champion St. Louis Rams, 31-28. Brooks threw four touchdown passes in the game. The Saints then lost to the Minnesota Vikings in their final playoff game of that season.

In 2001, the Saints only went 7-9, largely due to a lack of productivity from running back Ricky Williams.

In 2002, Deuce McAllister replaced Williams, and the running game improved. Brooks led the Saints to a 6-1 record before the team imploded the second half of the season. The Saints proceeded to split the next six games. At 9-4, their last three games were against the Minnesota Vikings, Cincinnati Bengals, and Carolina Panthers, all long since eliminated from playoff contention. With only one victory out of those three games required to reach the postseason, the Saints gave up a touchdown and two point conversion to lose at home to Minnesota, couldn't stop the 1-13 Bengals' running game led by fullback Nick Luchey, and fell flat on their face against Carolina, wasting their best defensive effort of the season (After giving up 20 or more points in each of their previous 15 games, the Saints only allowed 10) by only scoring two field goals at home in a 10-6 loss. Brooks was humiliated by the sellout crowd in the Louisiana Superdome that day, as they chanted "We Want Jake" (in reference to backup QB Jake Delhomme). To add salt to the wound, Delhomme signed with the Panthers in the offseason and led the team to the Super Bowl, coming up short to the New England Patriots.

Brooks' decision making, showcased in an infamous backwards pass thrown to an offensive lineman in a 2004 loss in San Diego, was the target of criticism from New Orleans fans, and may be a fundamental reason why he isn't mentioned with the upper echelon of quarterbacks in the NFL.

On March 16, 2006, after sitting on the bench the last several full games of the season, he was released by the Saints, after they signed former San Diego Chargers quarterback Drew Brees who went on to become the number one rated QB in the NFL in 2006 as well as leading the Saints to a first round bye in the playoffs and a division title. Meanwhile, Brooks went on to lead the Oakland Raiders to a dismal 2-14 season. Oakland ended the season with nine straight losses.

On February 22nd, 2007, the Oakland Raiders declined to reinstate Brooks for a second season according to ESPN.com.

[edit] Timeline

In 2003, Brooks threw the famous "cell phone" touchdown to Joe Horn in which Horn put a cell phone under the goal post and solidified himself as one of the most flamboyant receivers in the league. Horn earned a $25,000 fine for his antics. However, things only got sour for the Brooks and the Saints the following week after a bitter loss to the Jacksonville Jaguars. A fierce close game came down to the Saints executing a 5 lateral, miracle play started by a Brooks pass, dubbed "The River City Relay." The joy was short lived however, as kicker John Carney inexplicably botched the subsequent extra point that would have sent the game into overtime. The following week, during the final game against the Dallas Cowboys, Carney was heartily cheered and Brooks was booed. Another Saints season had ended with no success.

In 2004, after a 4-8 start, Brooks led the Saints on a 4-win streak to finish the season, reaching the brink of the playoffs, but tiebreakers did the Saints in, even though they were hottest team in the NFC at the season's end.

On December 13, 2005, after a loss on national television to the Atlanta Falcons, the Saints benched Brooks and announced Todd Bouman as the team's starter for the last three games of the season.

After being released by the New Orleans Saints for Drew Brees, Aaron Brooks was signed by the Oakland Raiders on March 22, 2006. He was the starter going into training camp. In his first regular season game as a Raider, he was sacked 9 times by the San Diego Chargers in a 27-0 shut-out loss. Brooks dislocated his shoulder in his second game against the Baltimore Ravens and missed the next several weeks. Second-year quarterback Andrew Walter started the next seven games for Oakland. There was some speculation that Brooks' injury was not as serious as reported and that he was really being benched in favor of Walter. After a 2-5 record over that span and even worse quarterback play from the first-time starter, a healthy Brooks was bumped back into the starter's role. Despite slightly improved play, Brooks was unable to lead the defensive Raiders to very much success, as Oakland's 2006 season was universally viewed as a disappointment. They finished with an abysmal league worst 2-14 record. Brooks was quoted as saying Art Shell should not take the blame for such a terrible excuse for a football team, that "I blame the players."
