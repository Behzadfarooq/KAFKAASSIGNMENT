Aaron Huey (born 1975) is a photographer from Seattle who is most widely known for his walk across America with his dog Cosmo. The journey lasted 154 days and covered 3,349 miles. Huey stayed with strangers he met along the way, averaging around 30 miles a day, with a record day (according to his journals) of 46 miles. His online journals chronicle his journey. His photography appears in Smithsonian, National Geographic, the New Yorker, and the New York Times, among others.

Color images of the people he met along his journey were made with a single camera and lens: a Leica M6 with a 35mm lens.

From Huey's BIO on his site:

Aaron Huey was born in a small town at the foot of the Big Horn Mountains of Northern Wyoming. At the age of 18 Huey studied as a Rotary Scholar in Bratislava, Slovakia, where he graduated with a degree in stone sculpture. From 1995 to 1999 Huey studied at the University of Denver, in Colorado, graduating with an Honors BFA in painting and printmaking.

As a professional photographer he has shot all over the world, from the religious schools of the Taliban, to anti-American protests in Iran, lost temples of Eastern Burma and the tribes of the Caucasus. Huey worked in New York City in 2001 as assistant to National Geographic photographer Steve McCurry.

Aaron has published both photography and writing in Smithsonian magazine, National Geographic, National Geographic Traveler, National Geographic Adventure, TIME, The New York Times, The New Yorker, Newsweek, Mens Journal, L'Espresso (Italy), Der Spiegel (Germany), GEO (Italy), The Discovery Channel, The London Times, The Seattle Times, The Sunday Times (London), Details, VICE, Photographerâ€™s International, Photo District News , Rock and Ice Magazine, Lenswork, Leica Fotografie Intenational, Kodak ProPass magazine, HObo (Vancouver), ODE (Holland), and several other publications in the European Press.

Huey is named this year in Photo District New's 30 top emerging photographers.

Aaron is currently based in Kabul, Afghanistan. In late 2007 he will begin directing his first documentary film, about the Pine Ridge Reservation.
