The Aaronids were the group of Levites who claimed to be descended from Aaron. In passages from the Torah which are attributed by textual scholars to the priestly source, the Aaronids are portrayed as the only legitimate priests in Judaism[1]. This contrasts with the passages attributed to the Jahwist, Elohist, and particularly the Deuteronomist, in which no distinction is made between Levites and priests, and in which it appears that any Levites can be a legitimate priest[2][3].

According to Biblical scholars, the Aaronids were engaged in a power struggle against other groups of Levite priests, particularly the group based at Shiloh[4]. The result of this political struggle is believed to have shaped the Biblical canon - the Deuteronomic Code being the reaction of the non-Aaronids to the earlier pro-Aaronid Priestly Code, and the Book of Chronicles being the Aaronid reaction to the earlier non-Aaronid Deuteronomic history[5]. Indeed, the priestly source itself is believed to be an attempt to rewrite JE, the earlier version of scripture, to put forward the argument that only the Aaronids could intercede with Yahweh[6].

The Biblical figure of Aaron, the eponymous founder of the Aaronids, is treated remarkably differently between the various sources of the Torah[7]. While in the priestly source Yahweh always acts through Aaron (or Aaron and his sons), and is generally placed in a positive light, the Elohist and Deuteronomist recount the episode of the Golden Calf, the Deuteronomist particularly emphasising Aaron's blame for the episode[8]; scholars believe the narrative is a fiction, a literary cipher for the two golden calfs of king Jeroboam, and used particularly by the Deuteronomist as a slur on the character of the Aaronids, by reference to their founder[9].

There were two branches of the Aaronids, the line claiming descent from Eleazar (a son of Aaron), and the line claiming descent from Ithamar[10]. According to the priestly source[11], the priesthood was originally in control of the descendants of Eleazar, but by the start of the Books of Samuel it must have transferred to the descendants of Ithamar, since Eli is argued to be descended from Ithamar. Apart from a number of non-Levites who performed priest-like functions (such as officiating over the korbanot), including Samuel himself and a son of king David, the text implies that control remained in the hands of Eli's descendants until Solomon transferred it from Abiathar to Zadok, and his descendants; the text portrays Zadok as a descendant of Eleazar. According to a number of biblical scholars, Zadok and Eli are not historic figures, but deities, with Eli being a humanised version of El, and Zadok being a humanised version of Tzedek[12]; with this view, the phrase sons of Eli simply referred to priests of El, and the similar phrase sons of Zadok simply referred to priests of Tzedek[13].
