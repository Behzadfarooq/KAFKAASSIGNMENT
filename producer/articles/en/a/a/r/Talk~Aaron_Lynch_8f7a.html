Curt Hicks - you were his friend at Fermilab, and you seem to be the current "curator" of this article. Do you know anything about what happened to him?

_________________________

Aaron & I were friends since our undergrad days in the mid-1970s. We kept in regular touch regarding his work--at least monthly-- for thirty years. The last I heard from him was a phonecall which I believe occurred in October, 2005.

Once his wealthy friend from Fermi discontinued support after a decade of helping, Aaron's commitment to independent scholarship quickly impoverished him financially. He had amassed considerable debt. He had recently been evicted from his Evanston apartment and was temporarily living in a borrowed place. He complained of intense chronic pain from gout which he lacked funds to treat. His call was a request for financial help which I could no longer offer, having already helped him repeatedly far beyond my means. When his phonecalls--which had come nearly weekly at that time--suddenly ceased, I could not contact him as he had not had a phone for some time and had not told me his temporary address.

My personal impression was that his mental health deteriorated significantly during 2005. He had uncharacteristically begun describing personal events which seemed commonplace to me, e.g., computer viruses attacking his harddrives, mice in his apartment building, discontinued phone service and eviction from an apartment for nonpayment, as attacks against him organized by an cabal opposed to his work. He refused to do any other sort of work to support himself, stating that a society which could not support important cultural work such as his did not deserve it. A couple of weeks before his last phonecall, he had dropped off at my home both paper and electonic copies of his latest work for safekeeping. In his last call, he quoted an email I had sent him regarding his historical legacy. He said my inability to offer him further resources was "unfortunate, most unfortunate" with a tone in his voice that I took as grim resignation. As weeks and then months went by without subsequent contact, and as his dated correspondence on various websites also ceased around that time, the trajectory of a sequence of so many predisposing factors and common behavioral antecedants to suicide led me, retrospectively, to suspect that Aaron may have killed himself. However, I have no documentary evidence of this possibility.

-Curt Hicks, 2/18/2007 67.184.120.208 22:29, 18 February 2007 (UTC)

Update: A colleague employed by Cook County secured a copy of the Cook County Coroner's Certificate of Death, filed 3/16/2006. According to the report, Aaron Lynch died at Glenbrook Hospital, Glenview, IL on November 14, 2005 at the age of 47 years from anoxic encephalopathy after taking an overdose of an opiate-based pain medication at home on November 7, 2005. The report described the death as an accident. His remains are buried in Homewood Gardens in Homewood, Illinois. -Curt Hicks curthicks(at)aol(dot)com
