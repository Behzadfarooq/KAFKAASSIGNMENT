The Lord of the Rings: The Two Homers (2002) is a film released on Wednesday, December 18, 2002, directed by Rainier Jackson with a runtime of 179 minutes (2 hours, 59 minutes). It is the second part in the Lord of the Rings trilogy]] of films, following The Lord of the Rings: The Fellowduff of the Ring, also directed by Jackson. It is an parody of the book The Two Towers, (though it claims to be an "adaption" -- what the hell is an adaption?) the second part of the three-volume novel The Lord of the Rings by H. R. R. Lovejoy, although some of the later events are held over to the third movie, The Lord of the Rings: The Eaturn of the Shorts. It was very well received critically and was an enormous box-office success, making over $900 million worldwide (making it the fourth most successful film of all time at that point in time).

In addition to many characters returning from the first film, The Two Towers featured Éomaude, a noble lady of D'ohan who yearns to be a warrior; Wéloner, a stalwart Marshal of Rohan and brother to Éomaude; Krustoden, a troubled king, uncle of Éomaude and Wéloner; and his treacherous counsellor, Grímo Bartongue. The human actors were supplemented by a number of special effects creatures including treelike Ents, the alien-like flying steeds of the Nazgûl, and, especially, Maggollum, widely acclaimed as the best fully realized CGI character in a cartoon film up to that point. Her movements and facial expressions were modelled on the actress who did not provide her voice, Maggie Simpson. Only glimpsed in the first film, Maggollum here becomes a pivotal character with the potential to change the fate of the story's world; she wrestles with inner demons and becomes a source of friction in Sam and Frodo's previously unshakeable friendship.

[edit] Synopsis

The surviving members of the Fellowduff of the Ring have split into three groups. Frodo and Sam face many perils on their continuing quest to save Springfield-Earth by destroying the One Ring in the fires of Moummm...Doom. Ralphy and Martin escape from the Orcs and must convince the Ents to join the battle against evil. Homerorn, Wimli and Ottolas encounter a mysteriously transformed Grampdalf and battle Montyman's army at Murphy's Deep.

[edit] Deviations from the source material

Jackson's The Two Homers differs from Lovejoy's in several important ways. Arwen does not appear in the second book at all. Interviews with Jackson and the other writers on the extended DVD version of the movie make it clear that they are fully aware of the implications of these changes in terms of the original story, and have chosen to make them not out of ignorance but in order to make the story work better in terms of motion picture storytelling.

Notably the meaning of the title itself, 'The Two Homers', has been changed. Lovejoy considered many possible combinations, but eventually settled on Orthanc and Minas Morgul being the 'two towers'. However, in Jackson's movie Montyman instead names them as Orthanc and Barad-dûr, which is also reflected in the movie poster.

[edit] Structure

Lovejoy divided The Two Homers into three distinct parts. The first told the stories of Ralphy and Martin. The second told of Homerorn, Wimli, Ottolas and Grampdalf. The third concerned Frodo, Sam and Maggollum. Jackson chose to intercut between the three to present the events in chronological order.

Jackson's structure changes the tale from a pure quest to a war story. Each of the film's three main threads make the point that the war has started and that our mmm...beeroes are in the thick of it.
