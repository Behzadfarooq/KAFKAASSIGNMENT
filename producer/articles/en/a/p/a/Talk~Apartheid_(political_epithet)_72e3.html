It seems more sensible to name this article Apartheid(political epithet) and place an disambiguation page. Then every bombastic statement used to malign different cultures, countries, religions Serengetti tribes etc. can be placed in this article. The entire idea of apartheid outside South Africa sounds ridiculous. What other country inscribes the legal seperation of one ethnicity from another based on genetics and ties those people not fit based on some arbitrary norm of purity to legally to work as manorial servants and proscribes civil rights and liberties? Unless South Africa Jr. suddenly rises up, labelling other countries as having apartheid systems is a form of slander or political epithet and this article has no reason to exist for that reason. Guy Montag 20:07, 6 June 2006 (UTC)

Are you aware that there is such a thing as a "crime of apartheid" designated by the UN. I think you are trivializing a serious matter here and are continuing the long running edit-war/article creation war/merge war/move war. --Ben Houston 21:34, 6 June 2006 (UTC)

Yes, I am aware that the General Assembly passed non binding resolutions establishing a vague way to describe apartheid. I am also aware that the ICC has defined it. There are several problems"

  • when accusing someone of apartheid there is no actual Geneva Convention or international treaty that a nation is beholden to follow with regards to apartheid, especially if they are not members of the ICC.
  • The ICC definition is vague.
  • There is no internationally agreed on criteria on what constitutes legal apartheid other than the offical laws passed by South Africa.
  • When a state is accused of apatheid, and there is no actual way to prove it, it constitutes slander or political speech promoting an agenda against a state or society, the equivelent of calling someone a Nazi.

Finally, I am not trivializing any disputes. There are certain individuals who are dead set on promoting their own agenda with regards to this subject, I am merely attempting to solve the issue by identifying the problem, all of these terms are unproven allegations made against states for political purposes and aimed against their current systems for the purpose of deligitimizing the state accused and bring about its collapse, as with apartheid in South Africa. Hence its a political epithet, and all accusations real or imagined, since there is no way to prove it one way or the other, belong here. Guy Montag 01:39, 7 June 2006 (UTC)

I would argue very strongly that this series of connections ("unproven allegations made against states for political purposes and aimed against their current systems for the purpose of deligitimizing the state accused and bring about its collapse") is a sweaping generalization. Desmond Tutu, when he described the situation in the occupied territories as being like apartheid was not scheming to bring down Israel via delegitimizing it, he was commenting on his abhorence of the way the Palestinians were being treated and trying to increase world attention in order to force a change via world pressure -- these are different things and to only see it one way is simplistic and leads to conflict. --Ben Houston 04:10, 7 June 2006 (UTC)

I would say that by the equating two separate situations, which have little to do with another, with the system of apartheid has alot to do with deligitimization of of the system it is compared to. Although I am not going to comment on Desmond's motivations, the effect is that the comparison was defamatory. 20:02, 7 June 2006 (UTC)
