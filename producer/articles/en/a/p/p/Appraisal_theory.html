Various approaches to the study of emotion exist in the world of psychology. Among these include physiological, behavioral, and cognitive approaches. In recent years, cognitive approaches to emotion have become a preferred approach to the subject by such theorists as Ira Roseman, Theodore Kemper, Richard Lazarus, and Craig Smith. Physiological theories have been found to lack explanation of exactly how the emotion was elicited, while behavioral theories fail to account for different individual reactions to the same event. In contrast, the appraisal theory accounts for these pitfalls, and proposes that it is the interpretation of an event as opposed to the event itself that causes particular emotions to occur (Roseman, 1984). The structural model of the appraisal theory allows us to understand the subconscious series of questions the brain constantly and subconsciously asks to determine which reactions are appropriate in adaptationally relevant situations. Among these questions include: 1. Is this situation motivationally relevant to my well-being? 2. Is this event motivationally congruent to my goals? 3. Who caused this event, can I influence the outcome of this event, can I emotionally cope with this event, and how can I psychologically make this event more or less motivationally congruent to my future goals? Next, the associative processing system uses priming and spreading activation processes to retrieve relevant information stored in memory, thereby eliciting the appropriate emotions determined by the appraisal.
