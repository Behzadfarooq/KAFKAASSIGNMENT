Appreciative System is a term invented by Sir Geoffrey Vickers [1] to refer to "the activity of attaching meaning to communication or the code by which we do so"

[edit] Quotes

[edit] Vickers

'I find it surprising that we have no accepted word to describe the activity of attaching meaning to communication or the code by which we do so, a code which is constantly confirmed, developed or changed by use. I have for many years referred to this mental activity as ”appreciation‘; and to the code which it uses, as its ”appreciative system‘; and to the state of that code at any time as its ”appreciative setting‘. I call it a system because, although tolerant of ambiguity and even inconsistency, it is sensitive to them and tries to reconcile them‘. [2]

'I'm interested in Systems from the personal up to the very large, human, social systems, I'm also interested in systems of concepts and values through which we see all the others which I call appreciative systems.'[3]

[edit] Checkland

"Vickers argues that our human experience develops within us 'readiness to notice particular aspects of our situation, to discriminate them in particular ways and to measure them against particular standards of comparison..."These readinesses are organized into 'appreciative system' which creates for all of us, individually and socially our appreciated world....the appreciative settings condition new experience but are modified by the new experience; such circular relations Vickers takes to be the common facts of social life, but we fail to see this clearly, he argues, because of the concentration in our science-based culture on linear causal chains an on the notion of goal-seeking."

"Vickers suggests replacing the goal-seeking and goal-seeking with feedback models by one in which personal, institutional ,or cultural activity consists in maintaining desired relationships and eluding undesired ones. The process is a cyclical one which operates like this: Our previous experiences have created for us certain ”standards‘ or ”norms‘, usually ”tacit‘ (and also, at a more general level, ”values‘, more general concepts of what is humanly good and bad); the standards, norms and/or values lead to readiness to notice only certain features of our situations, they determine what ”facts‘ are relevant; the facts noticed are evaluated against the norms, a process which both leads to our taking regulatory action and modifies the norms or standards, so that future experiences will be evaluated differently". [4]
