However, it is not necessary to know or judge the complex historical background and motivation of the parties; all that matters here is that Wikipedia policy is to reflect common English usage and general reference works.

The citations at the bottom show that the primary use of "East Sea" is as an alternate name for the "Sea of Japan." Wikipedia policies and practices, then, are to

  1. redirect East Sea to Sea of Japan
  2. link at the top of Sea of Japan to East Sea (disambiguation)
  3. at East Sea (disambiguation), indicate that the primary english usage is as an alternate name for the Sea of Japan

It's that simple.

[edit] East Sea, East sea

  • It is not disputed that East Sea may sometimes be ambiguous in certain special or regional contexts. There is already a disambiguation page, East Sea (disambiguation).
  • If a title has a primary meaning, even if it has other less common uses, it should redirect to the primary meaning, with a link to the disambiguation page at the top. Examples: South End, South Park, North River, North Island, and more
  • The primary meaning (see sources below) of "East Sea" is the sea between Japan and Korea, so it should redirect to the article titled "Sea of Japan."
  • There are exceptional & regional uses for East Sea that refers to something else, but these are properly listed on the disambiguation page, and none of these exceptions change the fact that the primary meaning is Sea of Japan. There are countless of examples of special & regional uses of South Park and North River, but the exceptions do not change the primary meaning of these terms, and a search for South Park or North Beach goes to the primary meaning article, not a disambiguation page.
  • Therefore, “East Sea” should redirect to the primary meaning “Sea of Japan,” with a disambiguation link at the top. This is consistent with English encyclopedias, dictionaries, Wikipedia policy, and Wikipedia practice.

[edit] East Sea (disambiguation): “East Sea” in English should be defined as primarily meaning “Sea of Japan”

  • See sources, below.
  • “East Sea” as an alternate name for the Sea of Japan is not merely a translation of a foreign term, but an English term found in English reference works. This use should be distinguished from other uses not recognized by English refrence works, and which are no more than translations of local names with only very rare & limited regional uses in English.
  • The primary meaning, the Sea of Japan, should be identified as the primary meaning. Something like "East Sea is an alternate English name for the Sea of Japan. East Sea may also mean:" This is exemplified by School (disambiguation).

[edit] Sea of Japan: Sea of Japan’s alternate English name is East Sea

  • For reasons already explained, there should be a hatnote, a link at the top of the page to East Sea (disambiguation). See hatnote at CD, Tropical cyclone, & and more
  • The introduction should remain Sea of Japan (East Sea), because East Sea is a widely recognized alternate English name. Sea of Japan is still the primary name, and the article should remain titled “Sea of Japan.”

[edit] Wikipedia policies

WP:NPOV, WP:V, and WP:NOR are "official policy," "absolute and non-negotiable" and "cannot be superseded by any other guidelines or by editor's consensus."

[edit] WP:NPOV

"From Jimbo Wales, September 2003, on the mailing list:

  • If a viewpoint is in the majority, then it should be easy to substantiate it with reference to commonly accepted reference texts;
  • If a viewpoint is held by a significant minority, then it should be easy to name prominent adherents;
  • If a viewpoint is held by an extremely small (or vastly limited) minority, it doesn't belong in Wikipedia (except perhaps in some ancillary article) regardless of whether it's true or not; and regardless of whether you can prove it or not."

Here, it is easy to substantiate the majority view with reference to commonly accepted reference texts. Even if there are "prominent" adherents for the minority view, it still only establishes that other uses of East Sea is a significant minority, not a majority. Majority is determine by commonly accepted reference texts.

"A good way to help building a neutral point of view is to find a reputable source for the piece of information you want to add to wikipedia, and then cite that source. This is an easy way to characterize a side of a debate without excluding that the debate has other sides. The trick is to find the best and most reputable sources you can."

"Wikipedia neutrality policy certainly does not state, or imply, that we must "give equal validity" to minority views." "views held only by a tiny minority of people should not be represented as though they are significant minority views, and perhaps should not be represented at all." "To give undue weight to a significant-minority view, or to include a tiny-minority view, might be misleading as to the shape of the dispute."

[edit] WP:V

"'Verifiability' in this context does not mean that editors are expected to verify whether, for example, the contents of a New York Times article are true. In fact, editors are strongly discouraged from conducting this kind of research, because original research may not be published in Wikipedia. Articles should contain only material that has been published by reliable sources, regardless of whether individual editors view that material as true or false." "Articles should rely on credible, third-party sources with a reputation for fact-checking and accuracy."

We shouldn't be engaged in theoretical discussions about whether the content of Britannica, Encarta, Nat'l Geographic, etc that refer "East Sea" to "Sea of Japan" are true or not. Our articles should contain the material published by these reliable sources, namely, redirecting "East Sea" to "Sea of Japan," regardless of individual Wikipedians' opinions as to the validity of that content.

[edit] WP:NOR

"the only way to verifiably demonstrate that you are not doing original research is to cite reliable sources which provide information that is directly related to the article, and to adhere to what those sources say."
