Hi. First of all, I'd like to attempt to clear up your problem in understanding the situation re British vs Irish. JayJasper is wrong when he says, "Ireland is part of the UK". Only Northern Ireland is part of the United Kingdom. As such, people from Northern Ireland are British. However, and this is where it starts to get complex, there are those of us in Northern Ireland who reject, to various degrees, any notion that we might be British. It doesn't stop us being British in the eyes of the law though.

Many people prefer to be called Northern Irish, as it combines a certain sense of Britishness (NI is part of the UK), with a certain sense of Irishness (self-evident).

JayJason is again wrong when he asserts that "only natives of Great Britain are commonly referred to as British". Great Britain is an island. The nationality of people born in the UK to British parents, is British. Likewise, the nationality of people born in the USA, to American parents, is American.

The UK consists of four countries: England, Northern Ireland, Scotland and Wales. People from England are English, but their nationality is also "British". Ditto with the Scottish, the (Northern) Irish and the Welsh.

People from the Republic of Ireland have it easier - they are no longer part of the UK, so they don't have a tier-structure nationality! They are Irish and their nationality is also "Irish".

For the UK though, you could think about Texans vs Americans, or Virginians vs Americans etc.

Van Morrison was born and grew up in Belfast, in the same area as I did (though at very different times). As such, he is definately British by virtue of his birth nation. I have no doubt that Morrison is proud, like I am, of his Irish heritage also. Like me, I would say that he probably self-describes as an Irishman or as British, depending on the context at the time. The same is true with the Scots and the English for example.

I hope that crash course doesn't confuse you all the more! Anyway, to your question about his membership of Clubsound. I'm not sure I'd go that far. As far as I remember, he used to jam with the band, and probably played a few gigs with them. I thought I had provided some sort of citation for his article. You should look up all the info you can on George Jones - I'm sure he mentioned Van the Man more than once!

Curiously enough, I was friends with George's son in school. I say "curiously" because his name is Jay - short for Jason. Well there ya go! Anyway, I hope I've been of some use. If you're still stuck on the Clubsound link, I'd be happy to give it some research time in the next week or so and see if I can dig anything up. --Mal 21:58, 27 January 2007 (UTC)

[edit] Re: Footnotes

If something is easily verifiable, it generally does not require a reference (Van is a singer for example). It really comes down to personal judgement, if you feel people will dispute any material on the article, cite a source for that material. Any and all negative comments about a living person have to be referenced, as per Wikipedia:Biographies of living persons. When it comes to entire paragraphs, I would say it's fine to put multiple page numbers in the footnote and only reference it once. Like I said, it really comes down to whether or not you feel people will question any of the material on the article, if they do question it, or you think they will, then it needs a reference. This is the problem with Wikipedia, which is why many people with expert info get turned off from bothering with it, and why the Middlebury department of History has banned its use as a source - certain editors begin to believe that they 'own' a particular page, and that nothing should be changed without their consent. That's actually understandable - an article can become your baby, and you don't want to see inappropriate things added; but such is the nature of the collective project that is Wiki. I myself have suffered from this - I put in a lot of work into the article on Crosby, Stills, Nash and Young (which isn't even one of my favorite bands it's just that I know the history very well) only to have some of it replaced by duller writing and inferior work. Now, if whoever had edited my CSNY article had done so with superior stuff, I would have been all for it and quite happy; but you can go to the talk page on the CSNY article and see what kind of arguments were used to rebut my edits. As regards the Van discography, I am willing to take out the word 'indefensible,' but I would like to add that this is not a non-neutral viewpoint. The 1911 Encyclopedia Britannica, acknowledged by scholars and historians to be the greatest encyclopedia ever written, is loaded with articles which fail the Wikipedia NPOV criteria. While science requires neutrality, and history requires presentation of opposing interpretation, any article dealing with art is fairly useless *without* a viewpoint. It's not enough to tell the who what and where with Art - you also need to explain the hows and whys. The 1911 editors understood this - read their fabulous article on Beethoven for proof. At some point, you *have* to violate neutrality to say something meaningful about culture and art, especially in an encyclopedia that is fast becoming the first source people go to for info. It could be judged wrong to state that the works of David Cassidy being out of print is an indefensible position, but would it be wrong to claim indefensibility for such a state of affairs regarding Duke Ellington? For Mozart? Beethoven? Would it really violate NPOV to state that Shakespeare being out of print is indefensible? Van Morrison was a fairly important artist of the 1970s, and many still agree Astral Weeks as being one of the greatest recordings ever made. Would it violate neutrality to say that he's the best musical entity in Western history? Yes. But does it violate neutrality to simply say that it's indefensible for an artist of his stature to have so many works out of print, denying people the ability to make an assessment of his worth via hearing his work? At some point, you have to make a judgment, and if the NPOV stands in the way of making a good judgment, then that rule should be altered. You bend rules to fit reality, not the other way around. So this is probably what will happen again' as a courtesy. I will remove that as well. PPS The Morrison albums *are* out of print; that is a fact, and again, that cannot be corroborated by an outside source beyond retailers unless you can get either Morrison's management, agents, or dep't directors at the record labels on the phone.
