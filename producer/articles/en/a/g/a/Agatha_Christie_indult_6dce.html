Agatha Christie indult

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

On 5 November 1971, in response to a request from Cardinal John Carmel Heenan, the bishops of England and Wales were authorized to grant permission for the occasional celebration of Mass in the old form, with the modifications introduced in 1965 and 1967.[1]

Traditionalist Catholics gave the indult the nickname of the "Agatha Christie Indult", a reference to the fact that the crime novelist Agatha Christie was apparently recognised by the Pope when reading through a petition[2] sent earlier that year to the Holy See, requesting that the old form of the Mass should be allowed "to survive, even though this survival took place side by side with other liturgical forms."[3]
