The Red-eyed Tree Frog (Agalychnis callidryas) is a tree frog native to the rainforests of Costa Rica and Central America.

The Red-eyed Tree Frog is a medium-sized tree frog, reaching lengths of 50-75 millimeters. Its dorsal surface is green of varying shades, and its ventral surface is white. The sides of the frog are purple or blue, with vertical white stripes and orange toes.

The Red-eyed Tree Frog lays its eggs in a gelatinous goo on the underside of leaves. In one or two weeks, the tadpoles hatch and wriggle until they drop off the leaf into a pool of water below, where they metamorphose into frogs. During their tadpole stage, their colors can change like a chameleon's, in response to their environmental conditions or their emotional state.

[edit] Diet and behavior

Agalychnis callidryas

Bright markings along the sides and on the limbs function to reduce predation. Most animals that prey on A. callidryas (some bats, snakes, and birds) often rely on their vision. When the frog moves to avoid the predator the bright colorations come into the pretitors view. Because the predator is so locked onto its target, when these bright colors flash into view (hence their name, flash colors) they throw off the predator. This is achieved by leaving a ghost image in the visual field of where the frog was originally. This confuses the predator and gives the frog time to hide. In other cases, these bright colors often startle the predator and throws them completely off. These flash colors are often well hidden and are only shown when the animal is moving.

The red-eyed tree frog is classified into 8 different groups, which give the animal a specific name. The first two are the largest, domain, eukarya, and kingdom, animalia. All animals are in both that domain and kingdom. The next two following are smaller, but more specific: phylum, chordata, and class, amphibia. Chordata merely means that the animal is a vertebrate, has a backbone, and amphibia means that the animal is an amphibian, rather than mammal or reptile. The following four are the smallest levels of classification, and most specific: order, anura, family, hydilae, genus, agalychnis, and species, callidryas. Anura is made up of Greek words and means “tail-less,” and hydilae is the family in which all tree frogs are categorized. Being made up of the genus and species, the scientific name of the red-eyed tree frog is Agalychnis callidryas.

The males around 2 inches, while females can reach 3-5 inches, and are always larger than the males. Both have 2 bulging red eyes with slits, and resemble the eyes of domestic cats. Their top sense is their sight; red-eyed tree frogs can see in-front, to the sides, above, and even behind them! They can also pull down their eyes through the roof of their mouth to help push down food. Other senses, such as smell and taste, may lack in ability, but touch is another very sensitive and strong sense. The top side of the frog is bright, neon green, with sides of indigo or purple, and a soft-skinned, cream-colored belly. Red-eyed tree frogs have soft, fragile skin on their belly, while the skin on their back is still soft, but thicker and rougher. Being a climber, the red-eyed tree frog has 4 yellow or orange separated toes which each have a padded suction cup attached, making holding onto things much easier. Red-eyed tree frogs have very long legs that are a slightly lighter green than the body. Being so brightly colored, they blend in perfectly with their environment.

Red-eyed tree frogs inhabit the lowland rainforests of South and Central America, and usually live by rivers or ponds, in order to reproduce. Being in such a green environment helps them blend in. Since red-eyed tree frogs are nocturnal, they need to blend in during the day. They sleep on the underside of leaves and stay completely motionless. During night they climb on the stems of plants, moving very slowly and swiftly, and avoid jumping so that they are not heard. Red-eyed tree frogs move like this so that they will surprise their prey by attacking them quickly, amidst their slow movement.

Red-eyed tree frogs aren’t poisonous and rely on camouflage to protect them. While sleeping during the day, they cover their blue sides with their back legs, tuck their bright feet under their belly, and shut their red eyes. That way, they remain almost completely green and are rarely spotted. During the night, they search for prey. Red-eyed tree frogs are carnivores and eat insects such as moths, crickets, beetles, flies, and sometimes feed on smaller frogs. Natural enemies of the frog may include birds, bats, turtles, lizards, snakes, and mammals larger than them. If any animal tries to attack a red-eyed tree frog while sleeping, they will suddenly open their humongous red eyes, possibly startling the animal, that giving them a second to jump off to safety. If a flying animal such as a bird or bat may try to attack a red-eyed tree frog, while flying, the frog will show off its vibrant coloration. Since the predator was so locked on its target, the colors will throw the enemy off, or give it the impression that it was poisonous. But animals are not the only enemy to the red-eyed tree frog, man is the worst. Our apparent interest in deforestation has caused loss of habitat for these creatures, and their numbers are slowly decreasing, but not dramatically. Man has also discovered the joys of pets. Red-eyed tree frogs are often now being captured and sold to zoos or to people as pets. With less males and females, the species cannot reproduce or mate—causing the numbers of the red-eyed tree frog to lessen. They are not considered threatened, but may become so because of pollution, loss of habitat, etc.

Red-eyed tree frogs only are known to make vocal noise when participating in a mating process called the “chorus-line”, which is the only time when they really communicate with other red-eyed tree frogs. Males may start to chirp, allowing the females to come out of hiding. Then, the males jump on the back of the females, more than 3 males per female, and hold on. In addition to the many males riding her back, females just continue to do their every day routine and this process continues until all but one red-eyed tree frog has fallen off. Once the mating process is over, the two mates lock into something known as an amplexus embrace, where the male forms himself into a ‘hugging position’ with his legs wrapped around the female’s body, reproducing sexually. The female red-eyed tree frog has a gestation period ranging from 6-8 days.

While the female lays her eggs, the male fertilizes them with a special sperm. She may choose to lay her eggs on the underside of leaves, or under water, and if the female chooses water, then the male may face attacks from other red-eyed tree frogs who have not found a mate. This is about the only time a red-eyed tree frog will compete with other animals. Even so, the female lays 30-50 pale eggs, in a safe area, to protect them from predators, and departs. Tadpoles may take 75-80 days to grow into a full sized red-eyed tree frog, but as an egg, they have a rubbery, gelatinous coating that helps protect them from predators. If an egg is attacked, it will sense intense vibrations and sometimes roll away before the predator can tear through the outer coating. They go through metamorphosis and start to develop color once they are a near-mature tadpole. From then on, they stay pretty healthy, but as temperatures change, even with not much variation, males may become somewhat underweight. Red-eyed tree frogs live about 3 to 5 years maximum, in the wild.

Red-eyed tree frogs are closely related to chorus frogs, which have the same body style, but chorus frogs are more vocal, hence their name. Chorus frogs have the same slim figure as male red-eyed tree frogs, and have many of the same habits.
