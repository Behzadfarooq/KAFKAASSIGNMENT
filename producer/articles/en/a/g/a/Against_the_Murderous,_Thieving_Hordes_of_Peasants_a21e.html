Against the Murderous, Thieving Hordes of Peasants (1525) A tract written by Martin Luther (German title: Wider die räuberischen und mörderischen Rotten der Bauern) in May, 1525. On 15 April, Luther had received a copy of the Twelve Articles of the Christian Union of Upper Swabia, a set of biblically-referenced articles which justified peasant revolt, and were being widely distributed throughout Germany. In response, he had published his Admonition to Peace, in which he argued that though many of the peasant grievances were justified, it did not warrant a recourse to the sword.

In late April and early May, Luther embarked on a preaching tour of Saxony and Thuringia. Only during this tour did he realise the full extent of peasant unrest. In response, on 6 May he appended this text, which he titled Against the Rebelling Peasants (German title: Wider die sturmenden Bauern), to the third Wittenberg edition of his Admonition to Peace. This was published on 10 May. Printers in other cities, however, quickly retitled it with the more radical title without Luther's approval, and sold it as a separate pamphlet.

At this time Luther's prince Frederick the Wise was fatally ill and refrained from putting down the peasant uprisings, hoping for a peaceful settlement. Luther saw the need to take the initiative of calling the civil authorities to action against the anarchy that appeared to be threatening law and order in Saxony and neighboring regions.

Luther was widely criticized for the violent language he employed in this tract, both immediately after its publication and in years to come. In early July 1525, Luther wrote An Open Letter on the Harsh Book Against the Peasants, in which he explained and justified his stance. In Luther's view the actions of the peasants were in direct violation of God's authority, making them instruments of the devil. Although he criticised the rulers for their wanton killing, he stated that they would reap God's wrath for their conduct.

The play Luther by John Osborne questions and explores the morality of this tract.
