[edit] Yet another confusing quote

One secret order dictated to the Kriegsmarine (the German Navy) mentioned:

"The military dominion of Europe an land space, permit later of Russian defeat considerably diminished the Wehrmacht land power. At contrary the Armoury weapon experimented one augment. The Kriegsmarine was debt to limited at measures related at war against England and United States."

- ???? I deleted this because it doesn't make any sense. If someone knew what it was supposed to mean I could paraphrase it in better English, but I don't get it. Hdstubbs 21:12, 3 February 2006 (UTC)

The first two sentences, from what I can glean, is referring to the fact that, especially after successful advances into Russian territory, the German armed forces were stretched to their limits maintaining "military dominion of European land space", which considerably reduced their true strength and power, especially their ability to make further advances on other fronts (like a direct assault on American soil), even though they were making headway with experimental weapons upgrades, of some sort (don't ask me, the sentence is far too vague).
The last sentence seems to be stating that the Navy, too, was over extending itself in it's efforts against british and American naval forces. Alternatively, it could also be referring to the Treaty of Versailles limits and constraints placed upon Germany after WWI, by Britain and America, dis-allowing the germans to re-build their naval forces to full capacity.
Hope that helps.
shabbs 15:53, 7 February 2006 (UTC)

[edit] References / poor facts?

While some of the items covered in this article reflect either common sense or established fact, there are no references. In addition, some of the other items do not seem to make sense absent backing. Eg.:

The Wehrmacht, in planning for the British invasion, developed some types of airborne and amphibious tanks and other special heavy armour, later considered for an eventual invasion of America. The Kriegsmarine ordered the design of some aircraft carriers (Based on Akagi-Type Great Japanese Navy Flag-Carriers):

The Akagi class aircraft carriers were conversions from cruisers and such. They would have been a poor template for series production, and in fact, Graf Zeppelin seems to have been of a superior design. This example is illustrative of problems with this article. Furthermore, neither Graf Zeppelin nor Peter Strasser were based on Akagi. —Joseph/N328KF (Talk) 21:32, 30 October 2006 (UTC)
