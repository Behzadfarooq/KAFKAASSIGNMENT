[edit] intro

The indium chalcogenides are compounds of indium and heavier members of group 16,sulfur, selenium, tellurium and polonium. They form the sulfides, selenides and tellurides.(Polonium compounds have not been studied). Many of these compounds belong to the III-VI group of semiconductors, as such they are the subject of a lot of research interest as they have potential uses in electronic equipment, LEDs, solar cells etc. A property that some of the chalcogenides have is that they are phase change materials, that is they change state from crystalline to amorphous, or vice versa, following the application of heat from a laser or from an electric current. This means that they could be used in optical recording media or in phase-change memory. The indium chalcogenides are a large group of compounds, with many varied stoichiometries, unlike the oxides where there are only two indium(I) oxide (In2O) and indium(III)oxide (In2O3). There are 3 sulfides, 4 selenides and 6 tellurides. The reasons for this abundance stem from, on the one hand indiums ability to form polyatomic units and to form two different ions, In+ and In3+, and for the chalcogenides to form polyatomic anions, e.g. Te32-. None of the indium chalcogenides can be described simply as ionic, they all involve a high degree of covalent bonding. However in spite of this it is useful to formulate the compounds in ionic terms to get an insight into the structures are built up. Compounds almost invariably have multiple polymorphs, that is they can crystallise in slightly different forms depending on the method of production or the substrate on which they are deposited. Many of the compounds are made up of layers and it is the different ways that the layers are stacked that causes the polymorphism.
The best characterised compounds are the In(III) and In(II)chalcogenides e.g. the sulfides In2S3 and InS.

[edit] In2S3, In2Se3, In2Te3

In2S3
Indium(III) sulfide is a compound of indium and sulfur. The solid has a high melting point, and has different colours depending on the crystal structure. Yellow, α-In2S3 has a defect cubic structure, β-In2S3 is red and has a defect spinel, tetragonal, structure and γ-In2S3 has a layered structure. The red, β, form is considered to be the most stable form at room temperature, although the yellow form may be present depending on the method of production. In2S3 is insoluble in cold water but dissolves in acids. Indium(III) sulfide is an n- type semiconductor. It has been proposed to replace the hazardous CdS as a buffer layer in solar cells. [1]
Traditionally it was prepared in the chemical laboratory by direct combination of the elements. Production from volatile complexes of indium and sulphur for example dithiocarbamates (e.g. Et2InIIIS2CNEt2,) have been explored as methods suitable for vapor deposition techniques. [2]

[edit] Other stoichiometries

In2Te5
This is a polytelluride compound and the structure is made up of layers that in turn are made up of chains of linked InTe4 tetrahedra where three of the Te atoms are bridging. There are Te atoms separate from the chains. The compound has been formulated as (In3+2 Te2-Te32-)n counterbalanced with separate Te2- ions. The structure is similar to Al2Te5.[3]
In3Te5
This was reported in phase studies in 1964 but its structure has not been confirmed.
In7Te10
Formulated as In4+2 In23+ Te2-20 with In-In of 276.3pm
In3Te4
This compound has been reported as a superconductor [4]. Its structure has not been confirmed.
In6S7, In6Se7
These compounds have the same structure and have been formulated with indium in 3 different oxidation states, +1, +2 and +3, e.g. In+ (In2)4+ 3(In3+) 7(S2-). The indium – indium bond length in the In2 units are 2.741 A (sulphide), 2.760 (selenide).[5]
In6S7 is known to be an n-type semiconductor. [6]


In4Se3, In4Te3
These are similar black crystalline solids and have been formulated to contain a non linear In35+ unit (that is isoelectronic with (Hg3)2+. For example the selenide is formulated as In+ (In3)5+ 3Se2-. The In –In bond length is 279 pm (cc In metal 324).
