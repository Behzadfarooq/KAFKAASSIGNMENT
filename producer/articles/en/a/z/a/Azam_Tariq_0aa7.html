Azam Tariq

The Wahhabi mullah ("maulana") Azam Tariq (Urdu: اعظم طارق) (March 1962 - October 4, 2003) was chief of the terrorist organisation and political machine Sipah-e-Sahaba Pakistan ("warriors of the Prophet's companions in Pakistan"), and was one of Pakistan's most powerful sectarian leaders between Karachi and Gilgit. Between 1997 and 2003 he coordinated massacres of hundreds of Shia Muslims in Pakistan, according to Shia sources. Sipah-e-Sahaba is a Deobandi extemist organisation, which was officially banned by General Musharraf, acting president of Pakistan, in August 2001. The successful assassination was the fourth attempt. According to his concept "Every thing is religion there is no importance of Humanity", and following this rule he orchestrated the killing of thousands of innocent fellow Pakistanis, just because they were Shia Muslims.

A graduate and teacher at a Karachi madrassa, Jamia Islamia, Azam Tariq came into the circle of Maulana Haq Nawaz Jhangvi, a cleric from Jhang, a south-central town of Punjab, who had become known for his sectarian sermons, and who invited him to run his madrassa for promoting the terrorisom. Following Jhangvi's assassination by Shia terrorists, in 1997 Azam Tariq inherited the top position in the group, when he survived the explosion in Lahore that killed Maulana Ziaur Rehman Farooqi. At least 13 police officers and a press photographer were also killed. During this time, notorious hitman of Sipah-e-Sahaba, Riaz Basra, developed differences with Azam Tariq and made his own splinter group, the even more ultra-violent Lashkar-e-Jhangvi.

Four times he was elected to the National Assembly of Pakistan from the group's stronghold in Jhang Sadr, even while he was jailed. Ironically, Jhang, and its surrounding localities, have historically been, and still are, heavily Shia populated areas.

In October 2000, at a party rally in Karachi, Azam Tariq outlined his plans for an Islamic republic in Pakistan. The militant Lashkar-e-Jhangvi movement was publicly presented as a splinter group of the party. In 2001 its chief, Maulana Akram Awan, headquartered in Chakwal, publicly called upon General Musharraf to impose Sharia on Islamabad or suffer the consequences. In 2001, while he was in jail on charges of terrorism, the Pakistani press reported a spate of killings of prominent Shia community leaders who were deemed complicit in his arrest and the crack-down on his organization. In August 2001, Musharraf banned seven alleged terrorist organizations, including Sipah e Sahaba, but Azam Tariq was acquitted of all charges on September 9, 2001, amid Shia charges that a deal had been brokered. He was released in November 2002 after he won his usual Jhang seat in parliament and soon announced that he was joining the pro-Musharraf coalition.

He was gunned down October 4, 2003 near Islamabad as he left M2 Motorway to enter the city. Another leader, Qari Zia-ur-Rehman was also killed. Qari Zia, who came from Balakot, had been with the maulana for three days. Qari Zia served as the secretary of Commander Baba, a famous jihadi, but later formed his own group called Tehrik Jihad-e-Islami. The body of Qari Zia was sent to his native village of Shangeer.

Afterwards a member of the National Assembly, Amanullaha Sial, a major suspect, was held on physical remand in connection with the murder. According to his counsel, reported in the on-line Daily Times October 7, 2004, the complainant's statement he gave to the investigation officer that the murder plan was allegedly hatched at the house of Qazi Hussain Ahmed, the acting president of the Muttahida Majlis-e-Amal. According to the complainant, Allama Sajid Naqvi, president of the Pakistan Islami Tehrek, was present at the MMA supreme council meeting at Ahmed’s residence when the murder plan was developed. The court has delayed Allama Sajid Naqvi’s indictment for the case till April 7, 2005. There was talk of this conspiracy being hatched as a result of Azam Tariq going back to his fiery anti-Shia rhetoric soon after release, and as a result was deemed a 'security risk' by the authorities, especially by the then Interior Minister, Moinuddin Haider, who incidentally was a devout Shia himself.

On October 4, 2004, at a nighttime memorial rally in the city of Multan, where several hundred supporters of the group, which had been renamed Millat-e-Islami but was banned once more as a terrorist front, had gathered to commemorate the anniversary of his death, a car bomb planted by the Shia opposition killed at least 40 people, prompting the new Interior Minister Aftab Khan Sherpao to call out the military to preserve public order.
