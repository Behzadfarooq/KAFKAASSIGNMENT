The Amur leopard (Panthera pardus orientalis or P. p. amurensis) is the northernmost subspecies of leopard. It is also known as the Far-East leopard and the Siberian leopard. Its habitat is the Sikhote-Alin mountains of Russia, and also the northern mountainous region of North Korea. It is extremely close to extinction with perhaps as few as 35 individuals remaining in the wild. Habitat destruction and the fur trade have diminished its numbers dramatically, and has resulted in it being the rarest cat on Earth today.

[edit] Behavior

Although their range overlaps with that of the Siberian tiger, the population is not as heavily affected as it is for leopards living near tigers in different regions. Amur leopards tend to avoid living or hunting too close to tiger territory to avoid direct competition for prey.

Like all leopards, they are very skillful and opportunistic hunters, though their usual diet consists of roe and sika deer, hares, badgers and smaller rodents. They are solitary animals with primarily nocturnal habits.

[edit] Biology

Amur leopard image

The Amur leopard stands apart from the ten other living subspecies of leopard with it's longer fur and legs, helping it better cope with the cold, harsh conditions of the taiga. It also has larger and more widely spaced rosettes. The fur is golden orange and about 2.5 cm long during the summer but grows to around 7 cm during the winter, when it also lightens to a pale cream color. Males are about fifty percent larger than females, with their weight ranging from 65 to 155 pounds (30-70 kg).

[edit] Conservation

While the Amur leopard inhabits the same area as the Siberian tiger, it has received far less attention from the media and from charities. The Amur leopard is the rarest of all the big cats, and is in danger of dying out in the wild. It is suffering from habitat loss and is especially vulnerable to natural disasters, such as fire, because of a fragmented range. Amur leopards prefer to live in forested territory, but most of it's remaining territory is surrounded by farms and villages, making poaching easier. It is estimated that only 35-40 of these elusive felines are remaining in the wild.

Russian plans for building an oil pipeline through the last remaining habitat of the Amur leopard were recently redirected to a safer route after pressure from the WWF and other environmental organizations.
