Americano Creek (Latitude: 38.317 N; Longitude: 122.898 W) is a stream in southeastern Sonoma County, California[1] and western Marin County that discharges to the Pacific Ocean. This watercourse rises in the low lying coastal hills west of Cotati and meanders to the Americano Estuary, an area of federal protection.[2] Its headwaters are in an area near Roblar Road where a closed landfill and a proposed hard rock quarry are situated. For approximately two miles of the upper reach and the entire seven miles of the lower reach, Americano Creek is the boundary line separating Sonoma from Marin County. The headwaters area is an historic habitat for a number of rare and endangered species including Sebastopol Meadowfoam, Limnanthes vinculans; Showy Indian clover, Trifolium amoenum; and Pitkin Marsh Lily, Lilium pitkinese.[3] Flow rates are highly seasonal with the heaviest flows occurring in the rainy winter months. A permanent Coast Miwok prehistoric village has been authenticated to have existed at the mouth of the mouth of Estero Americano Creek; the village name is Awachi.

Americano Creek has been designated by the U.S. Government as an impaired water body subject to the meaning of section 303(d) of the Federal Water Resource Statutes. It is also noted as an impaired estuary that discharges to a federally protected marine sanctuary, the Gulf of the Farallons National Marine Santuary. Primary water pollutants listed for impairment are nutrients (i.e. nitrate and phosphate) as well as sediment. Secondary pollutants of concern are ammonia, boron and coliform bacteria. All reaches of Americano Creek are considered impaired sue to the historic and ongoing overgrazing practices.[4]Water testing for pH (acidity) levels have generally found slightly alkaline values in Americano Creek and its estuary; typically pH levels in the range of 7.5 to 8.0 are found, but these results are within the guidelines of the State of California Basin Plan.

[edit] Upper Reach

Grazing in marshy creekbed of upper reach of Americano Creek.

The upper headwaters rise on the western slopes of low lying hills approximately seven miles due west of the center of Cotati in the vicinity of Roblar Road. The extreme upper reach has a controversial proposal for a rock quarry as of 2006. For the initial eight miles the stream meanders through a broad valley dominated by agricultural uses, principally cattle grazing. The valley floor is quite flat and generally wide, especially as the stream descends to lower elevations. Virtually no rocks are evident in the streambed, since heavy siltation has occurred from natural processes and accelerated from the grazing uses. The grazing is typically occurring in the streambed itself adding to the pathogen and nitrate problems of Americano Creek. In the rainy season (November to April) this bottomland consists, in places, of an expansive marshy area, although most of the native wetland vegetation has been destroyed by ongoing cattle use. Grazing activity is sufficiently intense that cattle are often seen wading in the broad channel marshy streambed over 40 centimeters in depth; many of these marshy areas are over 100 meters in width.

[edit] Lower reach

Beginning of lower reach of Americano Creek, looking downstream

Rather suddenly at a point approximately seven miles above the mouth, the stream follows a more regular channel with sufficient depth for kayaking in the winter months. In this reach Americano Creek continues to be surrounded by agricultural uses; however, the winding well defined and deepened channel is more separated from the grazing uses than in the case of the broad flat valley of the upper reach. The lower reach can be taken to begin at the 1985 bridge across Valley Ford Road. Under this bridge is an unpaved boat launch access road, accessed from Marsh Road, where kayakers embark downstream. Soon the lower reach becomes fully tidal and is known as the Estero Americano. The stream has virtually no roadway access or visibility from public roads in the lower six miles of its course; moreover, there are no bridges of any kind across the last seven lower miles of Americano Creek.

[edit] Bridges

The following bridges span Americano Creek: Gericke Road, Roblar Road and Valley Ford Road.[5] The newest of these, erected in 1985, is the 22.3 meter concrete slab structure across Valley Ford Road, at a point 3.6 miles (5.8 km) east of the junction with California State Highway 1. The oldest of these bridges is the Roblar Road span, which is a 8.2 meter long concrete culvert constructed in the year 1964; this structure is situated 0.7 miles (1.1 km) east of the junction with Valley Ford Road. The Gericke Road bridge structure is a 32.6 meter concrete continuous Slab built in 1972 at a point 0.2 miles (300 m) south of Valley Ford Road..
