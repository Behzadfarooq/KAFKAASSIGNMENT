Amerigo Dumini

Amerigo Dumini (1894—1967) was an Italian fascist activist. He was the leader of the group who assassinated Giacomo Matteotti in 1924.

[edit] Membership in "Ceka" and Matteotti's killing

Born in St. Louis, Missouri, the son of Italian immigrants to the United States, Dumini returned to Florence and became active in the local fascio organized by partisans of Benito Mussolini, taking pride in being referred to as "sicario del Duce" ("Il Duce's hitman"). He participated to the Sarzana incident on 21 July, 1921, leading a column of 600 squadristi to Sarzana to reclaim the liberation of Renato Ricci. Ricci, and others fascists had been arrested following armed confrontation with the Arditi del Popolo, an antifascist organization created in 1921 by the anarchist Argo Secondari as an offshoot of the arditi movement.

In 1924 he entered the ranks of "Ceka", the secret police which Mussolini had based on the Soviet Cheka.

Dumini was the leader of the group that carried out the killing of Giacomo Matteotti in the same year. Immediately after the homicide, he was abandoned by the Fascist leadership, with the exception of Roberto Farinacci, who defended him during his trial. While Farinacci declared himself "honored" by the task, he could not prevent Dumini from receiving a five-year prison sentence, of which he only served eleven months, benefitting from an amnesty ordered by Mussolini.

Once out of jail, he tried his hand at blackmailing the fascist leadership, trying to obtain rewards and the payment of his legal fees. Ostentatiously, he sought a meeting with Mussolini at the Italian Council of Ministers. He justified his entitlement with the words "I am here in order to wash Matteotti's blood off my hands". Arrested, he was put on trial for "offending Il Duce" and sentenced to eight years in prison—substantially more than for the previous crime.

[edit] Exile and new prison terms

Freed in 1927, Amerigo Dumini left for Italian Somaliland, having been awarded a large state pension (5,000 lire). Apparently, he was still viewed as troublesome, since he was detained and interned on the Tremiti Islands. Meanwhile, he warned General Emilio De Bono that he had filed a manuscript detailing Matteotti's murder with notaries in Texas. This claim led to his release and an increase in pension to as much as 50,000 lire. He left for the Italian colony of Libya, where his pension was further increased by 2,500 lire (together with a single payment of 125,000 lire).

He remained in the region for more than a decade and was captured by the British Army during the North African Campaign of World War II. Sentenced to death as a spy, he was hit by seventeen bullets and still managed to remain alive, escaping to safety in Tunisia during the night. Returning to Italy, he was received with astonishment and offered yet another generous pension. Dumini started a business as a transporter and bought himself a villa in a residential area of Florence.

With the 1943 fall of Fascism in the larger part of Italy, he joined the Nazi-backed Italian Social Republic. After the liberation of that region, Dumini was arrested in Bologna and placed on trial for Matteotti's murder. He was given a life sentence, but spent no more than eight years in prison and died at the age of 72 or 73 as the result of a domestic accident.
