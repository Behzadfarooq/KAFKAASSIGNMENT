[edit] September 24 to December 02, 2006

The Dallas Cowboys are a sports franchise situated in Irving, Texas, United States, the side of which contest professional American football in the East division of the National Football Conference (NFC) on the National Football League. The team, first owned by Clint Murchison, Junior, and Bedford Wynne and constituted originally as the Dallas Steers and briefly thereafter as the Dallas Rangers, was granted expansion status in 1960 and, holding home games as the Cotton Bowl in Dallas, began play in the 1960 season, when, under general manager Tex Schramm and head coach Tom Landry, each ultimately inducted into the Pro Football Hall of Fame and honored on the team's Ring of Honor at Texas Stadium, the team failed to win a game, losing eleven and drawing one.

Although the team did not post a winning record across its first six seasons, the team finished 10-3-1 in 1966, after the regular season of which the team lost the 1966 National Football League Championship Game, 34-27, to the Green Bay Packers. [edit] July 21 to September 24, 2006

The National Football League Players' Association (NFLPA) is the labor union representing the players of the United States' National Football League (NFL), organized in 1956, principally by Baltimore Colts defensive back Don Shula, New York Giants halfback Frank Gifford, and Los Angeles Rams quarterback Norm Van Brocklin, in order that players might formally and collectively demand a minimum salary, per diem pay, team-provided and -maintained uniforms and protective equipment, and injury pay.

Players from the Green Bay Packers and Cleveland Browns readily joined with Shula, et al., and the association subsequently presented franchise owners with check cards endorsed by a majority of the league's players, seeking the right to negotiate with owners without formal union certification; owners refused to meet with the players and consider the group's demands. In response, the association threatened to initiate an antitrust lawsuit against the League, a threat treated as credible after the United States Supreme Court, in Radovich v. NFL in 1957, ruled that the NFL did not enjoy the same exemption from the Sherman Antitrust Act as Major League Baseball; in view of the prospective action, the league's owners, through commissioner Bert Bell, acceded to most of the players' demands but refused to enter into a collective bargaining agreement or to recognize the association qua union.

The threat of antitrust litigation allowed the Association to leverage gains from the league's owners for several years, with the Association's eventually winning pension and health insurance plans and guaranteed salary for players participating in exhibition games. A formal organizing drive supported by the International Brotherhood of Teamsters was undertaken by several players, most notably Bernie Parrish of the Cleveland Browns, in 1968, but proved unsuccessful; after a brief lockout and strike action, the league and players agreed on a contract, albeit a weak one, in view of its having first been agreed to by the players' union of the American Football League (AFLPA), with which the NFL would soon merge.

The NFLPA, then led by Baltimore Colts tight end John Mackey, and AFLPA, led by Buffalo Bills quarterback Jack Kemp, pictured, overcame certain differences to effect a merger in 1970, and the new NFLPA soon successfully petitioned the National Labor Relations Board for union certification. The new union won small gains as regarded the right of players to employ sports agents to negotiate directly with teams and of occupational injury grievances to be decided by impartial arbitrators but was unable to satisfy its pecuniary goals. The union thereafter elected to file the antitrust lawsuit previously threatened, alleging that the Pete Rozelle Rule, which, as a reserve clause, prohibited players from leaving without restriction their teams, even upon the expiration of a contract, was an unfair restraint of trade. The lawsuit succeeded by the union was unable to attain elimination of the Rozelle Rule until 1977 and even then failed in its attempt to secure 55 per cent of league revenues for player salaries and free agency for players upon the expiration of contracts.

[edit] June 18 to July 21, 2006

The 46 defense (known also as the Bear defense) is an American football defensive scheme popularized by Buddy Ryan when Ryan was defensive coordinator for the Chicago Bears and later implemented by Ryan as head coach of the Philadelphia Eagles. The defense, which reached the height of its popularity in the National Football League when the Bears captured the Super Bowl XX title, is employed to varying degrees by several extant franchises, most notably the Baltimore Ravens, for whom Ryan's son Rex is defensive coordinator.

The defense, named for the jersey number worn by Bears strong safety Doug Plank, comprises four defensive linemen, three linebackers, and four defensive backs, and is thus a variation on the 4-3 defense used primarily by most NFL teams. In the system, the two cornerbacks typically play bump-and-run pass coverage and often blitz an opposing quarterback, playing in a manner not unlike that of the linebackers.

The scheme relies on the ability of the defensive squad, for which two outside linebackers play on the same side of the defensive formation, quickly to reach the backfield, either to disrupt rushing plays or to pressure the quarterback. Though vulnerable to offenses such as the West Coast that feature short, timed passes to wide receivers and tight ends, who take advantage of openings in the defensive secondary, the defense works well against offenses that depend principally on the rushing of running backs, especially where such rushing is feigned in the execution of a play action pass.

The 1985 Bears team that claimed the Super Bowl XX championship led the league in several defensive team statistics, permitting opponents to score just 198 points and to rush for only 1319 yards; each, at the time, was a single-season record for any team since the merger of the NFL and American Football League. [edit] May 28 to June 18, 2006

The NFL Europe League is an American football league that operates in Europe. It was founded in 1991 as the World League of American Football (WLAF).

There are currently six teams in the league: five based in Germany and one in the Netherlands. Players in NFL Europe are predominantly assigned by National Football League teams who want these younger, "developmental" players to get additional game experience and coaching. The expenses of these players and their coaches while living in Europe are assumed by the league.

NFL Europe was originally founded in 1991 as the World League of American Football. The original WLAF was an American football league partially backed by the National Football League and, like the current NFL Europe, was a spring developmental league. Originally, the ancestor to NFL Europe had 10 teams playing a 10-game regular season: six teams from the United States, three European teams, and one Canadian team. The two teams emerging from the WLAF semi-final playoffs met at the end of the season in the World Bowl.

[edit] February 26 to May 28, 2006

San Francisco 49ers
The San Francisco 49ers (frequently referred to as the Niners) are a professional American football team. The team plays their home games in San Francisco, California, while the club's headquarters and practice facility are located in Santa Clara, California. They currently belong to the Western Division of the National Football Conference (NFC) in the National Football League (NFL).

The 49ers began play in 1946 as a charter member of the All-America Football Conference and joined the NFL in 1950 after the AAFC merged into the older league. The team currently shares the record for most Super Bowl victories (5) with the Dallas Cowboys and the Pittsburgh Steelers.

The San Francisco 49ers have the distinction of being one of the first major-league professional sports franchise on the West Coast, and the first one based in San Francisco -in fact, they are the only "home-grown" San Francisco sports franchise.
