Amanda (Mandy) Chessell is a computer scientist and a Senior Technical Staff Member at IBM. She has a record of prolific middleware inventiveness and has been awarded the title of IBM Master Inventor. She is also a Member of the IBM Academy of Technology.

She is one of the youngest ever appointed to the corporate position of Senior Technical Staff Member and likewise to have been elected to the IBM Academy of Technology, a self-electing peer group comprising 300 of the companyâ€™s most distinguished scientists and technologists, worldwide. Outside IBM, Mandy is the first woman to be awarded the Royal Academy of Engineering Silver Medal and has subsequently been elected as a Fellow of the Royal Academy, a very high honour in engineering.

Mandy is a visiting professor at the University of Sheffield[1].

[edit] Achievements

Mandy has had a high profile career. In 2001 Mandy won the Royal Academy of Engineering Silver Medal for the invention and engineering of Reusable Software Component Architecture[2].

In 2004 Mandy won the British Computer Society "Karen Burt" award[3].

In 2006 Mandy won a Female Inventor of the Year Award for building capacity for innovation[4].

Also in 2006 Mandy was awarded a prize for the Best Woman in the Corporate Sector at the Blackberry Women in Technology awards[5].

Mandy frequently lectures on topics related to Computer Science and, in particular, innovation. Such lectures take place at universities such as Queen Mary University of London[6].

[edit] Education

Mandy studied Computer Science from an early age and has an O-Level in the subject. Mandy studied at Plymouth Polytechnic up to 1987 and obtained a Bachelors Degree in Computing with Informatics[7].

Subsequently, Mandy joined IBM in 1987 at Hursley Park, Winchester where she studied for a Masters degree in software engineering at the University of Brighton. Her Studies at Brighton were sponsored by IBM[8].

[edit] Technical Fields of Interest

Despite the numerous notable awards, prizes, and public recognition, it is difficult to identify substantially what Mandy's contribution to technology actually is. Searching for technical articles authored by Mandy reveals the paper "Transactional Business Process Servers: Definition and Requirements" (2000) (Chessell, Vines & Griffin) which seeks to consider the implementation of transactional business processes using business object components. In particular, the paper explores how business processes can be reflected as embodied technical objects.

Mandy has 14 issued U.S. patents[9] albeit largely in pseudo-technical fields and she continues to invent in this domain. She provides mentoring and guidance to young innovators both at [IBM] and in her capacity as a visiting professor.
