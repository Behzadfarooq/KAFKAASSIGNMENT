The RuneScape economy is complex and demonstrates some interesting similarities to real world economics. Many players judge their success in the game by the accumulation of money and valuable items. As an artificial system, however, the game economy contains a number of quirks that operate to encourage skill development and promote community spirit in preference to pure economic growth. Holiday rares are often found to be some of the most expensive and rare items in the game, and can be worth a lot of money.

[edit] Commodities and Raw Materials

RuneScape includes a number of commodity items that form the core of the economy. At the lowest level are items used in skills of trade, raw materials, and items acquired from NPC combat, such as basic uncharged rune essence or logs. Almost all raw materials demand an exceptionally high skill level to harvest efficiently. The value of these commodities lies in the fact that they can be processed into other items. The secondary tier of commodities is made up of those items that are processed from harvested items and that can be manufactured into other items like metal bars or tanned cowhide. Most secondary commodities require the development of specific player skills, (like smithing or crafting) before they can be processed. The third tier of commodities is made up of those items that have been fully processed but are still player-made, some examples being a set of platelegs or a completed potion. These tend to be more expensive than the materials needed to make them because it requires a higher skill level, and are rarely found in large quantities. A fourth level is items that cannot be made by players alone, but must be obtained through other difficult means, such as a quest, a rare reward for killing many powerful monsters, or an unusual reward for completing a difficult minigame. An example is Barrows and Dragon armor. These can be very expensive but very powerful, and players will rarely have many of these. Finally, the highest level of items are discontinued items, which are described below.

[edit] Value of commodities

The value of RuneScape commodities is determined by a number of factors. The primary factor is scarcity -- items that are not available on demand are more valuable. Items that require higher levels of skill to produce are more scarce and are consequently more valuable. Another factor contributing to the value of commodities is the rate at which they can be accumulated. Players can only accumulate large quantities of a given item by placing them in the bank, requiring time to travel between the bank and the various harvesting or processing locations. Some items are harvested at random intervals, making the rate of accumulation unpredictable. The final factor in the value of commodities is tied to the potential monetary value from the processing of the commodity. Alongside the monetary value of commodities is the potential experience gain from the processing of commodities into finished goods. In cases where this experience gain is disproportionate to other factors, the value of commodities is higher than would be expected.

[edit] Currency

right|frame|Pickpocketing NPCs is one of many ways to bring more currency into the game. The primary form of RuneScape currency is coins, commonly called gold pieces or gp. One thousand gp is known as 1K, one million gp is known as 1mil (1M) and one billion gp is known as 1bil (1B). There are also additional forms of currency in the game, each specific to a certain area or feature, such as tribal Trading Sticks or Tokkul in the TzHaar caves.

[edit] Consumption

Throughout the game, commodities are spent in one method or another, being used through Combat, skills, or an alchemical spell. Money is expended through trade of commodities, practice of skills, or through trades of rare items. The construction skill was designed to slow inflation and consumes a huge amount of money; several billion gp is spent on construction every day.

[edit] Fixed Prices/Alchemy Spells

The buying and selling prices for items at general and specialty stores is fixed by the game. These prices are determined by a base item value and the quantity of that item in stock, with increased supplies leading to lower prices both for buying and selling.

While general stores will trade in any tradable commodity, specialty stores will only trade in a specific type of item. Specialty stores will buy items for a higher price than the general store.

Players can use an alchemy spell to turn whatever item they may own into instant profit. The value is fixed, allowing bulk quantities of items to be liquidated faster than selling them to a store. As a result of the alchemy spells, a number of valuable commodities are collected by players simply for their alchemical value. This motivates players to develop a certain commodity producing skills, and acts to drain commodities out of the system while providing a consistent source of money. While alchemy provides an instant source of revenue, the demand for prices outpaces the supply, and inflation occurs.

There are two alchemy spells: Low Alchemy and High Alchemy. Low Alchemy gives the maximum general store price, whereas High Alchemy gives the maximum specialty store price. High Alchemy adds millions to the economy each day, due to the fact that it is an extremely rapid method to leveling Magic, rivaling that of the mini-game Pest Control.

[edit] Discontinued Items

In the past in RuneScape, there were holiday drops every Easter, Halloween, and Christmas, where special items were randomly dropped on the ground. After these events, you could only get those special holiday items by buying them from other players. As a result, they became extremely rare and expensive, and the price goes up at the incredible rate of about 500% a year for the earliest rares. The rarest wearable item, the Blue Party Hat, for example, costs over 15 times as much as any non-holiday item; almost all players want one, but only a few hundred exist. The rarest item in RuneScape, which is also the most expensive, is the Christmas cracker, which was the second holiday item in RuneScape. Often times these can cost over 900 million coins.

Many players want Jagex to destroy all Holiday Items, or make it so that players cannot trade them; however, this could crash the economy, so it is unlikely to happen. Recent Holiday Events are more complicated, and the items received cannot be traded or sold.

There are also 2 other discontinued items, the Half Jug of Wine and the Disc of Returning, that are not holiday items but are discontinued. They are discontinued as a result of changing game content and can no longer be made. A jug of wine used to be drunk in 2 servings, so after drinking one, you had a Half Jug of Wine. Now, a jug of wine is drunk all at once, so the Half Jugs of Wine became impossible to make. The remaining ones were discontinued. Likewise, a Disc of Returning came from a shop that no longer exists. The purpose of the disc was to allow players to visit rule breakers who were sent to a black hole near the shop several years ago during Runescape Classic. When a player was done visiting the rule breaker, they would "spin" the disc and leave the black hole.

[edit] Money making

One major factor in the game to most players is making money, in which the economy has a huge impact in. There are hundreds of ways to make money, both risky and safe. The economy can thrive, or suffer as a result of a small group of players. That makes the economy fragile, and in some cases risky. This is why Jagex stresses scamming prevention.
