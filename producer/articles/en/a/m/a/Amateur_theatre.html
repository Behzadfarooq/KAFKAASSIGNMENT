Amateur theatre

Amateur Theatre is theatre which is not staged chiefly for financial benefit. Normally, previously published plays are staged, rather than new, unpublished work.

Because the actors (and frequently the stage technicians) are unpaid and sometimes untrained, there is debate over the quality of amateur performance. Lower budgets for amateur productions also sometimes lead to lower production values and a lesser amount of spectacle than professional productions enjoy. While generally it lacks the prestige of professional theatre, tickets are commensurately less expensive, and thereby accessible to a larger and more economically diverse audience.

Amateur Theatre is common in most urban centres; notably, it is staged in summer schools (usually organised by a professional practitioner, such as a director) and in formal amateur companies. Amateur theatre is a convenient way for its practicioners to gain acting and stage experience. Other examples of amateur theatre are school Christmas plays, low-budget plays, and musicals staged in local venues in much of the Western World.

[edit] Examples of Amateur Theatre in the United Kingdom

Millions of people throughout Great Britain support the arts by participating in Amateur Theatre either as personnel or as audience members.

Many children first experience live theatre during local amateur performances of the annual Christmas pantomime. Many adults who lack access to professional theatre are easily able to obtain cultural entertainment by attending local amateur productions.

Most amateur theatre companies take names related to the locality or building in which they present their productions. Examples include Beeston Musical Theatre Group, Kingswood Players, Bristol Players, Romsdal Players, Downend Dramatic Society, Tideswell Community Players and The Actonians Drama Club, among others.

Many actors who have gone on to pursue professional acting careers had their first stage experiences in amateur theatre.

Many Bristol groups are now part of The Avon Association of Drama.

The National Operatic and Dramatic Association (NODA) is the major representative body for amateur theatre in the UK.

The Little Theatre Guild (LTG) is an umbrella organisation promoting and supporting independent amateur theatre companies which have control over their own premises and produce drama of a high quality for the benefit of their communities.
