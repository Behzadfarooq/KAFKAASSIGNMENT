From article:

Composed probably between 1760 and 1770 in Olney, Buckinghamshire, England, "Amazing Grace" was possibly one of the hymns written for a weekly service. Through the years other writers have composed additional verses to the hymn which came to be known as "Amazing Grace" (it was not thus entitled in Olney Hymns), and possibly verses from other Newton hymns have been added. However, these are the six stanzas that appeared, with minor spelling variations, in both the first edition in 1779 and the 1808 edition, the one nearest the date of Newton's death. It appeared under the heading Faith's Review and Expectation, along with a reference to First Chronicles, chapter 17, verses 16 and 17.

[edit] John Newton and slave trading

According to this article,

John Newton (1725–1807) was the captain of a slave ship. On 10 May 1748 returning home during a storm he experienced a "great deliverance". In his journal he wrote that the ship was in grave danger of sinking. He exclaimed "Lord have mercy upon us". He continued to traffic in slaves thereafter, nonetheless.

This seems on the face of it to clash with the assertion at John Newton that he did give up slave trading:

John Newton (24 July 1725–21 December 1807) was an English clergyman and writer who wrote the hymn "Amazing Grace" after converting to Christianity and abandoning his participation in the slave trade.

Can anyone clarify?

According to "Bury the chains: The Brittish struggle to abolish slavery" by Adam Hochschild Newton did not give up slave trading after his conversion but continued with it for many years. When he finally did give it up he did not do so out of any moral conviction that slavery was wrong. (Basically he had had a minor illness which he interpreted as a sign from God that he should not go back to sea.) He still did not have any moral problems with the slave trade when he wrote the hymn.

Flapdragon 18:04, 14 August 2005 (UTC)

[edit] Cleanup

The lyrics don't belong on this page, if someone transwiki's them to wikisource (An example: [1]), we can link to there, but for now we'll remove it. The list of artists recording the song is silly. We'll mention that its commonly recorded even today, and leave some note about how the melody lends itself to this and that. Allmusic.com kinda has a list of artists recording the song, so if someone wants to link to that, that is fine, too. I also removed the external links to the movie site, as they already exist from the movie's page, which is linked at the top of the article. Smmurphy(Talk) 00:05, 26 March 2007 (UTC)

I guess I didn't fully address what to do with mentions of popular recorded versions of the song. Many articles about popular songs exist, and popular versions of this song could have mention in the article, but probably only if the version is for some reason notable (released as a major single, or something like that). The mention of any particular version, then, must include why that version is notable. Smmurphy(Talk) 00:19, 26 March 2007 (UTC)
It is probably best to wait until the editors have a chance to discuss this before you make such major changes to an article. I'm reverting the changes until we can talk about this some more.
I have mixed feelings about the lyrics. For the most part, Wikipedia doesn't include song lyrics in the articles about those songs, but that's usually due to copyright restrictions, and there are no such limitations with a song written in the 18th century. Also, including them seems in-line with at least some other hymn pages (examples: Lift Up Your Hearts!, Corpus Christi Carol, O Sacred Head, Now Wounded) Also, Wikipedia:Lyrics_and_poetry states "You should include lyrics and poetry whose copyright has expired." And Wikipedia:Don't include copies of primary sources uses Ozymandias as an example of acceptably including lyrics or poems from shorter, non-copyrighted works. So I vote to keep the lyrics, although they require a citation of some sort.
As far as the list of artists who have recorded the song, I disagree that it's silly. It's actually kind of useful. Wikipedia has plenty of articles of the discographies [2] of various recording artists. This is entirely analagous. So it doesn't qualify as "silly". But it's almost completely uncited, and it will get very long very fast, quickly dwarfing the size of the main article. I propose breaking it off into a separate article if someone feels upto the task (maybe I'll even get around to it myself), but it requires a citation for every recording artist on the list. I agree that a link to the allmusic page might be a fair compromise in the meantime. It would also be appropriate to mention highly significant recordings of the song. For example, "It was first recorded by blah blah blah." Or, "the song faded from popularity until the 1980s, when it was brought back into the spotlight when it was recorded by blah blah blah".
The popular culture section has to go. As much as I enjoy them, they seldom work well in Wikipedia articles. See WP:TRIV for more info.
The movie should be mentioned in the main article. I mean, it's pretty much a movie ABOUT the song, right?
Lastly, the article needs LOTS more citations. As it stands, it has only one.
What are others' thoughts? -Bindingtheory 13:48, 27 March 2007 (UTC)
I like the lyrics in a box a lot more. In this form, it does feel like it belongs. The two lists (trivia and recordings) are still sore thumbs in the article. A separate article List of artists who recorded Amazing Grace might be difficult to get through afd these days, but creating such an article would be no problem if you are interested. I removed them both, the first because the trivia tag has been there for three months [3] and nothing has been integrated into the article from it. The popular recordings I removed, mostly thinking of WP:NOT, WP:CITE/WP:SNOW (the list seems over directory-ish, and if we tagged the entries with a cite tag, most would not get a citation within a "reasonable" amount of time, and thus their removal under snow is appropriate). I figured that doing so would prompt the interested to add back their favorite version with a citation. I would agree that versions which include the name of the album or the concert are in a way cited, however. I have less a problem with keeping those. Mention of the movie is fine, linking to the movies site seems like spam. Looking at those sites, there is content on them. Most of it is related to Amazing Grace Sunday [4], but even that is a bit flimsy for an article on the song itself.
Anyway, I'm sorry if I overstepped. With the WP citation revolution seemingly in full swing, and with the movie gaining in popularity, I felt that by starting a cleanup of this page, we could focus future editors on adding content. Hopefully we can agree to cut some of the WP:Listcruft and move this article along from there. Best, Smmurphy(Talk) 01:42, 29 March 2007 (UTC)
