Amadeus is a 1984 film directed by Miloš Forman and based on the stage play Amadeus. It won eight Oscars in 1984.

The stage play was written in 1979 by Peter Shaffer, and was inspired by "Mozart and Salieri", a short play by Alexander Pushkin (later adapted into an opera of the same name by Nikolai Rimsky-Korsakov), which was in turn based loosely on the lives of the composers Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart and Antonio Salieri.

[edit] Plot

Spoiler warning: Plot and/or ending details follow.

Although many think the film is a biography of Mozart, Amadeus is, in fact, the tragic story of Antonio Salieri, a competent but not brilliant composer who begins as a gentle, religious man, but ends in a furious battle with God. The story raises many questions about genius and jealousy, but the key theme is a vast question; God claims to be good, fair and loving, so why are our lives governed by fickle inequality and uncaring cruelty?

The film starts with Salieri in a lunatic asylum where he's been sent for attempting suicide. A young priest visits to take his confession and Salieri tells him his life story...

At the opening of his tale, Salieri is the court composer in Vienna at the court of the Holy Roman Emperor Joseph II. Life is good. He is successful and popular, satisfied with his position and with his music. He is grateful to God, whom he believes is honoring an agreement: that God will grant him success as a composer should he, Salieri, dedicate his whole life to God. He is a man at peace with himself and his creator.

Then he meets Mozart.

F. Murray Abraham as Antonio Salieri

An admirer of Mozart's work, Salieri is excited at the chance to meet him at a salon. But his first sight of Mozart, he finds him crawling on the floor, engaged in vulgar sex-games. Salieri is perplexed, how can such heavenly music be written by this shallow fool? Gradually, through a series of painful humiliations, Salieri realizes his abilities are limited and his work is uninspired, especially compared to Mozart's. He can't understand why God has betrayed him. Why would God give the sacrilegious Mozart such incredible talent but not Salieri? Salieri, who has been a devout Catholic all his life, cannot believe that God would choose Mozart over him for such a gift. More than anything, Salieri cannot understand why God encouraged him to be a composer, then crushed him. At one point he exclaims, "All I ever wanted was to sing to God. He gave me that desire, like a lust in my body, but then denied me the talent. WHY?"

He starts to believe that God is writing beautiful music through Mozart just to show Salieri how average he is. Salieri is broken-hearted. He gave God all his love and music, even remaining celibate for him. But now he feels God is laughing at his mediocrity. Furious and lost, Salieri rejects God. In a powerful scene, he takes a crucifix off the wall and burns it on the fire. He swears he is God's enemy and promises revenge against God by destroying Mozart.

Throughout the rest of the film, Salieri masquerades as Mozart's friend, while at the same time doing his utmost to destroy his career. Mozart is reckless, both socially and financially, and his initial court success soon fades. His drinking and partying threaten to bankrupt him. His health, marriage and reputation suffer, but his work is flawless. Too proud to teach, he writes, but does not make enough money to pay for his extravagances.

Salieri realises Mozart's money troubles and emotional frailty are the key to his revenge. As a child, Mozart was emotionally crippled by his father's strict unloving ambition, so in a clever trick, Salieri convinces Mozart that his dead father has returned from the grave to demand a requiem. The belief his father's ghost is haunting him pushes Mozart towards a nervous breakdown and his drinking becomes constant. Salieri's brilliant but insane plan is to secretly collect the commissioned requiem, kill Mozart and then play it at Mozart's funeral, claiming that he, Salieri, wrote it. By stealing Mozart's perfect requiem, he believes he will be stealing from God's mouthpiece. If history were to remember Salieri as a genius composer because of music he stole from God, he will have his revenge.

Tom Hulce as Mozart

Meanwhile, Mozart's friend Emanuel Schikaneder convinces Mozart to write an opera "for the people". Mozart agrees, and composes Die Zauberflöte. Zauberflöte is a big success, but during the initial performance, Mozart (who has been working much too hard to complete both the requiem and the opera) collapses from exhaustion and is taken home by Salieri.

Once home, Salieri pushes the dangerously-ill Mozart to continue working. Mozart says he's too sick to write. In the most moving scene, Salieri offers to take musical dictation for the bed-ridden Mozart. Now perhaps, Salieri hopes, he may discover the secret of musical genius. Mozart begins to describe the piece and Salieri transcribes it, getting carried away by the music, humming and singing and gradually building the great work's harmonies, Mozart puts layer upon layer of themes into the music. His musical vision stretches Salieri until, eventually, Salieri is simply baffled, unsure of what the music is supposed to be. The genius Mozart has to stop and spoon-feed the mediocre Salieri so that he understands the structure. Salieri will never achieve greatness. After some time, Mozart, getting weaker and weaker, pauses to thank Salieri for being such a good friend. Touched in spite of himself, Salieri candidly replies: "I tell you, you are the greatest composer known to me." Mozart's wife returns home to find Salieri there with Mozart. Salieri desparately tries to take the requiem but she locks the score away and demands he leave. The Requiem is unfinished, Salieri leaves empty-handed and Mozart dies soon after. Salieri's plan has failed.

Mozart has a small funeral and is buried in an unmarked mass grave.

The story jumps to aged Salieri in the asylum. The story we have just seen has been a confession to the priest. Salieri insists to the priest that God killed Mozart, not Salieri. He says God preferred to kill Mozart rather than let Salieri steal his music.

Then Salieri, joking bitterly, claims he is the patron saint of mediocrities, and will pray for the all the mediocrities in the world, including the priest. An attendant enters Salieri's room and wheels him to the latrines. As he's wheeled through the asylum, Salieri passes through masses of demented lunatics, some caged or restrained, others foaming or ranting. They represent the torment of being human. In the closing scene, Salieri greets and blesses them as a Pope being brought on his Palanquin, saying to them: "Mediocrities everywhere, I absolve you, I absolve you, I absolve you, I absolve you all." But the film ends with a close-up of Salieri painfully closing his eyes as the haunting memory of Mozart's annoying childish laugh fills the final seconds, signifying that God is omnipotent and unbiddable and there is no absolution.

(Amadeus, which was Mozart's middle name, means 'loved by God'. In the context of this film, the title could easily be refering ironically to Salieri rather than Mozart. The good, mediocre Salieri, who loved God dearly and was rewarded with crushing unsympathetic destruction.)

Spoilers end here.

[edit] Production

A young Kenneth Branagh was originally considered to play Mozart in the film, but was bypassed in favor of Tom Hulce when director Miloš Forman decided to make the film with an American cast so that audiences would not be distracted by the British accents. Meg Tilly was cast as Mozart's wife Constanze, but after tearing a ligament in her leg the day before shooting started, she was replaced by Elizabeth Berridge.

The film was shot on location in Prague and Vienna. Notably, John Forman was able to shoot scenes in the Tyl Theatre, where Don Giovanni had debuted two centuries before. Several other scenes were shot at the Barrandov Studios.

[edit] Reception

The film featured F. Murray Abraham as Salieri and Tom Hulce as Mozart, and in an Oscar rarity both would compete for the annual award for Best Actor.

The film won eight Academy Awards, including Best Picture and Abraham for Best Actor, as well as Director (Miloš Forman), Art Direction (Patrizia von Brandenstein and Karel Cerny), Costume Design, Best Makeup, Best Sound and Adapted Screenplay (Shaffer).

Isaac Asimov praised Abraham's depiction of Salieri in his essay collection "The Relativity of Wrong", noting that he deserved to win the Academy Award for Best Actor.

There was an awkward moment on Oscar Night, as aged icon Laurence Olivier presented the evening's final Oscar for Best Picture at a ceremony held March 25, 1985, in Los Angeles. As he thanked the Academy for inviting him he was already opening the envelope, and instead of announcing the nominees, he simply read "The winner is Amadeus."

An Academy official quickly went onstage to confirm Olivier had correctly announced the winner, signaled that all was well and producer Saul Zaentz, in his acceptance speech, mentioned the other nominees: The Killing Fields, A Passage to India, Places in the Heart and A Soldier's Story.

The film is generally regarded as one of the finest period-pieces in recent years, and was ranked the 53rd best American movie ever by the American Film Institute in 1998 during their special AFI's 100 Years... 100 Movies list.

[edit] Film soundtrack

The original soundtrack to Amadeus reached #56 on Billboard's album charts, making it one of the most popular recordings of classical music ever. All of the tracks were composed by Mozart, save an early Hungarian folk tune and the final movement "Quando Corpus Morietur et Amen" by Giovanni Battista Pergolesi, from his famous Stabat Mater. It should be noted, however, that the film features some music that is not included on the original soundtrack album release. As stated above, except where specified, all tracks were performed by the Academy of St. Martin in the Fields, conducted by Sir Neville Marriner, and all were performed specifically for use in the film.

[edit] Trivia

This article contains a trivia section.
Content in this section should be integrated into other appropriate areas of the article or removed, and the trivia section removed.
  • The movie was the inspiration for Falco's song "Rock Me Amadeus".
  • When Mozart meets the Emperor for the first time, Salieri presents Mozart with a little "March of Welcome". Mozart "improves" this piece later in the scene, transforming Salieri's "trifle" into the "Non più andrai" march from his opera, The Marriage of Figaro. [1]
  • Finnish metal band Children of Bodom uses F. Murray Abraham's quote "From now on we are enemies... you and I..." as the introduction to their song "Warheart".
  • Both F. Murray Abraham (Salieri) and Thomas Hulce (Mozart) were nominated for the Academy Award for Lead Actor for their performances in the film; Abraham won. Likewise, Ian McKellen (Salieri) and Tim Curry (Mozart) were nominated for a Tony Award for Lead Actor for their performances in the Broadway production and, once again, the actor portraying Salieri won.
