Amanda Jane "Milly" Dowler (June 25, 1988 – c.March 21, 2002) was a British schoolgirl who was murdered on the way home from school in Walton-on-Thames. Her disappearance in March 2002, followed by the discovery of her remains six months later, resulting in nationwide media attention, a police investigation (Operation Ruby) involving over 100 officers at its peak and numerous tributes. The police received over 6,000 telephone calls, questioned over 2,800 people and made 120 searches. Despite the vast attention to the case, as of 2007.[1], no one has been charged with her murder.

[edit] Disappearance

At 3 p.m. on March 21, 2002, Dowler left Heathside School for home on the train. Dowler got off at Walton-on-Thames railway station to visit a café with friends, one stop early from her usual stop of Hersham. After phoning her father at 3.47 p.m. to tell him she would be home in half an hour, Dowler set off for home on foot, and was last seen a quarter of an hour later walking along Station Approach.[2]It is believed Dowler was killed and buried shortly afterwards.[3] A nationwide search followed the disappearance, including 100 officers and helicopters searching the fields, streets and rivers around Hersham. Detectives who had worked on the abduction of Sarah Payne were called in to help.[4] Police and Dowler's family made multiple appeals for information, including a reconstruction on Crimewatch,[2] and a plea by pop star Will Young on the ITN news programme, as Dowler was a fan and had attended a concert of his the day before her disappearance.[5] The Crimewatch appeal included a direct appeal to Dowler in the hope that she had run away from home of her own accord, though the day before her father had already expressed fears that his daughter had been abducted. Dowler's mother expressed hope that her daughter had indeed run away, but admitted that she could think of no reason why her daughter would want to do so.[6] A week after Dowler's disappearance, the police stated that she was probably not taken by force, reasoning that, while Dowler was unlikely to have gone off with someone she did not know of her own free will, no-one had come forward who had witnessed a struggle, despite a number of apparent sightings of Dowler prior to her disappearance.[7]

On April 23, the discovery of a body in the Thames prompted media speculation that the body was that of Dowler, however the body was identified the following day as 73-year-old Maisie Thomas, who had gone missing a year earlier. Thomas' death was not believed to be suspicious.[8] In June of that year, despite further searches, the offer of a £100,000 reward by national tabloid The Sun[9] and Dowler's parents continued to text her mobile in the hope of a reply,[10] Dowler remained missing and police told Dowler's parents that she was probably dead.[11]

On September 18, Dowler's skeletal remains were discovered in Yateley Heath Forest near Fleet, and confirmed as Dowler's two days later. The discovery of the body led the police to reclassify the case from a missing person investigation to a murder investigation. None of the clothes Dowler was wearing, nor the purse, rucksack or mobile phone Dowler had with her at the time of her disappearance were found, and as of 2007 they are still missing.[12][13]

As of 2007, despite several arrests no-one has been charged with Dowler's murder. Only two people have appeared in the dock in relation to the case. 21-year-old Lianne Newman from Tewkesbury, Gloucestershire, who repeatedly phoned Dowler's parents, school and the police pretending to be the missing schoolgirl. Newman was jailed in April 2003 for five months after pleading guilty to five counts of making phone calls to cause annoyance, inconvenience or needless anxiety.[14] 49-year-old Gary Farr from Retford, Nottinghamshire repeatedly E-mailed Dowler's parents, school friends and police officers working on the case, claiming Dowler had been smuggled out of the country to work as a prostitute and stripper in nightclubs in Poland and that Dowler's death had been a cover up. Farr was sectioned indefinitely under the Mental Health Act on October 19, 2006, for being a serious psychological danger to the public, due to his history of paranoid schizophrenia after admitting a charge of harassment.[15]

[edit] Legacy

Dowler's parents, Sally and Bob Dowler, launched a charity called Milly's Fund on the day of Dowler's memorial service in October 2002.[16] The charity provides risk assessment advice to teenagers, youth workers and educators. Its work includes the "Teach UR Mum 2 TXT" campaign, which encourages children and parents to stay in contact via text messaging, including a glossary for mothers of commonly-used SMS abbreviations. The campaign was awarded "Best Use of Mobile for Accessibility" at the 2004 Groupe Speciale Mobile Association Awards.[17]

In October 2003, the running of the charity was handed over to the Suzy Lamplugh Trust.[18]

At the 2005 Hampton Court Palace Flower Show, a garden designed in Dowler's memory by Penny Smith won the Tudor Rose award, the show's highest honour.[19] A magenta sweetpea was named after her by Matthewman's Sweetpeas and is still for sale as of 2007.[20]
