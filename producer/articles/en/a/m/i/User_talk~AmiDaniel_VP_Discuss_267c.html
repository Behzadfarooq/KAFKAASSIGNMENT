[edit] Problem with automatic warning generation

First off: I am not a VandalProof user, and I'm not especially familiar with it. I've seen VP users leaving inappropriate warnings on user pages along these lines: 1) User adds an insult to an article. I revert, and leave a warning that doesn't use an official test template. For instance, "If you post any more insults I'm going to block you." 2) User puts the insult back in the same article, and a VP user comes around and leaves a friendly, welcoming Test1 message. This seems kind of silly to me. Maybe it's only a small percentage of cases, but VP users and designers need to be aware that the test templates are not the only way of warning users. Perhaps the software could be modified so that if there are messages on the offending user's talk page that are not recognized as test templates, it shows the user talk page to the VP user and gives them a chance to use their discretion about what message to leave. The idea that VP is enforcing the use of the test templates is worrying to me. FreplySpang 13:46, 19 October 2006 (UTC)

Vandal Proof has other user warnings that the user can issue. However, my concern with your example is that you warn a user first time with "If you post any more insults I'm going to block you." if it were a mistake or a new user that is not a very friendly welcome, I feel it is very important to assume good faith in the first warning. Also for someone to actually be block it calls for 3 warnings and vandalism after the last warning. Vandal Proof also allows a user to leave a custom warning that they write as well. Just my thoughts on the issue, hope it gives you a better idea of how things work from a Vandal Proof user stand point. In any case thanks for your help in the fight to keep wikipedia vandalism free and the best it can be!! TheRanger 14:20, 19 October 2006 (UTC)
I wanted to keep the posted example brief and clear. For the most part I actually do post friendlier warnings before bringing out the big guns. I even use the official test templates when they are relevant. But the same issue applies there too. Note, though, that it is not actually required to leave exactly three warnings before blocking. If a user has posted "Joe Smith is a FAG, his phone number is XXX-YYY-ZZZZ, call him and tell him he's a COCKSUCKER" to ten articles, I don't feel too much pressure to be friendly! FreplySpang 14:27, 19 October 2006 (UTC)
Oh ok I see what your saying, in those case I have issued a final warning or a custom warning. However, as you say it would be a good option to see other comments and respond based on that. The speed at which pages can be check with a tool like Vandal Proof helps greatly in the fight of the vandal. Thanks for your points and help in the battle. TheRanger 16:05, 19 October 2006 (UTC)

If the article has already been subsequently edited since the time of the RSS feed you clicked on, then the button does not become active. So just to make sure you're doing all the steps, here is what you do:

  • double-click on the program icon
  • login to Vp by entering your username and password, or wait for it to find your IE cookie, then press login.
  • click on the recent changes tab in the left pane header.
  • click <update>
  • scroll through the list and find a likely candidate for reversion
  • click on the title of the article in question in the RSS list.
  • on the right pane, your browser window should show a summary of the diff; if after careful review you determine the edit should be reverted, press the appropriate rollback button.
  • note that if the buttons are grayed-out, there is probably a phrase below the edit summary that says something to the effect of:
Since user badboy edited the page, it has been edited or reverted by goodguy. Rollback has been disabled as a protective measure.

If after seeing the diff, you open the article or the user talk or contribs, the rollback buttons will not be active. Just go back to the left pane, and click on the RSS entry again.

[edit] Removing security measures...

I want to throw out an idea and see if it gets the same resistance now that it did a year ago. Aside from the runtime 91, the warning retrieval error (both fixed in the next release, thanks to Alex), and the script errors (for which there may likely never be a fix unless Microsoft gets their shit together), the only real problem still present are the difficulties users are having "logging in". With VP's current security measures, our quite understaffed moderator body has had to take on a tremendous workload, leaving many users out to dry for weeks while their requests to use the tool are processed. Even then, once approved, users quite frequently have difficulties getting into the program due to bugs in the verification system; as such, many users are rendered completely unable to use the tool. With m:WikiMonitor, which I'm planning to put into beta-circulation in the next month, I plan on having absolutely no security mechanisms although the tool is debatably far more powerful and capable of damage than VP. My reasoning behind this is that I want WikiMonitor to be a fully open-source app (although likely not GPLed due to the MS EULA on some of its components), and that, as such, the security measures will be quite easily bypassed by any one with half a brain cell--putting them in serves only to encourage vandals and the like to bypass the measures, although they may not have had any interest in using the tool to these ends were the measures not in place. I also feel that this limited access seems to be in conflict with the free and open spirit of Wikimedia, and, as it will be an entirely interwiki-project, attempting to keep up with the approval process will be next to impossible. I expect to receive resistance to not securing WikiMonitor; however, this is what I currently intend to do. What I would like to propose presently is either removing or minimizing the security measures in place in VP. My preference would be to remove them altogether; however, I'm also open to implementing a system similar to that used by WP:WDM, which, at startup, checks the user's edit count and blocklog to determine whether s/he can use it, as opposed to checking and having to maintain an "approved user list". I will not, however, take either of these steps unless the users of VP and the Wikipedia community at large are in support of it. I used to constantly get the script error until just recently, when I installed a quick fix to my monobook.js. Seeing as you have a large amount of JavaScript, I'm pretty sure what the problem is. You have a large number of scripts that work in Firefox, but are incompatible with IE. To fix this, place the following in your monobook.js:
var detect = navigator.userAgent.toLowerCase();
var IE;
var place = detect.indexOf('msie') + 1;
if (place) IE=true;
if (!IE) {
   /* Place your Firefox scripts here */
}
Then, move in all scripts incompatible with Internet Explorer into this area in place of the comment. Any scripts you put in there will be disabled when you run IE. It works; see my monobook.js, which uses this. Pyrospirit Flames Fire 04:54, 10 March 2007 (UTC)
