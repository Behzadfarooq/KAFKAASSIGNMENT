Summary of prior controversy

Since the time of this article's creation, the card game Ambition has seen considerable success in achieving recognition. While its entry into the gaming mainstream has been slowed by its creator's lack of corporate backing and the financial means that would allow the heavy promotion afforded other games, Ambition is (as of April 27, 2004):

  • Published in a respected Japanese puzzle magazine.
  • Listed on Aaron D. Fuegi's ranked list of games, and within the top 500. Admittedly, I was a contributor, and cast one of its votes.
  • A game which has accomplished worldwide respect, if not fame.

However, there has been previous and present debate over whether or not Ambition deserves any presence at all within Wikipedia. I still maintain that it does.

In the light of Ambition's accomplishments-- which I dare call impressive given its lack of corporate backing, in either a financial or promotional sense-- some have ventured to call this innovative new game "unimportant". An anonymous troll post even referred to me as an "infantile game inventor". I do not take these sorts of extraneous personal attacks lightly.

I confess, however, that in some cases I have transgressed, by crossing the line from appropriate use of Wikipedia into outright self-promotion. A recent example of this was my inclusion of a link here on Assassin (game) justified with the tenuous connection of both games being popular at Carleton. In that case, I had gotten way ahead of myself, and the link had no right to be there. I apologize for this episode of wrongdoing, as well as other similar episodes. Please accept that I did so out of good intentions, and had merely "gone too far", so to speak. Offending people is not the reason I wrote the card game Ambition, nor my reason for exposing it to public view. I expose my game to public view because, having developed a game of high quality, I feel an obligation to share it with others. I have no commercial or political motivations. Mike Church 06:36, 27 Apr 2004 (UTC)
