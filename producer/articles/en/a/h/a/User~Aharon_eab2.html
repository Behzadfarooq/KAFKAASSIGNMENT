Aharon N. Varady was born in Cincinnati, Ohio in 1974. Since then, he has mainly spent time in school, most recently at the School of Planning at the University of Cincinnati (2002-2004) where he earned a graduate dergee in Planning. He's also spent time in Binghamton, New York as an undergraduate, and in Philadelphia, Pennsylvania, as a dilletante. Thanks to some good friends he learned some basic programming and worked for a time as an open source programmer, and before that as a computer dude at an IT department at the University of Pennsylvania.

Long a lover of space music, Aharon began actively engaging the electronic music scene in 1995 when he joined the Space department of WHRW Binghamton, 90.5 FM, SUNY Binghamton's student run radio station. For the following six years, on his show "Amplified Harmonic Resonance" he continued to dj ambient, space, and classical minimalist music on WHRW and (beginning in 1997) at WKDU Philadelphia 91.7 FM, Drexel University's station. In Philadelphia, Aharon found he could make a contribution to the diverse community of avant-garde music listeners and artists by forming The Philadelphia Ambient Consortium (Music and Noise). The philly_ambient listserve and the Gate to Moonbase Alpha performance series provided a virtual and real forum for Greater Philadelphia artists and listeners of ambient, space, avante-electronic rockers, and other new music styles.

Bicycling along the Schukyll River park in Philadelphia while eating mushrooms and listening to ambient music helped to inspire him to devote his life creating greenways and recreational bike paths. Returning to Cincinnati in 2002, Aharon enrolled at the College of Design, Architecture, Art and Planning (DAAP) to study Planning. Besides greenways and bicycle/pedestrian trails, Aharon is interested in environmental and historic preservation, the influence of Nostalgia and Romanticism on urban (and rural) design, and making new green technologies affordable for urban revitalization. In 2004, he wrote his master's thesis, Bond Hill: Critical Events in the History of a 19th Century Cincinnati Railroad Suburb.
