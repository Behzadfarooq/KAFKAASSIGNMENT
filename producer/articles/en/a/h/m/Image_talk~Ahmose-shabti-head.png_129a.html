Given that there are two shabti's in existance, "one in one of which resides in the Brooklyn Museum of Art, the other in the Khartoum Museum", I content that this is replacable and so fails to meet standard wikipedia criteria for fair use images Nil Einne 15:43, 6 January 2007 (UTC)

No offense, but I strongly object to that contention. After reading the fair use requirements, I find the following.
* The image stands on strong legal footing as fair use. There is nothing illegal about use of the image.
* The image and all similar ones which are widely dispersed so as to get one's hands on them are copyrighted by the British Museum, therefore it is impossible to replace it with another preexisting image.
* While the regulations for fair use on wikipedia say that in the case that the image can be rephotographed, that should be done, however I quote two of the following clauses on that same rule page,"but is also, in essence, not reasonably repeatable" and "If you see an image tagged as fair use that would appear to be quite easy to replace with a free alternative, add {{fairusereplace}} to the image description page."
It is not quite easy or reasonable to just waltz into the british museum and take such a picture. The waltzing part on land would take about five months for me, followed by one heck of a swim. I think it perfectly reasonable to prefer a free image. I think it absurd to demand one given the amount of work and personal cost it would entail. If and only if wikipedia had a page dedicated to listing requested fair use -> free image replacements for obscure images in all odd corners of the world, and therein locals could go get the requested images, I would agree with listing this image there and only there. However, the demand that one of us fly to britain by the 14th of january or this image will be deleted is absurd. Thanatosimii 20:35, 7 January 2007 (UTC)

I've had the chance to look over the legal guidelines page. They are not in and of themselves onerous, but nevertheless this is a case of having to jump over a number of special flaming hoops in order to replace an image which would have certinly fallen under the old fair-use requirement. While this image certainly is replaceable -- just about any image of an extant artifact is -- it is hard to underscore how hard it is in this case. There are only two known likenesses (apart from the mummy head, which is also in dispute as to whether it is really him) of Ahmose I, and they are located geographically in places that are far from where Thanatosimii and I reside. The image in question is a scan from an exhibition at which I was not allowed to take pictures of the exhibits, and I also do not know whether this object is necessarily on regular display at the Brooklyn Museum (it is a rare likeness of the man, but shabtis in general are not, as common as can be really). When the image was scanned the fair-use criterion at the time appeared to be valid, and the image quality deliberately downgraded from the original. Am more concerned that as some museums are now limiting the use of even non-flash photography (most notably the Cario Museum) getting access to replacement for fair use images will become increasingly limited. I would ask for some form of flexibility in this particular case (though I hope Marhk can come up with a legitimate free use version, assuming the version the British Museum has is also on display -- when I was there last I don't remember seeing it). Cheers! Captmondo 14:06, 8 January 2007 (UTC)

[edit] May not be reasonably replaceable

Did someone remember to check the British Museum's site for information on whether a photograph taken within it may be reused on a commercial site?

Photography with flash and video recording is permitted in most galleries for private purposes only, using hand-held equipment. The diary for commercial photography is closed until further notice.

For the time being, IMO, this image cannot be considered reasonably replaceable. This image should stay in the article. Daniel Case 01:59, 9 January 2007 (UTC)

[edit] My handling of the matter

I think I've made four mistakes in the way I handled this situation. Firstly, I should have checked out the Brooklyn Museum page better. I should point out here that although I didn't, I was the first one to point the BM policy, and upon noticing it, I immediately changed my comment. (It occured to me that many museums had photographic restrictions and I had even downloaded a PDF that I thought was helpful but unfortunetely as I didn't have a reader I couldn't read the PDF so I have up temporarily. I decided to look into it further when I was asking for advise and realised the PDF was sueless but the FAQ was helpful).

Secondly, I should not have added {{Replacethisimage}}. This template was superflurous and unnecessary. You may notice that only about 6 images are currently tagged with it.

Thirdly, I shouldn't have mentioned the part about encouraging editors to replace it. My intention here was to explain why I felt the policy was good. I want to make it clear at no time did I want to threaten anyone or whatever. My only intention was to enforce policy as I currently see it and still see it (which IMHO is a good idea for the reasons I've expressed). The behaviour in this case that I noticed continues to support my theory that the policy is a good idea but I repeat, it was only my intention enforce policy. I would not regret my actions even if the circumstances had been different, the museum policy had not existed and the article had remained without a replacament for 10 years.

Finally as I noted above, I made a serious mistake in regards to the location of the shabtis and what was located in Brooklyn. Although this didn't affect things that much, it does mean it was silly for me to concentrate on Brooklyn.

Anyway that's it. I've spent way more time on this then I ever wanted to and given what's happened I'm washing my hands for good. Hopefully, we've all learned something from this Nil Einne 16:00, 18 January 2007 (UTC)
