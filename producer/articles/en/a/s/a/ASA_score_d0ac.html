ASA stands for American Society of Anesthesiologists. In 1961 the ASA adopted a five category physical status classification system for assessing a patient before surgery. A sixth category was latter added. These are

1 A normal healthy patient

2 A patient with mild systemic disease

3 A patient with severe systemic disease

4 A patient with severe systemic disease that is a constant threat to life

5 A moribund patient who is not expected to survive without the operation

6 A declared brain-dead patient whose organs are being removed for donor purposes

If the surgery is an emergency, the physical status score is followed by “E” (for emergency) for example “3E”. Category 5 is always an emergency so should not be written without "E". The category 6E probably does not exist. The original definition of emergency in 1940, when ASA classification was first designed, was "a surgical procedure which, in the surgeon's opinion, should be performed without delay"1. This gives an oppertunity to the surgeon to manipulate the schedule of surgery for personal convenience. An emergency is now "defined as existing when delay in treatment of the patient would lead to a significant increase in the threat to life or body part2. If it is correct then severe pain due to broken bones, ureteric stone or parturition is not emergency.


These definitions appear in each annual edition of the ASA Relative Value Guide. There is no additional information that can be helpful to further define these categories.[1]

It is very unfortunate that different authors give different versions of this ASA definition.[2] It is because this classification is ambiguous and far from perfect. Many authors try to explain it on the basis of 'functional limitation' of patient which is not a part of actual definition. Often different anesthesia providers assign different scores to the same patient. This scoring system assumes that age of the patient has no relation to physical fitness, which is not true. Very old peoples, even in the absence of any systemic disease, tolerate anesthesia and surgery badly in comparision to young adults. A severe heart, liver, lung or kidney disease, although greatly affect physical status of patient and outcome of surgery, these cannot be labelled as “systemic disease” (which means a generalized disorder of the whole body like hypertension or diabetes mellitus). Similarly this classification ignores patients with malignancy (cancer). This scoring system could not be improved to more elaborated and scientific form, probably because it is often used to price reimbursement.


While anesthesia providers use this scale to indicate the patient's overall physical health or "sickness" preoperatively, it is regarded by hospitals, law firms, accrediting boards and other health care groups as a scale to predict risk and thus decide if a patient should have – or should have had – an operation.[3] To predict operative risk, age of the patient, the nature and severity of the operative procedure, the ability of the surgeon, anesthesia providers and assisting staff, duration of surgery or anesthesia and availability of equipment, medicine, blood, implants etc. are often more important than ASA score.

[edit] History

The first attempt to quantify risks associated with surgery was undertaken by Meyer Saklad1 in 1941 at the request of the American Society of Anesthesiology. This effort was the first by any medical specialty to stratify risk for its patients. Saklad’s system did so based on mortality secondary to the associated preoperative medical condition. Type of anesthesia and nature of surgery were not considerations in this system and the divisions were based on empirical experience rather than on specific sets of data and reflect the techniques and standards of practice as of 50 years ago. Four preoperative risk categories were established ranging from category 1 (least likely to die) to category 4 (highest expectation of mortality).[4] The 5th and 6th categories were initially emergencies of the first 4 categories [5] which were removed and fifth category was allocated for moribund patients undergoing surgery in a desperate attempt to preserve life. 'E' was assigned to any of the five groups to denote emergency.[6]
