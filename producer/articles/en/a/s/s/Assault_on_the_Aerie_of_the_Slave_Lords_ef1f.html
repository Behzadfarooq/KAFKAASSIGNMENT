Assault on the Aerie of the Slave Lords is an adventure module originally designed for the pen and paper role-playing game Dungeons & Dragons.

Allen Hammack wrote Assault on the Aerie of the Slave Lords (A3) as the third in a series of four modules presented at Gencon XIII at the University of Wisconsin, Parkside in August of 1980. The series of four modules, Scourge of the Slave Lords, played sequentially at the event as a tournament. The moduleâ€™s difficulty level is set for a group of six to eight characters of fourth to seventh level in experience. It is 34 pages long not including the front and back cover.

The other modules in the series include Slave Pits of the Undercity A1, Secret of the Slavers Stockade A2, and In the Dungeons of the Slave Lords A4, all by different authors.

[edit] Assault on the Aerie of the Slave Lords

[edit] Introduction

The introduction to the module reads:

Into the Drachensgrab Mountains! Hot on the trail of the marauding slavers, you and your fellow adventurers plunge deep into hostile hills. Spurred on by your past success, you now seek the heart of the slaver conspiracy. But hurry! You must move quickly before the slavers recover from your previous forays and attack!

For those playing the entire series the first section of this module, Part A, is the last section of the first round of elimination. Part B and part C together form the semi-final round of the tournament.

[edit] Pre-generated Characters

If playing the tournament from the beginning the group continues with the characters generated in Slave Pits of the Undercity. If the group is tackling this as an independent module they can throw in their own group of six to nine 4th to 7th level characters. Like the previous adventures it includes a great deal of combat and the party should contain several fighters.

[edit] Scoring

As a tournament module Assault on the Aerie of the Slave Lords includes a scoring scheme. It essentially takes the number of surviving characters and gives them points for visiting the largest number of rooms. The highest score comes from visiting the most rooms and having the largest number of survivors. After the group finishes Part A the two teams with the highest point totals continue on to Part B and Part C.

[edit] Plot

[edit] Part A

The first part of the module is relatively short and meant to finish quickly, within 3 hours, in tournament mode. It is a trap filled, monster heavy section used to lure the group to a secret tunnel leading to the hidden city of Suderham.

[edit] Part B

The group enters the city of Suderham and renews their quest to destroy the slavers in a completely different environment. Up until now the group spent most of their time in dungeons battling or avoiding hordes of foes. Now their role-playing skills come to the test.

While not completely detailed the city contains a number of non-player characters for the players to interact with and exchange information. The group must find one of several secret entrances to the catacombs where the final part of the adventure plays out.

[edit] Part C

The third section of the adventure also completes at a rapid rate and in tournament mode finishes with the capture of the party. Each room encountered on this level is filled with dangerous creatures and the final room traps the party and prepares them to begin In the Dungeon of the Slave Lords. If this section is played outside of a tournament it is possible for the players to defeat the slavers and exit the area victorious.

[edit] Enemies

The adventurers will encounter a fairly large number of foes as they make their way through the city of Suderham and the slaver's compound. Prominent among these are:

  • Flesh Golem
  • Gelatinous cube
  • Gnoll
  • Hell Hound
  • Mimic
  • Minotaur
  • Piercer
  • Shambling Mound
  • Storoper

[edit] Conclusion

This small tournament module is fairly action packed and quite suitable for a heavy combat game. A Game Master can easily insert the scenario into any current campaign simply by changes the names of locations to match those of their own creation. Thanks to scanning it is possible to purchase most of these older modules for a small fee as an electronic document.
