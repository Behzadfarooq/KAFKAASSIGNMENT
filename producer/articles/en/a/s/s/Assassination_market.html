Assassination market

An assassination market is a theoretical prediction market wherein any party can place a bet (using anonymous electronic money, and pseudonymous remailers) on the date of death of a given individual, and collect a payoff if they "guess" the date accurately. Because the payoff is for knowing the date rather than performing the action of the assassin, it is substantially more difficult to assign criminal liability for the action(s).

[edit] Origin of the concept

An early use of the term can be found in the Cyphernomicon by Timothy C. May, published around October 1994.[1] Jim Bell's later article Assassination Politics described the concept in detail, concluding that as well as being an "unholy mix of encryption, anonymity, and digital cash",[2] the concept could also be used for violent crime. Timothy C. May, Carl Johnson and Matthew Taylor later developed the protocols to implement the concept online to the point that the IRS, the FBI and the US Secret Service investigated their motives for doing so.

[edit] Theory

It has been argued that a consequence of this thought experiment prevents the development of any form of electronic money which can be used sufficiently anonymously to financially motivate assassinations [3] . Nation-states are capable either of arresting and prosecuting those underwriting such hypothetical payment systems as accessories to these crimes, or where the underwriters' assets are offshore, of attacking underwriters or underwriting assets using military options. This follows from the fact that anonymous digital cash requires publicly known underwriters, e.g. banks participating in guaranteeing and forwarding payments made using a hypothetical digital cash. Potential underwriters are very unlikely to be interested in participating under these circumstances. Therefore digital cash systems offering the required degree of anonymity are equally unlikely to arise in practice because the existence of an open assassination market conflicts with a primary purpose of the Nation-state, i.e. to provide military security to citizens.

[edit] Application and implications

These issues arguably left the realm of the theoretical when U.S. stock markets based in Manhattan took a trillion-dollar hit in September, 2001. A serious investigation was launched in Autumn 2001 by the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission to determine whether large blocks of short-sold airline stock could be traced back to Al Qaeda, the group allegedly responsible for the terrorist acts carried out on the 11th of September.

The extensive cooperation of militant Islamic fundamentalist terrorist groups, and suggestions that they were in fact trading targets and objectives in different nations, gave rise to the idea that a covert assassination swap market may well already be in global operation.[citation needed] A group would perform an action in one place and a seemingly unrelated group in another place would profit from the act. Meanwhile, the political motive would actually be that of a third group in the background.

If the assassination market is seen as civil disobedience instead of terrorism, as suggested by Jim Bell, then this could perhaps lead to a fundamental impact on how governments function: most public figures would have a price on their head. Bell argues that the price would be somewhat proportional to how much the people dislike the person, and that it would scare politicians from making bad decisions. However, it might rather scare them from making unpopular decisions, which is a different thing altogether.

[edit] Fictional precursors

The concept of assassination politics is anticipated in Swiss playwright Friedrich Dürrenmatt's 1964 work, "The Visit", in which the appearance of a wealthy benefactress in an economically stagnant small town prompts its poor citizens to purchase new luxuries on credit, all of a sinister yellow color. The woman has promised to give a huge donation to the town on the condition that a certain shopkeeper dies. More and more yellow items appear, signalling to everyone--including the target--that the townspeople are willing to enrich themselves on the promise of his death. Patricia Highsmith's novel Strangers on a Train explored the possibility of bartering murders with a stranger, but complications arose because the strangers met face-to-face, which need not occur in an assassination market.
