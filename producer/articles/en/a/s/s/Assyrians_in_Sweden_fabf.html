Assyrians in Sweden

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

In the latter part of the 1970s, about 12,000 Syrian Orthodox Assyrians from Lebanon, Turkey and Syria immigrated to Sweden. They considered themselves persecuted for religious reasons, but were never acknowledged as refugees. Those who had already lived in Sweden for a longer period were finally granted residence permit for humanitarian reasons.[1]

The dividing line in Sweden between Syrians and Assyrians lies between the religiously defined group: Syrians, who are Syrian Orthodox Christians, and the politically or ethnically determined category: Assyrians, whose members belong to several different Christian beliefs (the majority of whom are also Syrian Orthodox Christians), but whose religious affiliation is toned down.[2]

Södertälje, a municipality in Sweden, is often seen as the unofficial Assyrian capital of Europe due to the city's high percentage of Assyrians and the Swedish professional football (soccer) team Assyriska Föreningen. The team played in the top Swedish football league (Allsvenskan) in 2005, is often viewed as a substitute national team by the diaspora, and has fans worldwide.[citation needed] The international Suroyo/Suroyo TV, which broadcasts in the Assyrian language, is also based in Sweden.

Between 2005 and 2006, Assyrian Ibrahim Baylan served in the Swedish government as Minister for Schools.
