Associated Students of Arizona State University

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Associated Students of Arizona State University (ASASU) is the student government at Arizona State University's campus at Tempe, Arizona. It is commonly referred to by ASU students as the ASU Student Government.

[edit] Structure

ASASU as it exists today was created in 2003, when the Student Government at the time adopted a new Constitution under the oversight of several individuals. The constitution originally split the student government into 3 branches:

  1. Undergraduate Student Government (Acronym USG. Established to serve the undergraduate students at ASU)
  2. The Graduate & Professional Student Association (Acronym GPSA: Established to serve the graduate students at ASU)
  3. Programming and Activities Board (Acronym PAB: Established to coordinate student events and activities on campus, no longer exists)

All three branches are overseen by a Supreme Court, and communicate with each other through a coordinating board that consist of executive officers. The PAB was dissolved following a student referendum in late 2006. Its last executive officers were Neal Stephenson and Nicole "Nikki" Pannier, who were elected in a walkover (unopposed) election in 2006.

The USG and GPSA both have an elected assembly within its structure, which consist of representatives from all colleges on campus, and provides a forum for students to voice concerns that are particular to a specific college on campus. The USG Senate assembly is also known for its role as a springboard for various student leaders toward an eventual tenure in the USG executive.


[edit] Undergraduate Student Government Elections, Governments

As the branch of student government that arguably serves the most people at ASU, USG elections often receives the most attention from on-campus student medias, and its history of election often parallels real life politics.

Since its inception in 2003, there have been 4 government elections, with each elections mirroring more closely to real life elections. Also worth noting is that as of 2007, no USG executive candidates have won outright during the first round of voting. All races were decided in runoff elections.

[edit] Trivias, Voting Patterns

At the start of the campaign, the Werner Campaign was considered to be the frontrunner, receiving nearly 1,000 more votes than the Alamoodi Campaign. However, before the general election began, the ASASU Supreme Court suspended the election over the appeal of a campaign violation by the Hamm Campaign. The campaign period was subsequently extended by one week, which was considered by some to have provided time for the Alamoodi Campaign to build up momentum and contributed to the campaign's victory in the runoffs.

The Alamoodi Campaign was run primarily with the help of the ASU Young Democrats, while the Werner Campaign received most of its help from members of USG and Greek Life. At the runoffs, The Alamoodi Campaign defeated the Werner Campaign by a margin of roughly 50 votes, the closest election in USG history.

The Alamoodi Campaign also made history for being the campaign with the largest vote increase (in terms of percentage in increase) ever. The campaign increased their vote by roughly 200%, a record that still stands as of 2007.

[edit] Post-Election Controversies

After the runoff, the results were contested by the Werner Campaign over a number of alleged campaign violations including voter fraud and distribution of tobacco to minors. This forced yet another delay to be issued by the Supreme Court, which prevented Alamoodi from being inaugurated as USG President by several weeks.


[edit] Alamoodi Government: Achievements, Controversies, and Resignation

The Alamoodi Government was credited with the organization of a campus-wide grassroots lobbying effort that was seen as a contributing factor for lowering a proposed tuition hike by the ASU administration from 8.5% to 6.5%. The Alamoodi Government was also credited with the reestablishment of an ASU Bicycle Co-Op, after years of discontinuation. The new Bicycle Co-Op program was founded in conjunction with the ASU Department of Public Safety.

During the Alamoodi Government, the USG administration created the "Coffee with the Times" lecture series, where students attend lectures by professors over coffee on current event issues as listed in The New York Times. The series is, however, not active as of 2007.

The Alamoodi Government was also known for some controversies. One of the major controversies was a statement made by Yaser Alamoodi which suggests that female ASU students be forbidden by the ASU Student Code of Conduct from posing nude for magazines, citing that female ASU students posing nude for magazines embellish ASU's dubious reputation as a "Party School" and undermines the university's academic achievements. The ASU administration gave a somewhat public rebuke of the suggestion, saying that the Student Code of Conduct cannot be used in such manner on consenting adults.

Another controversy began when Yaser Alamoodi was temporarily stripped of the salary he was entitled to as USG President over his failure to appoint ASASU Supreme Court justices.

The suggestion to ban female ASU students from posing nude, and the public rebuke and national attention that the policy received and created, were seen by some as a reason for Alamoodi to resign in January of 2006, during the first week of the Spring 2006 semester at ASU. Alamoodi himself denied that the resignation stemmed from the controversy, citing financial concerns instead as a reason for him to resign.

  • First election to be held under a completely overhauled Elections Codes.

[edit] Voting Statistics & Patterns
  • The Meyer-Simonhoff ticket defeated the Sales-Zoebisch ticket by over 360 votes in the runoff election, and was 14 votes shy of avoiding a runoff during the primary.
  • Meyer-Simonhoff was supported largely by members of USG and RHA; Sales-Zoebisch received most of its support from on-campus fraternities and sororities.

[edit] Post-Election Controversy

After the election, Meyer-Simonhoff was disqualified by the ASASU Elections Director for failing to submit campaign expense documents, as was required under the USG Elections Code. The disqualification was made after a complaint by the Sales-Zoebisch campaign. The ruling instated Sales and Zoebisch as the incoming executive officers.

The ruling by the Elections Director was appealed by Meyer and Simonhoff in the ASASU Supreme Court, where the disqualification was overturned when it was discovered that Sales and Zoebisch had violated the same rule as well. Meyer's girlfriend at the time served on the Supreme Court at the time, but she recused herself to ensure impartiality in the ruling.

[edit] Constitutional Changes

The USG administration drafted a new constitution in Fall of 2006, and presented it to the ASU voters for a referendum from 28 November to 29 November. The new constitution took the Programming and Activities Board out of the student government system, and mandated a three-person executive for the student government. The original draft of the new constitution was to instate a dual-presidency, but that gave way to the proposal that was eventually presented to voters.

[edit] 2007-2008

The election for the USG presidency and vice presidency is to occur in late February 2007. This year will be the first time that a ticket will contain three candidates, due to USG constitutional amendments that now requires a co-vice presidency. The amendment was passed in a relatively unknown referendum in early December.

[edit] Post Election Controversy

The Casher Campaign has filed an appeal with the ASASU Supreme Court after the Elections Department barred campaigning by the Casher Campaign for 5 hours on Election Day due to alleged campaign code violations. The Casher Campaign claims the violations were not made by them, and that the 5 hours ban in campaigning had a high probability of changing the election results, due to the slim margin of 22 votes that the Simonhoff Campaign had won by. The case is still pending.

[edit] Programming and Activities Board (PAB)

The Programming and Activities Board (PAB) is led by the Board of Directors, of which the President serves as Chairperson. Three graduate students and three undergraduate students serve on the Board, as well as the executive officers and ex-officio members as appointed by the President. The Executive Chief of Staff serves as Executive Director of the Board of Directors.

PAB directs all student programs and activities for the ASU student body, including concerts, movies, lectures, comedy shows, diversity programming, late night events, and campus tours. The President also chairs the Diversity Awareness Programming Board (DAPB) that consists of the Executive Director and members of ASU's student coalitions.
