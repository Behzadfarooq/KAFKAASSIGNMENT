The article is about assignment in mathematical logic; for other uses, see Assignment

Assignment can be regarded as an auxiliary notion, an important step in a specific way for defining the concept of truth formally (e.g. for first-order theories). It enables us to give meanings to terms (truth to sentences) of a language which deals with (free) variables.

Technically, in most cases it is a function

from a set of variables to the possible values they can take on [1].

It is somewhat similar to notions like

  • environment in type theory, computing and mathematics, meant for binding variables),
  • parameter passing (in computing).

[edit] Formal definition

Let be a first-order language and let be a structure, both of them being of a common signature Σ.

An assignment is a function from the set of variables V of to the universe A of :

In more details:

Let

  • denote a formal language (here for simplicity's sake, a first-order language) with
    • set of variable names V,
    • and also with non-logical symbols structured in a way that each of them should be known [2]
      • whether it will be used to denote an operation or a relation
      • and of what arity it is;
  • denote a structure of signature Σ, with universe A and interpretation I (see the latter concept explained later), i.e. a structure with operations and relations “corresponding to the” signature Σ.

Thus, interpretation I enumerates the operations and relations of by their names, respecting the signature Σ: it takes each non-logical symbol of language to an operation or relation of according to the arity and according to whether it is a relation or an operation. It is a special function from the system [3] of non-logical symbols of to the system [3] of relations and operations of , respecting the common signature Σ of language and structure .

For short: interpretation I is a function from non-logical symbols of to their extension in , respecting the common signature Σ.

Although is a formal language, but we can make use of an analogy, a similarity to natural languages here (to a limited extent): also it has “sentences”, consisting of “nominal” fragments (organized around a “verbal” axis having valency). To bring the analogy with natural languges further, as we use languges for

  • making statements (being true or false) about the world
  • using names (denoting objects of the world) having meaning

so in a similar way, the formal language consists of sentences, with each sentence incorporating terms as fragments, centered around a predicate.

Each term can be thought of as being built in the shape of pattern [4] where f is the name for an n-ary operation, and t0,… tn are subterms [5]. In a similar [6] way, we build sentences. See details in formation rules.

Now let us fulfill the mentioned (premised) analogy with natural languages and let us define

  • the meaning of a term of
  • the truth of a sentence of

A structural definition (and using I in a straightforward way) enables us to define a meaning for each terms of : we can map all terms to an appropriate element of universe A (of )

In a similar way, we can define truth for each sentence of , but there are also important differences, e.g. handling logical connectives, quantifiers, and equality in an appropriate way.

In addition, the existence of variables (and quantifiers) forces us to deal with open terms and open formulas [7]: those “with free variables” . Thus, we build the notion for truth and meaning in a more complicated way:

  • first, we introduce these notions parametrized by an assignment
  • then, we define their corresponding “unparametrized” counterparts as being invariant for assignment i.e. holding for each possible assignment.
