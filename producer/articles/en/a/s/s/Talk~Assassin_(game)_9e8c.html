[edit] Assassin at Carleton

Assassin is a long-standing tradition at Carleton, dating back to at least 1998. Using "Caucus", an internal server at the school, the Assassin's Guild leaders establish the rules of the game. Most weekends, each assassin is given a "target" person to kill, though on some weekends "frag everyone" or team games occur.
The weapons used in assassin include guns, knives, poison, and bombs. Bombs are alarm clocks with "Bomb" written on them, poison is tabasco sauce, and knives can be any harmless object (usually a ruler) made silver (by paint or duct tape) and at least 8 inches long. Guns are commonly pellet and rubber band guns.
Many rules exist in assassin. For example, kills may not occur during classes, nor may they occur in the presence of more than a certain number of witnesses. To enforce these rules are players known as "enforcers" given special powers.

OK, I did some web research and made a major expansion. I'm sure there are variants not covered, but I think I got the gist of the common rules. I considered a list of colleges where assassin is known to be played, but it'd probably get quite long. I mentioned the two groups that seem to be the most well-established (MIT and Cambridge.) Anyone with other perspectives should certainly include them. This is the sort of subject where Wikipedia could easilly become one of the best sources of information available. Isomorphic 04:55, 5 May 2004 (UTC)

Mike, the weapons rules you wrote doesn't apply to all games of Assassin. Check out the links I posted. I was trying to stick to features common to most or all variants. Of the three rules sets I found, one group explicitly forbids poison. Another uses dart guns only, and issues the guns to players so that no other weapons are legal. Most of the details (like using alarm clocks as bombs, or rulers as knives) seem to be Carleton-specific, as none of the rules sheets I found agree with them exactly. [edit] Davidson Assassins

I'm a moderator of Assassins at Davidson and my reasoning for adding the info on the school's winners is very similar to Mike Church's. I wanted to know what other games at other schools were like, and I was intending on others to add information on their school's games and winners.

In addition, we know for the most part what the game of Assassins is, but there is little emphasis on the elite group of men and women who have won Assassins. These folks are awesome and deserve more recognition :-)

Furthermore, I'm planning the school's next game in the spring (it's a spring tradition for us), and unlike other years where it was played within or between dorms, the game will be open to the entire freshman class. This year's game could potentially have up to 470 players (has there been a game this large before anywhere else? If so, I would love to know how it turned out!) Lastly, I wrote the article so that it could be a resource to the new crop of players. These players need a sense of history of who came before them and how that lineage progresses.

  • Interesting, but I still think this info should be moved to the article on Davidson College. As for game size, I personally remember an assassin game being played that was open to my entire high school (potentially over 1,000 participants, but if memory serves only about half actually participated) circa 1984, but since it can't be "verified" I don't intend to mention it in the article. There is a high school in Springfield, Illinois that apparently has had an assassin game every year, open to its entire class (a few hundred, I believe). In 1993, U.C. Berkley had a 300-person game. Grand Canyon University had a school-wide assassin game in 1999 or so (potentially over 1,000 students). So while I agree that the planned game at Davidson is large, I'm not yet persuaded that it is unique enough to warrant this much space about the Davidson winners in this article. Instead I would just discuss the game as played at Davidson, mention that it is a major social activity there, and then discuss the actual winners in the college article. Crypticfirefly 04:54, 26 December 2005 (UTC)

[edit] Other Variations

I have played a version of Assassin before, not on college campus. You simply had to get alone with the person you were trying to kill and then say, "Bang, you're dead." This was at a camp, so there was a board where the people who were killed wrote their names at mealtime. The last non-assassin living would win (there were two assassins). It was a fairly simple version of the game, and could be played in places other than at college. 66.16.106.84 22:05, 1 August 2006 (UTC)
