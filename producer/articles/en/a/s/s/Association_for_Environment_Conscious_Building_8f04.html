The Association for Environment Conscious Building (AECB) is the leading network for sustainable building professionals in the United Kingdom. Membership of the AECB includes local authorities, housing associations, builders, architects, designers, consultants and manufacturers. Members share a 'broad green vision', reflected in their approaches to the design of buildings and their environment.

The Association was Founded in 1989 by builder, Keith Hall and his partner Sally, to "increase awareness within the construction industry of the need to respect, protect, preserve and enhance the environment".

[edit] Low-carbon building

While the AECB recognised that all aspects of sustainability are important, it believes that climate change threatens to overwhelm their achievements in other areas. It is therefore currently focusing on trying to help the reduce carbon emissions related to domestic buildings in the UK (almost 30% of UK CO2 emissions - excluding flying - relate to domestic buildings, and 50% to buildings in general). The Association believes that the Government's target of a 60% reduction in CO2 emissions by 2050 is too little, too late, and that a reduction of at least 85% is required to meet the challenges of climate change. Constructive criticism of the programme from interested individuals is currently being sought via the AECB web site.

[edit] Low-carbon standards

To promote low-carbon building, the Association has developed two advanced housing energy standards, which are expected to be finalised in 2007.

Buildings constructed to the AECB Silver Energy StandardTM emit around 75% less carbon dioxide than required by current Building Regulations. The standard is close to the Canadian R-2000, the German Low Energy House (Niedrigenergiehaus), and the Swiss MINERGIE Standard.

The AECB Gold Energy StandardTM is based on the German Passivhaus standard, but require a greater use of renewable energy sources to balance the use of electricity for lighting, appliances and ventilation, in order to become close to zero energy buildings.

Both standards ban the use of electricity for space heating and water heating, except via a ground source heat pump.

The AECB has been lobbying for the Government's Code for Sustainable Homes to be aligned with its Gold and Silver standards.
