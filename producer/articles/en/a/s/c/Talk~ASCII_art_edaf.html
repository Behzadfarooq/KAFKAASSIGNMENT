The first ASCII image that I saw was back in the 60's. It didn't use a computer. It used a Ham radio RTTY Teletypewriter and had been punched on a Paper tape. Persumably it had been composed by hand (computers were pretty scarce back then). It sure blew me away...
About two weeks ago (circa June 2004) I saw on Ripley's Believe It or Not a fellow in India? who was doing absolutly amazing things with a manual typewriter and colored ribbions. He was making huge photoquality images, one page at a time.
ErikFP 12:45, 15 Jun 2004 (UTC)
Typewriter art does not belong under the category of ASCII Art because it was often created by overlapping characters (partially or fully). --Mekanik 20:46, 19 Jul 2004 (UTC)
That's somewhat arguable as its a bit of a gray area. ASCII is just a standard method of character encoding. There were teletypes (which allowed for overstriking) which used ASCII encoding rather than five-level (5-bit) Baudot / ITA2 encoding. There is evidence of overstrike art created from teletype machines using ASCII character encoding. —[[User:Radman1|RaD Man (talk)]] 11:18, 5 Dec 2004 (UTC)

[edit] Advanced ASCII-art

What about the terminal-font-based ASCII art many warez groups have in their NFO's or coloured ASCII-art?

--> ANSI art

What do you mean? I added recently the references to "Block" or "High" ASCII art (no colors) that is limited to the PC ASCII scene, because it uses a proprietary extended Character Set unique to IBM PC's. This style was especially popular for ASCII Logos used in PC Group NFO Files. Some argue that this is ANSI and not ASCII, but I disagree. It is ANSI, if special ANSI Escape codes are used for color and style formatting. This is not the case for "Block" ASCII's.. I am doing ASCII and ANSI for over 14 years now and there was no discussion about "Block" ASCII being ASCII or ANSI until the era of the Internet and the replacement of Text based User Interfaces like MS DOS with Graphical User Interfaces (Windows 95 and later). --roy<sac> Talk! .oOo. 13:58, 24 May 2006 (UTC)
