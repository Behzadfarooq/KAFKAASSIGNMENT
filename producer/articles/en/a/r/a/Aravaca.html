Aravaca is a Madrid quarter, belonging to the Moncloa district. It is 9 kilometers (5,5 miles) away from the city downtown on the other side of Casa de Campo park.

During the Spanish Civil War, Aravaca was a war front for three years in the Siege of Madrid (1936-39). One may find in some places of the quarter, such as parks and woods, war bunkers used by the Franco troops when they were attacking Madrid. The old town was completely devastated and was rebuilt in the forties. From this years are the parish church and some houses in Baja de la Iglesia street, all designed in old castilian style.

Until 1951 Aravaca was an independent city within the Madrid province with its own council hall and major. During the long Spanish postwar (1940-1959) millions of Spaniards left their homes in the poor provinces to inmigrate to industrial areas like Madrid, Barcelona, Valencia or the Basque Country. Aravaca grew quickly between 1950 and 1980 and became a residential suburb for the middle class.

The population today is about 30.000 people, mainly commuters who work in Madrid and in the Metropolitan area. The public transport network is efficient with buses every 10 minutes to the center, a railway station and a Metro line in construction.
