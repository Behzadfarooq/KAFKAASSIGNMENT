The Arab lobby in the United States is a collection of formal and informal groups that lobby the public and government of the United States on behalf of Arab interests.[1]

[edit] Origins

Isaiah L. Kenen, the founder of what became American Israel Public Affairs Committee, says the Arab lobby has its roots in the "petro-diplomatic complex" that comprises the "oil industry, missionaries, and diplomats."[2]

Mitchell Bard, the American foreign policy analyst and historian who specializes in U.S.-Middle East policy, "from the beginning, the Arab lobby has faced not only a disadvantage in electoral politics but also in organization. There are several politically oriented groups, but many of these are one man operations with little financial or popular support."[2]

[edit] Formal Arab lobby

In 1951 King Saud of Saudi Arabia asked U.S. diplomats to finance a pro-Arab lobby to counter the leading formal Israel lobbying organization in the US, the American Zionist Committee for Public Affairs (AZCPA), the forerunner of the modern American Israel Public Affairs Committee (AIPAC).[2] The result was the formation of the National Association of Arab-Americans which has since been disbanded. No cohesive Arab lobby has ever come to existence, with the closest being the "American-Arab Anti-Discrimination Committee". This organization, which started in the 1970s, gained considerable support from politically active Arab Americans until the headquarters of the organization was bombed in the early 1980s. Even in its heyday, the [Arab American Anti-Discrimination Committee] served the interests of Arab Americans within the confines of domestic politics. Today, this organization does little in terms of pressing to change foreign policy that is against "Arab" interests, though they do serve as a source of protection and advice for Arab Americans who fall victim to civil rights abuses and intimidation by government and private agents.

[edit] Misconceptions of an Arab Lobby

The problem with the term "Arab lobby" has many layers, ultimately stemming from what Edward Said would call [Orientalism]. The Arab world spans from Morocco to the West to Iraq in the East. This vast region contains diverse populations who share little more than a common formal language, which is incompatible with most vernacular dialects spoken throughout the region. There is, however, a Jordanian lobby, just as there is a Saudi lobby and a Kuwaiti lobby. Each of these interest groups, however, acts to promote the best interests of its representative country, even at the cost of other Arabs and Arab countries. Case in point: the Saudi lobby pushed the US government for the 2003 invasion of Iraq, though the [Arab League] interpreted the war as another devastating blow to Arabs' right to national self-determination.

To put it quite simply, there is no unified Arab lobby for the same reason why there is no unified Arab nation. The region is divided by political, regional, and religious rivalries. Many who would be considered Arab, prefer to be referred to in terms of their home country. In fact, some are insulted to be referred to as Arabs, considering that term to be designated strictly to those whose lineage traces back to the Arabian Peninsula. Also, substantial immigration from the Arab world to the United States is a relatively recent phenomena due to the recent history of the region. These two factors help to explain why there is no unified Arab lobby and why it appears that Arab lobby groups do little to improve the collective Arab plight.
