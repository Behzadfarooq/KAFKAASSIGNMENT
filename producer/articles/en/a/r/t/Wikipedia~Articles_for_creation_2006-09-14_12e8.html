[edit] Single Index Model

  • Article Created. Thanks for your contribution!--nkayesmith 06:53, 13 September 2006 (UTC)

The Single Index Model (SIM)is an asset pricing model commonly used in the finance industry to measure risk and return of a stock. Mathematically the SIM is expressed as:

(rit − rf) = ai + Bi(rmt − rf) + Eit

Where: rit − rf is the return on the stock ai is the company's alpha Bi is the company's beta rmt − rf is the return on the market index Eit is the residual return

The accuracy of the model is enhanced by the stock return's influence by market (beta) and firm-specific risk factors (alpha), unexpected returns (residual) and the relation to the performance of a market index (such as the All Ordinaries). Security analysts often use the SIM for such functions as computing stock betas, evaluating stock selection skills, and conducting event studies.

[edit] Rick and The Goose

Rick and The Goose are the Australian based jazz/blues acoustic duo featured on Big Rock Records big-rock.com.au. Rick and The Goose are Rick Veneer rickveneer.com (guitar, vocals, slide guitar, harmonica) and Barry (Little Goose) Harvey barryharvey.com (double bass,hi hat and bass drum). Rick is best known for his veteran electric band The Vigilantes (also featuring Little Goose on drums and Phil Hoffman on bass) and Little Goose is wellknown as the long time drummer with the premier Australian blues band Chain and author of the well known drummers' handbook "Text of Music Phrase". The duo are unique in their presentation, with Little Goose playing bass and drums at the same time to create a "two man trio" effect.

Their 2005 debut album on Big Rock is Valley of The Blues (BRCD/037-2) and was recorded live in the studio with only one sole overdub - Rick's Telecaster solo over Bobby Troup's "Route 66". The album showcases a mix of original tunes such as the title track and some numbers from Howling Wolf, Robert Johnson, John Lee Hooker, Muddy Waters and Bo Diddley. The duo play gigs in their home base Brisbane, Queensland and are planning to travel to venues around Australia.

    [edit] K.P. Mohanan [Karuvannur Puthanveettil]

    K.P. Mohanan was born in Kerela, India. He became a student of Morris Halle, Noam Chomsky, Paul Kiparsky and Joan Bresnan at MIT.     [edit] Bulgarian hiphop scene

    Bulgarian hip hop has been in effect for almost 20 years, primarily beginning after the fall of communism. B-boying came on the scene first taking Bulgaria by storm and continues to play a major influence in the hiphop landscape. Rap was next in line as Avi MC dropped the first Bulgarian hip hop record, Problemi i Ekologia (Problems and Ecology) in 1989. This was followed up by the group Rap Nacia who delivered the first full album, Ocelyavat Samo Silnite (Only the Strong Survive), in 1992. Graffiti soon followed as the bombing of trains, streets and legal walls commenced, leaving DJing as the least developed element due to the high cost of vinyl and turntables.

    Over the years, the breakers developed their own community throwing jams for all the b-boys to travel to somewhere in the country every month. However, they still maintain ties to the other elements as they still invite rappers and writers to perform. Some of the best crews include the internationally known Electric Force, who also organize the biggest Bulgarian b-boy jam called B-Boys Breakdown, Diablo Crew, Soul Award, Eazy Snatch, Express Our Soul, and Sleep Walking among others.

    Today, illegal mp3s and rampant downloading has made it difficult to participate in the Bulgarian music business. However, despite this problem Bulgaria’s scene continues to grow and gain acceptance into the mainstream. In 2005, Music Mania (MM), the biggest Bulgarian music TV network, recognized DRS (Dope Reach Squad) as they won the station’s “People’s Choice Award.” Misho Shamara (a.k.a. Misho the Slap), owner of one of the more famous labels in the country (R ‘n’ B Records) is also planning a hip hop TV show to promote Bulgarian hip hop. There’s also a growing pool of underground including artists from the two big labels from Plovdiv - Udarnata Grupa (Hit Squad) and Sunrise Project, and mainstays like DRS, X-Team, Spens, Horata Ot Getoto (People From the Ghetto).

    [edit] Ande Parks

    Ande Parks (born 0ctober 1, 1964) is a professional American comic book artist, known for his work as an inker and writer in the industry. His greatest notoriety has come from his stint with fellow artist Phil Hester on DC Comics’ Green Arrow series from 2001-2004 and writing the graphic novels Union Station and Capote in Kansas. He grew up in the suburbs of Kansas City and currently lives in Baldwin, Kansas with his wife and two children.

    Parks began his professional career as an inker in comic books with Dark Horse Comics. He has worked at inking the penciled artwork of such titles as Wonder Woman, Catwoman, Superman, Daredevil, and Spider-Man. His artistic style has won him praise in the comic books industry, including an industry Harvey nomination as best inker. His idols and influences include Neal Adams, Klaus Janson, Jack Kirby, Dick Giordano, and Mike Grell. For characters from the comic books, Parks has stated he has most enjoyed Batman and Captain America as his favorite characters.

    He is probably best known for his work on the relaunched Green Arrow comic book series beginning in 2001, which he inked over his frequent collaborator Phil Hester. On Green Arrow, Hester and Parks worked with writers Kevin Smith, Brad Meltzer, and Judd Winick over their approximate 40-issue run. The book has been collected into several volumes. The American Library Association called it one of the best books for young adults. Parks and Hester have also done work on DC Comics’ Nightwing with writer Devin Grayson, allowing Parks to work within the Batman family of characters, and both are currently working on a new project for Marvel Comics involving the super-hero Ant-Man.

    Although better known for his artwork, Parks considers himself to be more of a writer than artist. He has contributed several articles in comic book trade magazines, such as Draw!, geared towards critiques of artwork and professional tips for inkers. Parks' combined his inking style with his fondness for writing when he created the superhero spoof Uncle Slam & Fire Dog for Action Planet Comics, based largely on his love of Marvel Comics’ Captain America. Uncle Slam appeared in three issues of the Action Planet anthology book series, and in two issues of his own book in the early- to mid-1990’s. The character also appeared in a completely on-line comic book for Komicwerks.

    For graphic novels, he has often turned to his neighboring region in the Midwest as source material for his writing projects. To date Parks has steered towards writing that is best classified as historical fiction. He has credited Alan Moore’s From Hell as being a large influence in his decision to pursue writing in the historical fiction genre. In 2004, Oni Press published Park's first original graphic novel, Union Station. The book dealt with the events surrounding Kansas City's Union Station massacre, which took place in 1933 in Kansas City, Missouri, and helped J. Edgar Hoover make the F.B.I. a powerful organization in law enforcement and government for decades to come. Eduardo Barreto provided the artwork, done in black-&-white to give the piece more of a Depression-era period look.

    In 2005 Parks returned to the genre of historical fiction with Capote In Kansas, drawn by Chris Samnee. The book details the time Truman Capote spent in Kansas while he worked on his literary masterpiece, In Cold Blood. He is currently developing his next writing projects, geared towards non-historical fiction.

    [edit] Helderberg Escarpment

    The Helderberg Escarpment, located in the center of Albany County, New York, is the county’s signature landform. The most dramatic part of the Escarpment, with relief of over a thousand feet, stretches for about ten miles from Knox and Altamont to southern New Scotland, Westerlo and Coeymans. The view from the cliff top encompasses the Mohawk lowlands, the southern Adirondacks and the western mountain ranges of Vermont and Massachusetts. John Boyd Thacher State Park, established in 1914 on the edge of the Escarpment, draws over 250,000 visitors annually to admire the striking scenery from the cliff edge. Exposed layers of sedimentary rock contain a rich array of Devonian fossils that have made the Escarpment an important scientific and cultural resource.

    [edit] Jñānaprasthāna

    The main Abhidharma treatise of the Sarvastivada school of Buddhism. It's name means the 'development of wisdom'. It was also one of the key texts that Vasubandhu referenced in his famous Abhidharma Kosa (treasury) and Bhasya (commentary) thereon.

    There are disputes over the author. In the chinese, and according to Nagarjuna's Mahaprajnaparamita upadesa, it is authored by Katyayaniputra. However, others claim it was taught by either the Buddha or Sariputra, and it was only Katyayaniputra who popularized it.

    The great chinese scholar and prolific translator of the Abhidharma - Xuanzang - believed it to be the work of Katyayaniputra himself.

    [edit] System Clock

    This clock is normally a quartz crystal that vibrates at a set speed.

    Each vibration is called a clock pulse or clock cycle.

    These pulses are the signals that allow data (i.e. data, instructions, signals) to move around the motherboard at a set speed. In this way the system clock regulates the speed of all activity on the motherboard.

    The speed of the system clock is measured in Megahertz (MHz). 1 MHz means 1 million vibrations or clock pulses per second. 75 MHz means 75 million vibrations per second!

    Currently most system clocks run at either 100 or 133 MHz.

    [edit] Ramon Vilanova - The Ultimate Landscape Painter

    70.111.164.13 16:47, 12 September 2006 (UTC) Acclaimed by art critics and represented in prominent and distinguished collections throughout Europe, Ramon Vilanova is one of the great landscape painters living in Europe. His works have brought him more than 30 1st place prizes in major competitions and his work is in the permanent collection of many museums, a further testament to the credibility and importance of this extraordinary artist. Vilanova works exclusively "plein-air", at that time and place, giving his compositions a special sensitivity, an emotional experience of the scene he has chosen to commit to canvas. He needs fully to experience the environment in order to paint, not just view the scene... He must smell the flowers.., He must be caressed by the breezes… He must experience the light streaming and filtering through the foliage and against the backgrounds. The overall feel is critical to his ability to create his paintings. A photograph or remembrance will not suffice; only real life can fulfill the need. You notice weeds and errant branches and leaves in his paintings, because he paints only what he sees. Nature, like life, is imperfect and Ramon, in his work, shows us all of nature, not only perfect beauty. And yet this uncorrected beauty is captured perfectly, as the paintings are perfect in their representation of the scene as the artist has felt them that day, in that moment. Ramon was born and raised near Barcelona, Spain. He continues to live and to create in these idyllic surroundings in rural Catalonia to the north. His love of the landscape is deep and he senses instinctively how to capture its beauty, as he is thoroughly a part of it. His technique is at once complex and unusual, yet necessary, so that he can infuse the paintings with the sense of what he wishes to convey. He stains his canvases by generously spreading oil paints on with a spatula. Then, still using the spatula, he models and organizes the oils into his representation of the landscape before him. Occasionally, he will further utilize a "dripping and splattering" technique analogous and reminiscent of the works of abstract expressionism. Vilanova's drippings are more contained, but appear in the canvas as an integrating element. All of the artist's creations are complex and symphonic in nature. The artist has a remarkable, intuitive understanding of color. Several prominent European art critics have described Ramon as a "colorist" as his works contain a palette that by its subject matter demands the nuance and surprise of color so that we may properly interpret his sense of immediacy. Vilanova is an instinctive painter with need to paint, in tune with his environment, he is acutely aware of the tonalities and half tones that light creates with color. The world of landscape is what intrigues him... a farmhouse in a field, an island offshore, an urn overflowing with flowers in a private garden, a field of brilliant wildflowers, an errant weed. Vilanova is a self-taught artist, who, though new to the U.S. art market, has been exhibiting in Europe with great success since the early 1970s. He has continued to refine and further define his craft and interpretation to where he really has no contemporary peer in the visual and emotional interpretation of landscapes and seascapes. A book of his works entitled “Latidos” was published earlier this year.

Expelled from school for vandalism in 1956 (he mischeviously repainted the facade of a popular teen hangout) "Stanley Mouse" completed his formal education at the Art School of the Society of Arts and Crafts in Detroit.

    In 1958 Mouse became fascinated by the "Weirdo Hot Rod Art" movement that had begun in California a decade before. Having developed skills using an airbrush he began producing art t-shirts at custom car shows. There he met and worked with Ed "Big Daddy" Roth. Mouse was also particularly influenced by the art of Rick Griffin, with whom he would later collaborate on posters and album covers. In 1959 Mouse and his family founded Mouse Studios, a mail-order company, which sold his products.

    In 1965 Mouse travelled to San Francisco, California with a group of art school friends. Settling initially in Oakland, Mouse met Alton Kelley. The following year the pair began working together to produce posters for The Family Dog, a group that promoted rock music dances at the Avalon Ballroom. Later the pair also produced posters for promoter Bill Graham and for events in the psychedelic community.

    The psychedelic posters Mouse and Kelley produced were heavily influenced by Art Nouveau graphics, particularly the work of Alphonse Mucha. Material associated with psychedelics, such as Zig-Zag rolling papers, were also referenced. Producing posters advertising such musical groups as Big Brother and the Holding Company, Quicksilver Messenger Service, and The Grateful Dead led to meeting the musicians and making contacts that were later to prove fruitful.

    In 1968 Family Dog promoter Chet Helms and Graham began turning to other artists for their poster work, and Mouse's career languished. After brief periods in London, Massachussetts, and Canada, Mouse returned to California to live in Marin County near Kelley. The pair resumed their partnership in 1971, producing commercial artwork related to the Grateful Dead. The pair are credited with creating the skeleton and roses image that became the band's archetypal iconography. Mouse and Kelley continued to work together until 1980.

    Mouse continued to produce album cover art and other music-related graphics through the 1980's. However early in the decade he moved to Sonoma, New Mexico, where he began producing fine art in a variety of media. Stanley Mouse survived a liver transplant in 2003 and continues to paint.

    [edit] History of Luttre

    From the Roman times till the Belgian independence

    Following the conquest of Gaul (from 57 B.C till 52 A.D), Rome re-organised the new territories. Northern Gaul became the province BELGICA with Reims (F) as it’s capital city. This province was further divided into ‘civitates’. The area that would later become Luttre belonged to civitas Tungrorum with Tongeren (B) as the capital city, and was near civitas Nerviorum with Tournai (B) as capital.

    The new rulers quickly developed a good road infrastructure (partly based on existing links). A ‘service station’ (roman baths, taverns, stables….) has been discovered by archaeologists at Liberchies (a village located 3 kms from Luttre) along the road “Bavai (F) – Tongeren (B) – Köln (D)”.

    The first document mentioning the name of Luttre dates back to the end of the XIIIth century. It was an agreement between the seignior of Rêves (also seignior of Luttre) and the Monastery controlling the church of Celles (which would become Pont-à-Celles at the end of the XVIIIth century). The agreement was signed in 1289 and, from that time on, the priest of Celles would come thrice a week to hold services in a newly built chapel in Luttre.

    It is generally agreed that the name of Luttre comes from an old German word “lutere” which means “pure, transparent, limpid water”.

    Luttre was in the duchy of Brabant, extending from Nivelles (B) till St Hertogenbosch (NL) with Leuven (B) as the capital city. Luttre depended on Nivelles – duchy of Brabant – for administrative duties (taxes, justice) while the birth, marriage and death registers were kept by the priest of Celles which depended on Thuin (B), and thus to the principality of Liège (B). The church kept such registers at that time.

    Luttre being a tax-free zone, the inhabitants were exempted from paying taxes on consumer goods. It also meant that people from the neighbouring villages were coming to Luttre for alcohol, wine or beer. We can also imagine a number of inns and pubs for travellers as the Roman highway was quite near.

    The area suffered a lot because of continuous wars throughout the centuries. Nevertheless, a traveller in 1615 going from Tournai (B) to Liège (B) wrote about the crystal clear water of the “Piéton”, the stream crossing Luttre, and the beauty of the landscape (green hills, forests and surrounding prairies).

    Because of the new ideas following the French revolution in 1789, the Austrian Emperor (who was governing present day Belgian territories) wanted to impose reforms. It was followed by a period of unrest that ended with the French invasion in 1794. The old regime (rights of the seigniors, the power of the monasteries, the church keeping official registers, …) was abolished.

    A new system based on municipalities was established in 1795. The municipality of Pont-à-Celles was created from 6 former seigniories (including Luttre). At the same time, the invaded territories were divided in 9 departments. Some municipalities, including that of the new Pont-à-Celles, were withdrawn from Nivelles (which went to the Dyle department, which became the province of Brabant in 1815) and have become part of the Jemappes (named after a French victory in 1792) department.

    After the defeat of Napoleon in 1815, present day Belgian territories were united to those of the Netherlands. The Jemappes department became the province of Hainaut, which still exists today.

    [edit] Cascaded Integrator-Comb Filter

    This request for creation has been reviewed. The result is below.


    • Article created Thanks! --nkayesmith 07:15, 13 September 2006 (UTC)

    A Cascaded Integrator-Comb (CIC) is a highly efficient digital filter typically used in digital signal processing where interpolation or decimation are required. As the name implies, the filter consists of several integrators and several comb sections. In the case of a decimating CIC (i.e. a filter which accepts data at a high sampling rate then filters and decimates down to a slower output rate), the filter consists of one or more cascaded integrators feeding a decimator and ends in one or more comb sections (equal in number to the number of integrators). An integrating CIC is simply the reverse of this architecture.


    [edit] Comparison With FIR Filters

    A CIC has both advantages and disadvantages compared to FIR filters. In cases where only a small amount of interpolation or decimation are needed, FIR filters generally have the advantage. However, when rates change by a factor of 10 or more, achieving a useful FIR filter anti-aliasing stop band requires exponentially increasing numbers of FIR taps.

    For large rate changes, a CIC has a significant advantage over a FIR filter with respect to architectural and computational efficiency. Additionally, CIC filters can typically be reconfigured for different rates by changing nothing more than the decimation/interpolation section assuming the bit width of the integrators and comb sections meets certain mathematical criteria based on the maximum possible rate change.

    Whereas a FIR filter can use fixed or floating point math, a CIC filter uses only fixed point math. This is necessary so that the integrator and comb sections perform complementary discrete mathematical operations. While the reasons are less than intuitive, an inherent characteristic of the CIC architecture is that if fixed bit length overflows occur in the integrators, they are corrected in the comb sections.

    The range of filter shapes and responses available from a CIC filter is somewhat limited. Larger amounts of stopband rejection can be achieved by increasing the number of poles. However, doing so requires an increase in bit width in the integrator and comb sections which increases filter complexity. The shape of the filter response provides even fewer degrees of design freedom. For this reason, most real-world filtering requirements cannot be met by a CIC filter alone. However, a CIC filter followed by a short to moderate length FIR proves highly applicable. Additionally, the FIR filter shape is normalized relative to the CIC's sampling rate at the FIR/CIC interface so one set of FIR coefficients can be used over a large range of CIC interpolation and decimation rates.

    An excellent in depth tutorial on CIC filters written by Matthew Donadio including mathematical formulas can be found at [8]. An alternate name for the CIC filter is the Hogenauer filter in honor of its inventor. This seminal paper is referenced below, but due to its highly academic nature, it is recomended only for the most mathematically oriented reader:

    Ref: E. B. Hogenauer. An economical class of digital filters for decimation and interpolation. IEEE Transactions on Acoustics, Speech and Signal Processing, ASSP-29(2):155{162, 1981.

    [edit] Billy Lawson/ Singer/Songwriter/Rockabilly Artist

    Billy Lawson was born and raised in the Muscle Shoals area of Northwest Alabama. His next door neighbor was studio musician, Junior Lowe, who played on some of the early R&B classics by artists such as Percy Sledge, Wilson Pickett, Clarence Carter and Aretha Franklin. When Billy was nine or ten years old, Junior would let him sit in and sing with his band at the Ponderosa Club on the Alabama-Tennessee state line. This club was owned by W.R.     Another friend of the family who influenced Billy was Carl Montgomery, the co-author of the truck driver hit “Six Days On The Road.” Carl was the brother of Earl “Peanut” Montgomery, writer of 38 singles by George Jones, and Melba Montgomery, star of the Grand Ole Opry.

    Billy knew three chords on his Silvertone and could sing along with Charley Pride, Buck Owens and Merle Haggard by the time he entered grade school. Wherever there was live music, that’s where you could find Billy soaking it all up like a hungry sponge.

    After high school Billy started working the Alabama-Tennessee state line club and dance hall circuit with his band. One night a CBS representative came to a Muscle Shoals Club and signed Billy “on the spot” to an album deal on Epic records. The legendary Sam Phillips was there that night to put in a good word. But, as luck would have it, Billy’s key man at Epic was moved to the West Coast and put in charge of the Pop and Rock division of the label. Along with several other acts, Billy was lost in the shuffle. Meanwhile, Orion Pictures was holding auditions in Selma, AL, for an up-coming movie staring Jessica Lange, Tommy Lee Jones and Powers Boothe. Billy and his band got the part and Jessica Lange got an Oscar for best actress in “Blue Sky.”

Don signed him one week and by the next week, Billy had cuts on three major artists. Within a little over a year Billy had two ..1 hits...”Learning As you Go” by Rick Trevino and “I Left Something Turned On At Home” by Trace Adkins also “Irresistible You” A ..20 by Ty England.

    [edit] BURGER DOG

    The burger dog is an oblong form of ground beef designed to fit the same bread bun of the traditional hot dog. It was first publically served in Franklin, TN in 2005 at the yearly community Fall harvest festival. The inventor/designer of the burger dog, Jack Sloan, was attempting to streamline his small food booth operation by eliminating having to purchase and store several different buns in the limited space of the serving booth. Therefore, he experimented at home with the design to get the exact size and weight of oblong ground beef which when cooked properly would fit and slightly hang off a standard hot dog bun. The burger dog can be complimented with the standard amenities of lettuce, tomato, onions, and cheese, as well as, any other additional items the connoisseur may desire. The burger dog was an instant hit at the event and actually out sold the tradition hot dog 2 to 1. It was priced just $.50 more than the hot dog and Sloan provided the same compliments of onions, relish, shredded cheese, mustard, and ketchup for the two items at this first outing of the burger dog to further assist with the streamlining of the food product line and to conserve space. Due to the success of the burger dog during the summer of 2005, he went on in 2006 to trademark the product and design a special 'burger dog' meat press to form the ground beef faster than having to hand press each one.

    [edit] Hadamard Variance

    The Hadamard variance is a 3-sample variance, with binomially-weighted coefficients that is similar to the 2-sample Allan variance. It considers the 2nd difference of the fractional frequencies, the 3rd difference of the phase variations. The Hadamard variance, HVAR or s²H(t), converges for the Flicker Walk FM (a = -3) and Random Run FM (a = -4) power-law noise types. It is also unaffected by linear frequency drift.

    Like the Allan variance, also the Hadamard variance is usually expressed as its square-root.

    [edit] The Pyramid of Cool

    The Pyramid of Cool was a visual concept devised by the reputable hipsters Roach, Davidson and Holdaway, while on a summer retreat in the Hamptons. It consists of three main categorised 'states' of Coolness: the executive, the spastic, and the bargain-bin.


    The Executive is situated at the top of the pyramid, and is reserved only for those who display coolness in excessive amounts. Roach/Davidson/Holdaway (hereby shortened to RDH) wish to make the distinction between 'coolness' and mere 'popularity'. At the core of the 'cool' philosphy lies the concept of internal coolness, an intrinsic quality which cannot be derived from social factors like acceptance or fame. Popular-culture examples of 'Executive' include:

    Frank Zappa

    Ween

    Lou Reed

    Johnny Depp

    Robert Altman

    Tom Wolfe

    Rafael Nadal

    Bill Clinton

    The Spastic refers to the middle section of the pyramid, a place reserved for those who lack the subtle social artistry of an executive, but avoid the embrassing trappings of social ineptitude present in the lower tier of the pyramid. Spastic has often be associated with the term tryhard, and is central to the understanding of the 'spastic' objective - to assimilate into a higher strata of coolness. Such attempts at a coolness class-change are entirely futile, as the perception of effort on the part of the spastic voids all semblance of 'executive' nature in the subject. Such luminaries of the 'spastic' clique include:

    Clayton Bennett

    John Updike

    The band Nickelback

    Tom Cruise

    David Beckham

    Oliver Stone

    Lleyton Hewitt

    The lowest level of the 'cool' pyramid, termed [[bargain bin]] by RDH, contains the portion of society that is foreign to the concept of cool. This includes the socially retarded; persons that may not even be aware of the damage caused when they engage in discourse with others. Bargain-bin members usually incur an element of uneasyness and pity in their peers, often inciting ridicule and public avoidance at all costs. Examples include:

    [edit] Pound-Drever-Hall Locking

    In Laser Physics, Pound-Drever-Hall Locking or PDH locking is a technique used to improve a laser's frequency stability.

    It uses an external high-finesse cavity (i.e. a Fabry-Perot interferometer) as the reference, and an electronic servo to control the laser.

    It was developed by Drever and Hall based on the Pound stabilizer, used to stabilize microwave cavities.

    The laser is frequency modulated, either directly by the current input on a diode laser, or by using an Acousto-Optical Modulator. This introduces two side-bands onto the carrier frequency of the laser. The two side bands, fc − fm and fc + fm have a 180 degree phase relationship between them.

    To be able to seperate the input signal from the return signal, an isolator is used, which makes use of the othongonalise polarisation to direct the input beam on a different path to the output beam. One simple system is to pass the beam into a Polarisation Beam Splitting Cube, e.g. a Thomson prism pair. This will pass horizontally polarised light into the cavity and reflect vertically polarised light to a fast-photodector. The reflected light is rotated by using a quater-wave-plate. However, good optical isolation is still required to prevent reflected light optically feedbacking into the laser, and creating oscillation.

    A resonant cavity will have a maximum reflection when on resonance with the input source. A simple locking technique uses the reflected light of the cavity, using the side-of-fringe technique, which locks just off-resonant with the cavity's mode. This means that if the laser frequency increases (e.g. due to thermal drift) it will come closer to resonance with the cavity. The error signal will increase. If the laser frequency drops, it will move further away from resonance and the error signal will decrease. Therefore, the error signal can be fed to the laser and used to drive the laser back onto the lock-point. There are two problems with this scheme though. It is very easy for the laser to move so far from the lock point that it is on the otherside of the reflection peak, and the above situation is reversed. This is sometimes called "anti-lock" and the laser will be driven rapidly to another mode. The other problem is a side-of-fringe lock will respond to intensity noise, as well as frequency noise on the laser. This can be corrected for with intensity locking schemes, but is not ideal.


    The power of the PDH system is that is uses the reflected signal on resonance. However, to know whether the laser's frequency is higher or lower than the cavity's, it is neccessary to know the phase of the signal. That way the laser can be driven back to resonance.

    The reflected beam from the cavity is made up of two parts. There is an immediantly reflected part, and a part that comes from the round trip in the cavity. We need to measure the phase of the reflected beam, as this will tell us whether our laser frequency is higher or lower than the the cavities.

    On resonance, the carrier will be perfectly transmitted. By adding a high frequency modulation, which are much higher than the cavity's finesse, or width, both sidebands are almost totally reflected.

    To measure the phase-difference of these reflected sidebands, we need to deliberately send in to the cavity two beams with a fixed phase relationship. FM sidebands provide exactly this. In a perfect FM case, the two sidebands will be 180 degrees apart. When these two interfere, on the ouput of the cavity, a beat signal is created, which is dependent on their phase difference. It is this beat frequency that is detected on the photodiode. This gives us a measure of phase difference as an intensity on the photodiode.

    For instance, suppose our laser is just higher than resonance. The upper sideband will experience a positive phase-shift, whilst the lower sideband sees a negative phase shift. When these two signals are beat together, the beat frequency will be lower than the modulation frequency. The reverse is also true.

    However, the beat frequency is still at the carrier frequency and therefore contains a high (typically MHz) conponent. To remove this and just leave a DC error signal, the signal from the photodiode is mixed with the original modulation frequency. So long as the phase difference between these two is zero, the resultant signal contains the difference between the beat signal and the modulation and 2f components. The 2f components are filtered out, leaving a DC signal which is positive when the phase-difference (and therefore the frequency difference between reference and laser) is positive, and vice-versa. The great thing about the PDH signal is it has both a wide capture range and a fast response. A typical signal looks like :

    Image:Pdh.png

    An ideal locking signal would be 1 when the laser frequency is too low (and thus would drive the frequency higher until it reached resonance) and -1 when laser frequency is too high. The change would occur very rapidly on resonance, with the error signal being zero when both frequencies match.

    The PDH signal is good because is has a rapid transmission in the centre and two flatish plateaus at the top and bottom. If the laser jumps frequency it will be in one of the two plateaus and will find itself back to the centre. While it is in the centre, a small frequency shift will result in a large error signal change, rapidly driving back the laser to resonance.

    The shape is determined by two things. The finesse of the cavity and the modulation frequency. The width of each peak is determined by the ratio of the two. However, the slope in the centre is determined by the finesse of the cavity. If the modulation frequency is too high the two "dips" in the curve increase. If these reach zero, the lock may be falled into locking to them instead of the centre. Therefore a careful choice of these two values is required.

    All teaching at Sheboygan Area Lutheran High School Chapel conforms with the Lutheran Confessions; however, the school accepts students from a wide variety of Christian backgrounds.

    History

    Sheboygan Area Lutheran High School was opened in 1978 after many years of hard work and prayer by a group of dedicated Lutherans who believed strongly in Lutheran education. The school dedicated its first building in 1980 and has since added two additions. It will celebrate its 3oth anniversary in 2008.

    Curriculum

    Sheboygan Area Lutheran High School offers a Christian college preparatory curriculum.


    Sports

    Sheboygan Area Lutheran High School's sports teams compete in the WIAA.

    Fall sports: Boys' Soccer, Boys' Football, Girls' Volleyball, Boys' Cross County, Girls' Cross Country

    Winter sports: Boys' Basketball, Girls' Basketball, Boys' Wrestling

    Spring sports: Girls' Softball, Boys' Track, Girls' Track, Boys' Golf, Girls' Soccer, Co-ed Trap Shooting, Boys/Girls Tennis

    Summer sports: Boy's Baseball

    Other Extra curricular activities: Mock Trial, Forensics, Student Council, Art S.C.R.E.A.M., National Honor Society, Soul Train, Drama

    [edit] Whore's Egg

    24.93.136.172 01:35, 14 September 2006 (UTC)

    Whore's egg is a slang term, used by fishermen or lobstermen, for a sea urchin. The expression is common in New England and the Canadian Maritime provinces.

    These spiny creatures have a habit of getting into lobster traps or fishing nets, and they are painfully sharp, earning them a salty and unflattering nickname from the people who have to handle them.

    Later they became quite commercially valuable on the Asian market, so they were a little less despised.

    "Son of a whore's egg!" is a colorful waterfront epithet.
