[edit] Teatro di narrazione

The "teatro di narrazione" (narrative theatre) is a kind of theatre in which there are not any actor playing a dramatic role, there is no action, but only a "narr-attore" who tells the story as he was the narrative voice of a novel. During the show, the "narr-attore" often sits on a chair, doesn't move, just speaks. In the teatro di narrazione there are no theatrical objects, no costumes and no lights: just the ones necessary to let the audience see the performer on the stage: sometimes also the theatre is enlighted so that the narr-attore can see the faces of the audience because the narr-attore speaks properly and directly to his audience, without any fiction or scheme. It started with Mistero Buffo by Dario Fo in 1969. In the following years, two generation of "narr-attori" has grown and obtained a lot of success: Marco Paolini, Marco Baliani and Laura Curino belong to the first generation (the one of the performer born in the '50); Ascanio Celestini and Davide Enia to the second (they both were born in the '70). The "teatro di narrazione" tries to investigate in the "dark holes" of italian history: like the murder of Aldo Moro by the Red Brigades in 1978 in "Corpo di stato" by Baliani; like the experience of the business-men Camillo and Adriano Olivetti in "Olivetti" by Curino; like the tragedy of Vajont in "Il racconto del Vayont" by Paolini; the bombing of the Alleys on Palermo during the second world war in "maggio '43" by Enia; the nazi-fascist occupation of Rome in "Radio Clandestina" and the american liberation of the city in "Scemo di guerra" by Celestini.

[edit] Faye Abdellah

Faye Abdellah, RN, Ed.D., Sc.D., FAAN, was the founding dean of the Graduate School of Nursing at Uniformed Services University located at Bethesda, MD. Abdellah was the first Deputy surgeon General of the U.S. Public Health Service.

She identified 21 nursing problems which were related to human needs in the 1960's. According to her, nurses use a problem solving approach to practice instead of only following the physicians' orders. She conceptualized nursing as an art and science that molds the attitudes, intellectual skills, and technical competencies of the nurse into the desire and ability to help sick or well people, and cope with their health needs.

[edit] Gregory Mendel

A very unique person. He was the best arichitect born in Berlin in 1883. The son of an architect, he studied at the Technical Universities in Munich and Berlin. He joined the office of Peter Behrens in 1910 and three years later established a practice with Adolph Meyer. For his early commissions he borrowed from the Industrial Classicism introduced by Behrens.

After serving in the war,he became involved with several groups of radical artists that sprang up in Berlin in the winter of 1918. In March 1919 he was elected chairman of the Working Council for Art and a month later was appointed Director of the Bauhaus.

As war became eminent,he left the Bauhaus and resumed private practice in Berlin. Eventually, he was forced to leave Germany for the United States, where he became a professor at Harvard University. From 1938 to 1941, he worked on a series of houses with Marcel Breuer and in 1945 he founded "The Architect's Collaborative", a design team that embodied his belief in the value of teamwork. He created innovative designs that borrowed materials and methods of construction from modern technology. This advocacy of industrialized building carried with it a belief in team work and an acceptance of standardization and prefabrication. Using technology as a basis, he transformed building into a science of precise mathematical calculations.

An important theorist and teacher,he introduced a screen wall system that utilized a structural steel frame to support the floors and which allowed the external glass walls to continue without interruption. He died in Boston, Massachusetts in 1969.

In the game you create a character and chose to be a member of one of the three warring races: Vampire, Werewolf or Hybrid. The choice of race ables you to use Battle Skills that you start the game with, it also affects which clans you may join.

The game is open ended and although a lot of emphasis of the game is on battle there is also a strong financial side to the game involving mines, gold, silver, platinum, weapons and armour.

The game is entirely free to play but there are packages availible that increase your stats in games of VIP packages that give you bonuses for a period of time.

So he went from town to town, hoping one day to find the right method for achieving his goal.

No one knows exactly what happened in that castle, or what was being done to Lord Delvagan. Only this is known. Krelon created something in that Nobleman that would change history forever. Death was not cheated that day. Death was allowed to come into this world.

Citizens of Crystallion that were the missing neighbors and friends. They surrounded and subdued Delvagan and took him to the town square where he was immediately hanged.

History says, that the whole time he hung there, as his life was seeping from his body, he smiled. And at the moment of his death, something else came into this world at that very moment.

Wanting to purge this evil from the land, the people of Crystallion buried Delvagan in the forsaken wasteland of U'Moria. Never looking back, and never returning. So, of course they never knew that the grave in which they laid Delvagan was empty.

Many years past, and strangely enough, this wasteland of U'moria begin to spring to life. Fields of grass, trees, and all sorts of creatures began inhabiting the land. But still, the people of Crystallion would not venture forth into this awakening land and they declared it cursed and forbid anyone to enter it. Those that chose to ignore this edict were more times than not, never seen again.

Rumors began spreading when people began disappearing only to be found murdered.Everyone was perplexed as to what was causing these deaths, for the victims were always found with no blood left in their body and two small puncture wounds at the neck. Many suspected something Evil had come out of the Cursed Land and was slowly devouring the people of Crystallion.

After some time, the remaining residents of Crystallion formed a chosen band of Crusaders who marched to the Forbidden Land. Their Mission, to stomp out any Evil they found there.

It was here, in an area called The Dark Forest that they came upon Castle Vastill. This is where the Crusaders of Crystallion had their first encounter with a Vampire. Delvagan was the first of his Kind. The strength of Delvagan was unmatched by any mortal man. He tossed these Crusaders into the ston walls of the Castle as if they were molding putty. That night, Delvagan decimated half of the Citizens of Crystallion before they stabbed him in the head. Thinking him dead, they buried him in a hole 20 feet deep. When the last shovel of dirt was thrown on the grave, they left and never looked back.

Unfortunately for the people of Crystallion, it was only the beginning. For when they walked away from Delvagan's grave, how were they to know that the next night Delvagan would return to Crystallion along with his new army of Undead Soldiers? That was the beginning of the first Vampire Clan know as The Elders.

Krelon used Mayor Grayden's despair to manipulate him into being his next lab rat. For Krelon had found hidden Plasma Energies in the blood of the Wolf. The Wolf was a creature of the Night, and Krelon spent many weeks fusing the blood of Wolf with that of Mayor Grayden. This is how the first Lycan came into being, giving Mayor Grayden the ability to change at will into a half Man, half Wolf creature with incredible strength and agility.

These creatures, filled with rage and violence, would soon become a worthy opponent for the Vampires of the Wasteland. Grayden spent that first night killing and turning several townsfolk and dragging them into Mt. Ravania in the wasteland of U'Moria. [edit] Perique

Perique is a style of smoking tobacco originating from the area of St. James Parish in Louisiana. Perique is a spicy tobacco, typically not smoked alone but rather in tobacco blends containing a larger proportion of more subtly flavored tobacco styles, such as Virginia or Burley.

The process, orginally developed by Choctaw Indians (and later produced by the early French settlers of Louisana) involves fermenting the tobacco under high pressure in barrels, being periodically turned and again fermented, until the final product is achieved.

[edit] TIRMA MICROSCOPE by Charley boy 23XXX

total internal reflection fluorescence microscope

Principle of the TIRFMA total internal reflection fluorescence microscope (TIRFM) is a type of microscope with which a thin region of a specimen, usually less than 200 nm, can be observed.


Background In cell and molecular biology, a large number of molecular events in cellular surfaces such as cell adhesion, binding of cells by hormones, secretion of neurotransmitters, and membrane dynamics have been studied with conventional fluorescence microscopes. However, fluorophores that are bound to the specimen surface and those in the surrounding medium exist in an equilibrium state. When these molecules are excited and detected with a conventional fluorescence microscope, the resulting fluorescence from those fluorophores bound to the surface is often overwhelmed by the background fluorescence due to the much larger population of non-bound molecules.


Solution To solve this problem, the TIRFM was developed. A TIRFM elicits an evanescent wave that selectively illuminates and excites fluorophores in a restricted region of the specimen immediately adjacent to the glass-water interface. The evanescent wave is generated only when the incident light is totally reflected from the glass-water interface. The evanescent electromagnetic field decays exponentially from the interface, and thus propagates to a depth of only approximately 200 nm into the sample. Thus the TIRFM enables a selective visualization of surface regions such as the basal plasma membrane(which are about 7.5 nm thick) of cells as shown in the figure above. The selective visualization of the plasma membrane renders the features and events on the plasma membrane in living cells with high axial resolution.

Importantly, the fluorescence excitation energy of the evanescent wave is the same as the energy of the wavelength of the light that was totally internally reflected.

TIRF can also be used to observe the fluorescence of a single molecule, making it an important tool of biophysics and quantitative biology.

[edit] Vietnam War Films

Although few American feature films about the Vietnam War were produced during the war years (The Green Berets, released in 1968, is one notable exception), after the war a number of mainstream films dealt with this divisive conflict. Films such as The Deer Hunter (1978) and Coming Home (1978) considered the effects of the war on American veterans, but in many ways it was director Francis Ford Coppola's Apocalypse Now (1979) that signaled an intense interest in revisiting the war as a theme.

The 1980s saw the theme reworked in movies such as Missing in Action (1984) and especially in the three Rambo films that starred Sylvester Stallone. The second in the latter series, Rambo: First Blood, Part II (1985), showed the hero returned to Vietnam after the conflict to free American soldiers still held as prisoners of war. It was an audience favorite despite a generally acknowledged a lack of realism. Later in the 1980s, movies such as director Oliver Stone's Platoon (1986) brought a more realistic portrayal of the experience of American soldiers in Vietnam.

Hollywood continued to revisit the Vietnam War as a subject in the 1990s and 2000s, in films as different as Forrest Gump (1994) and We Were Soldiers (2002).

The portrayal of the Vietnam War in feature films has changed over the years, often reflecting political themes of the day.

[edit] Magnetizing force

Magnetizing Force (H) is the electrical force that generates magnetic fields in a given material or the free space within it.

Superconductors are limited to a certain ammount of magnetizing force known as the critical field (Hc), if they exceed this limitation, they start to resist and generate heat.

[edit] Contextual Objectivity

Contextual Objectivity is a principle with roots in quantum mechanics that was adapted and applied to the explain and describe the operations of news media organizations during times of war. Proposed by Adel Iskandar and Mohammed El-Nawawy in their analysis of Al-Jazeera as a case study, the term expresses the attempt "to reflect all sides of any story while retaining the values, beliefs and sentiments of the target audience." The concept has been applied by some scholars to explain FOX News Channel's news programming in the run-up to the Iraq war in 2004. Other studies used contextual objectivity to describe differences between mainstream media and alternative ethnic media's coverage of post-Hurricane Katrina relief efforts. [edit]

The anode is traditionally written on the left, and the cathode on the right.
Phases are separated by single vertical lines (ex: solid | liquid, liquid | gas, etc.)
The porous disk or salt bridge is represented by a double vertical line (ex: anode || cathode)


Standard Line Notation conventions are written as follows:
anode || cathode
site of oxidation || site of reduction
negative electrode || positive electrode


Here is one example of a reaction written in standard line notation:
Fe(s) | Fe2+ (aq) || Ag+ (aq) | Ag(s)
In this reaction, the iron is oxidized and the electrons travel through the electrode to the silver electrode, where a build up of silver solid occurs as the silver ions are reduced.


In a solution where there are several aqueous species, each species is separated by a comma
Ex: Pt(s) | ClO3- (aq), ClO4- (aq), H+ (aq) || H+ (aq), MnO4- (aq), Mn2+ (aq) | Pt (s)

[edit] Ston Easton Park

Ston Easton Park in Somerset was built in the 18th century for John Hippisley Coxe. The Hippisley family had been Lords of the Manor of Ston Easton since 1544, and in the 17th century had moved from the old manor house by the parish church to a new Jacobean house. This Jacobean house was converted into a Palladian mansion by John Hippisley Coxe.

The identity of the architect is not known, although the design is reminiscent of the work of William Kent, and it is also not known exactly when the house was built. John Hippisley Coxe died in 1769 and his sons Richard and Henry further embellished the house and grounds. Henry Hippisley Coxe employed Humphry Repton to design a landscape park, although only part of this plan was actually created. Henry's widow Elizabeth further developed the grounds in about 1814.

In 1956 Richard John Bayntun Hippisley died, and his son John Preston Hippisley was obliged to sell Ston Easton in order to pay death duties. The house was neglected and fell into disrepair - it was even proposed that the building should be demolished, though it was thankfully saved by a Preservation Order in 1958. In 1964 Ston Easton was purchased by William Rees-Mogg who restored the building. In 1978 Rees-Mogg sold the house to the Smedley family, who further restored the house and grounds and subsequently converted it into a luxurious hotel. The house was sold again in 2001 to Von Essen Hotels, the present owners.

[edit] Industrial Development in South Asia

[edit] Introduction

Industries can be categorised into groups on the basis of Finished Product or Function, which are also the 3 sectors of the economy –

1. Primary or Basic Industries : These industries form the core industries on which the other industries depend for their manufacturing. For example, iron and steel industry and petroleum industry.

2. Secondary or Consumer Industries : These industries process the basic raw materials into primary goods for direct use by the consumers. They include textiles, sugar, paper making etc.

3. Tertiary Industries : These industries provide public utility based services like railways, transport, banking, post and telegraph etc.

[edit] Need for Industrial Development

Industries satisfy man’s needs, provide employment and raise the living standard of people. Manufacturing industries convert raw materials into manufactured products that are useful to man.

In the South Asia, industrial development is required for the following reasons :

1. Many countries including India are predominantly agricultural countries and more than 50-60 per-cent of the population is dependent on agriculture for its livelihood. However, agriculture cannot support the growing population and only industrial development can solve the problem of rising unemployment.

2. Industrial development provides support and strength to the agricultural base.

3. Industries produce goods and equipments which are required for the defence of the countries.

4. Industries are required to keep pace with the fast moving world in terms of advanced technology and know how.

5. Industrial development is needed to make the countries self-reliant and independent for all their needs.

6. In many of the countries, even in India, some of the regions are economically backward whereas others are quite advanced. Expansion of the industries in these backward areas is needed to correct the regional imbalance.

7. Industrial development is required to maintain a favourable balance of trade, i.e. more of exports than imports.

[edit] Factors Required for Industrial Development

1. Raw Materials : The decision regarding location of a particular industrial activity is guided by the availability of raw materials in a particular area. The earliest industries in India developed near the sources of raw material. The nature of raw material also decides the location of industries. For example the perishable raw materials have to be processed without loss of time before manufacturing. That is why sugar mills are located in areas of sugarcane production.

2. Water Supply : Water is required for the development of industries as it is needed in the process of manufacturing, for cleaning, cooling, washing etc. Some of the industries depend heavily on the availability of water for one purpose or the other. These include iron and steel, textile, paper and pulp, chemical, food processing, jute, leather, nuclear power etc. Therefore, these industrial units are located at places where water is easily available.

3. Energy : Energy is required to process the raw materials into manufactured goods. That is why iron and steel industry is usually located near the coal resource, as it uses cooking coal for fuel. Similarly, the electro-metallurgical and electro-chemical industries which require power are located where electricity is easily available.

4. Transport : Transport is an essential prerequisite for industrial development as transport facilities are required to carry raw materials to manufacturing units and finished products to the markets. The availability of transport facilities has led to the development of the industries near the port towns that are linked with rail and road to the hinterland.

5. Labour : The availability of both skilled and unskilled manpower is an important factor in the location of industries. The mobility of labour is also a significant factor. It is because of the mobility of cheap labour that a large number of industries are located in metropolitan cities. Some of the small-scale industries, traditionally associated with labour are glasswork, brasswork, utensils, silk sarees, carpets etc.

6. Market : The existence of market is the ultimate requirement of every industry because whatever is produced needs to be sold. High demand and a satisfactory purchasing power provide impetus to industrial development. For example, heavy chemical industries or machine industries are located in industrial areas because their products are required by other industries of the region. Similarly, petroleum refineries are established near the markets as the transport of crude oil is easier. However refineries using import crude oil are located near the ports.

7. Climate : It plays a significant factor in the location of industries especially agro-based industries. For instance, cotton textile industries have a favourable climate and soil for the growth of cotton. Extreme type of climate, i.e. is either too hot or too cold climate is also not favourable for the location of industries as it not only affects the availability of raw materials but also the efficiency of the workers.

8. The Government : The government has a very significant role in the industrialisation process, especially in the developing and the under-developed countries. The big industries which require large infrastructure and capital are set up by the government. The government makes rules and regulations for running the industries and also provides the basic facilities like land, water and electricity. Further, it is the government which makes policies and takes decisions to protect and encourage indigenous industries.

9. Organisational Skills : Proper and efficient management skills and technical know-how is a must for running an industrial unit. The management must have sound organisational skills to deal with a crisis situation.

10. Capital : Capital is required at every stage of setting up and running an industrial concern. Mostly financiers and capitalists are available in major cities. That is why most of the major cities are industrial centres. These cities have good banking and credit facilities which are necessary for setting up large-scale industries.

[edit] Availability of Major Factors for Industrial Development

INDIA –

Natural Resources :

Mineral-based : India is a land of vast natural resources. It has fairly high reserves of iron ore, coal, mica, manganese, chromite, bauxite and limestone. Almost all of its petroleum is obtained from offshore Bombay High and from Gujrat and Assam. India has one of the largest programmes for renewable energy, such as biogas, biomass, solar, wind and hydropower.

Forest-based : With a wide range of climatic and topographical conditions. India has rich and varied types of forests. India has a forest cover of 6,75,538 sq. km, which constitutes 20.55 per-cent of its geographical area. The Deciduous forests found along the Himalayan foothills and on the north-eastern hilly areas of the Deccan Plateau and the eastern slopes of the Western Ghats are the most important from the commercial point of view and include trees like sal, teak, sandalwood, rosewood and shisham.

Agro-based : Rice is the leading crop, in terms of both area under cultivation and total yield. Wheat ranks next in both area and total yield. Other major grain crops are millets, corn and pulses. A variety of fruits and vegetables are grown in different parts of the country. Crops like sugarcane, cotton, tea and coffee are grown on plantations in Assam, West Bengal, Tamil Nadu, Karnataka and Kerala. India is known as the home of spices and produces a wide variety of spices like black pepper, cardamom, ginger, garlic, turmeric, chilli etc.

Animal-based : India has the world's largest cattle population. But because cattle are sacred to Hindus, the animals are rarely used for meat, except by Muslims and Christians. Farmers keep cattle and water buffaloes for ploughing and for milk. Most commercial milk production comes from water buffaloes. Hides from cattle and water buffaloes are used for leather after the animals have died. Sheep are reared mostly for wool and sheepskin. The production of chickens and eggs is increasing.

Fish-based : India is a major fishing nation. Croakers, mackerel, sardines, sharks, and shrimp are caught in the surrounding seas. People also catch Bombay duck, a small fish that is dried. Bombay duck is eaten either as an appetizer or as a relish with curried food. Rivers yield carp and catfish.

PAKISTAN –

Natural Resources : The Indus and its tributaries provide water for irrigation in Punjab and Sindh provinces. Hydroelectricity accounts for only 18 per-cent of the energy produced, obtained mainly from large dams. Pakistan has substantial deposits of natural gas (mainly at Sui, Baluchistan) and crude oil (chiefly at the Meyal oil field, near Rawalpindi). It has very limited resources of petroleum. Wheat is the chief crop of Pakistan. The farmers also grow cotton, rice, sugar cane, chickpeas, oilseeds, and fruits and vegetables. Canal irrigation has transformed arid lands in Punjab and Sindh to fertile areas and has enhanced crop production. Fishing is an important industry in the coastal regions of Pakistan. Shrimp, sardines, sharks, and other fish are caught in the Arabian Sea. Pakistan exports a considerable amount of fish and shellfish.

NEPAL –

Natural Resources : The mineral resources are small, scattered and under-developed. The important minerals are coal, iron ore, copper, limestone and mica. These minerals are though, virtually untapped. The river systems of Nepal provide immense potential for hydro-electric development. The crops grown include jute, maize, rice, millet, and wheat. A wide variety of trees and bamboo grasses grow in thick forests in this region. Buckwheat is grown at higher altitudes. Tobacco is grown in the Tarai area and cardamom in the eastern hills. Timber represents a valuable resource and a major source of potential revenue.

BHUTAN –

Natural Resources : Bhutan has significant mineral resources, found in its central and southern region. These include slate, sand gravel, quartzite, limestone, marble, coal, dolomite and gypsum. The Chhukha hydel project financed by India is the principal source of Bhutan’s hydro-electric potential. Barley, rice, and wheat are the chief crops grown. Most people in the high mountain areas herd cattle and yaks. Thick forests grow on the rain-drenched southern slopes of the mountains.

BANGLADESH –

Natural Resources : Bangladesh’s principal resources include the fertile soils of the delta region, heavy rainfall and the long growing season. It uses its abundant water supply for production of hydroelectricity and irrigation. It also has large reserves of natural gas and some petroleum deposits. Farmers in almost all parts of the country grow jute, rice, sugar cane, tobacco, and wheat in the rich, wet soil. Shrimps and prawns are the main catch in the seas.

SRI LANKA –

Natural Resources : Sri Lanka is not rich in minerals but it does have some iron ore, graphite, limestone and gems like sapphire and ruby. Since Sri Lanka is an island country, fish are abundant offshore. It has utilized the water resource of its many rivers by building dams to produce hydroelectricity. The chief agricultural products are coconuts, rice, rubber, and tea. Many of the country's farmers grow world-famous Sri Lanka tea.

MALDIVES –

Natural Resources : The only significant natural resource available in Maldives is fish including yellow-fin tuna and skipjack. The total catch is above 134,000 tonnes. Maldives has the highest per capita consumption of fish and fish products in the world. The people grow breadfruit, chilli peppers, coconuts, millet, and sweet potatoes. The women also collect cowrie shells from the shores.

[edit] Major Industries in South Asia

INDIA – (information as of 1991-96)

Andhra Pradesh : The main industries are textile manufacture, sugar-milling, machine tools, pharmaceuticals, cement, chemicals, glass, fertilizers, electronic equipment, heavy electrical machinery, aircraft parts and paper making. There is an oil refinery at Vishakapatnam, where India’s major ship-building yards are situated. In 1994, a major steel plant was built at Vishakapatnam.

Assam : Sericulture and handloom weaving, both silk and cotton are important home industries together with the manufacture of brass, cane and bamboo articles. The main heavy industry is petro-chemicals. There are 3 oil refineries with 1 under construction in 1996. Other industries include manufacturing paper, nylon, electric goods, cement, fertilizers, sugar, jute and plywood products, rice and oil milling.

Bihar : There are 28 industrial areas and 33 industrial estates. Iron, steel and aluminium are produced and there is also an oil refinery. Other important industries are zinc and copper smelting, machine tools, fertilizers, electrical engineering, sugar milling, paper milling, silk spinning, manufacturing explosives, chemicals and cement. There were 85,000 small industries in 1993-94.

Delhi : The modern city is an important industrial centre in India. Industrial units established include factories for the manufacture of razor blades, sports goods, electronic goods, bicycles and parts, plastic and PVC goods including footwear, textiles, chemicals, fertilizers, medicines, hosiery, leather goods, soft drinks and hand tools. There are also metal forging, casting, galvanizing, electroplating and printing enterprises. Some traditional handicrafts like ivory carving, miniature painting, gold and silver jewellery, and paper mache work still flourish.

Gujarat : Gujarat is one of the four most industrialised states. In 1995, there were 176,350 small scale units and (1994) 16,325 factories including 1050 cotton textile factories and 950 rubber, plastic, petroleum and coal product factories. Principal industries are textile, general and electrical engineering, oil refining, fertilizers, petrochemicals, machine tools, automobiles, heavy chemicals, pharmaceuticals, dyes, sugar, soda ash, cement, man-made fibre, salt, sulphuric acid, paper and paper board.

Haryana : Haryana has a large market for consumer goods in neighbouring Delhi. In 1994-95, there were 756 large and medium scale industries and 125,975 small units. The main industries are cotton textiles, agricultural machineries and tractors, woollen textiles, scientific instruments, glass, cement, paper and sugar milling, cars, tyres and tubes, motorcycles, bicycles, steel tubes, engineering goods, electrical and electronic goods. An oil refinery is being set up at Panipat.

Himachal Pradesh : The main source of employment are the forests and their related industries. There are a few factories making turpentine and resin. The state also makes fertilizers, cement, electronic items and TV sets. There is a foundry and a brewery too. Other industries include salt production and handicrafts, including weaving. The state has 142 large and medium units, 23,265 small scale units, 5 industrial estates, 10 industrial areas and 7 electronic complexes.

Jammu and Kashmir : There are 2 central public sector industries and 30 medium scale. There are 35,576 small units (1994-95) employing over 125000. There are industries based on horticulture, traditional handicrafts are silk spinning, wood carving, paper mache and carpet weaving. 750 tonnes of silk cocoons were produced in 1994-95.

Kerala : There are numerous cashew and coir factories. Important industries are rubber, tea, coffee, tiles, automotive tyres, watches, electronics, oils, fertilizers and chemicals, pharmaceuticals, zinc-smelting, sugar, ceramics, rayon, glass, matches, pencils, monazite, limonite, titanium oxide, rare aluminium, electrical goods, paper, shark-liver oil etc. The state has a refinery and a Shipyard at Kochi(Cochin). The number of factories was 15,357 in 1994.

Karnataka : There were 7765 factories in 1994. In 1994-95, there were 163,524 small industries. The Vishveshwaraiah Iron and Steel Works is situated at Bhadravati, while at Bangalore are national undertakings for the manufacture of aircraft, machine tools, telephone, light engineering and electronic goods. Other industries include textiles, vehicle manufacture, cement, chemicals, sugar, paper, porcelain and soap. In addition, much of the world’s sandalwood and its oil is processed. Sericulture is a more important cottage industry.

Maharashtra : Industry is concentrated mainly in Bombay, Pune and Thane. The main groups are chemicals and products, textiles, electrical and non-electrical machinery, petroleum and products, aircraft, rubber and plastic products, transport equipments, automobiles, paper, electronic items, engineering goods, pharmaceuticals and food products. In 1995, there were 26,642 working factories and 176,402 small-scale industries.

Madhya Pradesh : The major industries are steel, aluminium, paper, cement, motor vehicles, ordinance, textiles and heavy electrical equipment. Other industries include sugar, fertilizers, strawboard, vegetable oil, refractories, potteries, textile machinery, steel casting and rerolling, industrial gases, synthetic fibres, drug, biscuit manufacturing, engineering, electronics, optical fibres, plastics, tools, rayon and art silk. The number of heavy and medium industries in the state is 759.

Orissa : 276 large and medium industries are in operation (1993-94), mostly based on minerals, steel, pig iron, ferrochrome, ferromanganese, ferrosilicon, aluminium, cement, automotive tyres and synthetic fibres. Other industries of importance are sugar, glass, paper, fertilizers, caustic soda, salt, industrial explosives, heavy machine tools, textile mills and electronics. Also there were 44,777 small scale industries. Handloom weaving and the manufacture of baskets, wooden articles, silver filigree work and hand woven fabrics are especially well known.

Punjab : Punjab has always been an agricultural state. The main industries are – Cotton textile, Woollen textile, Agricultural implements, Fertilizers, Sports goods, Bicycles, Sewing machines, Paper, Plastic goods, Engineering goods and Hosiery. Electrical fans are also manufactured in Ludhiana district.

Rajasthan : In 1993-94 there were 167,400 small industrial units. Total capital investment (1993-94) was Rs. 13,160 million. Chief manufactures are textiles, cement, glass, sugar, sodium, oxygen and acetylene units, pesticides, insecticides, dyes, caustic soda, calcium, carbide, synthetic fibres, fertilizers, shaving equipments, automobile components, tyres, watches, nylon tyre cords and refined copper. In 1993-94, there were 583 large and medium industries.

Tamil Nadu : The number of working factories was 18,480 in 1994. In 1993-94, there were 178,114 small industries. The biggest central sector project is Salem steel plant. Cotton textiles is one of the major industries. There were 449 cotton textile mills in 1991-92 and many spinning mills supplying yarn to the decentralized handloom industry. Other important industries are cement, sugar, manufacture of textile machinery, power driven pumps, bicycles, electrical machinery, tractors, motor cars, rubber tyres and tubes, bricks and tiles and silk.

Uttar Pradesh : Important large-scale industries in the state are sugar, textile (cotton, woollen and jute), glass, leather and tanning, vanaspati, paper, locomotive, aircraft, sports goods, matches and cement. Over 50 per cent of the total sugar produced in India is produced in Uttar Pradesh.

West Bengal : West Bengal is industrially much advanced. Chief industries are Iron and Steel, Jute goods, Cotton textiles, Silk textiles, Paper, Chemicals, Bicycles, Sugar, Aluminium, Ship-building, Cable, Fertilizers, Engineering goods and Plastic products.

PAKISTAN –

Pakistan has developed some new industries in recent years. It now produces a variety of goods. The major industries are Cotton textiles, Woollen textiles, Cement, Sugar, Paper, Chemicals and Fertilizers. The major industrial centres are Karachi, Lahore, Multan, Faisalabad, Hyderabad, Daudkhel and Taxila.

Service Industries : Service industries are especially important in the largest cities. Wholesale and retail trades are the largest employers. Other service industries include education, finance, government, health care, and transportation.

Manufacturing Industries : Cotton textiles and clothing rank as the nation's leading manufactured products. Industrial output from other major industries also includes refined sugar, vegetable ghee, urea, rubber tubes, electric motors, electrical consumer products (light bulbs, air conditioners, fans refrigerators, freezers, TV sets, radios, and sewing machines) and pharmaceuticals.

NEPAL –

The traditional industries of Nepal include forestry, dairy and cattle rearing. Household industries manufacture butter, cheese, oil and ghee, wool and skins. Some of the modern industries include brick and tile manufacture, paper, jute, carpet making, food grain processing, vegetable oil extraction, sugar refining, molasses and brewing, chemicals, cigarettes and matchworks.

Major industries in Nepal include tourism, carpets, textiles, small rice jute, sugar, and oilseed mills, cigarettes, cement and brick factories. Aside from small-scale food processing (rice, wheat and oil mills), light industry, largely concentrated in southeastern Nepal, includes the production of jute goods, refined sugar, cigarettes, matches, spun cotton and synthetic fabrics, wool, footwear, tanned leather, and tea. The carpet, garment and spinning industries are the three largest industrial employers, followed by structural clay products, sugar and jute processing. Heavy industry includes a steel-rolling mill, which produces stainless steel.

BHUTAN –

Industrial production in Bhutan is mainly based on traditional handicrafts like paper-making, carved lacquer wood woodwork and intricate metal and leather work. Modern manufacturing plants in Bhutan have also been started. These include food and beverage processing, cement manufacturing units etc.

Craft manufacture is the predominant industry, and homespun textiles, woven and embroidered cottons, wools, and silks are the most important products. Other Bhutanese handicrafts include daphne paper, swords, wooden bowls, leather objects, copper, iron, brass, bronze, silver work, wood carvings, and split-cane basketry. Bhutan Ferro Alloys Limited makes export quality ferrosilicon.

BANGLADESH –

Jute processing is the principal industry of Bangladesh. Bangladesh supplies more than 50 percent of the world’s jute. Other important industries include textiles (yarns and fibre products), petroleum products, leather and leather products, cigarettes, steel, cement, fertilizers, chemicals, paper, sugar, tea, matches and glass.

Many Bangladeshis work in their homes making items that are sold in shops or exported. Some craftworkers weave cotton, jute, or silk into cloth. Others make embroidered items, leather goods, pottery, woodenware, and articles of brass, copper, gold, or silver. The people catch large quantities of fish for their own use. Shrimps and prawns from the Bay of Bengal have become valuable exports.

SRI LANKA –

Manufacturing Industries : Major industrial activities include the processing of agricultural goods, including coconuts, rubber, tea and tobacco and the manufacture of textiles. The construction industry employs about 5 per cent of the workers. Other important industries are electronics and components for electronic assembling, industrial and machine tools (a new emphasis), ceramics and glassware, rubber-based industries, and light and heavy engineering.

Industrial development can be seen particularly in textiles, food and beverages, wood products, rubber and plastics, and other consumer goods. Textiles and apparel is the largest industrial sector. Food, beverages and tobacco, the second-largest manufacturing sector, depends more on the domestic market. The third largest industrial sector—chemicals, petroleum and rubber products has been more volatile. Paper industries also grew up in Sri Lanka.

MALDIVES –

Fishing is the major industry in Maldives. One-fifth of the income is derived from tourism. Shipping and garment manufacturing are the other major industries.

Maldivian lacquerwork and finely woven mats are famous for their quality and design. Coconuts, copra, shells, tortoiseshell, bone dust, red stone, ambergris, and handicrafts are also produced locally. Modern industry is limited to tuna canneries and other fish-processing, several apparel factories, built during the past decade, a soft drink bottling plant, and small scale manufacturing enterprises that produce PVC pipe, soap, furniture, and food products. Tourism has been developing since the first resort was built in 1977.

[edit] Constraints in Industrial Development in South Asia

INDIA –

1. Coal deposits, which form a major part of the infrastructure, are very unevenly distributed within different regions of India. Most of the coal deposits are located in the north-eastern parts of India, leading to uneven industrial and economic development.

2. There is heavy wastage of natural resources due to unscientific methods of removal and also due to the lack of suitable processing techniques. Also, the technology used in many of the industries, especially handloom, is obsolete.

3. Indian industries are also losing foreign markets due to the continued increase in the cost of production and the development of industries in a number of Asian countries like China and Japan, whose goods are cheaper and of better quality.

4. Also shortage of raw materials and irregular supply of raw materials has led to the importation of raw materials from other neighbouring countries. This has in turn led to the shortage of capital for investment in industries.

5. Thus in general, the lack of resources, energy, poor health of the labourers, inefficient system of transport in industrial regions, insufficient support from the government, the ever-growing competition from the world-market, lack of capital investment in industries, and primarily, lack of economic development has led to the limitation of industrial development in India.

PAKISTAN –

1. Pakistan is predominantly an agricultural country. Lack of modernization in agriculture has lead to a lower production of crops, and a shortage of raw material for the industries. Many of the farmers own only a small area of land, in which they work with simple tools and teams of oxen or buffalo.

2. Pakistan is still dependent on neighbouring countries like India, China and Japan for its resources, especially petroleum. Also lack of modern techniques in industries has left Pakistan way back in the list of developing countries.

3. Only the major cities in Pakistan have suitable conditions like climate, land (topography), energy, transport etc. for industrialization to take place. This has led to a regional imbalance in the country, preventing further industrialization.

NEPAL –

1. In Nepal, the mineral resources are small, scattered and under-developed. The minerals here are virtually untapped, preventing the growth of industries here.

2. Nepal has a rugged terrain and this terrain creates difficulties in transportation. Also, there is no railroad system in Nepal. The only possible method of transport is the airplanes. Hence, due to insufficient transportation, the minerals do not reach the industries on time.

BHUTAN –

1. Bhutan has only about 10 per-cent of its total land area exploited for agriculture to take place. Also, the labour force is generally engaged in subsidence agriculture. This shortage of land and sufficient labour has left Bhutan economically and industrially backwards.

2. There is no railroad track in Bhutan. Also the roads are undeveloped and improper for transportation.

BANGLADESH –

1. The farmers in Bangladesh use outdated tools and machinery in the farms. Thus, enough cannot be produced for the industries (agro-based). This shortage of raw materials for the industry has left Bangladesh undeveloped.

2. Road building is difficult in Bangladesh because of the need for bridges across the rivers and streams. Many of the country's roads and railways become unusable in the monsoon season. Thus, transportation is difficult here.

SRI LANKA –

1. Sri Lanka is not rich in minerals. Hence, there are hardly any mineral-based industries in Sri Lanka, which has led to a low economy graph. Also the total land area available is very small for major industries to be built.

MALDIVES –

[edit] St James' Church, High Melton

St James’s church dominates the village of High Melton, near Doncaster, in South Yorkshire. Originally built by Avice-de-Tania, (who also founded the Priory at Hampole) in 1153AD, it has been extensively extended.

Until at least the reign of Richard II (1377-1400), the church was known as the "Church of All Saints". About this time, a tower was added and a Lady Chapel to the south-east.

The ground plan is rather unusual, having two aisles - oen through a short nave within the chancel and the Western Tower, and also a continuous south aisle to the whole length of the church.

It has had several generous benefactors over the years, including the Fountayne family and its descendants, the Wilson family, which changed its name to Montagu following an inheritance. There are substantial memorials to members of these families in the Church.

Benefactors helped to improve the state of the Church in the early 20th Century. In particular, there is a spectacular rood screen and 'Corpus Christi' set of figures (1905) and a reredos and altar frontals (1907) which are the work of Sir William Ninian Comper, who also did much work for York Minster.

St James' is also known for its beautiful windows. John Fontayne, DD Dean of York, is stated to have installed several pieces of mediaeval stained glass obtained throughout the county, and also introduced quite a lot of painted glass (by Peckett of York), giving the church a rich inheritance.

The parish registers of High Melton begin in 1538 (this is unusual as in many other parishes the Registers only date back to the beginning of the reign of Elizabeth I, 1558).

It is part of the Diocese of Sheffield, now under the joint Benefice of St Peter's, Barnburgh, with St. James, Melton-on-the-Hill, and holds morning worship every Sunday starting at 9.30am.

[edit] Roller furling

Roller furling refers to systems that furl a headsail by rolling it around the forestay, or around its own luff wire. These systems, which range from very simple to very sophisticated, usually have a revolving drum at the base, worked by means of a line run aft to the cockpit. All systems have a swivel at the head of the sail, which allows the luff (and forestay, if part of the system) to rotate freely under load, although twisting of the luff or forestay can be a problem on simple systems.

The simplest systems consist solely of the drum and swivel. More sophisticated systems have a luff foil, a long hollow fitting that goes around the forestay. The sail is raised in a groove in the foil, and the foil is attached to the drum. When the drum rotates, the foil rotates around the forestay without putting any twisting stress on it. Recently, some vendors have offered continuous loop furlers, where the line runs once around the drum and exits, and is spliced into a continuous loop.

[edit] Cisco StackWise

Cisco StackWise is a technology offered by Cisco that allows for up to nine Cisco Catalyst 3750 series switches to operate as though one 32-Gbps switch. This allows for greater resiliency, and performance.

One switch from the stack will act as the master switch. The master switch will maintain the stack and allow you to configure and monitor the whole stack as though one via a single console.

If one switch fails the remaining switches will continue to operate by looping back any information that would normally traverse the failed switch, effectively bypassing it. If the master switch fails, the next switch in the stack will automatically take over as master. This feature means greater redundancy, as one switch's failure will not bring about a failure of the entire stack.

[edit] Arlington Heights v. Metropolitan Housing Corp

Arlington Heights v. Metropolitan Housing Corp. 429 U.S. 252 (1977) In this case the village of Arlington Heights, Illinois refused to rezone property so that low-income housing could be built. The developers seeking zoning changes sued, saying that the decision not to rezone violated the equal protection clause of the Fourteenth Amendment to the Constitution. The theory of the case was one of discriminatory impact. The plaintiffs relied on Yick Wo and Gomillion v. Lightfoot, in which plaintiffs succeeded in challenging laws that were written in racially neutral terms, but that severely detrimented one racial group. The court has never recognize racial impact as an adequate basis for a constitutional violation all by itself, but has always required that the plaintiffs prove a discriminatory intent. However, in Yick Wo and Gomillion, the court accepted an extremely disproportionate impact as evidence proving a discriminatory intent. The plaintiffs in this case asserted that the disparate racial impact of the zoning proved that the city intended to discriminate against racial minorities.

The SCOTUS dealt with two significant issues: First, whether the plaintiffs had standing. Second, whether the zoning was in violation of equal protection. Regarding the second issue, the court stated that in order to use disparate impact alone to prove discrimination, they plaintiffs must show a "clear pattern, unexplainable on grounds other than race." Ultimately the court ruled against the plaintiffs. This case reaffirmed the principle articulated in Washington v. Davis, that discriminatory impact was not a per se violation of equal protection, but merely evidence of discriminatory intent. While appearing to allow considerable latitude for the use of statitstical evidence of discrimination, in application Arlington Heights and similar cases almost entirely foreclose using discriminatory impact to prove intent. Ironically, many of the most fervent opponents of the use of statistics to prove racial discrimination end up using those statistics themselves to support overturning affirmative action. See Grutter, Rehnquist J dissenting.

[edit] Robert L. Howard

Colonel Robert L. Howard U.S. Army Special Forces is one of America's most decorated soldiers. He served five tours in Vietnam. Colonel Howard is the only soldier to be nominated for the Congressional Medal of Honor three times for three separate actions within a thirteen month period.

He received a direct appointment from Master Sergeant to 1st Lieutenant in 1969, and was awarded the Medal of Honor by President Richard M. Nixon at the White House in 1971. His other awards for valor include the Distinguished Service Cross, the Silver Star and eight Purple Hearts. He received his decorations for valor for actions while serving as an NCO (Sergeant First Class).

Robert L. Howard grew up in Opelika, Alabama and enlisted in the U.S. Army in 1956 at age seventeen. He retired as a full Colonel in 1992 after 36 years service. During Vietnam, Howard was a veteran long-range reconnaissance team leader when he served in the U.S. Army Special Forces (Green Berets) and spent most of his five tours in the super-secret MACV-SOG (Military Assistance Command Vietnam Studies and Observations Group) also known as Special Operations Group, which ran classified cross-border operations into Laos, Cambodia, and North Vietnam during the war. The sixty-man recon company at Kontum, in which he served, was the Vietnam War's most highly decorated unit of its size awarded five Medals of Honor. It was for his actions while serving on a mission to rescue a fellow soldier in Cambodia, that he was submitted for the Medal of Honor the third time for his extraordinary heroism.

In later years, he served as chief of the Special Forces Qualification Course at Fort Bragg, N.C.; and his last command was chief of the Special Operations Command in Korea. The Medal of Honor was awarded to 17 Special Forces soldiers during the Vietnam War eight of them posthumously. Howard was the last Special Forces recipient still on active duty when he retired on Sept. 29, 1992.

[edit] George Cox (Cincinnati Politician)

George Cox (1853-1916) was a powerful member of the Hamilton County, Ohio Republican party who controlled a political machine in that county's main city, Cincinnati.

Cox began as a barkeeper in the 1870s, but was soon furnishing illigal and fictitious voters for local candidates he favored, most of whom were Republicans resentful of the Democratic party's control of the city government. Cox ran for and was elected to the Cincinnati city council in 1879. Almost immediately he became a hugely influential member of Hamilton County's Republican party.

With the city council later falling into Republican hands, Cox built his political machine on the types of typical election fraud, graft, and bribery that typically accompany such machines. His control of Cincinnati's politics was so complete that by the 1890s, a Republican essentially had to have Cox's blessing to be elected. To neutralize the Democrats, Cox arranged for 40% of the city's appointed jobs to be held by the opposition party, preventing true opposition from coalescing in a Democratic candidate. As Cox himself one said: "The people do the voting. I simply see that the right candidates are selected."

But Cox's control of the city began to fall apart after he encouraged the city to annex some of the nearby independent parts of Hamilton County. Many residents of these areas were middle-class, and saw Cox's political machine as seedy and undesirable. Cox found he had much greater difficulty controlling this newer, larger group of voters, and in 1911 Cox's candidate for mayor lost the election. Cox, who had always promised more favors than he cpuld realistically deliver, never regained his power.

Cox suffered a stroke in 1916, and died in May of that year when he had another, fatal stroke.

[edit] That Feeling, You Can Only Say What It Is in French

That Feeling, You Can Only Say What It Is in French is the 11th story in Stephen King's Everything's Eventual: 14 Dark Tales, a collection of 14 short stories written by King and published in 2002.

[edit] Plot

Spoiler warning: Plot and/or ending details follow.

The story focuses on Carol Shelton, a woman in her fifties who is going on a second honeymoon with her husband Bill. As they're driving to the house they rented in Florida, Carol gets a strong feeling of déjà vu, which she's been feeling since they were on their chartered plane. She is obsessively predicting what she'll see next on the side of the road as they approach the house, but for the most part her predictions are inaccurate. As they draw near, she wakes up and finds she's still on the plane. Things progress again as they had in her dream, and this time her dream seems to be coming true. She becomes panicked as they approach the house for a second time, and once again she wakes up to discover it had been a dream. She realizes that she is in fact dead, and is reliving the same period of time indefinitely.

King's note, at the end of the story, reads: " I think this story is about Hell. A version of it where you are condemned to do the same thing over and over again. Existentialism, baby, what a concept; paging Albert Camus. There's an idea that Hell is other people. My idea is that it might be repetition."

[edit] [ [Mary Durack (nee Costello)] ]

Mary Durack (nee Costello) married Patrick Durack in 1862. He was one of the great pioneer pastoralists who crossed uncharted lands to the Cooper’s Creek area of Queensland in the 1860s, and later worked his way overland to settle in the Kimberley District of Western Australia. While his wife is not given a place in the Australian Dictionary of Biography, she deserves a place in history, being an archetypal colonial wife, surviving all the difficulties of the outback. She gave unstinting support to her adventurous husband, who was often away for long periods, and she reared to adulthood six of her eight children. Mary Durack, her grand-daughter, gives an insight into her story in Kings in Grass Castles. While her inclination was for a settled suburban life she showed endurance and resourcefulness when travelling with small children through harsh lands. She experienced drought and flood, having at times to live off the land, economic depression, loneliness and fear of hostile Aborigines. She died in 1893, a loved and respected matriarch. Miami Lakes Educational Center, commonly referred to as MLEC, or Miami Lakes Tech (the school's previous name was Miami Lakes Technical Education Center, having been shortened when the high school division opened) is a vocational education school along with a secondary school located at 5780 NW 158th Street, in Miami Lakes, Florida, off of NW 57th Avernue.

MLEC HS was built in 1998 and opened its doors in 2000; the school was built to serve students from all over Region 1 of the Miami-Dade County Public School, which serves American Senior High, Hialeah High School, Barbara Goleman Senior High, Hialeah-Miami Lakes High School, and Miami Carol City Senior High School. MLEC HS is a school of choice.

MLEC is one of only three area vocational-technical training centers. It is one of the two dual-delivery schools in Miami-Dade County, providing adult vocational training in conjunction with a full-service high school. This allows MLEC's high school students to attend regular academic classes and technical or vocational training programs during the same school day on the same school campus. At time of graduation, high school students can receive both a high school diploma and a certificate of completion from the vocational training program in which they participated.

Between 2006 and 2010, all high schools in the Miami-Dade County Public School System will shift over to the Collegiate-Honors System at which time the Valedictorian, Salutatorian, and Top 10 system will be abolished. Instead, Cum Laude, Magna Cum Laude and Summa Cum Laude honors will be awarded to the top members of each class. The Class of 2008 will be the last class to be designated a Valedictorian, Salutatorian, and Top 10 students list. The change came about as a complete overhaul of the high school curriculum system modelled after the New York Public School System and implemented by the school board and superintendent. The system is already in place at the newly built John A. Ferguson High School and will be implemented at the new Ronald W Reagan Doral High School beginning Fall of 2006. The system will be gradually applied to every high school in the system over the next four years.

The high school has no athletic teams.

3.... That we may take the lamp of research into the dark recesses of things unknown, and make our contribution to our fellow man.

Today, Rho Pi Phi is a co-ed fraternity that welcomes both men and women of good character who are dedicated to the profession of pharmacy.
