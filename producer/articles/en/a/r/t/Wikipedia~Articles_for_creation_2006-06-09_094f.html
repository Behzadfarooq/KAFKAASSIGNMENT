[edit] White Bear, Saskatchewan

White Bear, Saskatchewan is a small hamlet located approximately 55 miles northwest of Swift Current, SK on the north side of the South Saskatchewan River. Its name comes from the sighting of a probable but now extirpated white prairie grizzly bear[1] by an Assiniboine warrior on the shores of a neighboring lake during the Palliser Expedition of the 1850’s. Records from early Metis settlers and the NWMP state the last roaming herd of American buffalo being slaughtered in the hills of the Missouri Coteau located 25 miles northeast around 1879.

During the 1930’s White Bear was a bustling community of approximately 250 residents with two grocery stores, a school, four elevators and three garages servicing an area of 200 families, but has since dwindled to a population of 13 in 2006. Part of the death is attributed to federal policy changes regarding agriculture, the routing of the No. 4 Highway through the neighboring town of Kyle, and the later abandonment of the CN Rail line in that area of Saskatchewan. It is interesting to note that the region rarely suffered poor crops, except during the droughts of the Great Depression and 1988. It is connected to the rest of the province through Highway 342, albeit a now-decrepit road featuring signs with Imperial units in portions. Farmers from the area played prominently in the socialism that would later define Saskatchewan then Canada through the introduction of Medicare [2] and state-ran insurance[3]. The White Bear Hotel remains the only business in operation, noted for its hot wings and hospitality.

[edit] E. R.G. Eckert

Eckert was born in 1904 in Prague, where he studied at the German Institute of Technology. He developed methods for jet engine turbine blade cooling at a research laboratory in Prague. He emigrated to the United States after the World War II. He authored more than 500 articles and received several awards for his contributions to science. His book "Introduction to the Transfer of Heat and Mass," published in 1937, is still considered a fundamental text in the field. He had many students including R. J. Goldtein and his last students G. Bar-Meir.

[edit] Vicki Hyde

Vicki Hyde is a science author, editor and co-owner with her husband Peter of a New Zealand-based software and web development company, Webcentre Ltd.

Hyde is best known as the Managing Editor of the popular science portal site ScitechDaily, which she began as a sister site to Arts & Letters Daily soon after the latter was launched in 1998 by her friend, Denis Dutton.

Hyde's company also worked with Dutton in 1998 to launch the Cybereditions Ebook and print-on-demand publishing operation, which Webcentre owns outright.

Hyde was also founder and Managing Editor of New Zealand Science Monthly magazine which was published for the full decade from 1990 to 2000. During that time she authored Godzone Skies, Astronomy for New Zealanders and helped launch and market Webcentre's flagship TurboNote software.

She is the long-standing Chair-Entity of the New Zealand Skeptics (NZ Committee for Scientific Investigation of Claims of the Paranormal, Inc) and in 2006 will be publishing a book on paranormal phenomena and investigation in New Zealand.

In her leisure time, Hyde can be found in an alternate persona as the Baroness of Southron Gaard, a branch of the international Society for Creative Anachronism.

[edit] six basic sentence pattern

[edit] Sources

The Sentence: Six Basic Patterns Built On Verb Types Basic sentence patterns are built around verbs. There are six different verb types that are followed by structures always associated with those verbs. Therefore, there are six basic sentence patterns, which are also called sentence nuclei. Once these basic patterns are understood, other structures may be attached to the sentence nuclei to form larger sentences. These other structures added to the sentence nuclei are usually phrases or clauses that function as nouns, verbs, adjective and adverbs.

1. The first verb type is called "intransitive." This type of verb can end the sentence, or it can be followed by an adverb phrase. Intransitive verbs do not need to be followed by noun phrases or adjective phrases.

1a. The president spoke.

1b. Margo slept.

1c. Margo slept soundly.

1d. The small boy jumped from the high oak tree.

1e. Jimmy went to London.

2. The second verb type is called "linking." This type of verb must be followed by noun phrases or adjective phrases, which refer to the same person or thing as the subject of the sentence. Some typical linking verbs are become, appear, grow, remain, stay, and seem. Some other linking verbs involving the senses are feel, look, smell, sound, and taste. Note that the forms of the verb "be" are placed in a special category below, because forms of "be," unlike other linking verbs, can be followed immediately by an "adverb of place."

2a. Clark Kent became Superman.

2b. The president looked weary.

2c. Jamal remained an honest man.

3. The third verb type is the word "be" and all its forms, which is a special category of linking verb. The verb "be," unlike other linking verbs, can be followed by a phrase functioning as an "adverb of place," in addition to the noun phrases or adjective phrases that follow other types of linking verbs. The word "be" has eight forms: be, is, am, are, was, were, been, being.

3a. The student is a math whiz.

3b. Marilyn was insecure.

3c. The new houses are around the corner. (The verb is followed by a phrase functioning as an adverb of place.)

4. The fourth verb type is "one-place transitive." This verb must be followed by a noun phrase that functions as a direct object. Often the subject "does something" to this object. Sentences with transitive verbs can usually be turned into passive sentences. Note that some intransitive verbs can also be used as transitive verbs.

4a. The office manager typed the letter.

4b. Satellites monitor the country's military bases.

4c. The country's military bases are monitored by satellites. (Sentence 4b is changed to a passive sentence.)

5. The fifth verb type is "Vg two-place transitive." This "Vg" verb can be followed by one of two structures. In the first pattern, the verb is followed by two noun phrases, with the first noun phrase functioning as an indirect object, and the second functioning as a direct object. The alternate sentence pattern is related to the first, but the first noun phrase is a direct object, and the second is an indirect object placed in a prepositional phrase introduced by "to" or "for," or more rarely by "of." Sentences formed from "Vg" verbs must always have both direct and indirect objects. Although indirect objects are usually living things, they can also be inanimate objects.

5a. Dennis bought Ann some fancy flowers.

5b. Scientists fed some nutritious food to the laboratory rats.

5c. The Governor gave the schools additional money.

5d. The senator asked a question of the Supreme Court Justice.

6. The sixth verb type is "Vc two-place transitive." This "Vc" verb is followed by a noun phrase that functions as a direct object, plus another noun phrase, adjective phrase, or infinitive phrase that functions as a complement. The term "complement" refers to a structure that completes a phrase or clause.

6a. Republicans consider Democrats big spenders.

6b. Excessive tax cuts make some people uncomfortable.

6c. Some historians believe Franklin Roosevelt to be our most effective president.

[edit] Attraction to Disability

Sexual deviations like acrotomophilia (amelotasis), apotemnophilia, abasiophilia, teratophilia and the desire for partners with other disabilities.

Resume

An overview of disability paraphilias. Terms: devotees (disability paraphiliacs), pretenders (ones pretending they have disabilities), wannabes (ones desiring to become disabled). Keywords: acrotomophilia, amelotasis, amputation, amputee(s), apotemnophilia, BIID (Body Integrity Identity Disorder), body dysmoprphia, devotees, disability devotees, disability fetishism, disability wannabes, disability pretenders, fetish, fetishism, pretenders, wannabes.

History

Allusions to the attraction are found in Herodotus, works by Breugel the Elder, Michel de Montaigne, Pushkin, Bulgakov, Dali, Brecht, von Stroheim, Bunuel and James Dean. It has become better known since the late 19th Century and the 1960s’ Sexual Revolution. A community of disability devotees is emerging in the internet.

Nature

Devotees desire sexual partners with disabilities, mostly amputations. An invisible pale desires partners with developmental and intellectual disabilities.

Over half of all devotees feel the attraction since childhood. Some cannot attribute its emergence to a specific occasion. Others cherish memories of objects of their future attention. About a quarter of devotees discover the attraction in puberty, and some do so after sexual maturity.

Each devotee has strictly defined preferences. Thus, most acrotomophiles feel weak desire or no desire for partners with other disabilities. There are plentiful additional definitions for each devotee on matters such as whether the disability was acquired or congenital, et c.

The desire to acquire a disability may be viewed as a peculiar variation of the attraction. Called wannabes, those who experience it suffer from Body Integrity Identity Disorder (BIID) or body dysmorphia (apotemnophilia if they desire to become amputees) and appear to number not more than one in 20 of all devotees.

That wannabeism is a logical extension of devoteeism is an assumption. The similarities and differences between devotees and wannabes are hotly debated in the devotee community.

Population

By mid-2006, the virtual world devotee community comprised some 100,000 members of dedicated internet fora. The uneven distribution of internet, the passivity of most members of each community, and the probable ‘grey zone’ of those who belong to the community only partially may bear out assumptions that there are about a third of a million devotees worldwide.

There are indications that while in most countries there is one or fewer devotees per million population, in First World nations the incidence rises to about 100 devotees per million.

By sex over nine in every ten devotees are male, yet the subculture does contain female devotees. By sexual orientation, the share of homosexual and bisexual devotees appears identical to that in the general population.

An appropriate glance at the population of people with disabilities shows them as predominantly elderly: up to 80 per cent of them are over the retirement age. According to different data and thresholds, disabled people form up to a tenth of the population. Delving further, amputees are not more than one per cent of disabled people, and over 80 per cent of them are men. Leg amputations predominate at almost 80 per cent of this total, with above-knee ones accounting for under 20 per cent of the subtotal.

Behaviour

For devotees, a disability does not reduce a potential partner’s attractiveness but significantly boosts it. No cases are known in which a devotee has physically harmed a partner with a disability or caused disability to an able-bodied partner. Wannabes regularly engage in self-harm, often with fatal outcomes.

It appears that a fair number of devotees succeed in establishing the relationships they desire. It is often claimed that such relationships are ordinary, the attraction being sated by the mere presence of a disability. There appear, however, to be certain peculiarities in such relationships. Successful devotees often go through a series of relationships: something explained by the fact that sexual satiety may not necessarily satisfy broader emotional needs (similar patterns exist among other sexual minorities such as some gay men). Also, devotees may press their disabled partners to put their disabilities to the forefront in intimate situations and exhibit them in social ones.

Over half of all devotees fail to establish their desired relationships. Those who have not become aware of themselves as devotees often harbour guilty thoughts and feel themselves to be unique. Over the past decade, a number of devotees have opened websites with images of their ideal partners (often retouched photographs of able bodied people). Other sites carry descriptions of fictional encounters between devotees and desired partners, drawings of such partners, and devotee pornography (often produced in cooperation with disabled people).

Many devotees direct their desires into creative pursuits. Ukrainian theatre director Andriy Zholdak produces plays with amputees, British sculptor Mark Quinn displays statues of amputees on Trafalgar Square in London, art photographers Gerhard Aba, Eric Croll and Yuriy Solomenko photograph amputees, directors produce films like Crime Delicado and Boxing Helena with amputees, balletomanes produce dance shows with amputees.

Some devotees collect personal data about desired persons, follow and photograph them candidly, engineer seemingly spontaneous meetings with them, call them by telephone, and seek them out in healthcare establishments and civic gatherings of disabled people. An unknown number of devotees hold jobs providing contact with disabled people. Others are members of societies, sporting clubs, et c., of people with disabilities. Collecting objects linked with disability is a pastime for some devotees. Pretending is another expression of the paraphilia. Some devotees derive sexual pleasure by pretending to have a disability. Others persuade partners to pretend. Pretending usually takes place in privacy or intimacy, but may occasionally be practiced in public. There have been complaints that, in dedicated devotee chatrooms, male devotees en masse pretend to be women with disabilities; if true, this would suggest the broadest aptitude to pretending among devotees. The putative continuum between non-pretending devotees, pretenders, and wannabes has not been researched.

Online, devotees exchange descriptions of sightings of disabled people, experiences of meetings with them, biographies of famous people with disabilities, techniques for attracting their attention, and opinions on acceptable/unacceptable devotee behaviour. There are attempts to condemn the objectification of disabled people. Wannabe fora are dominated by enquiries as to which doctors may be willing to perform elective amputations of healthy limbs.

The devotee community has a growing English-based internal terminology which emerged in the 1930s. The word ‘devotee,’ adopted in the early 1980s due to its alliteration with ‘amputee’ is entering research usage.

Explanations

The attraction is rarely visited by scientific literature and has not been subjected to rigorous research. While depriving interested parties of information, this suggests that devotees have not caused concern among disabled people and the public. In contrast, there is much interest and many publications on the much smaller number of wannabes, indicating significant public concern. Some explanations are at odds with each other, yet a number are repeated by all relevant sides.

Psychiatric

Psychiatry ranks devotees alongside sadomasochistic fetishists, though aside from their mainstream:

1. Devotees are suppressed homosexuals. This first explanation (by Krafft-Ebbing in his 1872 Psychopathia Sexualis) rests on the assumption that amputation stumps resemble phalli and is in line with the contemporaneous linking of sexual oddity with homosexuality;

2. Devotees are sadists who consider disabled people as having a lower status, from which they derive satisfaction;

3. Devotees are masochists who seek self-denigration by associating with disabled partners;

4. Devotees are voyeurs intrigued by the unusual behaviour and appearance of disabled people;

5. Wannabes are exhibitionists seeking satisfaction in the reaction of other people;

As regards points 2 to 5 above, psychology views sadism and masochism as interchangeable, with voyeurism and exhibitionism as their respective aspects. Devotees’ preference for exhibitionist-minded partners would seem to support these explanations. Further explanations include:

6. Teratophilia: the desire for deformed partners;

7. Classical fetishism: shifting the focus of desire from a person to objects. Here, it could be expected that crutches and other disability aids would feature among devotees’ sexual foci, and they do indeed feature in devotee websites;

8. Coprophilia: the fetish for excreta; since amputation stumps may be interpreted as resembling stools, devotees are assumed to be coprophiliacs.

  Sexological

Sexologists do not consider the paraphilia a deviation unless exercising it infringes the rights of one of the parties and/or those of third parties. Explanations include:

1. Imprinting: the impact of influential events on sexual behaviour. Meeting visibly traumatised people awakes strong emotions. In children, they could give rise to quasi-logical reasoning and a desire for people with the type of trauma encountered.

2. Implied parental approval: if, when encountering a disabled person, a future devotee’s parents express admiration, infantile logic may conclude that disability is admirable, later ranking it among sexual preferences;

3. Flight from pressure: strict parenting requirements for good behaviour and academic or sporting achievements may cause the future devotee to seek respite in sickness and disability. With time, the desire to become disabled is ‘projected’ onto others. The analogy with the Munchhausen Syndrome (self-inducing illness as a way to obtain compassion and benefits) in 11 above is plain in wannabes, where projection has failed, leaving them to see themselves as more attractive with a disability. The fact that most devotees feel the attraction from childhood also backs the above explanations.

4. Transvestism and transsexuality are put forward as explanations for pretenders and wannabes.

5. The “Mars-Venus” explanation put forward by Anne Hooper (“The Amputee Fetish,” Forum, Penthouse Publications, London, June 1978) states that all men see all women as ‘amputees’ and vice-versa, the physical difference aiding attraction. Devoteeism could be regarded as a version of this;

6. “Darwinian Theory”: devotees see disabled partners as proven in natural selection, having already cheated death.

  Of Devotees

The devotee community constantly debates the origins of the paraphilia. The Amelotatist: A Statistical Profile (Ampix [ex.], Lawndale, California, 1979), a survey setting out the results of a poll among some 400 devotees is an internal contribution and the first synthesis of many explanations. Current internal community explanations include:

1. Devoteeism as an unusual variant of physical criteria which everyone has for potential partners;

2. Disabled people perform a number of actions in original ways which capture onlookers the same way a ballerina captures audiences;

3. Devoteeism as extreme curiosity; everyone is curious about the appearance, sensation and residual functions of bodies affected by disability;

4. Devoteeism is a sexualised extension of normal human compassion;

5. It is possible that each devotee became one for a variety of personal motivations rarely replicated in other cases;

6. Devoteeism is not sexual as such, since it is often felt prior to puberty. As regards 6 above, the Freudian view is that sexual preferences (paraphilias included) are indeed formed in childhood.

7. Devoteeism is programmed so as to provide partners for disabled people;

8. Religious, occult, and esoteric explanations include hypotheses that devoteeism is sent from a higher force to test devotees and improve them by means of denial or that it is evidence of past lives.

  Wannabe Explanations

Wannabes have not formulated explanations for the discomfort they feel, other than those listed above. A formulation doubtless borrowed from transsexuals is that for reasons unknown the soul of one with a disability sometimes finds its way into an able body and desires adjustment to the physical reality.

  Of Disabled People

Since the disabled community is broad and diverse, explanations here emerge from that portion of it which is aware of devoteeism and whose members have in some cases adhered to the devotee community. Generally, they support devotee explanations, but with a deeper distinction between ‘devotees’ and a denigrated category of ‘fetishists’ who engage in aforementioned unacceptable and/or intrusive behaviour.

Standpoints

  Of Devotees

Devotees reject scientific approaches to the paraphilia on the grounds that scientific enquiry would throw an unhealthy shadow over them. Devotees are minded to move towards coalescing as a lifestyle community after the fashion of the gay community; today, scientific enquiry concerning the latter is generally viewed as unnecessary and discriminatory. Similarly to the gay community which rejected the term ‘homosexuality’ in favour of ‘gayness,’ devotees reject terms like ‘amelotasis’ and ‘apotemnophilia’ in favour of internal ones like ‘devoteeism.’

The community harbours conflicting views on wannabes. While pretending, until recently condemned, is today accepted as an unedifying fact of devotee life, many devotees feel wannabes have no place in their community. Devotees claim that the attraction appears deviant only from the unacceptable position that disabled people are intrinsically unattractive. Other claims are honed to present the attraction in a better light, such as the statement that devotees support the civic movement of people with disabilities.

  Of People with Disabilities

Despite the proliferation of devotee websites and fora, most disabled people remain unaware of devoteeism. Those newly-introduced to it often react with shock and incomprehension. Nevertheless, hundreds of disabled people offer photographs and video clips of themselves for sale to devotees. This may be interpreted as approval, but also as economic accommodation. If membership of devotee fora is a sign of approval, by mid-2006, there was one disabled person for every ten devotee members of such fora.

Among disabled people aware of devoteeism, positive opinions are that the two sides were “made for each other.” They state that disabled people ought not to be seen as a-priori unattractive and devoid of sexuality, and that devotees may play a positive role in this regard (Inside, The Outsiders Club, London, 1979 et seq.).

Neutral opinions of disabled people aware of devoteeism are that everything in a relationship with a devotee is entirely ordinary. Negative opinions focus on the aforementioned unacceptable behaviours. A fundamental criticism concerns the inevitable stress placed by devotees on the difference between people with disabilities and able-bodied people. This is seen as a brake on integration, an aspect of which is public ‘blindness’ as to such differences.

  Of Observers

An issue raised by feminists (though political correctness hampers its airing to some extent) is the disparity of motivations. In a putative relationship, devotees would first seek physical satiety, while their potential partners would seek satisfaction of a significantly broader range of human aspirations. This disparity ceases to be a consideration only in a purely sexual contact, yet there one ought to bear in mind possible inequalities between devotees and their partners. The major issue of wannabes is rather less abstract. It concerns medical and general ethics, as well as a number of legislations which prohibit self-harm.

Treatment

Literature describes therapeutic consultations with devotees, but does not describe therapeutic procedures undertaken, or their outcomes. Psychiatry considers devoteeism a deviation which can be treated by psychoanalysis, psychotherapy, counselling, or aversion therapy. Sexology does not consider the mainstream aspect of devoteeism a deviation and does not prescribe treatments. The devotee community is against therapeutic intervention for devotees but is possibly in favour if it with wannabes.

[edit] Genetic Counsellor

Genetic counselling translates scientific knowledge into practical information. Providers of genetic counselling have a strong background in biology, and in particular, genetics. Genetic counsellors are health professionals with specialized education, training, and experience in medical genetics and counselling. They help families understand birth defects and how inheritance works. They provide information to help families make personal decisions about pregnancy and child care. A genetic counsellor works with a person or family who may be at risk for inherited disease or an abnormal pregnancy outcome by discussing the chances of having children who are affected. When you go to see a genetic counsellor, he or she will record your medical background and family medical history. The counsellor may arrange appointments for blood tests, physical exams, or amniocentesis in order to put together a picture of how your family's health may affect your children. Genetic counsellors also help you interpret medical information about any risks present and explain the role of genetics for these conditions. Often, they can determine the risk of occurrence or recurrence of a condition and the availability of tests for it.

[edit] Nobilissima visione - Suite for Orchestra

Composed in 1938, Nobilissima visione was premiered by the Ballet Russe de Monte Carlo under the composer’s direction at Covent Garden, London, on July 21, 1928, with choreography by Massine.

Scoring: 2 flutes (one doubling piccolo), 2 oboes, 2 clarinets, 2 bassoons, 4 horns, 2 trumpets, 3 trombones, timpani, percussion (snare drum, tenor drum, bass drum, glockenspiel, triangle, cymbals, and small cymbals), and strings.

Aside from Hindemith’s highly developed conception of the composer’s role as a social one, involving a moral duty to provide music that people can use and enjoy, the all-round character of his musicianship—he acquired a degree of competence in playing practically all of the orchestral instruments—suggests a return to the practice of the 18th century, when composers were normally skilled executants too. This tendency is in one sense reflected in his music, in which counterpoint often regains the central role it filled until the late baroque period. But it was perhaps in the sphere of harmony, where he developed a method admittedly less influential on other composers than that of his older contemporary Schoenberg but better fitted for the creative tasks he set himself. The great merit of his system is that, unlike Schoenberg’s 12-tone method, it is based on a natural rather than an arbitrary division of the octave: he treated notes as harmonics ultimately derived from one fundamental, grading chords from strong to weak according to how near their constituent notes were to the fundamental.

The ease and fluency attainable through such methods are clearly to be heard in a score like Nobilissima visione, which postdates the composer’s early period as a kind of enfant terrible, and instead calls to mind the warmth, underlying serenity, and, indeed, nobility of such familiar works as the symphony he similarly drew from his opera Mathis der Maler. The Nobilissima visione suite consists, the composer explained, “of those sections of the ballet score which are self-sufficient and comprehensible as concert music and which do not depend, therefore, on supplementary stage action.” His description of these sections is as follows:

The introduction consists of that part of the original music during which the hero of the action (St. Francis of Assisi) is sunk in deep meditation. The Rondo corresponds to the music in the stage score for the mystic union of the Saint to Mistress Poverty, the scene having been inspired by an old Tuscan legend. The music reflects the blessed peace and unworldly cheer with which the guests at the wedding participate in the wedding feast—dry bread and water only. The second movement pictures the march of a troop of medieval soldiers. First heard but distantly, their gradual approach is observed. The middle portion of this movement suggests the brutality with which these mercenaries set upon a traveling burgher and rob him. The third and closing movement, Passacaglia, corresponds to the portion of the ballet score representing the Hymn to the Sun. Here all the symbolic personifications of heavenly and earthly existence mingle in the course of the different variations through which the six-measure-long theme of the Passacaglia is transformed. In the ballet this closing piece bears a special title borrowed from a chapter heading in an old version of the Cantique du Soleil, which reads, “Incipiunt laudes creaturarum”—“Here begin the praises of all creatures.”

Perhaps the loveliest part of the whole score is the contemplative Pastorale that concludes the second movement, based on a theme remarkable for its combination of poetry and elegance. This melody exemplifies the rhythmic suppleness and the expressive, but never excessive, chromaticism at the heart of Hindemith’s finest music.

[edit] Lydiate Hall

Before the fifteenth century manor houses needed to be fortified in case of attack to do this they had outer walls, a moat, a drawbridge and a portcullis. Early drawings of manor houses illustrate this. Lydiate hall itself shows evidence of a moat. The windows were small as well. This changed in Elizabethan times when houses developed to have big, high windows with a symmetrical building. An example being Hardwick house – 1590. Lydiate hall doesn’t show evidence of this. Manor houses were usually 2 to 3 stories high built around an open courtyard. The manor houses built before the fifteenth century and were built from wood and so the kitchen was situated away from the main house to prevent the spread of fire. The main living room had different uses including eating, sleeping and recreation. The great hall at Lydiate reproduced from Rimmer in 1852 shows the large room with a table for eating and a fireplace. This source is reliable because other manor houses show the same styles of great halls from that period.. Rufford old hall’s great hall shows exuberance of decoration and five hammerbeam trusses it also shows a table and fireplace. A bed can also be seen to the left of the picture.

During the fifteenth century threat of attack was lessened and so fortification was lessened. The kitchen also became part of the house because the roof was vaulted to prevent the spread of fire. Manor houses were built from brick, stone or half-timber. There was more space for private bedrooms or receptions. A porch was built and a barn for animals. Pictures from the guidebook of Lydiate hall from this period are useful to historians because it shows what manor houses looked like during the fifteenth century and they can compare the pictures from earlier illustrations and compare the sources to see how they have developed.

During the early sixteenth century timber became expensive and houses were built from stone or brick. They had very large windows like those of Hardwick house. The houses became a lot grander and spacious. Pictures of Speke hall show it made these changes. The house was rented out and used as a farmhouse. Houses during Tudor times were still based around an open courtyard i.e. they were quadrangular. The Speke hall guidebook shows a picture of the house designed around an open courtyard and the plans of the house also illustrate this clearly. Victoria county history describes Lydiate hall to be quadrangular around an open courtyard as well. So there is sufficient evidence to support this.

Lydiate hall was typically tudur showing evidence of bay windows and chimneys being introduced. The floor was made from wood and staircases were becoming more common in houses. Pictures of Lydiate hall’s great hall show a staircase. Timber framed bricks were introduced. Other houses like Rufford old hall and Speke hall show these developments as well. This proves that Lydiate hall did develop so it is useful to historians. Hardwick hall is a late Tudor house built during the reign of the last Tudor monarch Elizabeth I. This shows typical Tudor symmetry and large high windows and a high building.

The plans of 1637 show the layout of Lydiate Hall before the demolition of the east wing in the 18th century. It is therefore useful to historians who can look at how the layout of the manor house changed considering it is in ruins now.

During the 17th century manor houses became even larger and very symmetrical however Lydiate doesn’t show evidence of this and so shows it did not develop along with other manor houses like Speke hall. The 18th century saw a farm building constructed in 1741 but elsewhere few changes took place at the manor house. In 1741 it was found that the roof of the east block needed repairing but because the family live in another manor house, ins Blundell, it was pulled down and the house was rented out to be used for farming to a man called John Rimmer

Since that date the manor house has not developed to follow styles like others did but it was reconstructed to copy Elizabethan styles, like red brickwork and 16th century style windows. This restoration could be useful to show historians how Lydiate hall would have looked like but may also not be useful because it might not show exact styles of the times.

Lydiate hall has had lots of restoration carried out and so there is little left of the original building work. When I visited there was mainly remains of the red brick, which was used to restore the Manor house. In 1960 there was a lot more timber at the site and some of it containing carvings and other examples of sixteenth century timberwork. This would have been useful to historians. Historians can only use restored material and sources, which could be slightly inaccurate. There is however opportunities with the decay of the house to see the materials used in the building and these can be dated. Archaeological excavations have found the timber, which can be dated back to the medieval times when it was thought most of Lydiate hall was built.

In conclusion from when Lydiate hall was built until the end of the Tudor period it followed trends of the time and developed. This is evident in sources. However after this period it stopped following the fashions and when the family decided not to repair the building it started to deteriorate and so is not a reliable source of development after that period because it is based on restoration work which may not be as reliable as other manor hoses like Hardwick house and Speke Hall.
