Kirby Wright is best known for his coming of age island novel PUNAHOU BLUES. The setting for the book is his high school alma mater Punahou School.

EARLY YEARS

Wright was born at Queen's Hospital in Honolulu, Hawaii. His father Harold Wright was a t r ial attorney for the firm Smith, Wilde, BeBe, and Cades. His mother, the former June McCormack of Boston, was a former secretary at MIT. Starting at the age of 4, Wright spent summers with his part-Hawaiian grandmother Julia Gilman on her ranch on the east end of Molokai. It was here Wright met Sophie Cooke, a cattle rancher who wrote the memoire SINCERELY, SOPHIE about her life and times in the islands. This meeting proved influential to the young Wright because Cooke wrote about his grandmother.

HIGHER EDUCATION

After graduation from Punahou School, Wright studied writing under the tutelage of Maxine Hong Kingston at the Univesity of Hawaii, Manoa. He transferred to the University of California San Diego, where he met Kurt Vonnegut, Allen Gin sb e rg, Jerome Rothenberg, and Bobbie Louise Hawkins. After graduation, he became the PR Director for the Carlsbad Inn in Carlsbad, California. Unhappy in sales, Wright pursued an MFA in creative writing at San Francisco State University and became the first student in the history of the school to sweep the poetry awards.

In 1999, Treaty #3 set out on a course to form their own police force. It took four years and the dream is just beginning.

"The eagle has landed" Formally incorporated on April 1st 2003, Treaty Three Police assmed all policing duties from the OPP administerd First nations Program to become the latest stand alone police force. Currently serving officers were signed on and the intial group of 12 newly hired recruits attended the Ontario Police College were sent to Aylmer Ontario in spring 2003

Treaty Three Police services 18 550 First Nations residents in 28 First Nation Territories in the Kenora and Rainy River districts. Due to the vast patrol area, (55,000 square miles) T3PS was divied into two major "subdivisions" with smaller satellite detachments left over from the OPP administered program. The Kenora subdivision was headquarted in Dalles First Nation. Territories in the Rainy River district operated out of the Couchiching Sub Divsion, located on Couchiching First Nations. The City of Kenora hosts the Treaty Three Headquarters, where Chief of Police Brian Rupert oversees daily operations. Until his recent retirement , Deputy Chief Wally McLeod surpervised the Kenora SubDivision from both here and the Kenora OPP detachment. Deputy Chief Ernest Jones was his counterpart in Couchiching until his retirement in the summer of 2006. Staff Sgt Larry Indian has since taken over. Deputy Chief Louie Napish currently serves as second in commnad of T3PS. Assisting them are several Staff Sergeants, some of whom are retired or seconds OPP officers. Treaty Three has its own Crime Unit also based in Kenora led by Detective Sergeant Rob Bears, assisted by several Detective constables. Like other police services, the Detective branch runs parallel to the front line officers.

Each Sub Division (sometimes referred to as "detachment", a holdover term from the OPP program) employs a number of Sergeants who answer to the sub-division commander in the day to day operations. Front line policing is performed by Constables. After graduating Ontairo polcie college, each recruit is grante the rank of Fifth Class constable (roughly equivalent to the OPP recruit constable), promoted based on experience, reaching the rank of First Class constable within three years.

Currently T3PS uses the Chevy Impala and Chevy Tahoe after the OPP Ford Victoria, Expedition and Excursions were phased out. Also in service are boats, fourwheelers and snow machines, allowing for a variety of deployment methods.

T3PS also uses the same personal equipment, radio and computer systems and training system (such as the annual "block training" ) as the OPP, since the majority of the equipment and operations used by the First nations program is still used by the currrent service, allowing ease of use, and greater cooperation between neighbouring forces until T3PS can provide its own in service training united new police facilities.

T3PS's motto is "policing for the people by the people"

The Lower Paleolithic site of Ganj Par located at Gilan province, at north of Iran. It is discovered by a team of archaeologists from center for paleolithic research of National Museum of Iran in 2002. About 150 stone artifacts made of limestone, volcanic rocks, and sandstone have been found at this site which located on an old terrace of the Sefid Rud River that flows at east of the site. The artifacts composed of Handaxes, cleavers, a pick, choppers and smaller tool made of flakes. There are some similarities between this Acheulian stone tools with those found in Caucasus region. Discovery of Ganj Par indicates that Iran is part of the Acheulian territory.

[edit] Danse Villageoise

--156.34.212.18 17:03, 18 July 2006 (UTC)

Overview

Danse Villageoise is a piece originally written for violin and piano in 1929 by Montreal composer, Claude Champagne. He later arranged the work for string quartet in 1936, then for string orchestra somewhat later, and finally, sometime after 1954, for full orchestra.

In complete contrast to the extended phrases of his romantic compositions, Champagne has based almost the entire composition on a single clear eight-bar melody in A major.

The second melody is a variation of the first, in the key of E (dominant). All of the other sections are closely related to the original theme with one exception.

Character

Champagne recalls a rustic scene in Old Quebec with the diffler playing as the villagers dance a lively and ageless round dance.

Tempo is indicated vif. This is French for lively. Duple meter (2/4) moves the piece along quickly.

Attractive, folklike theme is reflected in the accompaniment after each statement by the violin. The piece also features an off-beat rhythm throughout.

[edit] The Tree Council

The Tree Council, the organisation that was founded to follow up the success of Plant A Tree in ’73 (National Tree Planting Year) is the umbrella body for over 150 organisations working together for trees – planting, caring for and enjoying them – and a forum for tackling issues relating to trees and woods. It promotes the improvement of the environment by the planting and conservation of trees and woods in town and country throughout the UK. Members range from professional, non-governmental, specialist and trade organisations, including other conservation charities, to government departments and local authorities.

It works towards

Making trees matter to everyone
More trees, of the right kind, in the right places
Better care for all trees, of all ages
Inspiring effective action for trees


It is responsible for an annual programme that includes Seed Gathering Season, National Tree Week, and Walk in the Woods month, supporting the groups organising local events. To find out more about what is happening near you, click on the events link on the website for details of events or call the Tree Council info line on 020 7940 8180 (office hours). It also co-ordinates the national volunteer Tree Warden Scheme and operates a fund giving tree-planting grants to schools and communities, as well as working with partners on an agenda for change that includes its Tree Care Campaign, Green Monuments Campaign and Hedge Tree Campaign.


Seed Gathering Season: Seed Gathering Season starts on 23 September (the autumn equinox, considered to be the first day of autumn) and continues until 23 October, giving everyone plenty of days on which they could hold events. These could be anything from guided walks enjoying the beauty of autumn colours to workshops and other activities to gather seeds, nuts and fruits to grow a whole range of trees.


National Tree Week: This is the Tree Council’s festival to mark the start of the tree planting season, and a nationwide celebration of trees and woods. It begun in 1975, is launched on the Wednesday before the last weekend in November and runs through until (and including) the first weekend in December. Across the country there will be lots of opportunities to plant trees, or to take part in events, walks, talks, songs, story-telling and tree dressing. How to get involved: Plant a tree in your garden, school grounds or neighbourhood. (Tree Planting Tips are available free from the Tree Council, with an S.A.E.) Enjoy one of the National Tree Week events organised by The Tree Council's members, its Tree Wardens and other supporters. Organise your own event. Get ideas from the list above, or do anything interesting to raise awareness of the value of trees!


Walk in the Woods Month: Walk in the Woods, throughout May, is the time to encourage everyone to enjoy trees and woods in spring. It’s a time to go out for a quiet walk among trees with family and friends or take part in local Walk in the Woods events. It’s a great month to go down to the woods or enjoy the trees in a local park. Spring flowers, birdsong and fresh green leaves make them particularly inviting. Walks, talks and other events take place in town and countryside; events are organised by Tree Council members, its Tree Wardens and other supporters and include bluebell walks,teddy bears’ picnics, dawn choruses, woodland open days, tree trails around towns, treasure hunts, bat and badger watches, games and story telling.


The Tree Council's Tree Warden Scheme: The Tree Warden Scheme is a national initiative to enable people to play an active role in conserving and enhancing their local trees and woods. The scheme was founded and is co-ordinated by The Tree Council. It is currently funded as part of the Government’s Cleaner, Safer, Greener initiative and in partnership with National Grid, which has a well-established policy of community involvement and environmental care. Tree Wardens are volunteers, appointed by parish councils or other community organisations, who gather information about their local trees, get involved in local tree matters and encourage local practical projects to do with trees and woods. Since the Scheme's launch in September 1990, The Tree Council has been working closely with Local Authority and voluntary sector partners to set up Tree Warden Networks throughout the UK. By the end of 2000, there were over 7,000 Tree Wardens.


Tree Care Campaign: Just a handful of simple tasks — that’s all it takes to give a recently planted tree a better start in life. The Tree Council campaigns to remind everyone who has planted trees in the last five years to revisit them in early spring and give them some aftercare. The ideal time for this vital aftercare is March or April, although there are tasks that can be undertaken right through until September. Gardeners, volunteer Tree Wardens, tree professionals and anyone else who has planted trees during this winter’s planting season or in recent years are urged to make time to go back and check on their young trees.


Green Monuments Campaign: The value of trees of historical, cultural or ecological importance is already formally recognised in many countries. This is not the case in the UK. In contrast to historic buildings, there are no legal safeguards specific to ancient trees or others of heritage significance. Many of them could be felled tomorrow without penalty. Such protection that does exist for historic trees is coincidental and not by design. Often, heritage trees survive today largely thanks to the individuals, past and present, who have cared for them. However, it takes only one person in the life of a tree to neglect, mutilate or destroy it. The Tree Council’s Green Monuments Campaign is a concerted effort by the UK’s lead tree campaigning partnership to gain special protected status for trees of great historical, cultural or ecological significance — heritage trees. The Tree Council has brought together its member organisations to campaign for safeguards for green monuments; encouragement for custodians to look after them; support and advice on their care.


Hedge Tree Campaign: Hedge trees are a traditional part of the UK landscape and havens for wildlife. Yet, of an estimated 1.8 million hedge trees, nearly a third are over a century old and may disappear from the landscape at any time over the next 25 years. Without an immediate effort to establish new hedge trees, there will be profound changes to the UK landscape and its biodiversity. Trickster is a 2D, isometric view MMORPG published by NTREEV Software.

[edit] Gameplay

Gameplay in Trickster is mostly point-and-click based, very much like Ragnarok Online. There are, however, some unique elements. First, there is the Drill System, which allows a player to drill in certain types of terrain for items and EXP. Second, there is the Card Battling System, which sets two characters against each other in a game of cards, played with card items found in various places in normal gameplay.=

[edit] Characters

There are four basic character types, and eight basic characters:

[edit] Power Type

This type focuses on physical combat. It includes Bunny and Buffalo characters.

[edit] Magic Type

This type focuses on casting spells, both for combat and recovery. It includes Sheep and Dragon characters.

[edit] Sense Type

This type uses long-range attacks, as well as having bonuses with drilling. It includes Fox and Lion characters.

[edit] Charm Type

This type gets special bonuses to evasion and defense. It includes Cat and Raccoon characters.

[edit] Tabitha Yim

Tabitha Yim, of Korean American descent,(known as Tabby by friends and fans) is a former United States Women's Artistic Gymnastics elite. Touted as a junior international elite to watch for her expressive dance and strong balance beam perforamnces, Tabitha quickly progressed as one of the major gymnasts in the 2001-2004 gymnastics quad. In her first major senior competition (where she was coached by Beth and Steve Rybacki of Gliders), 2001 U.S. Nationals, she placed second to 2000 Olympic Team squad member, Tasha Schwikert. Her runner-up placement secured her a spot on the World Team, occurring in Ghent, Belgium, where she placed 7th in the AA competition, and 6th in the FX finals. A fall on the balance beam in preliminary team competition cost her a position in the balance beam final, where she was a legitimate contender for a medal. The United States gymnastics team won a surprising bronze. Many had disregarded the team's chances even though every single member of the 2001 WAG World Team (comprised of Katie Heenan, Tasha Schikert, Tabitha Yim, Rachel Tidd, Ashley Rae Miles, and Mohini Bhwardwaj) made an event final (Katie Heenan went on to win bronze on the uneven bars). Tabitha's team bronze at World's 2001 would be the last major international medal she would receive as injuries would hamper and cut short her elite career.

Out for most of 2002 with a series of lower body injuries, Tabitha returned to 2002 Nationals with just two weeks of full training. Considered to be a legitimate contender for a world team position, Tabitha did not disappoint, turning in a wonderful performance that garnered her a surprise consecutive runner-up All-Around placement, second once again to Tasha Schikert. However, an injury post-nationals sidelined her from the 2002 World Event Finals team, where she could have contended on balance beam and floor exercise for medals.

Out for most of 2003 with a series of injuries again, Tabitha did not compete at 2003 Nationals and missed out on qualifying for the 2003 World team which went on to win the team gold. Tabitha did return in time for compete in the Olympic Year. At nationals, she placed a resurgent 7th in the AA, which would place her within striking distance of the Olympic Team. At the Olympic Trials, Tabitha continued in her bid to become an Olympic Team member with an electrifying 4th place AA performance, a performance many have argued would have placed her on the team. However, with a lengthy selection process, her selection will always be in doubt. Just pior to the closed door Olympic Selection Training Camp, Tabitha sustained a serious Achilles tendon injury that ended her Olympic aspirations and effectively terminated her elite career.

Tabitha is currently an NCAA gymnast, competing for Stanford, where she continues to impress.
