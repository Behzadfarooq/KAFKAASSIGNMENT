"Josh Tierney" From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia Jump to: navigation, search

Josh Tierney is a short story writer from London, Ontario. Although his work has been published in numerous magazines, he is mostly known for his website which is unique in that not only does it feature stories, but artwork created for the stories by notable artists from around the world, ranging from the cartoons of Ramon Sierra to the paintings of Winston Chmielinski. Because of this his stories are mostly popular with artists. Josh Tierney's stories are usually surreal or absurd, involving characters who are placed in impossible dreamlike situations. Josh Tierney has been accused of hiding shallow stories under a layer of gross verbiage, and indeed his story 12 November 1993 was written through the use of a thesaurus. Not much is known about his personal life, but he is considered a notable writer of London, Ontario by the CSLC.

[edit] Udo Grashoff

Udo Grashoff is both a biochemist and history graduate who now works as a print and radio journalist with a particular interest in medical history.

Let me Finish (2004) The Times said Let me Finish is "a remarkable anthology of suicide letters... signalling a new fascination and openness about a centuries-old taboo."

Mr Grashoff first discovered the suicide letters used in Let Me Finish through academic research and has lectured widely on the subject of suicide.

Now that I know they are all dead, I can do it too. I gave them life, so they will die with me too. Believe me, it's better for them this way." [page 43]

[edit] bobby cash

Bobby Cash grew up and resides in Clement Town, near Dehradun (NE India) in the foothills of the Himalayas.

A pioneer in his own country, Bobby is the first Indian ever to release a country music album in India. Born Bal Kishore Das Loiwal, his father used to call him “Babu” which became “Bobby”- and Kishore became “Kish”, which in turn became “Cash”.

For as long as he can remember country music was played in his house. In the early 1960’s an aunt moved to Nashville and would regularly send the latest country releases to Bobby’s mother. With an uncle who made electric guitars and sisters and cousins with a similar passion, Bobby’s home became a kind of incubator of country music in India.

After briefly dabbling in “Hindi pop” he returned to his first true love- Country Music. In New Delhi whenever any Australian heard him they would tell him he that had to go to the Tamworth Country Music Festival. A chance meeting with Australian Producer/Director Colin Bromley in Jan 2002 turned that dream into a reality when he and co-Producer Gerry O’Leary, through their newly formed company Gobsmacked Television, arranged for Bobby to attend the 2003 Tamworth Country Music Festival as the subject of a documentary. As India’s population had just passed the one billion mark, and Bobby was the only guy in India making a living playing country music, it became “The Indian Cowboy…One in a Billion”.

Tamworth was a chance for Bobby to play for, and with, people who truly love the same music he does. While knowing nothing about Bobby, he offered to help Bobby record a CD - in the hope that some of the profits would go to help underprivileged children in India. Bobby returned to Australia in June 2003 and recorded his first country music album - “Cowboy at Heart”. In June 2004 the documentary screened on the Discovery Channel in India in both English and Hindi. In September the album was released through Universal Music (India) and in conjunction with Showtime Events, India, and the Taj Hotel Chain, Bobby toured for 2-3 weeks through Pune, Bangalore, Hyderbad, Mumbai, Emakulam and Colombo in Sri Lanka to full houses.

While country music in India is a niche market, Bobby believes it will have great appeal because it fits in with the Indian psyche. It shares a lot of common values such as loyalty, respect for elders, family and neighbours… and that some of the biggest selling music in India has been country music, John Denver’s “Country Road” being just one. It’s just not known as country music. Due to public demand, in July 2004 Bobby re-entered the studio and recorded an album of cover versions of crowd favourites. Titled “Phoenix to El Paso” it reflects the music Bobby and his family grew up listening to –cowboy and love songs.

Bobby attended the 2005 CMA music festival in Nashville after being nomination for CMA Global Artist Award and received a standing ovation from the head of the CMA, Ed Benson, who declared him to be “the real deal”. “Nobody plays Country Music like this anymore”.

He performed at the 2006 Tamworth Country Music festival and has just recorded his third album to be released in April 2006 when he returns to Australia for his eleventh tour.

[edit] Zigge Holmgren

Zigge Holmgren, artist and photographer particularly partial to digital media. Born 1953 in Stockholm, Sweden. Educated in art- and photo schools. Exhibitions continously especially in Gotland, Sweden, where Zigge is situated, but also in Milano and London. The images are usually digitally edited with his earlier experience of oil painting and photo lab technique as a base. During the last years Zigge Holmgren has experimented with modern portraits partly built upon 18th century style and renaissance works but is also influenced by comic strips and other styles.

Zigge Holmgren is also continuously working with his "Muzak Art" project, where the pieces depict fragments of nature's perfection, from the Swedish island of Gotland. When synthesised with mural surfaces from the medieval town of Visby the pictures are reduced into organic patterns. The style is romantic and decorative and with a longing for purity of mind like an icon. The pictures are intended to realize the inclination of the Nordic, almost ethnic rite, of bringing Nature into the home, like potted plants and Christmas trees. The concept of Muzak Art is an attempt to reconstruct this random process where perfection is built upon pure chance.. A fantasy around the “non-image”, to find the code between the small print of nature. The artist Zigge Holmgren like to see his pictures as xerox copies of life, as we would experience existance in Afterlife... Like images from children's picture books, for grown-ups.

Zigge Holmgren has been given a lot of attention world-wide and has a permanent exhibition in Cuneo e Associati in Milano, Italy. He is a member of KRO (The Swedish Artist Organization).

[edit] Ken Foster

Ken Foster is an American author. Born and raised in Central Pennsylvania, he attended Columbia University's MFA program in creative writing. While at Columbia, he founded and curated the KGB Bar Reading Series. His first book, The KGB Bar Reader (William Morrow, 1998) collects 28 writers who appeared there, including Rick Moody, Jennifer Egan, Junot Diaz, Meghan Daum, Kathryn Harrison and Jacqueline Woodson. His second book, a collection of stories titled The Kind I'm Likely to Get (Morrow), was a New York Times Notable Book of 1999. In 2006, he published a memoir, The Dogs Who Found Me, which was featured on NPR's "Fresh Air". In this book, he recounts his experiences with stray dogs, beginning just after 9/11 through the evacuation of New Orleans during Hurricane Katrina. He also writes about the death of two friends, the writers Lucy Grealy and Amanda Davis, and his own near death experience due to a heart problem. He received a pacemaker in 2004.

Foster's work has appeared in numerous magazines, including McSweeney's, Fence, Salon, The New York Times, The Village Voice and elsewhere. He lives in New Orleans.

[edit] La Grota - Favela-Biarro, Rio de Janeiro

Rio de Janiero is famous for its slum districts, which have been allowed to spread uncontrolled. An estimated 1.2 million people live in wooden shacks across these districts.

In the last few years, the govenment has started providing basic ammenities such as electricity and sewers to these huts.

La Grota is one of the 600 slum neighbourhoods with a population of 5000. The residents first came in the 1960s/70s from poverty stricken northeastern Brazil in search of work and a better way of life.

[edit] British Double Reed Society

The British Double Reed Society (BDRS) was founded in 1988 to promote and enhance the interests of all double reed players, whether students, amateurs, professionals or teachers. Thanks to the efforts of the prime movers, George Caird and William Waterhouse, the BDRS has flourished and continues to grow, offering a widening range of valued services to its members as well as providing a forum for the interchange of expertise, knowledge and opinion on all matters relating to double reed instruments.

Joint Presidents Lady Barbirolli OBE, William Waterhouse

  • Annual convention

  • Regional convention

  • A chance for all to contribute

The BDRS is a non-profit making organisation established to further the interests of all involved with the oboe and bassoon. The BDRS acts as a national forum for debate and the exchange of ideas, information and advice on all aspects of double reed instruments. It also fulfils an important role in encouraging greater interest in the instruments, and securing their place in the wider cultural and educational environment.

The BDRS is a registered charity, number 1080461, in the United Kingdom.

[edit] Regenstrief Institute

The Regenstrief Institute, Inc. is a private, non-profit research organization affiliated with the Indiana University School of Medicine (IUSM).[4] It was founded in 1969 by philanthropist Sam Regenstrief through his charitable, self-named foundation. According to the a biography [5], Regenstrief founded a company that manufactured and popularized the low-cost home dishwasher, at one time producing nearly 37 percent of the world's dishwashers. He and his wife Myrtie Regenstrief promoted a philanthropic legacy of interweaving medicine, engineering and technology. According to their website, the Regenstrief Institute strives to realize this vision through several focus areas (see below) primarily utilizing the collaborative and translational sciences of medical informatics and health service research.

[edit] Health Services Research and Development (HSR&D)

Health services research (and development) is a form of medical research and most simply defined as the study of health care delivery. More extensive, formal, and authoritative definitions can be found at the HSRProj website.[6] Other excellent resources for HSR are the Deptartment of Health and Human Services' Agency for Healthcare Research and Quality, the NIH's health services research resource website, and the peer-reviewed journal Health Services Research (TOC).

The Regenstrief's efforts in Health Services Research and Development[7] efforts are diverse and well published (search on 'Regenstrief' at the National Library of Medicine's PubMed). Many of these efforts are in close collaboration with the medical informaticians and affiliated organizations at the IUSM. These include:

  • Center for Heath Services and Outcomes Research
  • IU Center fo Aging Research
  • Indiana Children's Health Services Research
  • Academic Model for the Prevention and Treatment of HIV - a collaboration between the IUSM and Kenya


[edit] Medical Informatics

Medical Informatics is the collaborative science utilizing and combining knowledge of computer and information science with clinical medicine and medical research.

The Regenstrief Institute is recognized as contributing to over 25% of the high quality evidence on the impact of information technology on the quality of health care ( News Release, RAND Report, or Annals of Int Med Article).

[edit] Regenstrief Medical Record System

Since the 1970's, this group has been developing the Regenstrief Medical Records System (RMRS)[8], one of the earliest electronic medical record systems (EMR), perhaps the longest continually opperating EMRs, and the research platform for many research activities. As well as being a critical research tool, RMRS serves as the production electronic medical records system at Wishard Hospital and its community clinics. Additionally, this group has created a citywide electronic medical records systems which currently allows physicians, with the patient's permission, to view as a single virtual record all previous care at any of a number of regional hospital systems, labs, clinics, pharmacies, and other clinical and administrative data sources. It is the keystone for the Indiana Network for Patient Care (INPC).[9], [10] This is one of the best examples of a Regional Health Information (RHIO) or Health Information Exchange HIE.

RMRS has decades of diverse clinical and administrative data on millions of patients. Additionally, it provides a variety of an [[Electronic medical record | Electronic Medical Record] (EMR) functionality including results delivery, documentation, clinical communication, and computerized provider order entry that utilizes unique clinical decision support and guidelines. RMRS has a data base of over 3 million patients, with hundreds of millions of on-line coded results, gigabytes of text reports including diagnostic studies, procedure results, operative notes, discharge summaries, and tens of millions of radiology images.

Additionally, the RMRS has been utilized to form a Shared Pathology Informatics Network (SPIN) to facilitate an "Internet-based virtual database that will allow investigators to locate appropriate human tissue specimens for their research".[11], [12] This is a collaborative effort between the Institute (and affiliated hospitals), Harvard Medical School (and affiliated hospitals), UCLA, University of Pittsburg, and the NCI.[13]

[edit] Open Standards

"The Regenstrief Institute is an international leader in medical informatics standards and strongly committed to open source development."[14], [15]

[edit] HL7

The Institute's informatics group has also pioneered international electronic message standards for medical records including the development of HL7, the standard for electronic data interchange in health care. Past efforts have included various aspects of the core messaging standard often referred to as "V2" or "Version 2" and continue to use and develop various aspects of the standard. Additionally, Institute scientists have been very active in developing and implementing the most recent version of the standard- "Version 3" or "V3". See the HL7 article for more on Version 3 and other aspects of the standard.

[edit] LOINC

They also developed the Logical Observation Identifiers Names and Codes ( LOINC©) system, a standard nomenclature that enables the electronic transmission of clinical data from laboratories to hospitals, physicians' offices and payers for clinical care and management purposes.[16] Logical Observation Identifiers Names and Codes (LOINC) is a database and universal standard for identifying laboratory observations. It was developed and is maintained by the Regenstrief Institute, Inc. in 1994. LOINC was created in response to the demand for an electronic database for clinical care and management and is publicly available at no cost. It is endorsed by the American Clinical Laboratory Association, the College of American Pathologist, the National Library of Medicine, and the American National Standards Institute. Since its inception, the database has expanded to include not just medical and laboratory code names, but also: nursing diagnosis, nursing interventions, outcomes classification, and patient care data set.

[edit] Arden Syntax

Realizing the need for a standard to represent logic in a decision algorithm, Institute scientists have been actively involved in the development and implementation of Arden syntax, in part based on their expertise with the home-grown CARE language.[17] The Arden syntax is a language for encoding medical knowledge. These Medical Logic Modules (MLMs) are used in the clinical setting as they can contain sufficient knowledge to make single medical decisions. They can produce alerts, diagnoses, interpretations, and contain a quality assurance function and administrative support. An MLM must be run on a computer that meets the minimum system requirements and has the correct program installed. Once this is provided, the MLM can give advice when and where it is needed.[18]

[edit] EHR-Lab Interoperability and Connectivity Standards (ELINCS)

The ELINCS are a national standard for the delivery of real-time laboratory results from a lab's information system to an electronic health record.[19] Institute scientists are actively involved in the development of this standard to obtain patient data from laboratory systems including local, regional, and national reference laboratories.

[edit] Related Efforts

  • OpenMRS selected as Google Summer of Code 2007 project.[20]
  • Regenstrief, in collaboration with Partners In Health (Boston) and MRC (South Africa), helped to found the OpenMRS project, an open source effort to build a framework for electronic medical records development in resource poor areas. Much of the foundational design for the OpenMRS project was gleaned from Regenstrief's decades of experience with electronic medical record systems.
  • The Regenstrief Foundation and Regenstrief Institute have formed a partnership with Purdue University to for the Regenstrief Center for Healthcare Engineering.[21]

[edit] Groundwater purification

Archived discussion follows below. Please do not modify it.
This request for creation has been reviewed. The reviewer comments appear below the article text.
If your request was declined and you want to make changes and try again, copy and paste your article into a new submission and make your changes there.

There are many methods of ground water purification. These methods include chemical precipitation, chemical degradation, volatilization, biological degradation, biological uptake, and adsorption. Chemical precipitation is when harmful chemicals undergo a chemical reaction, to become a solid. When the water is sinking through the soil the newly formed substance is caught up in the dirt. This substance will not enter the groundwater. Chemical degradation is when the harmful compound is broken down into a less complex compound. The new compound might enter the groundwater, but it is no longer harmful. Volatilization is when the harmful substance is vaporized, then rising into the air. The vaporization will sometimes separate a complex substance or the substance will fall as rain. Then it will either repeat the cycle or undergo another method of purification. Biological degradation (biodegradation) is when microorganisms consume and breakdown the harmful substance. The substance might enter the ground water, but it will no longer be harmful. Biological uptake is when animals or plants eat/absorb the harmful substance; the substance is not broken down. Instead the substance is trapped in the plant or animal cells. When this plant or animal dies the substance can sink into the ground and undergo another method of purification. Adsorption is when the harmful substance(s) accumulates on the surface of a solid.
