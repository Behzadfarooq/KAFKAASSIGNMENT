Florida State Road 55 is the unsigned name of US 19 which runs from Memphis,Fl (the southern termini) at US 41 to the Florida-Georgia border.

US 19 remains independent of I-75, even as the routes converge on Tampa and St. Petersburg, Florida. The route is co-signed with US 27 between Capps and Perry, Alternate U.S. 27 between Perry and Chiefland, US 98 between Perry and Chassahowitzka, and Interstate 275 over the Sunshine Skyway Bridge, a cable-stayed bridge over the mouth of Tampa Bay.

An "Alt 19" exists in Florida, beginning at the intersection of US 19 in Holiday, Pasco County, Florida, it runs for 34.6 miles west of US 19 near the Gulf coast ending at the intersection of US 19 and 9th Avenue North in St. Petersburg, Florida. It used to continue east of this along 9th Avenue North, to the intersection with US 92. The planned St. Petersburg-Clearwater Expressway, or Pinellas Beltway, would have followed the current alignment of "Alt 19" from I-275 to Clearwater, Florida. The intersection of Seminole Boulevard and Bay Pines Boulevard is a remenant of this proposed road. The beltway road was proposed in 1974, but dead by 1980.

Currently, US 19 between Clearwater(State Road 580) and Pinellas Park(Pinellas County Road 611) is getting a freeway-style upgrade, due to the cancellation of an extension on Interstate 375 in the late 1970s.

Statistically US 19 is the most dangerous road in the United States. A Florida Highway Patrol test period beginning in 1998 and ending in 2003, as mandated by the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration, showed the strech of US 19 from Pasco County to Pinellas County to average approximately 52 deaths a year, or 262 deaths in the 5 year duration of the study. 100 of these deaths were pedestrian related making US 19 the #1 worst road to walk on in these two counties.

[edit] The Ultimate Ric Flair Collection

The Ultimate Ric Flair Collection is a three disc DVD set covering some of Ric Flair's greatest matches and promos. It was released in November 2003 and was one of the fastest selling DVDS in WWE history.

[edit] DVD Format

A chapter starts with Ric Flair talking about an opponent and a notable match then follows. This was the first WWE DVD to feature matches as part of a feature as opposed to standard extras. Extras include promos and segments leading up to and in some cases after each match. Some promos and segments stand by themselves with nothing to do with a match on the DVD. For example, in one promo, the Four Horseman talk about the Road Warriors. Another extra is from WWE Confidental dealing with Flair's plane crash in 1975.

[edit] Matches On Disc 1

NWA Championship Match

Extras include Bob Orton and Dick Slater "breaking" Flairs neck, the $25,000 bounty put on Flairs head by Harley Race, the Four Horsemen "breaking" Dusty Rohdes ankle, Flair's retirement address, and a press conference concerning Starcade 83.

      Just like his contemporaries Little John and Little Harry, Billy Boyo was still in his teens when he emerged in the early eighties. At that time a new era in Jamaican music had begun as roots reggae had become dancehall. Then one of the most prolific and influential producers in dancehall music was the late Henry "Junjo" Lawes. He more or less launched dancehall as a fully developed musical style and records on his "Arrival" and "Volcano" labels dominated the dancehall market for years.

      With the powerful Roots Radics Band and the innovative young engineer Hopeton "Overton" Brown aka Scientist working on the majority of his releases, "Junjo" Lawes began voicing a dazzling array of talent including Michael Prophet, Barry Brown, Frankie Paul, Wailing Souls and Johnny Osbourne. Quick to spot new talent he also employed rather unknown names like for example a young boy who called himself Billy Boyo. In 1982 "Junjo" Lawes recorded this talented youthman deejay together with Little Harry for a two artist "clash" album entitled "DJ Clash Volume 2", released by Greensleeves Records. Billy Boyo also became part of Junjo's famous Volcano sound system and performed alongside Burro Banton, Little John, Toyan, Josey Wales, Lee Van Cleef, Yellowman and others.

      In 1983 Billy Boyo (together with Little John and Ranking Toyan) went to London where he linked up with producer Silver Kamel. The latter took him into the studio and recorded tracks for Boyo's second album. Due to various reasons the project got put on hold and it took almost twenty years before the album, "Zim Zim", was unleashed. Silver Kamel recalls... "What I liked most about Billy Boyo was his easy style and infectious ear-to-ear grin that would break out just about anytime. In the studio he was all-pro. He knew how to work the mike, what he wanted as a sound, and was prepared to experiment. Ridin riddim and stabbin toasts at the drum and bass... all the sessions were fast and fun-packed."

      All in all Billy Boyo wasn't a prolific recording artist as he did not record much before or after the tracks for the aforementioned two albums. His musical career was short-lived and little was heard of Billy Boyo since the second half of the eighties. Although many rumors state that he had been shot, he actually died of a brain tumor in 2000.

      [edit] Petrarch

      Francesco de Petrarca was one of the greatest 14th century Italian poets. Born Francesco Petracco, he was ushered into history in Florence in 1302 having been indicted as a lawyer for falsifying a legal document. He refused to stand trial, but was convicted and banished from Florence. He and his young wife escaped to Arezzo, where he changed his name to Petrarca. In 1312 he moved his family to Pisa, then by various assignments to Avignon on the Rhone, Carpentras, Montpellier, and Bologna where he turned his thoughts away from law, and toward reading great classical works, who inspired him to write, so that by 1326 his entire devotion was to romantic poetry. When he first saw a woman named Laura in 1327, in the church of Sainte-Claire d'Avignon, it awoke in him a lasting passion, and the result was a series of over 200 sonnets. They had little contact and she refused any of his advances since she was married to Count Hugues de Sade (ancestor of the infamous Marquis de Sade). His writings won him recognition in cultured society as he traveled and continued to impact the literary world. In fact, he is considered the "Father of the Renaissance."

      One of the sonnets follows with an explication appended.

      It was the day the sun's ray had turned pale with pity for the suffering of his Maker when I was caught, and I put up no fight, my lady, for your lovely eyes had bound me. It seemed no time to be on guard against Love's blows; therefore, I went my way secure and fearless—so, all my misfortunes began in midst of universal woe. Love found me all disarmed and found the way was clear to reach my heart down through the eyes which have become the halls and doors of tears. It seems to me it did him little honour to wound me with his arrow in my state and to you, armed, not show his bow at all.

      Explication:

      The poem is addressed to "my lady", some time after the speaker’s experience, a simple glimpse of a woman, the object of his love. Petrarchus alludes to “Good Friday“, the day the sun darkened “for the suffering of his Maker”. Whether the speaker’s experience was literally on this day of religious observation, or whether it was used as a poetic device, his choice is appropriate to his mood. The church may refer to it as “Good” Friday, but it was the day of sorrow, woe, and literal darkness upon the earth, before Redemption was complete. The speaker's focus remains constant on the suffering, dark aspect of Good Friday. He was “caught” and he made no resistance as her eyes had captured him. The metaphor is that of a bandit or surprise attack. Would it be too conventional or pedestrian to identify the attacker as Cupid? Any attacker will do for the metaphor. But her eyes are what bound him, leaving him frozen, vulnerable.. He admits to making no resistance, continuing the metaphor of the Friday of Christ’s sufferings, because it “seemed no time” to be offering resistance, or being “on guard against Love’s blows.” Christ offered no resistance to the blows he suffered, and in keeping with this attitude, the speaker remained passive. He goes on his way, “secure and fearless” while expressing that all his misery, appropriately, ironically began in the “midst of universal woe”. Love, the attacker personified , found his weakness: his eyes (because he simply beheld the woman of his passion) which have now become metaphorically “halls and doors” of tears. Eyelids and doors open and close, halls and tear ducts are avenues. His attention turns back to the bandit--who had no honor in attacking a man in such a weakened condition. He was no worthy foe, indeed. She was “armed” and with her great beauty, rendered him defenseless. The bandit had not even the decency to reveal his weapon. Such is the profound defeat of the speaker in this poem! Charles David Land, B.S. Ed.

      [edit] Fazıl Hüsnü Dağlarca

      Turkish poet, born in 1914 in İstanbul.

      One of Turkey's most prolific (he has written more than seventy poem books) and frequently translated contemporary poets, Fazıl Hüsnü Dağlarca received a military education but began publishing poetry while still in middle school. His poem "Yavaşlayan Ömür" (Slowing Life) brought him to the attention of the reading public in 1933. As a career officer he lived in various parts of the country until his resignation from the army in 1950, after which he worked in the Directorate of Press and Tourism and then in the Ministry of Labor for another ten years. He spent some time in France in 1952. Together with a friend he founded "Kitap" (Book) Bookstore and Publications in İstanbul which he managed until 1970. Since then he has concentrated on writing poetry. He currently lives in the district of Kadıköy, İstanbul.

      Dağlarca was selected as the best Turkish poet by the International Poetry Forum meeting in Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania in 1967 and received a golden wreath in August, 1974 at the Thirteenth Struga, Yugoslavia, Poetry Festival. If Dağlarca sometimes neglects the refinements of image or form, his verse is generally accepted as some of the most vigorous Turkish poetry of the mid-twentieth century. If Dağlarca constitutes, in the words of critic Doğan Hızlan, "a school unto himself", his patriotic themes are firmly bound to Turkish nationalism:

      We are Turks, Ataturkists, free from earth to heaven
      It's crystal clear, the sea is ours, the soil, the sky,
      Anew we'll build the mountain, stones, the plains
      Our heart, which beating says, `modern civilization.'

      Dağlarca's delibarate use of assonances, reinforcement of concepts by repetition and a mixture of the literary ``purified language with the spoken tongue have been admirably conveyed in a number of English translations. But Fazıl Hüsnü Dağlarca is not all driving rhythms and strident themes:

      I draw my world in the air with a flower
      And look with wonder at this dreamlike form.

      Some of his books include

      Havaya Çizilen Dünya (1935), Çocuk ve Allah (1940), Daha (1943), Çakır'ın Destanı (1945), Toprak Ana (1950), Aç Yazı (1951), A^su^ (1955), Türk Olmak (1963), Haydi (1968), Horoz (1977), Nötron Bombası (1981), Uzaklara Giyinmek (1990), Dildeki Bilgisayar (1992).
