“As to Negro Masons”

“Whereas, the question of the recognition of Negro Masons has been made more than usually prominent during the last year; and whereas this Grand Lodge has a well-settled opinion upon this subject, which she desires most respectfully and fraternally to express to her sister Grand Lodges everywhere, and especially to those of the United States; she deems the present a fit opportunity to set forth the reasons which impel her to that opinion.

1. It is indisputable that whatever theory we adopt as to the origin of Masonry- whether that which carries it back to the original Father of mankind, and his immediate descendents;, or to Enoch and Noah; or to the building of King Solomon’s Temple; or arising from the constitutions of Pythagoras; or if we trace it back to the Eleusinain Mysteries; or to those of Ceres, and the institution of the Bacchanalia; or, what is most probable of all, the incorporation of the Roman builders under Numa Pompilius that theory carries us back to the Caucasian Race. 2. Masonry was originally, what it is mainly today, a social institution; intended for those who daily mingled in the ordinary walks of life, in business, in pleasure, and in the family circle; into which it is not credible that anyone of the Negro or any other of the inferior races, could have been admitted. 3. That Negroes have of late years been admitted into Lodges of Free Masons is due, it is believed, to the sympathy which has been excited for them by anti-slavery societies generally, and particularly by those of the United States; and that any were admitted during the revolutionary war by traveling Lodges belonging to the British Army, was due to the feeling which existed at that time against American patriots; a proceeding entirely at variance with the object of the formation of such Lodges, they having no right to confer the degrees upon any citizen or resident of the county in which they might be sojourning, but only upon members of the army to which such Lodges belonged. 4. Although it is usually said that Masonry is universal, and that in every clime Masons are to be found; yet it is only universal in so far as the Caucasian Race has carried it into every quarter of the globe; and if that race has sometimes admitted Negroes, and others of the inferior races, it has done so in violation of the original and fundamental laws of the Fraternity. 5. In view, therefore, of these facts, indisputable as they are conceived to be, the Grand Lodge of Free and Accepted Masons of Alabama seizes the present as a fit and proper occasion, to acknowledge the legality of Negro-masons, such acknowledgement being foreign to the original purpose of the Fraternity, and introducing an element of demoralization into the society.”

Resolution passed

      [edit] Forest Farming - Solution to Forestry Woes

      Forest Farming - Solution to Forestry Woes

      The forests of the Northwest have not been managed well in the past. They have been utilized for one purpose---the production of wood fiber. Little care has been given to prevent habitat destruction and less to rehabilitating these abused forestlands.

      Since the 1980's, several Southern Oregon forestland owners have been at the leading edge of a new generation of forest managers. These forest land owners have found that the best method of managing these cut over lands is to try to enable them to recover as a forest ecosystem, as they were prior to man's entry into the forest.

      Many forest professionals and land owners think of these lands only as "secondary" forests of even-age stands which are to be left alone until time to come back in and cut them all again. Forest farmers believe that these forests have been so abused that they are completely out of balance and must be manipulated in order to bring them back into ecological balance.

      They must be thinned, but no species is ever to be completely decimated. There must never be so much thinning that the crown cover is depleted leaving the forest floor exposed to excessive sun or rain. There must be a cross-section of trees of all ages. Trees are not thinned solely for the removal of the largest healthiest individuals- in fact wherever a healthy large tree can be left, it is left to grow larger and stronger. Only the weaker, sick or damaged trees are removed to give the healthy ones more space, food and sunlight so that they may thrive.

      Today, forest farm owners take heart in the knowledge that, although their efforts may not bring them financial reward, it brings forth an even greater reward... a natural and varied forest habitat that can support many creatures and a healthier, happier earth. To forest farmers, sustainable levels of harvest are those levels that are sustainabile by the earth in her ability to recover from disruption of man's efforts. These levels are far less than those utilized by industry at present.

      In 1984 Orville Camp published his book, "Natural Selection Forestry, A Guide to Natural Selection Forestry", which galvanized landowners to resist standard forest practices and allow nature to tell them how to manage forests.

      In 2005, Ms. jeannie Kendrick wrote her book "Alex's Forest Farm", which chronicled, both verbally and photographically, the actual forest management activities carried on at their forest farm. This book exhibits an incredible array of native plants and animals actually living in their forest.

        The Sextet's six male singers are drawn from all faculties of the university. There has been a sextet-type group of singing men (sometimes there were more or less than six members) in every Capping Show at Otago University since 1903. The Otago Capping Sextet pre-dates by three years another well-known all-male collegiate a cappella group at Yale University The Whiffenpoofs.

        Costumes For several decades the Sextet has worn two costumes: white tie and tails and a clown or harlequin outfit in the colours of the University and province of Otago: blue and gold. In earlier years conical hats were worn with the clown outfits. The Sextet uses harlequin-style makeup.

        Music While the Sextet's satirical and vulgar lyrics have stayed similar over the years the Sextet's music has varied considerably. For many years the Sextet sang accompianed by piano and the accompanist was an essential part of the group. Since the 1990s the trend has been to sing a cappella. Unison, two or three parts songs were usual for the Sextet for many years. For several years four-part Barbershop music has been important in the Sextet's repertoire. Since the 1970s the Sextet has borrowed heavily from the King's Singers and therefore has increased the number of parts it sings in to five and six. Latterly the Sextet has tended to sing unaccompanied mostly six-part music. Many people have made arrangements for the Sextet, notable amongst arrangers are: Dr. Nigel Eastgate, Professor John Drummond and Associate Professor Patrick Little.

        [edit] Heat Rate

        Heat Rate 101: The basics Heat rate refers to the efficiency of an electric generating plant. Essentially, it is an expression of the number of MMBtus of natural gas that are needed to generate 1 kW of electricity. The lower the heat rate, the more efficient the plant and the higher the heat rate, the less efficient the plant. In Texas, marginal heat rates are generally lower in the winter and higher in the summer due seasonal differences in demand. Generally speaking, heat rates in the Texas market starting in May 2002 have remained between 7.7 and 9, which is fairly stable. These stable heat rates reflect two unique characteristics in the Texas market—excess capacity of approximately 35%1 and the fact that the recently constructed plants have been efficient users of natural gas.


        Natural gas is also tied to heat rate because as natural gas prices increase, this creates an incentive for generators to run their most efficient generating units at higher capacity.2 This drives down heat rates as less efficient plants are run less often. Conversely, when gas rates are lower, generators will tend to run less efficient, older plants. From an economic standpoint, this makes sense, as a generator’s total cost to run older plants is lower because they have recovered the construction costs of the facility whereas the newer, more efficient plants have greater capital costs associated with them that must be recovered.

        The above graph confirms two basic premises of the heat rate product. First, marginal heat rates in ERCOT since the summer of 2002 have tended to fluctuate between 7.7 and 9. Excess capacity and the movement of the natural gas market have been the key drivers in this fluctuation. Second, there is an inverse relationship between heat rate and natural gas prices.

        Using a heat rate product for a retail electric contract A heat rate product in a retail electric contract determines final price through a formula. The customer selects a heat rate, typically between 8 and 9. The retail electric provider then provides an “adder” which will, in conjunction with the heat rate and natural gas price, determine the final cost of the electricity supplied under the agreement. The adder covers the retailer’s ancillary services, congestion costs, unaccounted for energy (UFE) costs, back office support, and margin. The customer then chooses a day on which to lock in natural gas for a period of time, which can range from one month to the complete term of the contract. Multiplying the heat rate by the price of natural gas and adding the adder to this result determine the final price. For instance, if a heat rate of 8.5 is chosen with an adder of $20 and the natural gas price is $6.00/MmBtu, then the cost for electricity would be $71/ MWh, or $.071/kwh.3 Understanding what the heat rate of a plant signifies as well as the relationship between natural gas prices and heat rates in the Texas market is an important first step for determining whether the benefits of a heat rate product outweigh the negatives

        Heat rate products: Pros and cons In the Texas market, as natural gas prices go, so go electric power prices. In fact, there is a 90%+ correlation between natural gas prices and electricity prices in the ERCOT region.4 This means, especially in a market with excess supply such that generation is readily available, that the cost of natural gas will be the predominant determinant of the cost of electricity at both the wholesale and retail levels.


        Pro: Heat Rate offers increased customer control over final pricing The main advantage of a heat rate product is that it allows the customer increased flexibility to determine their final price when compared with a traditional fixed price product. This is especially important in the Texas market where gas prices are a significant determinant of overall electric costs. Assume that a customer enters in to a fixed price agreement on a Wednesday when the price of natural gas is $6.00/MMBtu on the 12-month strip. This would generate a fixed price of around $.066/kwh5 including all ancillary and other energy related charges.

        Suppose that same customer enters in to a 12-month contract for a heat rate product. The heat rate is 8.5 and the adder is $16.50. The customer waits a week for the 12-month strip to fall. The strip moves down to $5.50/MMBtu when the customer decides to lock in gas. The total cost under the heat rate product would be $.063/kwh. This is a total decrease of 4.5%. Assuming an annual electric bill of $1,000,000, the customer has saved $45,000 on their electric costs alone. This cost savings was generated when the gas market moved down over 8%, meaning that the customer was able to capture a little more than half of the downward movement in the natural gas market.

        Of course, the more sophisticated the customer, the more the customer is able to take advantage of this situation. Using the same heat rate contract from above, assume that the customer only locks in the first 6 months of the contract price at $5.50/MMBtu and the second six months at $5.25/MMBtu. The price for the first 6 months would be $.063/kwh and the cost for the second six months would be $.061/kwh. The average price that the customer would pay under this arrangement is $.062/kwh. This would generate a total savings of 5.75% versus the fixed rate contract. Using the same estimated spend, this would generate a savings of $57,500 per year.

        As the above examples illustrate, a heat rate contract enables the customer to better control the price and to take advantage of downward movement in the gas market. However, there are potential pitfalls to this product that are discussed in greater detail below.


        Cons: Increased costs and risk for the customer, with no additional benefit While a heat rate contract can provide additional savings by allowing a customer to wait and lock in the natural gas price component of the total electric costs at a time when the market is falling, a heat rate product entails substantial risk. This risk manifests itself in three ways—first, what happens if the gas market moves against the end-user; second, the heat rate product makes it more difficult for the end user to budget their electric costs, and third, the customer does not have unlimited flexibility in a heat rate contract resulting in the customer being forced to take electricity at higher rates. Finally, there are contract tools available to customers to take advantage of decreases in the price of natural gas without incurring the risks associated with a heat rate product.

        First con: The gas market moves against the customer In the above examples, the gas market moved down, meaning that the customer was able to secure a lower price through the heat rate product versus a traditional fixed cost contract. However, assume that the gas price increased during the heat rate deal. Once again, using the numbers above, if gas increased to $7.00/MMBtu from $6.00/MMBtu at the date the contract was signed, the price for electricity would be $.076/kwh. This is an increase of almost 15% in electric costs as a result of the gas market increasing by approximately 16%. This also illustrates that the customer’s price will move in almost direct correlation with the natural gas market if the natural gas market moves up while capturing only about half the percentage decrease if the natural gas market moves down.

        In a fixed price contract, the customer would have no risk and all the risk would be on the retailer. If the retailer did not completely hedge the contract at the time it was closed, meaning that they did not buy all the gas they needed to generate the customer’s electric needs, the retailer would suffer the loss instead of the customer. Of course, retailers will typically be more financially sophisticated and have hedging instruments in place that would prevent, or at least mitigate, this loss.

        There are options available to customers to guard against this kind of loss. Customers can purchase caps6 on the natural gas price such that it never exceeds a certain value. Suppose that the customer in the above example purchased a cap at $6.50. This would result in an overall electric cost of $.071/kwh, resulting in approximately an 8% increase in costs, corresponding to the increase in the price of natural gas. Of course, there will be additional costs because the retailer will have built in the cost for the caps on natural gas. Even more disconcerting is that if the natural gas market never reaches the level of the caps, the customer has paid for something that is useless.

        Second Con: Budgetary Uncertainty One of the most troubling aspects of the heat rate contract is the budgetary uncertainty it engenders. A customer’s total electric cost consists of the price per unit and the volume. In a fixed rate contract, the customer knows the total costs and can use historical load data as a proxy for anticipated consumption. This allows the customer to predict their total electric costs with some level of certainty. However, in a heat rate contract, especially one where the cost may change several times, budgetary certainty is far from assured. For instance, in a 12-month contract, if the customer locks in half of the contract when the natural gas price strip for the next six months is $6.00/MMBtu, the customer will pay $.0675/kwh for the first half of the contract. If, when the customer decides to trigger the natural gas price for the second half of the contract, the price has increased to $6.33/MMBtu, the customer will now pay $.07/kwh. In other words, the cost of electricity may change in a heat rate product and the only way to obtain budgetary certainty is to look back after the contract is over and determine the actual costs.

        Third Con: Flexibility is not always available Implicit in the above examples is the assumption that the customer has the ability to lock in the natural gas price component of the electric costs at any time. However, this is not an entirely accurate assumption. At some point, the gas price will have to become fixed. This will typically occur when the natural gas contract for prompt month closes on the exchange. If the customer fails to lock in the natural gas price before that time, they are stuck with the monthly closing price. Depending on the structure of the contract, the closing price may apply only to the next month or for the entire contract term. This can be especially detrimental if a customer was waiting for the natural gas market to hit a target price.

        Alternative contract structure: Similar results without the risk The great advantage of the heat rate product is that gives the customer the ability to take advantage of a decrease in the cost of natural gas, especially in Texas. However, the Texas market has already evolved to offer a product with similar results without the risks. This can be accomplished through a “blend and extend”. Basically, this contract structure allows the customer to extend their contract beyond the initial term in exchange for a lower rate for the remainder of the term of the initial contract as well as a lower rate for the extension period. This allows a customer to take advantage of a decline in the price of natural gas while not exposing the customer to all of the risks associated with a heat rate contract.

        A “blend and extend” option is actually attractive to both the customer and the retailer. It benefits the customer by lowering their costs and helps the retailer by avoiding any additional costs associated with capturing the customer again when the current contract expires.

        Conclusion: The risks outweigh the benefits Retailers in the Texas market promote heat rate products by telling customers that these contracts give them more control over their final electric costs and empower them to take advantage of decreases in the natural gas market. However, the risks of this contract structure—cost increases if the price of natural gas increases, lack of budgetary certainty, and limited flexibility in terms of locking in electric costs—outweigh the potential benefit, especially because the benefits of a heat rate contract can be obtained without the risk through the use of blend and extend contracts. For buyers in Texas considering heat rate products, the adage caveat emptor should take on a whole new meaning.

        [edit] Jo Motyka

        EDUCATION

        Postgraduate Studies - New York University, School of Continuing Education and Professional Studies, NYC, 2002, 2004. Concentrations in Art Philosophy, History.

        B.A., Montclair State University, Upper Montclair, NJ, 1991. Concentrations in Drawing, Painting, Color Theory, Art Philosophy, History, Education (N.J. C of E), and Criminal Justice.


        CAREER

        Artist, Studio in Verona, NJ, June 1991 to Present; Currently works in oil, acrylic, encaustic and alternate materials on canvas and hard substrates, paper works, wall mounted and free-standing sculpture.

        Biograpy

        “The earliest memory I have of working at art, is from sometime around the age of five. I drew a baker in his kitchen and was going to give it to a friend of mine, but never did. I think I still have it somewhere."


        Jo Motyka received his Bachelor of Arts degree from Montclair State College in 1991, with concentrations in drawing, painting and color theory. This was the last time he had an institutionalized format of study in the arts. Well before the end of his college studies he had developed a reputation as one of Calcia Fine Arts Department’s most prominent members and was one of the longest consecutively showing student artists. During the years between 1989 and 1992, Motyka participated in a number of well-received exhibitions.


        “I felt I had a rather unique position in college. I had held a job relating to the arts since I was in high school and I found that this gave me a slight advantage over many of the people who were studying with me. By the end of my college career I was helping to manage what was, at the time, a pretty good art gallery that also did art auctions as well as off-the-wall sales. When an artist approached the gallery I was one of the people who would give the thumbs up or down to all their hard work. All we had to do was say yes or no-nothing more. We either said we could sell their work, directed them to another gallery that we thought could sell their work with a recommendation if we liked them, without, if we just wanted to get rid of them but still be nice. But, most often we just told them that we didn't think that we could sell their work. Some we just plainly dismissed from the gallery floor. So when everyone who took fine arts like I did, thought that they were going to be the next big thing after graduation from the artist mill, well, I just turned my part time job into a full time one, at least it was one in the arts."


        Upon completion of his Bachelors in 1991 he took a full-time position at David Gary, Ltd. that soon placed him in the number two position. It would not be until he left the employ of for profit gallery culture in 1997 that new horizons in the art world would be realized. At the time, the artist undertook a small number of transitional works in an effort to fill the gap that time had left from his last portfolio without having a giant leap of progression to account for in imagery or statement.

        Concurrently another transition was taking place. Motyka left his gallery position and formed a company of his own, Äpomo, Associates. Seeking to broaden his prospects, he took a position with Professionals for Non-Profits (PNP), a NYC based agency that provided temps exclusively to non- profits; from cultural centers to major museums and international philanthropic institutions. PNP was his first real insider's view into the museum culture, its workings and the people who worked in it. Within the year, Motyka had left PNP and went to work independently for institutions such as The Jewish Museum and The Metropolitan Museum of Art and privately for a gambit of clients that range from collectors to the Alexander S. Onassis Public Benefit Foundation.


        “I had been dealing in the light restoration of paintings since 1989 and more extensive restorations since 1992. I studied the practices of Gustav Berger, the internationally recognized painting conservator and theorist in the field, but have mainly used the traditional methods employed by Max Doerner, the noted professor of The Academy of Fine Arts in Munich at the turn of the last century. I just sort of picked it up and then set about trying to get better at it because I was interested in restoration, just to see if I could do it. I spent a number of years studying rather esoteric books and papers on the subject. My attempts to put out a transitional portfolio chronically fell along the wayside. From 1997 to the middle of 2000, my artwork was going nowhere at all. I did a couple of pieces now and again, but nothing was right on. Then it dawned on me that maybe the materials that were used for my restoration projects could be manipulated to construct my art."


        In March of 2000 Motyka turned to the world of high end picture framing at Knight Works, framers catering to a very select client base in NYC such as: Louise Bourgeois, Cheim and Read, Todd Eberle, Elliott Erwitt, Adam Fuss, Houk Gallery, Pace MacGill, Robert Miller Gallery, Bruce Webber, as well as the estates of Diane Arbus, Jean Michel Basquiat, Robert Mapplethorpe, Keith Haring, Andy Warhol. Also during this time, Motyka taught a gallery and museum studies class at William Paterson University and was the Student Projects Coordinator of their Power Arts Center, during of the fall and spring of 2000 - 01 semesters.


        “So here I am, the new adjunct for the fall semester for a class that was originally taught by the Dean of Fine Art, no pressure. I haven't produced any real artwork in years and I've committed to Midland Gallery of Montclair for a works on paper show in October of 2000, a media I haven't worked in actively for many years, no pressure. My nine to five job is slowly killing me mentally and physically, it's only a question of time before my head or body quits. Pressure, there's a lot of pressure."


        In August 2001, Motyka returned to work on his own art making. By October the first elements of his latest work were being unveiled to the public, with an overwhelming response. The work consisting of mainly encaustic and resins on linen and cotton canvas. More works on paper and a number of encaustic and resins encapsulations of found object assemblage sculptures were now in the process of being completed for 2002. Over the summer of 2002 the first showings of the new portfolio began at Midland Gallery with the portfolio being shopped around to New York Galleries for the first time in years by the fall.


        ‘I remember the first time I met Ivan Karp. I was meeting a friend outside his gallery, O.K. Harris in SOHO. I knew a bit about him from art history classes and books, but after all these years had never been inside the gallery – I had been in the cigar shop that is built in to the front of the gallery, but never the gallery itself. He was standing in the foyer of the gallery mopping the floor, in his Brooks Brothers suit, smoking a cigar; little did he know or care that I was stalking him today for a review of my work. The gallery prides itself on reviewing everything that it is sent to them, but only affiliates about one in ten thousand applicants. This was my first day out of the house with the new portfolio in hand and when I had a good look around and he was back at his desk, I presented my portfolio for his review and asked him to send it back to me when he was done with it. To my surprise he said, “Let’s do this now, just give me a minute to look this over.” I just walked off nonchalantly to look ant some photographs in the back galley and thought ‘Boy he can’t wait to kick me in the ball today, can he!” As it turned out he called me back over and said,”Mr. Motyka, I see something here.’ to my further surprise. He asked me, “You live in Verona?’ I replied, “Yes.”, he continued, “Your studio’s in Verona?’, I, “Yes!”, he said, “Well I ain’t going to Verona, but you get together some work to bring in and I’ll look at it, also take this to Stephen Heller and tell him I sent you and he should have a look at this it’s just his thing and get some others to come and see the work at the same time.” “OK.”, I said, and thanked him for his consideration. Now I didn’t get a show right off the back…..It took quite a bit of not so gentle persuasion to get Ivan to come see the work, especially since we couldn’t get anybody to come out and view the work with him; it is nearly imposable to get two art dealer’s schedules to sync at the same time even if they were mildly interested in the first place. Eventually we coxed Ivan into a meeting, at the IBM building, in mid-town Manhattan and in fifteen minutes he declared to all present that I was to have a show within the next year or two……..this was going to be a rough couple of years.’


        In the early part of 2003 a number of the Paper were exhibited at Midland Gallery. The fall of 2003 fostered new wall mounted and paper works and early in 2004, Innuta, a NYC design firm, incorporated a number of Motyka’s works into their collection at their East Village location. More and more research and technical development of paints and finishing was the total focus now that the O.K. Harris show had been set for the first month of 2006. In the late spring of 2004, a number of large scale canvases where accompanied by a series of large works on paper. A small selection of these works on paper where shown first at Midland Gallery in 2005. One of the largest paintings from this time, ’Mass Yellow Field’ was to be included as part of Motyka’s largest show to date at O.K. Harris. In 2005 we also saw the launch of a new web site for the artist, [http//www.jomotyka.com].

        The exhibit had full page ads for January in Artforum, Art in America and the December/January issue of Modern Painters magazines and ran from January 14th – February 11th of 2006. The schedule also included galleries of work by Richard Friedberg, Moses Hoskins, Lilyan Aloma, & Ellen Stavitsky. Motyka’s exhibition of large abstract paintings, comprised layers of powdered pigment and glass in polyurethane and methyl-cellulose on canvas and medium density fiberboard, where in, the artist imparts brush stroke and pallet knife effects that build the layers of the work and then uses physical and chemical reduction of the materials that enhance the monochromatic surface of the painting. The series of works formed the back bone of the show at Harris.


        ‘Together we brought in some one thousand people, by Ivan’s estimation that first night and between three to five hundred people on the subsequent Saturdays. Sales were dismal though nothing went and only local press coverage, don’t get me wrong the news stories were great some of the best writing that I’ve ever seen on the work, they just didn’t get very far a field. Knight Landsman stopped in at some point and left me a note that he enjoyed the work and requested that I keep him apprised of my future endeavors which was nice of him, but I found a number of bones to pick with the ‘NYC Metro Area’ out of work poets that fashion themselves ‘Art Critics’ and told one or two what I thought of the bullshit that surrounds them and makes fertile their subsidiary existence.’


        January of 2006 was to be spent in research and reflection, but two new works on wood panel somehow came to light and are on displayed over the summer with a few works of paper and sculpture at Midland Gallery. The second work in The Medusa Series is set for production over this same time period and should be on offer for viewing sometime in the fall of 2006.

            Sohail Rana, who is the son of renowned poet Rana Akbar Abadi, was born in his native city,Agra, Uttar Pradesh, India, in 1938, to a highly literary, academic and respectable family.

            Rana Akbar Abadi migrated to Karachi, Pakistan.Soon after partition, young Sohail, who was in his ninth year at that time, resumed his education and subsequently passed his intermediate examination.

            He demonstrated an unusual aptitude for music as a teenager. He learned music from various experts and familiarized himself with numerous musical instruments, which added to his experience.

            During his primary education days, he was admitted to late A.M. Qureshi's Mary Colaso School, Karachi, where Sohail Rana and Waheed Murad were class fellows.

            In 1989 and 1990, Mr. Dick Scott was named "Manager of the Year" by the music industry Poll Star Magazine. It was not only a title for which he had worked diligently - it was an honor for which he had prepared exetensively and richly deserved.

            Dick Scott has been the manager of some of the hottest and most successful artists in the music business. In Fact, he is known throughout the industryas a merchandising and marketing genius. One of his great successes was the management of The New Kids On The Block, who made merchandising history by generating more than $800 million dollars in merchandising income alone in 1990. His concepts revolutionized the way recording artist and motion picturea are now being marketed. Some of his other successful artists have included; Boyz II Men, Marky Mark, James Ingram, Patti Austin and Teddy Riley, all of whom continue to have distinguished careers.

            In short, Dick Scott not only knows, but understands talent and its requirements to be nurtured. Having worked in nearly all aspects of the music business, he has guided artists to achieve more than they even dreamed of doing.

            Mr. Scott's path to artistic management and development began in Macon, Georgia. Although he graduated from the historic Talladega College with a degree in chemistry, his love for music and drama won out and set him on his course. Among his many early endeavors, he served as a Band Director at his former high school, organized a touring A'cappella chorus for the military; and became Assistant to the President and Director od Public Relations for Talladega College.

            In 1960 he moved to Detroit, Michigan, and after serving as the Assistant to the President of the Detroit Institute of Commerce became the Assistant Station Manager at WCHB-WCHD FM Radio Station - the first Black owned and operated radio station in the country. Incidentally, it was during this period that Dick honed his merchandising and marketing skills by developing concepts for the station that won many national merchandising awards.

            He then joined the staff of Motown records as Assistant to President Barry Gordy, and ultimately became the road manager to the number one female singing group of all time - The Supremes. He then went on to a string of achievement. He became the first black manager of the Michigan State Fair, including responsibility for overall entertainment and space allocation. Next, he was the Director of Administration for CBS Records (now Sony Music), which then became the most successful marketer of Black Music in the history of the music business.

            Deciding to get involed with the motion picture production, Dick moved to Los Angeles to gain knowledge of the film business. He aligned himself with Martin Poll, considered to be the Dean of Hollywood Producers, where Dick learned the business from his most repected mentor. His first original idea was bought by Universal Studios and palced in development. Recently, he joined forces with long-time friend Russell Rocke, and acquired the rights to "Tom", the best selling biography of Tennessee Williams, which they palm to film, either as a motion picture or as a television documentary or mini series. Going back to his roots in Macon, Georgia, he is planning a film based on the amazing story of the Douglas family, who created a touring show called "Salas Greene from New Orleans." This touring show show palyed all over America traveling via a lavish railroad train, which served as living quarters, rehearsal space and tranportation. During this period, Blacks were not allowed accommodations in major hotels.

            Dick is an amazing writer and conceptualist himself, but has surrounded himself with brilliant young witers with whom he served as mentors. Several scripts by his group are in development and are being submitted. It is generally agreed that his entry into the film business will be as spectacular and successful as his other ventures.

            At the historic Pasadena Playhouse his 1996 production of Larry Hart's "Sisterella" became the most successful production in the eighty-year-old history of the Playhouse. The following year, the life story of the famous sisters, The Emotions, who became platinum selling recording artists, was told in an oringinal musical, "Bigger Than Bubblegum". It too, was an acclaimed success at Pasadena and subsequently palyed Lincoln Center in New York.
