  1. I am in principle interested in meeting for a root beer. However, with the semester winding down and having been sick recently, the near future isn't such a good time. Remind me again in January. ;)
  2. If you're referring to the Higgs boson diagram in the physics program section of the ATLAS experiment article, then the answer is yes. The particular diagram I made has a great deal to do with the capabilities of the detector. The top quarks make the event very distinctive, which aids in detecting and analyzing the event in various ways. One produces a high-pT lepton which aids in triggering on the event, while the other decays entirely into jets so that its mass can be reconstructed with good accuracy. Both produce a bottom quark jet, which can be identified to confirm that there were top quarks and not just random jets. (The presence of top quarks, in particular, is by far the most likely, because the Higgs boson couples to quarks in proportion to their mass, and it's by far the heaviest quark.) Then the Higgs itself decays into bottom quarks (the most likely quark if it's too heavy to decay into top quarks), which can hopefully also be identified. So we end up with (if everything works) four b-jets and two light jets. We can add one b and the two light jets so that we reconstruct a top quark, then pick two of the three remaining b's (by trial and error) and get one whose invariant mass is likely to be the higgs. If we see a bunch of events like that, with the same invariant mass, we've made a discovery. Of course, complex as the above is, with mis-tagging and so on the analysis is actually much more complicated. ;)

Hope that answers your question. Feel free to ask if you've got more. -- SCZenz 23:25, 1 December 2005 (UTC)

Thanks for uploading Image:Lalibela.jpg. However, the image may soon be deleted unless we can determine the copyright holder and copyright status. The Wikimedia Foundation is very careful about the images included in Wikipedia because of copyright law (see Wikipedia's Copyright policy).

The copyright holder is usually the creator, the creator's employer, or the last person who was transferred ownership rights. Copyright information on images on Wikipedia is signified using copyright templates. The three basic license types on Wikipedia are open content, public domain, and fair use. Find the appropriate template in Wikipedia:Image copyright tags and place it on the image page like this: {{TemplateName}}.

Please signify the copyright information on any other images you have uploaded or will upload. Remember that images without this important information can be deleted by an administrator.
