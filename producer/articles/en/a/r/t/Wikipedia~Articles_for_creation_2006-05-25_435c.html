[edit] United States v. Oakland Cannabis Buyers' Coop

United States v. Oakland Cannabis Buyers' Coop

Criminal Procedure: Federal Rules of Criminal Procedure, Narcotics Facts of the Case

Under California's Compassionate Use Act of 1996, the Oakland Cannabis Buyers' Cooperative was organized to distribute marijuana to qualified patients for medical purposes. In 1998, the United States sued to enjoin the Cooperative and its executive director. The government argued that the Cooperative's activities violated the Controlled Substances Act's prohibitions on distributing, manufacturing, and possessing with the intent to distribute or manufacture a controlled substance. Although the District Court enjoined it, the Cooperative continued to distribute marijuana. Rejecting the Cooperative's medical necessity defense, the court found the Cooperative in contempt. On appeal, the Court of Appeals concluded that the medical necessity defense was a legally cognizable defense. On remand from the Court of Appeals, the District Court modified its injunction to incorporate a medical necessity defense, under which medically necessary distributions were to be permitted. Question Presented

Does a medical necessity exception to the Controlled Substances Act's prohibition on the manufacture and distribution of various drugs, including marijuana, exist? Conclusion

No. In an 8-0 opinion delivered by Justice Clarence Thomas, the Court held that there is no medical necessity exception to the Controlled Substances Act's prohibitions on manufacturing and distributing marijuana. "The statute reflects a determination that marijuana has no medical benefits worthy of an exception, " wrote Justice Thomas, therefore, "medical necessity is not a defense to manufacturing and distributing marijuana." Justice John Paul Stevens' concurring opinion, which was joined by Justices David H. Souter and Ruth Bader Ginsburg, argued that "[b]ecause necessity was raised in this case as a defense to distribution, the Court need not venture an opinion on whether the defense is available to anyone other than distributors." Justice Stephen G. Breyer took no part in the consideration or decision of the case.

[edit] Clearlooks

Clearlooks is a theme engine for GTK, the main widget toolkit used by the GNOME desktop environment. It is based on Red Hat's Bluecurve them. The theme engine draws the actual widgets, and can be configured with 'themes' that invoke the theme engine with different configurations. It is the default theme for many Linux distributions including Fedora core 4 and 5, Ubuntu before 6.06 and SUSE. Many users have contributed themes that have changed the colours and some visual effects, leading to a huge number of clearlooks based themes on theme sites such as art.gnome.org art.gnome.org and www.gnomelook.org GnomeLook

The README distributed with Clearlooks 0.6 reads

This is a GTK+ 2.x engine based on Bluecurve. It features a modern look without sacrificing (much) speed. The lead developers are Richard Stellingwerff and Daniel Borgmann


There is currently a development version that uses Cairo and a stable one that just uses gdk to draw the widgets.

Ubuntu Linux uses a derivative version called Ubuntulooks as of version 6.06 that is more shiny than Clearlooks, which uses smooth gradients and soft colours.

[edit] Connection Slot

Minimum time a host must transmit for before it can be sure that no other host's packet has collided with its transmission. If the maximum propagation delay from one host to any other is T, then a host that starts to transmit at time t0 may collide with a host that starts just before t0 + T. The first host will not detect the collision until time t0 + 2T.

[edit] Physical Nullification

This is a hypothesized process in Physics, named such by Dr. Robert Forward, but possibly first suggested by Isaac Asimov. In Quantum Mechanics, various Symmetry rules are associated with fundamental descriptions of how things work. Charge Conjugation, for example, allows anti-matter to exist, which feature particles in every way like ordinary protons and electrons, except that they have opposite electrical charges. [edit] Order of the Torch

[edit] History

Realizing there was no single organization which recognized Florida International University's campus life leaders, five students gathered in October of 2003 to establish the first honorary leadership society at FIU named "Order of the Torch." These five student leaders include: Omar Castillo, Sanjay Dhawan, Marlon Font, Kadian McIntosh, and Clayton Solomon. The initiators acknowledged that involved students at FIU would most likely follow the same path of being leaders and playing important roles within all aspects of society. Moreover, they were likely to remain committed to their alma mater. The founders sought to apply the brilliant individual strengths towards united goals. The organization stressed leadership development and university advancement as seen in the motto, “Non Mihi, Non Tibi, Sed Nobis.”translated to mean "Not for you, Not for Me, but for Us". By September of 2004, Order of the Torch had inducted nineteen outstanding student leaders, who by election and attainment, held places of prominence and confidence in the student body.


[edit] Purpose

The purpose of the organization is to provide recognition to students who have excelled in their leadership roles and made significant contributions to the Florida International University community. Furthermore, this organization seeks to foster the vision and mission of the university while providing its members with opportunities for personal and professional advancement.


[edit] Membership

The Order of the Torch will have as its members the uppermost percentile of the most prominent student leaders on campus. Among the university population of 37,000, the society very selectively admits only 30 or so members from the university's undergraduate and graduate population. The Order recognizes students that have demonstrated their moral character, integrity and exemplary leadership through active service to the university. Becoming part of this selected group of students is a prestigious honor. Membership to this organization is a lifetime commitment.

[edit] Parametric equaliser

A parametric equaliser is an audio filter that allows the frequency of maximum cut or boost to be set by one control, and the size of the cut or boost by another. These settings, the frequency level of the peak or trough, are two of the parameters of a frequency response curve, and in a two-control equaliser they completely describe the curve. More elaborate parametric equalisers may allow other parameters to be varied, such as skew. These parameters each describe some aspect of the response curve seen as a whole, over all frequencies. A graphic equaliser provides individual level controls for various frequency bands, each of which acts only on that particular frequency band.
