Don Christian Willie is a nationally recognized Humanitarian and Academic Scholar.

Don was born to Jane Pasimeni Willie and Ren Willie in March of 1988 in Salt Lake City, Utah. As an attendant of Wasatch Elementary, Bryan Middle School and graduate of East High School in Salt Lake, Don has become a well known figure in the local community and nationally as well. Throughout his primary and secondary education, Don excelled academically with Advanced Placement courses and high honors marks as well as participated whole heartedly in many organizations, committees, programs and recreational team as a participant and leader. These include: The National Honor Society, SPOTS Club (President), Key Club, Rotary Interact, Health Science Students of America (Secretary), Best Buddies (President), LDS Seminary (Co-President), Politically Active Students (President), Hugh O'Brian Youth Leadership Conference, NAMI National Teen Board, YouthLINC STARTS Program (Student Director), Maryland Boy's State (Fort City Mayor and Senate Minority Leader), National History Day (National Contestant), The United States Senate Page Program and many more. Atop his community involvement, Don is also physically active through his participation in: Soccer, Lacrosse, Cross Country, and Tennis.

Don's participation in these organizations has brought about prestigious recognition through many accolades. In 2006, Don was named Utah's Young Humanitarian of the Year and was awarded a $5,000.00 scholarship. As a humanitarian, Don has coordinated many successful efforts to assist others throughout the world. Recipients of his efforts include: United States Armed Service Members in Afghanistan, infants in Ghana, peoples with mental and physical disabilities and many more. Don was also named Utah's Young Volunteer of the Year for Utah in 2005 through the Commission on Volunteers, the Salt Lake City School District High School Volunteer of the year in 2005, recipient of three Gold Presidential Service Awards as well as the Salt Lake Rotary Club Service Above Self Award and Scholarship. Don's passion for service has made him an amiable person and an understanding and compassionate citizen.

Along with his Humanitarian work, Don has a passion for the Political Realm. In the spring of 2005, Don was appointed as a United States Senate Page by the Honorable Robert F. Bennett (R-UT). As a Page, Don relocated his life to Washington, D.C. where he lived just a few minutes away from the United States Capitol Building in Webster Hall. Along with twenty-six other students from around the nation, Don worked daily in the Senate, interacting with many prestigious national officials. Don also received a Senatorial Nomination to the United States Naval Academy from Senator Bennett in 2006. To follow his passion in public service and civic duty, one of Don's majors at the University of Utah is Political Science.

Along with his major in Political Science, Don is also a Biology Major. With his Double Major, Don will attend Medical School to specialize in Pediatric Neurosurgery. He is currently as sophomore at the University of Utah and active in the Bennion Center service organization as a Co-Chairperson of Freshman Service Corps and a Member and Balanced Man Scholar of Sigma Phi Epsilon Fraternity.

Methanococcus
Scientific classification
Kingdom: Archaea
Phylum: Euryarchaeota
Class: Methanococci
Order: Methanococcales
Family: Methanococcaceae
Genus: Methanococcus

Methanococcus is a hyperthermophilic genus of methanogen. The sequencing of its genes have resulted in the discovery of many novel and Eukaryote-like sequences. A notable species, 'Methanococcus jannaschii', discovered at the base of a white smoker on the East Pacific Rise, was the first organism from Archaea to have its genome completely sequenced.

Panaque maccus, commonly called the clown pleco or ringlet pleco, is a dwarf loricariid. It originates in Colombia and Venezuala in a clear water biotope. Their natural environment driftwood tangles near riverbanks.

[edit] Panaque Maccus in the Aquarium

The clown pleco attains a maximum size of 4" which makes it an ideal species for small (10 gallons+) aquaria. In the aquarium, panaque maccus eat primarily vegetable matter and they must be provided with driftwood, as wood forms a large part of their diet. Their diet should also occasionally be supplemented with higher protein foods such as shrimp pellets, bloodworms, glassworms, etc. Loricariids are constant grzers, due to this fact, they produce large amounts of waste. They should be provided with a strong current in the aquarium and plenty of surfaces to attach themselves. Ideally, the aquarium should be a river set up, using pvc pipes to achieve a one directional flow. The water should be fairly warm, 78F-81F. Multiple individuals should be kept in a larger tank with plenty of hiding places to allow each fish to establish a territory. The clown pleco is a reclusive fish and will rarely stray from its chosen piece of driftwood, usually swimming no more than 6" in either direction in search of food. They do well with tankmates and are a good species for a community aquarium. Mature males of this species will develop interopercular odontodes on the rear half of the body.

[edit] Early Life

Major General Robert Jesse Travis was born on January 13, 1877 at Conyers, Georgia, the son of Dr. A. C. W. Travis and the former Allie Livingston. He received no formal education until the age of twelve. In 1891, he enrolled in the preparatory school of Emory College; he later attended Emory College and was graduated with first honors in 1897. While at Emory, Travis distinguished himself as a champion debater, and as editor of two campus publications, the Phoenix and the Zodiac. He received scholastic medals in Greek, Latin, the natural sciences, and mathematics; he was also president of Phi Gamma society and a member of Chi Phi social fraternity. After graduation, Travis took a position as principal of a Madison, Georgia high school to finance graduate work in law and economics at the University of Georgia. He received the Bachelor of Law degree in 1899. While attending the University of Georgia, Travis was awarded the Ready Writers' Medal, was president of Phi Kappa society, and a charter member of the Sphinx men's honorary society.

[edit] Military Career

General Travis' military career began in 1899, when he entered the 1st Georgia Infantry on August 25. He was transferred to the Coast Artillery in 1902. In World War I, Travis (then a colonel) commanded the 118th Field Artillery. For his valorous wartime service, he was awarded the Commander's Cross of Poland in 1919, but was not allowed to accept this award immediately, as the U. S. State Department did not officially recognize decorations of the Polish government at that time. Travis received his commission as major general in the National Guard in 1924. In the same year, while attending a convention of the National Guard Association in Louisville, Kentucky, Travis proposed to the convention that the National Guard become a part of the U. S. Army on a regular component basis. The convention adopted this resolution, as did Congress two years later. In 1932, General Travis participated in the dedication of a National Guard memorial in Paris, presided over by General John J. Pershing.

During his military career, General Travis served as president of the Georgia Reserve Officers' Association, the Georgia National Guard Association, and the National Guard Association of the U. S. respectively. In 1911, General Travis was officially retired from active military service at a military review at Fort Jackson, South Carolina in which 17,000 troops marched in his honor.

[edit] Attorney at Law

In addition to his military career, General Travis practiced law from 1899 until his retirement in 1951, serving twice as attorney for the city of Savannah, and also as attorney for McIntosh and Effingham counties. In 1932, Travis was an unsuccessful candidate for the Georgia Supreme Court. He was rent control attorney for Savannah under post-World War II restrictions set down by the federal Office of Price Administration (OPA).

[edit] Civic and Fraternal Memberships

General Travis was actively involved in a number of civic and fraternal organizations. He served as the first president of the Municipal League of Georgia. In 1918, Travis became Grand Master of Georgia Masons. In 1919, General Travis was decorated by the Grand Masonic Lodge of England. In addition to his Masonic activities, General Travis served as preceptor of the Scottish Rite, and prior to his death, he was the oldest living past potentate of Savannah's Alee Temple of the Shrine. Travis was a past president of the Georgia Sons of the Revolution and the University of Georgia Club. He was also a member of the Colonial Wars Society, the Telfair Academy of Arts and Sciences, the St. Andrews Society, and the American Legion, among others. The Wesley Monumental Methodist Church of Savannah claimed General Travis as a member.

General Travis was an amateur historian of some note. He turned over historic deeds and records to the Georgia Historical Society, and his endeavors in this field were instrumental in leading to the discovery of a Revolutionary War battlefield in Savannah. After exhausting genealogical research, Travis also compiled a Travis family history, published in Savannah in 1954.

[edit] Family Life

General Travis married the former Rena Falligant (1881-1964) of Savannah on November 27, 1902. The couple had four children: two sons, Brigadier General Robert Falligant Travis (1904-1950), USAF, and Colonel William Livingston Travis (l908-), USAF (retired); and two daughters, Rena Travis White (1915-) and Cecilia Darracott Travis Smith.

His son, Brigadier General Robert F. Travis, was a distinguished flyer in World War II, and later commanding general of the Pacific Air Command until its termination in 1949, was the man for whom Travis Air Force Base in California and Travis Field, Savannah's municipal air-port, were named.

Publications of Robert J. Travis include: an article, "Sidney Lanier," in The Georgian, III, no. 4 (January, 1899); The Travis (Travers) Family and Its Allies: Darracott, Lewis, Livingston, Nicholson, McLaughlin, Pharr, Smith, and Terrell (Savannah, 1954); Travis, George M. Napier, and Robert L. Colding co-compiled the Masonic Manual and Code of the Grand Lodge of Free and Accepted Masons of Georgia, 3 eds. (Nashville: Brandon Printing Co., 1920).

James Paul Hilton (1961-) is an internationally published essayist, poet, and alternative health author, as well as a medical educator and civil liberties activist. Born in Jersey City, New Jersey, in 1961 Hilton dropped out of high school, became an Eagle Scout and certified emergency medical technician(1977), all by the young age of 16. By his ninteeth birthday he was working full time as a paramedic in Tulsa, Oklahoma and then continued his education in the heart of "Amish Country"(Lancaster,Pennsylvania). By 1981 he was employed by busy Broward County, Florida as as an emergency medical specialist where he earned a GED, studied medical hypnosis, and began to teach advanced cardiac life support techniques to doctors, nurses, and other paramedics. In 1984 while working as educator of paramedics for the City of Hallandale, Florida he blew the whistle on inadaquate training and "sloppy medical care," landing himself on the front page of The Miami Herald, triggering an official inquiry, ending up blackballed from working, and then for the next year and a half literally homeless on the same streets where he once saved lives. In 1987 he met his soon to be wife, Salome, and resumed teaching at hospitals and medical schools across the United States. In 1989 he ended up as the first paramedic in Gallup, New Mexico, epicenter of the Navaho, Zuni, and Hopi Indian tribes where he learned about the highly secretive "medicine men." That same year he went to his wive's native New Zealand were he ultimatley became a dual United States/New Zealand Citizen, a businessman, an outspoken environmental activist, and authored an alternative health book sharply critical of profit driven medicine entitled, Burden of Proof (1992). While promoting the book in neighboring Australia, the Australian Medical Association (AMA) in three states tried to have it banned, propelling it to become a best seller. After repeated appearances on national television and after crisscrossing both countries on a lecture tour covering every major city, twice over, the book was reprinted three times. Because of the historical link between the martial and healing arts, while in Australasia, Hilton took up Shotokan Karate (traditional Okinawa style), went on to study and teach judo, and ultimatley earned a black belt in karate. In 1999 he was keynote speaker at the Natural Health Society of Australia's annual conference in Sydney and earlier that same year was invited by tribal elders to speak at a Maori Marae in Rotorua, New Zealand. In 2001 one of his his poems, Arlington County Medic -75, penned in honor of the victims of 9/11, was republished by Zondervan International Publishers and was also commended by several United States Senators including presidential candidate John Edwards, who described it as "inspiring." Hilton has written nine books, hundreds of published essays, poems, and letters, and was awarded honrary membership in the Natural Health Society of Australia as well as, for several years, a member of the National Association of EMS Educators in The U.S.A. Much of his work can be found in the Library of Congress, the National Library of New Zealand and the National Library of Australia.

[edit] Brementowne Mall

Brementowne Mall was located at 159th Street and Oak Park Avenue in Tinley Park, Illinois. It opened in the early 1970s, with twin movie theaters and a variety of stores. The mall lacked a big-name department store anchor; instead, it featured Gately's, an independent department store that opened in 1975 (its other location, in the Roseland neighborhood of Chicago, closed in 1994). Other "name" stores included Walgreens and a Dominicks grocery store, as well a Hallmark card shop and numerous smaller retailers and a video arcade.

In the mid-1970s, the movie theater moved to the other side of the mall, where is was rebuilt with four screens.

The mall began to decline in popularity when the much larger and well-anchored Orland Square Mall opened a few miles away. To draw more traffic, the movie theaters were increased to six small screens in the 1980s and began offering second-run movies at bargain prices.

The mall's troubles continued, with Gately's closing in 1994 and Dominicks soon following. After its closure, the Gateley's building went through several temporary incarnations, including a Lands End outlet, before becoming office space for the Star and Southtown Economist newspapers. The Dominick's space became a supply store for teachers.

The movie theaters hung on for awhile, along with the adjacent Lindy's/Gerties restaurant. With the rest of the mall virtually deserted, it was eventually purchased by the Menard's home improvement chain and converted into a mega Menard's store. In the early 2000s, Menard's also absorbed the movie theaters.

[edit] An interview with Cosmus Cozier

Cosmus Cozier was born in Bequia, St. Vincent and the Grenadines (SVG) in the Caribbean in 1943. He grew up in St. Vincent and attended primary and secondary school there. He sat the General Certificate of Education (GCE) and being successful in seven subjects, entered the civil service in 1961 as a junior auditor. He held the positions between 1964 through 1978 as follows: Senior auditor, Administrative officer, Senior Executive Officer in the Ministry of Labor, senior accountant, Treasury Department, Deputy Post Master and Senior Executive Officer in the Public Works Department. In November of 1978, he immigrated to The United States with his wife. His four children –Rohan, Colin, Anthony, and Garry followed in 1979. In that same year he returned to St. Vincent and the Grenadines and contested the General Elections held there. He was successful but his party lost. He was named leader of the opposition but he resigned and returned to the U.S. to resume his job as a banker at Chemical Bank- New York. He remained there as an officer until the merger with Chase Manhattan Bank came about. He left the bank in 1998 and became a consultant to the said bank. In 2000, he returned to St. Vincent and contested the General Elections held in 2001. This time, his party won but he was unsuccessful. He was however given the position of Consul General of his country to the United States; a position he holds currently. He holds an Associate Degree in Business administration and a Bachelors Degree from Baruch College and a certificate in International relations from New York University. He is a member of several civic organizations and is a Past Grandmaster of the Fraternity of Independent Mechanics.


1. What are the duties of Consul General?

The duties of Consul General are to:

• Carry out the foreign policy of St. Vincent and the Grenadines • Ensure that nationals are protected and provide representation • Issue passports and other travel documents • Consult with U.S. immigration on matters relating to nationals • Assist nationals with obtaining legal representation • Intercede with deportation officers when nationals are to be deported • Act as intermediary between home country and the State Department • Deal with all issues relative to nationals of St. Vincent and the Grenadines

2. Where is St. Vincent located?

St. Vincent and the Grenadines is located at 13˚ 15N, 61˚ 12W in the Caribbean Sea. It is located in the south-western hemisphere. It has a Mountainous Interior with La Soufriere, an active volcano rising to its highest point, 4,000 ft. The average yearly temperature is 81˚F. St. Vincent is the largest of more than thirty islands called the Grenadines. The major islands are Young Island, Bequia, Mustique, Canouan, Mayreau, Union Island, Palm Island and Petit St. Vincent.

3. What type of government does St. Vincent possess?

St. Vincent and the Grenadines is a parliamentary democracy within the common wealth of nations. Queen Elizabeth II is the head of state and is represented on the island by a Governor-General. St. Vincent became independent on October 27, 1979 from Britain. Parliament consists of 15 elected representatives and 6 senators. The parliamentary term of office is 5 years.

4. What is the population of St. Vincent and the Grenadines?

The population is estimated to be 110,000. The ethnic mix consists of 77% African descent, 10% mixed race, 3% Carib, 9% East Indian, .5% Asian and .5% European.

5. How is the local currency related to the U.S. dollar?

The currency is called Eastern Caribbean Currency. The exchange rate is tied to the U.S. dollar at a rate of $2.68.

6. What are some unique things or symbols of St. Vincent and the Grenadines?

The Nine Mornings Celebration is a pre-Christmas tradition that is only held there. It consists of cultural presentations such as singing, caroling, bicycle racing and includes local food and drinks. This is held nine mornings preceding Christmas. The St. Vincent parrot (Amazona Guildingii) is the national bird. It is found only on the island of St. Vincent where it lives in the Tropical Mountainous forests of the island. It is listed as an endangered species and extensive measures have been made to protect it. The flag consists of three colors: blue, yellow and green. The blue represents the blue skies and the blue seas. The yellow represents the sun. The green represents the green verdant nature of the country.

7. What part does tourism play in the economy?

Tourism plays an important part in the economy of SVG since it is the number one in revenue earnings. It is a haven for yachts because of its landlocked harbors and beautiful reefs which attracts divers from all over the world. It appeals to the divers because of its spectacular underwater world.

8. What kinds of animals/mammals are found in St. Vincent?

Since St. Vincent is a tropical island, the animals found there are the usual farm animals and it is one of the few islands where whales are still hunted and killed by harpoon. St. Vincent is allowed by the whaling commission to catch four whales per year. The season runs from mid-January through April. Underwater creatures hunted are called parpus (dolphins) and blackfish.


9. What are the historical and cultural sites to visit?

• St. George’s Anglican Cathedral: It was built in the early 1800s and was built in the Georgian architectural style. It features spectacular stained glass windows one of which was commissioned by Queen Victoria. • Fort Charlotte: This is situated just west of town and over 600 ft. above the bay. It was built in 1796 and named after King George III wife. • Botanical Gardens: Considered one of the most beautiful in the world. The natural wonders of St. Vincent can be seen there. It is the oldest such gardens in the western hemisphere. The gardens were founded in 1762 as a breeding ground for plants brought from other parts of the world. These gardens were Captain Bligh’s original destination. When the mutiny on HMS bounty delayed his first scheduled arrival. A descendant of one of the original breadfruit trees he brought still thrives in the garden today.

10. What kinds of food does St. Vincent offer?

Exotic seafood dishes such as lobster, crabs, conch, wilks and fish are available from most restaurants and hotels. Local dishes such as curry goat, oxtail, grilled fish and North-American dishes can also be provided.
