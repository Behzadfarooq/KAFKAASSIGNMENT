The Compromise of 1850 and the Fugitive Slave Act 1850

Henry Clay, U.S. senator from Kentucky, was determined to find a solution. In 1820 he had resolved a fiery debate over the spread of slavery with his Missouri Compromise. Now, thirty years later, the matter surfaced again within the walls of the Capitol. But this time the stakes were higher -- nothing less than keeping the Union together.

There were several points at issue:

-The United States had recently acquired a vast territory -- the result of its war with Mexico. Should the territory allow slavery, or should it be declared free? Or maybe the inhabitants should be allowed to choose for themselves?

-California -- a territory that had grown tremendously with the gold rush of 1849, had recently petitioned Congress to enter the Union as a free state. Should this be allowed? Ever since the Missouri Compromise, the balance between slave states and free states had been maintained; any proposal that threatened this balance would almost certainly not win approval.

-There was a dispute over land: Texas claimed that its territory extended all the way to Santa Fe.

-Finally, there was Washington, D.C. Not only did the nation's capital allow slavery, it was home to the largest slave market in North America.

On January 29, 1850, the 70-year-old Clay presented a compromise. For eight months members of Congress, led by Clay, Daniel Webster, Senator from Massachusetts, and John C. Calhoun, senator from South Carolina, debated the compromise. With the help of Stephen Douglas, a young Democrat from Illinois, a series of bills that would make up the compromise were ushered through Congress.

According to the compromise, Texas would relinquish the land in dispute but, in compensation, be given 10 million dollars -- money it would use to pay off its debt to Mexico. Also, the territories of New Mexico, Nevada, Arizona, and Utah would be organized without mention of slavery. (The decision would be made by the territories' inhabitants later, when they applied for statehood.) Regarding Washington, the slave trade would be abolished in the District of Columbia, although slavery would still be permitted. Finally, California would be admitted as a free state. To pacify slave-state politicians, who would have objected to the imbalance created by adding another free state, the Fugitive Slave Act was passed.

Of all the bills that made up the Compromise of 1850, the Fugitive Slave Act was the most controversial. It required citizens to assist in the recovery of fugitive slaves. It denied a fugitive's right to a jury trial. (Cases would instead be handled by special commisioners -- commisioners who would be paid $5 if an alleged fugitive were released and $10 if he or she were sent away with the claimant.) The act called for changes in filing for a claim, making the process easier for slaveowners. Also, according to the act, there would be more federal officials responsible for enforcing the law.

For slaves attempting to build lives in the North, the new law was disaster. Many left their homes and fled to Canada. During the next ten years, an estimated 20,000 blacks moved to the neighboring country. For Harriet Jacobs, a fugitive living in New York, passage of the law was "the beginning of a reign of terror to the colored population." She stayed put, even after learning that slave catchers were hired to track her down. Anthony Burns, a fugitive living in Boston, was one of many who were captured and returned to slavery. Free blacks, too, were captured and sent to the South. With no legal right to plead their cases, they were completely defenseless.

Passage of the Fugitive Slave Act made abolitionists all the more resolved to put an end to slavery. The Underground Railroad became more active, reaching its peak between 1850 and 1860. The act also brought the subject of slavery before the nation. Many who had previously been ambivalent about slavery now took a definitive stance against the institution.

The Compromise of 1850 accomplished what it set out to do -- it kept the nation united -- but the solution was only temporary. Over the following decade the country's citizens became further divided over the issue of slavery. The rift would continue to grow until the nation itself divided.

Gaetan Jacques Paquette was born in Drummondville, Quebec, on October 30, 1955, the last of a family of six. His father, Emile Hormidas Paquette, was a house painter and his mother, Rosanne Rosa Lepage, was a home builder. When he was only six years old, the family moved to St. Joseph of Sorel. The young Gaetan went to an English school because his parents were Protestants. Rapidly, he finds out that studying is easy for him. He finishes high school as the runner-up boy and he enters the College regional Bourgchemin, Campus Sorel-Tracy. Two years later, he's accepted at the Institut biblique Beree; this is where he studies to become a minister with The Pentecostal Assemblies of Canada.

Gaetan J. Paquette marries Nicole Tasse in 1978, a young girl from Montreal who is only nineteen years old. They will have two sons, Alexandre Gaetan Paquette (1980) and Jeremie Emile-Simon Paquette (1988). The couple starts in their religious ministry in 1979. While working as a pastor, Gaetan continues his studies. Among other degrees, he obtains a BA in History and a MEd in Andragogy from The University of Montreal. He starts to write and he continues to study. His success can be measured by the fifty-seven honorary doctoral degrees that he receives throughout the years.

Paquette was the Canadian Alliance candidate in the Chambly riding in the 2000 Canadian federal election. A few months beforehand, he had officiated at the wedding of Marc Dupre and Anne-Marie Angelil.

In 2006, Gaetan J. Paquette was accepted as a distinguished member of Global Who's Who and Heritage Who's Who.

Zachary Novak was a member of St. Ambrose Catholic Church in Anderson, Indiana, where he worked as a pianist, organist and choral conductor for several years. He also served as a pianist and choral conductor at Bethel United Methodist Church in Anderson, Indiana. On April 20th, 2006 Novak died in a plane crash with 4 other fellow musicians (Robert Samels, Georgina Joshi, Chris Carducci and Garth Eppley) who where on their way back from a rehearsal with the Lafayette Bach Chorale in Lafayette, Indiana. Joshi piloted the single engine Cessna 206 plane owned by her family, which crashed due to extremely foggy weather conditions approximately a half mile from the Monroe County Airport in Bloomington, Indiana at 11:45pm, on Thursday, April 20th, 2006.

All five of the crash victims of April 20th were well known as rising stars in their fields. Their death rattled the music world on an international level.

Besides his parents Dan and Misty, Zack was also survived by siblings Joel and Brytni Novak, and girlfriend Jillian Shaw. Several scholarships have been organized in memory of Zachary Novak, including those at Anderson University, First United Methodist Church in Bloomington, and the Anderson Community School System.

[edit] System High Mode

System High Mode (also referred to simply as System High) is a mode of using an automated imformation system (AIS) that pretains to an environment that contains restricted data that is classified in a heirarchial scheme, such as Top Secret, Secret and Unclassified. System High Mode is distinguished from other modes (such as Multilevel Security) by its lack of trust of the host AIS system to separate classifications. As a result, all information in a System High AIS is treated as if it were classified at the highest security level of any data in the AIS. For example, Unclassified information can exist in a Secret System High computer but it must be treated as Secret, therefore it cannot be be declassified (unless by reliable human review, which itself is risky because of lack of onmiscient humans.) There is no known technology to securely declassify system high information by automated means because no reliable features of the data can be trusted after having been potentially corrupted by the untrusted host. When unreliable means are used (including Cross Domain Solutions and Bypass Guards) a serious risk of system exploitation via the bypass is introduced. Nevertheless, it is has been done where the resulting risk is overlooked or accepted.

[edit] Continental Air Defence Command

Continental Air Defence Command (CONAD) is a part of the North American Aerospace Defence Command (NORAD). CONAD can act unilaterally of Canada if Canada declines to participate. It is based in Colorado Springs and is purely American. In 1954, CONAD was given operational control over all U.S. continental air defense forces. The Commander in Chief of CONAD is "dual hatted" as he is also the Commander in Chief of NORAD. He does not have a command, but operational control of forces that are lent to him in particular missions.

[edit] Little Hell Gate

Little Hell Gate was a waterway in New York City. It passed between Randall's Island and Ward's Island, near the confluence of the Harlem River and East River.

The east end of Little Hell Gate opened into the Hell Gate passage, of the East River, opposite Lawrence Point, Queens. The west end met the Harlem River accross from E116th St, Manhattan.

In 1936, the Triboro Bridge opened, allowing easy access to Ward's and Randall's Islands. This spurred the conversion of both islands to parkland. Soon thereafter, the city began filling in the passage between the two, to expand and connect the two parks.

Few traces of Little Hell Gate still exist. Only indentations in the shoreline of the joind island in the East River indicate the former entrances to the waterway. Today, parkland and part of the FDNY Academy occupy it's former location.

67.175.92.250 17:17, 19 October 2006 (UTC)== Life & Career ==

After years of frustration - eased somewhat by acting appearances in local Chicago-area community theatre productions - Harlan threw caution to the wind, cobbled together his resume, head shots and first voice-over demo tape and began pounding the pavement as a full-time actor.

Harlan is also a published author and in-demand speaker. His first book, "VO Tales and Techniques of a Voice-Over Actor" demystified the world of commercial voice-overs; showing readers exactly what happens during a voice-over session, how the field has changed, and how they too, might forge a career as a voice actor. Hogan shared his secrets to success and a fascinating personal story that's informative, insightful, and often hilarious.

Harlan’s the author of numerous educational and corporate programs as well -winning both a Silver and a Bronze medal in The New York Film Festival for; "An Almost Painless Guide to Government" a light-hearted five-video series. He’s authored three plays and his second book, "A Voice Actor’s Guide to Home Recording" - co-written with audio engineer Jeffery P. Fisher - is in it’s second printing. It's prized by VO’s and Podcasters alike for it’s direct, non-technical and humorous approach to the art of audio. Hogan & Fisher also teamed up on camera, to create a DVD for Vasst Training called; "Voice-Over Success – What you must know in today’s very different VO marketplace" HArlan conducts seminars for working voice-over professionals in San Francisco, Seattle, and New York entitled; “The Long-Haul - where do we go from here?" Recent speaking engagements included the National Association of Broadcasters in Las Vegas and The International Radio Creative and Production Summit in Los Angeles.

The extent voice-over work he has done over the past three decades amazes even Hogan. Once, in a Twilight Zone like experience, he got a ticket for speeding and the judge ordered him to watch a National Safety Council film -that he had narrated.

Although more often heard than seen, Harlan returns from time to time to his theatrical roots appearing as Edvard Lunt in the Chicago production of Beth Henley’s "Impossible Marriage," Cantor Robinovitz in "The Jazz Singer" and instead of being the “Voice of God” playing the man himself, in a feature-length film: "Dimension".

Based near Chicago, Harlan "Tele-Commutes" worldwide via his home recording studio.

[edit] How to Leave Effective Voicemessages

If many of your outbound calls end up in voice mail, how do you make voice mail pay off for you?

Five Tips for a Successful Voice Mail Message

  (1.) Using first and last name; 
  (2.) Sounding professional, positive and polite; 
  (3.) Stating a description of your company and how your services can benefit the listener; 
  (4.) A clear and easily understandable voice;
  (5.) Repeating name and telephone number again at the end of the message.

Did you Forget Something?'


A sample voice-message began like: "Hello, this is Jessica with [name of her company] and we have been in business for 23 years . . ." What was wrong with this message?

1. She did not use her last name.

On a first call, using your last name sends a clear message of professionalism. Some telesales people feel that using only their first name in a cold call or voice mail message sounds friendly. But . . . not using your last name diminishes the importance of your message.

2. ". . . we have been in business for 23 years. . ."

Why would the listener CARE that Jessica's company has been in business for 23 years?


Answering the "What's in it for me?" Question

What should the caller have said after stating her first (and last) name? "We specialize in [benefit, like "saving time for businesses like yours by . . . “] [description of and benefits gained by using her products] . . ."

Following is an example of a good voice mail message that answers the listener's mental "What's in it for me?" question within the first 15 seconds:

== "Hello Mr. Jones, this is Douglas Charles with the GSS America, Chicago based IT consulting and professional services firm.

I'm calling today to talk to you about the various open/vacant IT positions listed on your website in the ‘Careers’ section. With our experience and expertise we can save your time and effort by providing cost effective and efficient consulting and staffing solutions. If you would like to learn more, please call me at [his telephone number]. That's [his telephone number].

Thank you for your time." ==


One Chance to Make a Good First Impression

The other voice mail message went like this:


== "Hi Ann, this is Monica. I'm sorry it has taken me so long to return your call. Please call me at [her telephone number]." == You might be asking: "In addition to not using her last name, what was wrong with that message?"

Nothing, if I had actually called Monica. Sure, her message made me curious enough to return her call because I had been making quite a few calls last week and thought I may have forgotten who Monica was. But once I called her back, it was clear she left that message just to get her call returned. I did not know Monica and had never called her.

With that deceptive message, she ruined her chance to make a good first impression


Make it Easy For the Customer

Did you ever listen to a voice mail message from a sales person with a good message – good enough that you wanted to return the call? But the telephone number at the end of the message was unclear?

No Problem… We have a solution for this……

"The voice mail message should start with first and last name but also the phone number at the beginning of the message (as well as at the end). This way if the person receiving the message misses the number at the end and wants to return the call they do not have to listen to the whole message again."


Getting prospects to return your voicemails

Effective voicemails must meet certain criteria. The following is a list of points for leaving great voicemails. After you understand the criteria, create specific messages that are in-line with these points.

                           Criteria for effective voicemails

Be short – Voicemails should be no longer than 20 seconds. Less is better. It’s more effective to leave prospects wondering who you are and what you called for, than for them to delete a long-winded message.

Arouse curiosity – This is the single most important piece of advice. The only goal in leaving voicemails for cold prospects is to get them to call you back. If you tell them everything, they don’t need to call you back. Spark their interest by having them think about who you are. Being vague may sometimes create interest in the prospects mind. If they are curious, they will call you back.

Be different – Think of all the voicemail messages that you receive. Do they inspire you to call back? If not, leave messages that are completely different than the ones you receive. Uniqueness will grab a prospects attention and will be more likely to compel them to return your messages.

What’s in it for them? – If you decide to leave additional information on your call, only state information that is a clear benefit to the customer. What benefits of your products or your services will most likely intrigue them enough to call you back?

Warm up the call – Take liberty in how you warm up the call. Use whatever you may or may not know about the company. Do you know any other people at the company? Do you know what they do? Do you know their competitors? Any information you know about the company can be used in a voicemail to make you sound more familiar with the prospect and his company.

Be confident – Don’t ask them to call you back, tell them to call you back. You must believe that you are doing them a favor by contacting them, not vice versa. Successful sales calls ensure parity between you and the prospect. Failed sales calls are ones in which you are the subordinate to the prospect.

Be creative – Think of new and different approaches every day. Even voicemail messages that seem odd at first may be the most effective in getting prospects to call you back.

Be prepared – When customers do call you back, make sure you’re able to quickly and efficiently move into your sales call. Fumbling around, asking them multiple times for their name, telling them to hold on while you bring their name up in the database, or trying to find questions you want to ask, will kill your credibility with the customer. You should be able to easily and effectively move into a sales presentation with only a pen and a blank piece of paper.


                                Some Other Valuable Points


Plan Your Message: Consider the points you want to make and jot down a few key words. Limit the points to one or two per message.

Speak Slowly: The listener needs time to process information and write it down. Be sure to pause as you say your telephone number. "My number is 530 (pause) 752 (pause) 5612."

Enunciate Clearly: The telephone can distort high frequency sounds such as /f/ and /s/. Pronounce word endings and do not swallow syllables. This is especially important when giving your name.

Put Vitality in Your Voice: A monotone voice lacks enthusiasm. Try to stand up or smile as you leave your message (standing to increase energy). People can hear a smile over the phone, really.

Modulate Your Volume: A loud voice irritates the listener. A soft voice won't always be heard, and the listener may miss vital information or nod off. Stand or sit up straight and speak directly into the receiver. Do not cradle the phone in your neck or use a speakerphone.

Modulate Your Tone: Avoid sarcasm and irritation if you want your call returned. Keep an even temper and state your request.


Avoid Telephone Tag: Give the listener options. Tell them when you can best be reached to prevent frustration. "I will be out most of the afternoon and will return to my office at 4pm. Tomorrow I'll be at my desk in the morning."

End with your phone number: Although you have already given your phone number, say it again and let it be the last thing the listener hears. This will give time to write it down.

[edit] Shin Gi Tai Kempo

[edit] Kempo style of 3 elements

Shin Gi Tai Kempo means the road of the three elements kempo. Shin means sky which refers to the mental aspect of the martial art. Gi translates as ground, this refers to the technical knowledge of the kempo style. Tai is the human body of the martial artist.

Shin Gi Tai is a style evolution of Shin Gi Kempo which was developped by Soke Ceuliman. Shin Gi Kempo had been synthesised out of the Chinese Pai Gai Noon Chuan Fa, the Kosho Ryu Kempo and the Nakamura's Kempo style.

[edit] Goho and Juho

In Shin Gi Tai Kempo we find Goho and Juho techniques. Goho techniques are techniques in which the use of the feet and fist stand central. Juho techniques are locks, throws, sweeps, immobilizations, and pressure points.

[edit] Force-lines

This school bases its work on the system of the force-lines (height, width, depth) or dimensions. Each movement determines the right reaction of the student. Each attack is a block and vice-versa. Each movement flows into the next and represents the logical play of force lines.

[edit] Shihan

[edit] Messermeister

Messermeister is the smallest of 3 major forged cutlery manufacturers in Solingen Germany (Wusthof Trident & J.A. Henekels being the other two). The two forged lines manufactured by Messermeister (Meridian elite & San Moitz elite) are known by cutlery enthusiasts and professional chefs to be among the finest single piece forged cutlery on the market, primarily due to the innovative "bolster-less" heel and the sharper 16° polished edge (vs. the standard 20° edge found on forged cutlery made by Wusthof & Henckel).


Manufacturing
Messermeister does not mass-produce cutlery, which is quite different from other German companies with far broader retail distribution. Each fully forged knife is drop forged from a single billet of German Krupp Tool Steel (.55% Carbon, 15% Molybdenum & Chromium + Vanadium) and every segment of the knife is finished completely by hand.

The resulting knife possesses:

• A handle and tang which are contoured and rounded by hand (there is no change in texture from Bolster & Tang to Handle).
• A bolster-less heel on Chef’s knives (also see “Innovation”) which allows the entire edge to be sharpened from tip to heel (while the upper half of the bolster is left in-tact for the important finger-guard).
• A razor sharp edge created by a detailed three-step finishing process which produces a sharper (16° vs. 20°), smoother angle (these are among the sharpest kitchen knives in the world).
• A smooth, polished spine (provides comfort for professionals who use their alternate hand along the spine for leverage).
• A thicker, more substantial blade on every knife, along with wider Chef’s blades.

Innovation
Many design concepts now used by the top cutlery manufacturers were innovations brought to the culinary industry by Messermeister. Prior to one of Messermeister’s more important innovations, every forged Chef’s Knife had a full bolster reaching from the top of the knife where the spine meets the handle, to the heel of the blade. This meant that there was a thick, awkward piece of steel where the rear of the blade met the bolster. Messermeister was the first (and is still primarily the only) company to remove the bottom half of the bolster so that Chef’s Knives could now be sharpened from tip to heel. Meanwhile, yet another product improvement through innovation was to manufacture a Chef’s Knife with a gently curved blade. Many people have used a Chef’s Knife and rocked it back and forth in order to make cutting easier. Previous to Messermeister’s design change, the blades on Chef’s Knives were straight, making Chef's Knives less functional.

Messermeister is also well known for developing new products that make working in a kitchen easier. The Culinary Torch for carmelizing sugar on Creme Brule & similar deserts was introduced to the gourmet industry by Messermeister. Prior to this, hardware torches were used for this purpose. Other products introduced to the gourmet industry were knife-rolls for chefs and culinary students to transport their cutlery & tools, as well as edge-guards for knife blade protection.

Distribution
Messermeister does not mass-produce cutlery, allowing the level of quality control to be unmatched. These knives are manufactured and finished as individual pieces, resulting in a flawless finished product.

You will only find Messermeister cutlery in the very exclusive specialty retail stores (primarily stores which are independantly owned) and on select specialty websites where consumers can be educated on the quality of the products and advised on product care, product warranties and general customer service information.


History
The Solingen-based Messermeister factory dates back to the early 1800's and through 1865 the products were only available in Europe. Messermeister Cutlery and related kitchen tools were not available in North America until 1866 when a German immigrant named August Klinkerman began to establish the brand with home cooks throughout the Western United States. Following many years of increasing business, Mr. Klinkerman returned to Germany, thus ending the availability of Messermeister products in North America.

Nearly a century later in 1967, August Klinkerman’s great-grandson Bernd Dressler immigrated to the United States from Germany and accepted a prominent position with a large German cutlery company. Years later, Mr. Dressler fulfilled his dream of owning the Messermeister brand in which his great-grandfather had been responsible for building within the U.S, when he and his wife Debra acquired the company.

Following the passing of Mr. Dressler in 2002, the company continues to grow under the leadership of the Dressler family and the original Messermeister factory continues to manufacture what is considered by many to be the finest single-piece forged cutlery available today.
