Maen is a greeting as in good maen or simply maen which eliminates the sense of time of day as in good morning. It comes from the initial letters of morning, afternoon, evening, and night.

It is very handy in communications when the recipients can be anywhere in world.

The sender may indicate time of day at his/her location by capitalizing the appropriate letter e.g. good meAn indicates it is afternoon in the senders location.

Aggressor Six is one of the earliest works of science fiction writer Wil McCarthy. In this book humanity is under assult by an overwhelming technologically superior alien race called "Waisters" who are threatening to exterminate the human race. Unlike his later books which focus on humanities technological evolution and how it affects the defination of humanity, Aggressor Six is a piece of military science fiction. It also examines the idea that aliens, if encountered, will be truely 'alien'- driven by desires and motives which will not be immediately obvious to humans. [edit] Jamoke

At the end of the 19th century, the word "jamoke" first appeared as a combination of the words "java" and "mocha," which both mean coffee. However, "jamocha" eventually lost its third syllable and became simply "jamoke" or "jamoch" (pronounced "jah-mohk")

By the early 1920's, "jamoke" moved away from being synonomous to "coffee" and, instead, became slang for someone who lacked mental abilities beyond that of a cup of coffee. As time moved forward, "jamoke" eventually became used in conversation quite often to describe an idiot. This was especially true around the timeframe of the second World War (circa 1940).

In the 1960's, an even more exciting phase in the word's evolution came about when some started using it as slang for male genetalia.

[edit] LA Music Academy

[edit] History

LA Music Academy was founded in 1996 by a number of prominent LA-Music Players who were experienced in the teaching of Music Theory. Their goal was to create a school in which students can learn all about music in the shortest possible time.

[edit] Educational Experience

The School Focuses on:

  • Maintaining an environment that is in our belief the best environment for learning: a small, friendly atmosphere that ensures personal attention and a "real world" understanding of your strengths and weaknesses.
  • Ensuring the development of "real world" playing skill by performing everyday with professional accompanists from the LA music scene. Some of our accompanists perform or have performed with Ray Charles, Coolio, Sergio Mendes, Steve Vai and many other prominent artists.
  • Offering a faculty that has no peer in terms of playing skill, practical, professional performance experience at the highest levels in the music industry. Our names speak for themselves both in terms of their performing ability but also their ability to teach what they know.
  • Covering a wide variety of styles with instructors and players who know those art forms.

[edit] The Campus

Pasadena was chosen as an ideal location because of its safe streets and neighborhoods, still relatively close to the LA music scene with its nightclubs, concert venues, recording studios and entertainment companies. The LA Music Academy building was originally built in the 1940’s for a school of NASA scientists learning-as-they-went to build satellites. The press release announcing the first man on the moon came out of an office in this building. An average of 150 students attend the LA Music Academy each year. With 38 faculty members on-staff, the student/teacher ratio is better than 3 to 1. The building’s facilities include large, modern classrooms equipped with Mackie Sound Systems, Remo drumsets with Zildjian cymbals, and Tech 21 guitar and Gallien-Krueger bass amps. Ten fully-equipped practice labs are for drummers. Five practice labs and several practice stations are for guitar and bass players, as well as vocalists. All students have access to the library and keyboard lab. The performance hall has room for 25 players onstage and holds an audience of 160. Outside, the building has a sunny, tree-lined courtyard with tables, chairs, and enough outdoor space to hold impromptu jam sessions. The school is open 24 hours a day except during breaks and holidays. Starbuck’s coffee shop is just a half a block away and Saladang Restaurant, a favorite with students, is right across the street. Old Town Music is the closest of 3 excellent music stores, within walking distance of the LA Music Academy campus. Just around the corner is the hip, vibrant neighborhood of Old Pasadena. It’s a fun place, teeming with shops and restaurants, that people in the LA area go out of their way to visit. Students love the close proximity of the LA Music Academy campus to bustling streets and abundant nightlife, with none of the safety hazards of some other Los Angeles locations.

[edit] Sid Hatfield

http://www.umwa.org/history/sidhat.jpg

In the early spring of 1920, unorganized coal miners in Mingo County, West Virginia began seeking to join the UMWA. The coal operators became alarmed by the organizing activity and locked out the miners. On May 19, 1920, twelve men were killed at Matewan, West Virginia in a gun fight in which the local police and the people of Matewan faced a group of hoodlums hired by the infamous Baldwin Felts Detective Agency at the behest of the coal operators to unlawfully evict miners from their homes.

In the Matewan battle, Albert Felts, wearing a badge as a "deputy sheriff" of Harlan County, Kentucky, fired the first shot but was killed by Matewan Chief of Police Sid Hatfield. Hatfield had warned the thugs that they had no legal warrants to evict the citizens of Matewan and that he would not permit eviction without proper legal procedures. Felts then attempted to forcibly arrest the Chief of Police. Felts had been one of the chief gunmen used by the coal operators in the Ludlow, Colorado massacre in 1914, in which twenty persons were killed, including twelve women and children who were burned alive in their tents.

Sid Hatfield, a hero to West Virginia miners, was himself killed in August of 1921 in Welch, West Virginia by C. E. Lively, a Baldwin Felts gunman. Hatfield's murder was in all likelihood a set-up. He was arrested on July 28 on an allegation that he had shot up the town of Mohawk more than a year before. He was taken from his home town of Matewan to McDowell County, West Virginia, a stronghold of anti-UMWA coal operators. As he entered the court house with Ed Chambers, who had been called as a witness, both were shot down in front of their wives by Lively and two other gunmen. No charges were brought against Lively or the other murderers.
