[edit] 2004 Illinois Senate race

In 2004, Illinois elected Democrat Barak Obama as its new senator to the United States Congress, replacing Peter Fitzgerald, a Republican who voluntarily retired.

Obama won many newspaper endorsements in the Democratic primary, but was not expected to win because an opponent had much stronger financial support. However, that candidate suffered an embarrassing scandal, and Obama won the primary.

The Republican primary winner, Jack Ryan, appeared to be a formidable candidate. He was a banker who had gone to teach in the Chicago public schools, and earned the backing from the state's Republican party leaders for this senate run. Press reports often referred to him as good-looking and he had formerly been married to actress Jeri Ryan. However, his campaign imploded when he suffered his own embarrassing scandal. Since he was running for public office, an Illinois judge decided it was in the public interest for portions of his divorce records (which had been sealed) to be opened. These records showed that he had repeatedly pressured his unwilling wife to attend sex clubs with him where they could publicly make love alongside other couples. Social conservatives were upset at this information. State Republican party bosses were outraged because Ryan had assured them there was nothing damaging in his divorce proceedings. Ryan was forced to withdraw from the race.

This set in motion a new round of embarrassments for the Republicans as they could not find anyone willing to take his slot. Two of the three living former Republican governors, Jim Edgar and Jim Thompson were asked to fill the slot but turned it down. (The third ex-governor, George Ryan was being investigated by federal authorities for corruption). State party head Judy Baar Topinka refused to take the seat. A few other Republicans of lesser note were offered the post, including at least one man who ran in the primary against Ryan. All refused. In desperation, the state Republican party began looking at unorthodox choices. Former Chicago Bears coach Mike Ditka was asked to run. He declined. A fan of former Chicago Bear Dave Duerson faxed in that ex-NFL star's resume to party headquarters. They mistakenly thought Duerson himself was interested and asked him to run. He declined, informing them that he had already publicly endorsed Obama for the office. This dragged on for well over a month.

During this period where the Republicans could not find a senatorial candidate, the Democratic party asked Barak Obama to give the keynote address at the 2004 Democratic National Convention. Obama's speech made him an overnight sensation and made a lopsided race that much more a certain Democratic success.

Finally, the party succeeded in getting a candidate, talk radio show host Alan Keyes. In his favor he was a talented speaker, and willing to take controversial stands. However, he had never held office before. He also did not live in the state, as he was a Maryland resident. He was also a social conservative and Illinois tended to lean to the left.

Keyes's campaign mostly gave news for his controversial statements. He took extreme stands against abortion and gay rights. At the Republican National Convention he said gays were selfish hedionists. When a reporter asked him if that meant the daughter of Vice President Dick Cheney was a selfish hedionist, Keyes responded that she was. The Illinois Republican party openly distanced itself from Keyes at this point. Moderate Republicans such as Topinka openly denounced his statements. Conservative Republicans commented that he was making his points in needlessly off-putting ways. Keyes continued to make a series of rather questionable statements throughout the campaign.

On election night, Obama unsurprisingly won. His was the biggest victory ever in an Illinois senate race, breaking a record previously set in 1920. Keyes, for his part, never gave a concession speech that night, a decision some felt typified his ineffective campaign style. Shortly after the election Keyes pledged to stay in Illinois and help rebuild an effective social conservative political force in Illinois, but he moved back to Maryland very shortly later instead.

Professor Sir James Clarke Holt is an English medieval historian, and an Emeritus Professor at the University of Cambridge. Holt took his DPhil at the University of Oxford, and later worked at Fitzwilliam College, Cambridge, where he served as master from 1981 to 1988. He has also been made honorary DLitt at the universities of Reading and Nottingham. He has been knighted for his work, and is a Fellow of the British Academy.

Holt made his fame with the book Magna Carta, which came out in its original edition in 1965. Here he treated the famous charter in the context of the political framework of its time. The book has since been fully revised, and is still considered authorotarian within its field. He has also published other works on the same period, such as The Northerners: A Study in the Reign of King John, and Robin Hood.

[edit] Criticism of Che Guevara

Though he has been labeled by some as a hero, opponents of Guevara, including most of the Cuban exile community and some refugees from other countries under communism, view him as a killer and terrorist. They claim that he ordered the execution of hundreds of people in Cuban prisons and peasants against the Cuban Revolution in the regions controlled or visited by his guerrilla forces. New York Sun writer, Williams Myers, labels Che as a “sociopathic thug”.[1] Other US newspaper critics have made similar remarks. These critics point out that Che Guevara was personally responsible for the torture and execution of hundreds of people in Cuban prisons, and the murder of many more peasants in the regions controlled or visited by his guerrilla forces. They also believe that Guevara was a “blundering tactician”, not a revolutionary genius, who has not one recorded combat victory. Guevara founded Cuba's forced labor camp system, establishing its first forced labor camp in Guanahacabibes to re-educate managers of state-owned enterprises who were guilty of various violations of "revolutionary ethics".[2] Many years after Guevara's death, Cuba's labor camp system was used to jail dissidents of the Revolution. [3] [4] [5][6] [7] Critics believe Guevara was ineffective and in reality a poor tactician. They dispute accounts of the Battle of Santa Clara; on the capturing of a train supplying heavy reinforcements, critic Álvaro Vargas Llosa writes, "Numerous testimonies indicate that the commander of the train surrendered in advance, perhaps after taking bribes."[8] [9]

Some claim Guevara was a failure at managing the Cuban economy, as he "oversaw the near-collapse of sugar production, the failure of industrialization, and the introduction of rationing—all this in what had been one of Latin America’s four most economically successful countries since before the Batista dictatorship."[10][11] There is also the belief by some critics that, because there is no documentary evidence of Guevara having earned a medical degree, he was not actually a doctor.[12]

In "The Cult of Che",[13] writer Paul Berman critiques the film The Motorcycle Diaries and argues "that modern-day cult of Che" obscures the "tremendous social struggle" currently taking place in Cuba. For example, the article discusses the jailing of dissidents, such as poet and journalist Raúl Rivero, who was eventually freed after worldwide pressure due to a campaign of solidarity by the International Committee for Democracy in Cuba [14] which included Václav Havel, Lech Wałęsa, Árpád Göncz, Elena Bonner and others. Berman claims that in the U.S., where Motorcycle Diaries received standing ovations at the Sundance film festival, the adoration of Che has caused Americans to overlook the plight of dissident Cubans. This glorification of Che is also satirized by online site che-mart.com, which, among other things, markets T-shirts poking fun at both Guevara and his supporters, casting aspersions on what they perceive as an irony: Che Guevara as one of capitalism's hottest-selling images.[15]

Critics also believe Che executed more than just political figures, but also political dissidents[16]. Although much criticism of Guevara and his legacy emanates from the political center and right, there has also been criticism from other political groups such as anarchists and civil libertarians, some of whom consider Guevara an authoritarian, anti-working-class Stalinist, whose goal was the creation of a more bureaucratic state-Stalinist regime.[17]

[edit] Creation and Controversy

Thank You Mask Man was created by Imagination, Inc., produced by John Magnuson(a friend of Lenny's) and directed by Jeff Hale. The cartoon itself attributes direction to Lenny Bruce and John Magnuson. A recording of Lenny Bruce performing 'thank you mask man' is used as the source for all dialogue. Primarily, the film was criticized by the gay community who believed it to be anti-gay, and by others who were disturbed by the references to homosexuality. After some screenings fist fights and arguments reportedly occurred. Several theaters avoided showing or cancelled scheduled showings of the cartoon for fear of controversy.

However, Thank You Mask Man is now interpretted as lampooning the redneck and homophobic populace, and is occassionally shown in gay film festivals.

[edit] French composer Jan LEONTSKY (born 1970)

The art of Jan LEONTSKY

Jan LEONTSKY is born in the small city of Privas, south of FRANCE, in 1970. As a child he played piano and church organ. He began learning organ & piano with his professor Abbot Maurice Delehedde, himself former pupil of international soloist Samson François. Jan Leontsky began quickly to perform in Trumpet & Organ duettos concerts. After harmony and composition studies, he has recorded a first album devoted to J.S BACH - " The Goldberg Variations". Since 1990, Leontsky has recorded over 14 albums, mainly for the piano. His style is definitely post-modernist. Contrary to dodecaphonic music, he expresses himself in a romantic language. For Jan LEONTSKY,the atonal crisis of music has to be passed beyond. Contrary to Vienna school (mainly Schönberg), the composer believes in tonality and have faith in the tonal tradition. Many composers have to be mentioned when listening to Leontsky's works. From Guillaume de Machaut to Bach, from Gregorian to Chopin, the composer is following the tradition of classical and tonal Music. However, Jan LEONTSKY is going beyond the classical form. He invents new means of expressions, especially for the piano. Leontsky is also the author of a short essay about piano playing, intitled "the 30 minutes piano lesson".
