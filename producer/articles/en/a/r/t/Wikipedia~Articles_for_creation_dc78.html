This page allows unregistered and new users to create new articles with the assistance of experienced Wikipedians. The easiest way to create a new article, however, is to create a free Wikipedia user account and then log in. No personal information is necessary to register, and registered users can create new articles without the hassle or delay of the Article for Creation process.

If you have an idea for a good article subject, but do not want to provide text for the article, you should make an article request instead. Alternatively, if you want to discuss and plan an article go to the drawing board.

Most articles are declined... don't let this happen to you!

The majority of submitted articles are declined, because they are not suitable for Wikipedia.
Read Wikipedia:Your first article before writing your article, to learn how to write it well
and read below to avoid some common mistakes other people make.
Unregistered users: Submitting an article Registered users: Reviewing submissions

Follow these directions to submit an article for creation.

  1. Check whether the article already exists by searching for it.
  2. Ensure that your topic meets Wikipedia's notability guidelines, including ones for:
    • Companies and corporations
    • Musical groups, songs, and artists
    • Websites
    • People, alive or dead
    • Yourself
  3. Ensure that your article is verifiable and based on published sources.
  4. Click the "Click here to start" link below.
  5. Keep in mind that there are a lot of article submissions, and established users may not have time to immediately look into your article. Sometimes, due to the large backlog, a submission may not be explicitly rejected or accepted.
  6. Give the following information in the proper places on the form:
    • The title of the article
    • Several sentences of original content, written by you. Do not copy the text from a website or printed source; observe copyright.
    • At least one published source for your information, such as a URL or a book title and author. If you do not list at least one source, your article will be declined.
Click here to start

Extra tips for advanced users:

  • Do not add any stub templates or categories to your submission— they should only be added when the article is actually created.
  • Use three equal signs for your section headers, ===like this===, rather than the usual two.
  • Avoid indenting paragraphs (beginning them with one or more spaces); doing so can cause the text to be formatted oddly.

Registered Wikipedians should follow these directions to review submitted text and create new articles.

  • Verify that the submission is not a copyright violation and meets applicable notability guidelines.
  • If the article looks suitable for Wikipedia:
    1. Create the article, crediting the submitter's IP address or username in the initial edit summary for GFDL compliance.
      • Consider adding the new article to categories, and tagging it with appropriate cleanup templates.
      • If the article is a stub, add the {{stub}} template, or the appropriate category-specific stub templates.
    2. Leave a reply below the submitted text, noting that the article has been created. You can use the template {{subst:afc accept}} to do this quickly. Be sure to sign your reply with ~~~~.
    3. Mark the submission with {{subst:afc top|accept}} just below the headline, and {{subst:afc b}} at the very bottom.
  • If the article does not look suitable for Wikipedia:
    1. Immediately remove any copyright violations, personal attacks, or patent nonsense from the submission.
    2. Write a quick reply explaining why the article is not a good idea. These templates provide quick responses to common problems. Be sure to sign your reply with ~~~~.
    3. Mark the submission with {{subst:afc top}} just below the headline, and {{subst:afc b}} at the very bottom.

Once a registered user is ready to make the new article that was requested, they can use this tool to make the article fast.

Dizzy (book)

Dizzy is a book written by Cathy Cassidy, who is also known for writing Driftwood, Indigo Blue, Scarlett and Sundae Girl. This story is about a 12-year-old girl named Dizzy who lives alone with her father in Birmingham. She waits every year for something from her mother who, as the book says, parted with her father when she was four. Dizzy is turning twelve, and she wakes up on her birthday when her father brings her a toasted cheese, banana milkshake and Smarties for breakfast. Later in the day Dizzy goes to a coffee shop called Dimitri's with her friends, Sasha, Sara and Jade. They celebrate and eat fudge cake. Dizzy walks home, thrilled, but when she arrives home she's surprised: her mother, who she hasn't seen for eight years, was standing on the living room carpet. They hug and cry. Later in the evening, her mother reveals that she is a vegan when they plan dinner. Her father says that they were supposed to get pizza, but the mother objects. She says they can eat miso soup, bean stew and muesli cake. Dizzy is disgusted, but she says yes. After they’ve eaten, Dizzy goes to bed, only to get woken up again at five in the morning by her mother. Her mother tells her that she must pack, for they were going to festivals. Dizzy packs, and they go off. They both survive until the festivals are over, including Dizzy having her first kiss, but Dizzy finds out some horrible things about her mother; that she smokes weed or spliff, that she has a boyfriend and a stepson, and that she is fleeing to India when Dizzy gets roped in for shoplifting. Her mother realises that Dizzy has been put on the Missing Persons list, and because of that she is going to India. The story ends by Dizzy looking at her sari that her mother sent her.
