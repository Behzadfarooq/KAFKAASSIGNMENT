[edit] Michael Aaron

Michael Aaron is a gay activist living in Salt Lake City, Utah who has been active in gay Utah politics and organizations since 1981. In 1994, he was awarded the "Diversity is Great" award for his work with the Utah Anti-Violence Project. He was president of the Lesbian and Gay Student Union at the University of Utah from 1982-1984, Publisher of Triangle Magazine from 1984-1985 and chair of Gay and Lesbian Utah Democrats from 1989-1992. He also has been involved in leadership positions of Utah Male Naturists, Community Reporter, qVinum wine club, and the Warm Springs Neighborhood Council of Salt Lake City. In 2004, he began publishing Salt Lake Metro, and in 2006 left his business partner to begin publishing QSaltLake. He won the Founders Award of the Gay and Lesbian Utah Democrats in 1993, the Organization of the Year award in 2005 for his work with the Salt Lake Metro, and the Lifetime Achievement Award of the Royal Court of the Golden Spike Empire in 2006.

A graduate of the University of Utah, he worked in the pre-press industry from 1984 through 1994, when he took on a producer job at Dahlin Smith White, an ad agendy for many large businesses within the high-tech industry, including Intel, Iomega, and Red Hat Linux. He was the producer for San Francisco Opera as well. After the September 11, 2001 attacks, the high tech industry collapsed, taking high-tech advertising agencies with it, including DSW.

Aaron has since restarted his work in the gay publishing field, starting Salt Lake Metro in 2004 and now publishes QSaltLake.

[edit] WCMC-FM 99.9

WCMC signed on in 1990 as WFXQ, "99.9 The Fox", a country station operating with sister station WMEK (now WJYK) 980 AM in Chase City, Virginia. The station continued with this format for the next eleven years. When veteran Raleigh broadcaster Tom Joyner bought WFXQ and several other radio properties across southside Virginia in 2001, 99.9 FM's format switched to black gospel as "Rejoice Radio 99.9". Shortly thereafter, 99.9 FM went popular standards as "Stardust 99.9". In May of 2004, Joyner Radio won approval from the FCC to have 99.9 FM reallocated from Chase City, Virginia to Creedmoor, North Carolina, moving the rural Virginia station into the Raleigh-Durham-Chapel Hill radio market. In February 2005, Joyner sold WFXQ to Raleigh's Capitol Broadcasting for $7.25 million. Lakes Media, which had recently purchased Joyner's other Southside Virginia properties, operated the station under a local marketing agreement, continuing the standards format until October 10th, 2005, when the format moved to the company's WSHV, 1370 AM, in South Hill, Virginia. Three days later on the afternoon of October 13th, WCMC "99.9 Genuine Country" hit the Triangle's airwaves from its new Creedmoor facilities with 22,000 watts of power. The station has applied to the FCC for a boost to 25,000 watts from a tower site near Falls Lake which better covers the market. WCMC's studios are co-located with sister station WRAL, 101.5 FM, at 711 Hillsborough Street in Raleigh.

The beginnings of the NUS LGBT Campaign The lesbian and gay movement (as it was then known) within NUS grew out of the wider gay liberation movement in the early 1970s. At this stage there was no formal national campaign, and only a few lesbian and gay societies in student unions. In 1976 NUS commissioned a research project on the discrimination faced by lesbians and gay men. The first explicit policy on lesbian and gay liberation was passed in 1971 and substantially debated in 1973.

In the 1970s, lesbian and gay societies, or 'gay socs' as they were known, were set up in many student unions, although very few had a continued existence. Individual societies worked with non-student bodies such as the GLF (Gay Liberation Front) and CHE (Campaign for Homosexual Equality). From the late 1970s there were a few national meetings on lesbian and gay liberation, the earliest ones being open to all.

The student movement campaign did not develop until the early 1980s with, for the first time, individuals on the NUS National Executive Committee having responsibility for lesbian and gay rights. In the mid-1980s, the campaign's current structure, with its twice-yearly conferences, was established.


In December 1987, Clause 14, later known as Clause or Section 28, was introduced to the Local Government (Finance) Bill. The NUS Lesbian and Gay Campaign took a full part in the wider movement of people campaigning against the clause. Many lesbian and gay societies organised demonstrations in their towns. Thousands of students attended the two national demonstrations. In student unions, lesbians and gay men were passing positive policies, and lesbian and gay societies were being formed all over the UK. Unfortunately, when the clause became law, the campaigning activity returned to its previous level.

The Lesbian and Gay Campaign continued to monitor the effects of Section 28, including its use by Essex County Council to prevent further education colleges establishing lesbian and gay societies. A campaign organised by NUS and local area student organisations defeated the council. Successful campaigns also overturned the ban on setting up lesbian and gay societies at two Leeds colleges: the City College of Music and Kitson College.

The NUS Lesbian and Gay Campaign was also actively involved in work around access to donor insemination when the Embryology Bill was passing through Parliament in 1989/90. It also worked with a range of lesbian and gay organisations on the issues raised by Paragraph 16 of the guidelines to the 1990 Children's Act, and what was then Clause 25 of the Criminal Justice Bill.

The LGBT Campaign is based on the principles of self-organisation and autonomy. Initially the campaign was for lesbians and gay men only. In Summer 1991 this policy was changed, and the campaign was expanded to include bisexual students.

In Summer 1991, the NUS LGB Campaign (as it was by then known) supported the Leeds Lesbian and Gay Youth Group's successful protest against the Royal Bank of Scotland's decision to refuse them a bank account. We were also involved in the 1992 campaign that led to the decriminalisation of homosexuality in the Isle of Man.

The Special Regions

There are eight NUS Regions within England (Each has its own LGBT Committee member or members allocated to it) and three special regions: NUS Wales, NUS Scotland and NUS-USI in Northern Ireland. Wales and Scotland have their own LGBT Officers (in Wales, this is still an LGB Officer). These positions are autonomous and elected by conferences held in Wales and Scotland.

The Mutual Recognition Directive provides for a General System for Mutual Recognition of Professional Qualifications. The General System has been established with the purpose of enabling individuals to transfer their qualifications and skills between Member States of European Union.

The General System is founded on the premise that an individual qualified in one Member State to exercise a given profession should be treated, in principle, as qualified to exercise that same profession or occupation in another Member State, without having to re-qualify from the beginning. Where there are substantial differences between the education and training, the migrant is asked to compensate for these differences.

The principle behind the system is that of accredited prior learning - but with the important proviso that the applicant for recognition must be treated as a qualified professional with some gaps in his or her training, not as a student.

[edit] Ovingdean

Ovingdean is an eastern suburb of the city of Brighton and Hove, East Sussex, separated from Brighton by open downland on which are Brighton racecourse and a golf course. Its population is approximately 1000. Despite being so close to the sea, it was historically a landlocked farming village, but since its incorporation into the County Borough of Brighton in 1928 there has been housing development on its eastern and southern sides. Some of this early housing was rough-and-ready plotland development like that of adjacent Woodingdean, but modern Ovingdean is prosperous and almost all trace of this inter-war past has vanished. The extreme west of the historic parish was annexed by Brighton in the 1930s for part of its Whitehawk housing estate.

The historic centre of Ovingdean still retains a rural feel. At its heart is a small flint church dating from the latter part of the 11th century, which may be the very one mentioned in Domesday Book, and which has a rare dedication to St Wulfran, an eighth-century archbishop of Sens, one of only three in England (the others Grantham and Dorrington, both in Lincolnshire). Its churchyard contains the remains of Magnus Volk, the inventor, Charles Kempe, stained-glass artist, and Helena Normanton QC, one of the first female barristers. There is also a memorial to Sophia Jex-Blake, one of the first cohort of British female medical students.

Other significant buildings include the late eighteenth-century gentry farmhouse Ovingdean Hall, now a well-known school for hearing-impaired children, and Ovingdean Grange, a farmhouse with a 16th-century core which features in a local legend of a visit by Charles II before his escape to France in 1651. The legend in its best-known form is an invention of the novelist W. Harrison Ainsworth in _Ovingdean Grange_ (1857). There are other probably eighteenth-century flint buildings in the village, and the whole centre is a conservation area.

Ovingdean has one shop (a post office) and no pub or cafe. Near its historic southern boundary, but strictly speaking in historic Rottingdean, are (1) the rehabilitation centre for blind ex-servicemen, St Dunstan's, now (snce April 2006) home to Britain's oldest surving veteran of the Great War and oldest surving man, Henry Allingham (110), and (2) the famous girls' public school, Roedean.

[edit] Swami Satyeswarananda Giri

Swami Satyeswarananda Giri also known as Vidyaratna Babaji, was born in Bengal, India and is considered a Himalayan Master of Kriya Yoga. He received Kriya instruction and guidance, as well as, monastic training from Swami Satyananda Giri, whom he met whilst studying at the hermitage of Paramhansa Yogananda for eight years.

Swami Satyeswarananda Giri, in concordance with his 20 year service to Swami Satyananda Giri, completed his MA in Philosophy (specialising in Vedanta) and a BA in Law from the University of Calcutta. Eventually practicing as an attorney and a Law proffessor.

Shortly before Swami Satyananda's passing, Swami Satyeswarananda Giri became part of the Swami Order in Puri. After the passing of his spiritual preceptor, he then would often go the Himalayas to meditate for years at a time - primarily to Dunagiri Hill, the place where he would go to practice Kriya Yoga every summer in the past.

It is during this period that Swami Satyeswarananda Giri came into contact with Mahamuni Babaji, also known as Mahavatar Babaji. Mahamuni Babaji lived and stayed with Swami Satyeswarananda Giri for periods of time in various caves and a small hut. Swami Satyeswarananda Giri was then initiated into the Purna Kriya ('Whole' or 'Complete' Kriya Yoga practice) by Mahamuni Babaji. He was then instructed by Mahamuni Babaji to re-establish the original Kriya Yoga, as brought to the world by Lahiri Mahasaya, throughout the world.

Currently, Swami Satyeswarananda Giri resides in San Diego teaching Kriya Yoga and writing books on sacred scriptures and Kriya Yoga.

Swami Satyeswarananda Giri has also authored many books mostly published by Sanskrit Classics on Kriya Yoga and the biographies of many of the luminarious within the Kriya Yoga tradition, as well as, being involved with the translations of commentaries and letters by these authorative figures within the Kriya tradition.
