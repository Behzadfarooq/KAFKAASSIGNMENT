[edit] Triangle Youth Brass Band

The Triangle Youth Brass Band, located in Raleigh, North Carolina, was formed in 1997 as an expansion of the Triangle Brass Band's educational endeavors. The organization consists of two bands, the Triangle Youth Brass Band and the Triangle Youth Brass Ensemble, and nearly 80 of the Triangle’s most talented young musicians participate. The TYBB’s mission is to provide an opportunity for the finest area high school brass and percussion players to supplement their wind band experience by performing in a British-style brass band and to raise the level of brass playing in the area.

The Triangle Youth Brass Band maintains a strict instrumentation based on the British brass band tradition. B-flat and E-flat cornets, E-flat tenor horns, and B-flat baritones are used in addition to trombone, bass trombone, tuba, and percussion. In addition to contemporary pieces written for brass band, the group’s repertoire includes orchestral and band transcriptions, and students also perform chamber music in small quartets or quintets. For both ensembles, membership is by recommendation and audition, and the season lasts from November to May each year. Both bands perform at the Progress Energy Center for the Performing Arts’ Meymandi Concert Hall in Raleigh. The TYBB program also sponsors clinics and masterclasses with guest artists and conductors and hosts Brassmas, a December event in which middle- and high-school students from all over the Triangle area perform an outdoor concert of holiday music.

In its first season, TYBB gave only one performance, but the program grew swiftly; Matt Edwards, Stephen Lytle, John Enloe, and Randy Guptill conducted that year. Guptill took over as director for the 1998-1999 school year and served until its current director, Tony Granados, came aboard in January 2001. This year, the program has expanded with the formation of an additional band, the Triangle Youth Brass Ensemble. Although this ensemble does not adhere to the TYBB’s strict British instrumentation, the slightly larger TYBE is patterned after the British style. Repertoire is challenging yet appropriate to the skill level of these young musicians. This additional ensemble provides more students with the opportunity to develop their skills as musicians in a youth brass band program that has become the premiere organization of its kind in North America

As the program has flourished, so has its reputation. The Triangle Youth Brass Band often travels and performs outside the Triangle area of North Carolina. Recent tours have taken the band to the North American Brass Band AssociationChampionships, where the band earned First Place Honors in the Youth Division in 2001, 2002, 2004 and 2006. The TYBB were featured performers at the Great American Brass Band Festival in 2003, visited Atlanta to perform with the Georgia Brass Band in 2005, and performed for the City of Greensboro’s Independence Day fireworks celebration in 2006.

A cornerstone of the TYBB program is its commitment to bringing first-rate musicians from all over the world to the Triangle. Guest soloists and clinicians have included Keith Wilkinson (Brass Band of the Western Reserve), Terry Everson (Professor of Trumpet, Boston University), Michael Kris (Professor of Low Brass, University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill), Mark Clodfelter (Professor of Trumpet, University of Kentucky), Ray Farr (conductor/composer), James E. Jackson III (euphonium, U.S. Coast Guard Band), Jens Lindemann (Professor of Trumpet, UCLA and former trumpet, Canadian Brass), Steve Sykes (tuba soloist, conductor, and adjudicator), Chris Martin (Principal Trumpet, Chicago Symphony), Colin Williams (Principal Trombone, Atlanta Symphony), Demondrae Thurman (Professor of Euphonium, University of Alabama and Sotto Voce Quartet), Tom Bratten (U.S. Army Band), and Vince DiMartino (Professor of Trumpet, Centre College, Danville, Kentucky).

[edit] Gerónimo Lluberas

Born on January 7, 1956, Dr. Lluberas headed multiple humanitarian relief efforts to Belle Anse, Haiti, helping treat over 10,000 Haitians. "Dr. Gerry", or Gero (pronounced HEH-roh), as he liked to be called, also was a music composer.

His Christian musical, Butterflies in the Rain Forest was recently produced into a 21-track CD with the colaboration of his close friend Christopher Crommett, Grammy Award winner and Woody Herman Band alumnus Gary Anderson and others, the proceeds of which benefit the Health & Education Relief Organization (HERO) which continues his work in Belle Anse.

The recording of Butterflies in the Forest brought together a wide range of artists and collaborators of Dr. Lluberas, most of which donated their talent. Anderson arranged and orchestrated Lluberas' musical. Forrest Buchtel, lead trumpet and featured soloist for Woody Herman, Duke Ellington, Blood Sweat and Tears, and Malo, Lluberas' daughter Cristina, bassist Claudio Alvarez, Luis Reyes, an accomplished "güiro" (typical gourd-based Puerto Rican musical instrument) player, University of Tampa music professor J.J. Hobbs, 3 Mo' Tenors performer Victor Robertson, Michael Fauss and others had a hand in performing and recording the musical.

He also collaborated with Christian compositions posthumously recorded by the Camino de Fe organization at Lluberas' home parish, Transfiguration Catholic Church in Marietta, Georgia.

Dr. Lluberas, a Puerto Rican rheumatologist, died of cancer on May 7, 2003. At his request, two songs from his musical, Lord, I Surrender to You, and On My Knees, were sung at his funeral.

[edit] Landcare Queensland

Queensland Landcare Foundation was formed in 2003 after key landcare professionals in Queensland identified a need. The need was twofold. Firstly, there was a lack of corporate investment in Landcare throughout the state. And secondly, there was no other organisation providing resources, capacity and support to community landcare groups in a consistent manner across the state.

The Foundation spent its first three years building partnerships and trust between landcare groups, regional bodies and other support organisations.

The organisation changed its name to Landcare Queensland in December 2006, to reflect the true nature of its work and approach to fundraising.

[edit] KNCN (FM) Radio Station

KNCN is an FM station licensed to Sinton, Texas playing to the Corpus Christi metro area. It signed on in 1976 from a combined studio and tower site north of the bay north of Corpus Christi and east of Sinton, Texas. Facilities were the same as now a 100,000 watt signal from a 410 foot above average terrain antenna. The studio site had on air studios, a bathroom, and the transmitter room. The first main transmitter was a Collins 831-G1.

The 101.3 frequency had been used previously by KTOD-FM and by KMIO.

KNCN was known as C-101 and went on the air with a rock format. In later years, sales offices were established in the business center of CCT. They were once in the "600 Building" downtown, and later had their own building on Leopard street. Early on the station recieved permission from the FCC to identify as a Sinton-Taft station. A bit later they changed that to a Sinton-Corpus Christi identifier.

The station was founded by local owners. It was sold to Tippie Communications (whose shareholders had been involved with KHFI-AM-FM-TV in Austin, with Rollins/Terminix, and by the eighties with KVLY-FM in Edinburg, TX and an FM in the Colorado Springs, CO area.

The station was purchased by a company that has now been rolled up into Clear Channel Communications

Mission

KDS’s mission is to help corporations manage and reduce their Travel and Expense (‘T&E’) costs. Through KDS Corporate, their ’on-demand‘, end-to-end software solution, KDS enable business travelers to book their journeys and manage their expense claims online, whilst simultaneously allowing employers to enforce corporate travel policies. KDS market leadership is built upon the anticipation of customers’ needs for advanced web-based solutions that achieve cost reductions, enhanced employee security and improved corporate governance.

Background

KDS is an independent, privately owned company founded in Paris, France. The company’s founders saw a major opportunity to improve upon prevailing conditions in the corporate travel arena:

1. T&E is one of the greatest areas of controllable expenditure in most companies, and yet one of the least managed and optimized.

2. The complexity and rapid evolution of the travel industry value chain generates significant inefficiencies but also numerous cost saving opportunities for corporations.

3. No player had yet unleashed the full power of web-based technologies and an on-demand approach in the T&E space.

4. No company had yet delivered an end-to-end T&E solution that combined an intuitive online self-booking travel tool with an efficient travel and expense reporting system.

KDS therefore set about developing a revolutionary T&E solution which over the past decade has helped transform the travel management landscape, been adopted by blue chip organisations around the world, and become recognized as an industry leading technology.

KDS Today

KDS Corporate is today used thousands of enterprises around the world, with implementations in over 30 countries. They serve customers of all sizes, from major multinational corporations with highly complex requirements through to SME businesses needing ‘off the shelf’, easy implemented solutions. The scalability of KDS Corporate can cater for hundreds or thousands of employees, and from tens to tens of thousands of T&E transactions per month.

The KDS solution has been expressly designed to minimize operating costs and maximize convenience for customers. Its web-based, on-demand technology eliminates the need for expensive hardware investments and ensures that implementations and system upgrades are managed smoothly, efficiently and without interrupting the flow of the customer’s business.

KDS is an independent company. KDS takes no commission from travel providers, it has no conflicts of interest and is able to focus exclusively on reducing the T&E costs of its customers. In addition to the corporate customers, KDS serves many travel management companies and intermediaries, providing the ‘white label’ technology that underpins their own online booking portals.

KDS is the clear market leader in Europe and is undergoing rapid growth in the United States. Headquartered in Paris, They have further offices in San Francisco, London, Frankfurt and Stockholm. KDS is backed by Accel Partners and Atlas Ventures.

[edit] Mission

The mission of the Jersey City Museum is to present, collect, and interpret American visual art and material culture of the highest quality. The museum places particular emphasis on exhibiting and supporting the work of contemporary artists who represent the diversity of our society, and on preserving and making accessible the art and the historical and industrial objects of significance to the region in its collections. Through its exhibition, educational initiatives, and public programs, the museum serves as a center of cultural life for the people of Jersey City, Hudson County and the region.



[edit] History

The history of the Jersey City Museum begins in 1901 when the stately new Jersey City Public Library, designed by architects Brite and Bacon, opened in downtown Jersey City with a series of sky-lit galleries on its fourth floor to house a museum. Funded by the City of Jersey City throughout the first half of the century, the Library director initially served as museum director and was assisted by volunteers for the museum's operations. A center of events, Jersey City Museum was visited by thousands of citizens who viewed exhibitions of art and collections of artifacts and participated in an assortment of classes. In 1931, local benefactors established the museum Association with goals "…to acquaint all the people of the community with matters of the past and present . . . to emphasize the values of all cultures and to show the people what they can learn from each other." These goals endure today.

Despite being an important repository of art and regional artifacts, as well as a center of community events, Jersey City Museum was largely dormant in the 1950's. With its future in doubt and financial support to the library dwindling, the museum's Permanent Collection was stored and its activities were curtailed. In the mid 1970's, the community - including students from Jersey City State College, local artists, educators and neighbors - initiated efforts to rescue the collection and revive the museum. In 1987, in its first move toward independence, and with strong community and municipal support, Jersey City Museum separated fiscally from the Library.


In the years following this reopening, the professional staff - armed with conservation surveys, assessments and appropriations from the State of New Jersey, Jersey City, and the Institute of Museum Services - began the recovery program for the Permanent Collection. Objects were retrieved from improper storage and examined, assessed and inventoried. Storage areas were reconstructed and a prioritized conservation plan was developed with the assistance of two MAP Surveys and a professional conservator.


As the collection became more accessible, Jersey City Museum was recognized as a leading presenter of historic- and collections-based exhibitions and as the preeminent museum in New Jersey for the promotion of works of contemporary art and artists. The only full-service art and history museum in Hudson County, JCM serves a broad and diverse urban community by maintaining, preserving and interpreting the region's cultural heritage.

In the early 1990's, moving Jersey City Museum from its remote location became a top priority. A search began for a suitable location that would provide a climate-controlled environment, the ability to set different hours (free from the library schedule), ADA accessibility and a street presence. In 1993, the Jersey City Redevelopment Agency donated an ideal building to Jersey City Museum in the Van Vorst Historic District at 350 Montgomery Street (circa 1929). Located only two blocks from the Library location, the space was donated for the purpose of renovation, modernization, and museum relocation.

Over the next several years, extensive work was done at the Montgomery Street location. Contracts were drawn, studies were conducted, an architectural team was hired, architectural drawings were developed, approvals and permits were secured, budgets were developed and initial public funding was secured. The new $11 million facility was completed in December 2000 and officially opened its doors to the public on October 19, 2001.

Background

1. T&E is one of the greatest areas of controllable expenditure in most companies, and yet one of the least managed and optimized.

2. The complexity and rapid evolution of the travel industry value chain generates significant inefficiencies but also numerous cost saving opportunities for corporations.

3. No player had yet unleashed the full power of web-based technologies and an on-demand approach in the T&E space.

4. No company had yet delivered an end-to-end T&E solution that combined an intuitive online self-booking travel tool with an efficient travel and expense reporting system.

KDS therefore set about developing a revolutionary T&E solution which over the past decade has helped transform the travel management landscape, been adopted by blue chip organisations around the world, and become recognized as an industry leading technology.

KDS Today

[edit] Papa Joe Smiddy

The story of UVa-Wise, founded in 1954 as Clinch Valley College, mirrors that of Papa Joe Smiddy. As the College came from humble roots, so did Papa Joe. Born in Jellico, Tennessee in 1920, young Joe grew up the son of a miner/merchant and a housewife. His father moved the family into town so the eight children could study music. Be it the organ and piano for his older sisters or the mandolin for his brother, the Smiddy children all played. By age five, Joe played guitar, and by age eight, trumpet.

They also all listened...to their father’s “fun” songs and banjo playing, to their mother’s Bible verses and preaching, and to the radio. “Uncle Dave Macon” was a regular visitor from Cincinnati, via an Atwater-Kent radio, and the Grand Ole Opry from Nashville filled the Smiddy living room on Saturday nights.

By high school, Joe played trumpet in dance bands, a talent that kept him engaged through his days at Lincoln Memorial University and landed him a spot in Army bands during World War II. When Joe volunteered for overseas duty, he had visions of meeting his brother (who was serving in Europe) in Paris. Instead, Sergeant Smiddy found himself on New Caledonia Island in the South Pacific, and at war’s end, in the Philippines.

Joe shared his love of music and learning with students at Jonesville High School after the war, where he quickly became principal. Sam Crockett lured him to the fledgling Clinch Valley Branch of The University of Virginia in 1953. He began as a biology instructor, but within three years was named “Director.”

The College struggled, but grew. In its early years, it offered two-year degrees and an opportunity for good students to continue at a four-year school. It offered higher education where there had once been none. It offered hope. Papa Joe saw to it personally that any student with promise, regardless of his or her financial situation or skin color, was given a chance. Women were admitted from the first day, and black students were admitted while segregation was still the law in Virginia.

Papa guided the school through the turbulent 60’s, a time when the folk music of Joan Baez, Pete Seeger, and Judy Collins echoed through campus, and when students asked Papa Joe to play along with them in their dormitories.

[edit] Juan Carlos Quintero-Herencia

Born in Santurce, Puerto Rico in 1963. Member and co-editor of the Poetry journal Filo de juego (1982-1987) the Instituto de Cultura Puertorriqueña published, in 2002, his early poetry under a volume entitled El hilo para el marisco/Cuaderno de los envíos (2002). This volume received the Puerto Rican Pen Club Poetry Prize in 2004. Quintero Herencia has published other collections of poetry such as La caja negra (1996, Isla Negra) and Libro del sigiloso (2007, Terranova). Quintero Herencia poetry displays an intense desire for experimentation and baroque imagery. In Quintero's recent poetry a concern for the specific nature of the political gesture of poetry is a recurrent topic. As an essayist Quintero-Herencia published Fulguración del espacio: Letras e imaginario institucional de la Revolución cubana (1960-1971) (Beatriz Viterbo, 2002), a contextual and critical reading of the crucial debates which defined the Cuban intellectual field during the 1960’s. His second book, La máquina de la salsa: Tránsitos del sabor (Ediciones Vértigo, 2005) is a comprehensive critical balance on the Salsa phenomenon. As a Professor, Quintero-Herencia teaches undergraduate and graduate courses in Latin American and Caribbean literatures and cultures at the University of Maryland, College Park (USA). He also teaches Literary Theory. Before going to the University of Maryland, he taught at the University of Puerto Rico, Río Piedras and Brown University.
