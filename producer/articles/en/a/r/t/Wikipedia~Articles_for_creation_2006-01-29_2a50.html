[edit] LOTUS Self Defense

This request for creation has been reviewed. The result is below.


LOTUS is an effective self-defense fighting art from Thailand, which employs joint manipulations, locks, pressure points, judo throws, kicks, blocks, sweeps, and strikes primarily for self-defense. It is more accurately described as a war art as it was developed to utterly destroy an aggressor quickly and completely. Unlike Taekwondo or Karate, there is little sport competition in LOTUS. The very powerful techniques of LOTUS can pose a great risk of injury or even death if applied uncontrollably for such competitive events. LOTUS contains both outfighting and infighting techniques. The strategy to end most self-defense situations is to get near for a close strike, lock, or throw to control the attacker. LOTUS practitioners seek to gain advantage through techniques, avoiding the use of strength against strength. While training methods vary, a typical training session will contain dynamic stretching, warm-up, various master drills known as kata, punches and kicks, break-falls, rolls, self-defense techniques, and sparring. Physical strength is not a requisite of the LOTUS style. The strength and fitness that is necessary to perform the techniques develops naturally as a result of consistent and vigorous training. LOTUS is appropriate for all persons of all ages and helps develop physical fitness, mental discipline and tenacity of spirit. Since its founding, LOTUS has continued to spread throughout the United States with dojos in Louisiana, Washington state, Oregon, Georgia, and North Carolina.

HISTORY: At the time of LOTUS creation, the United States was at war with North Vietnam and had military troops stationed in Ubon Ratchathani, Thailand. Many of the United States military personnel began studying a martial art called LOTUS, created by Mr. Precha Mahachanavong. Mr. Precha was a professional Muay Thai fighter in addition to his other martial arts background. Mr. Precha created LOTUS by using his Muay Thai skills and combing them with the best techniques from Aikido, Judo, Kenpo Karate, and Tang So Do.

A food enzyme is a naturally occuring enzyme found in raw foods, such as uncooked vegetables and fruits. Food enzymes aid in the digestion of food within the human body.

[edit] Food Enzymes

Food enzymes occur naturally in raw, uncooked foods, especially edible plants. Among plants, sprouts, seeds, and legumes are particularly rich in food enzymes. Specific enzymes found within foods are listed here:

  1. Amylase: Breaks down carbohydrates
  2. Bromelain: Breaks down proteins
  3. Cellulose: Helps in fiber digestion
  4. Hydrochloric Acid: Cleans bacteria from the stomach, stimulates the pancreas to produce insulin and other products, and starts pepsin
  5. Lactase: Breaks down lactose
  6. Lipase: Breaks down glycerol, fatty acids and other fats
  7. Ox Bile: Helps the gallbladder, the liver, and fat digestion
  8. Pancreatin: Containing amylase, lipase, and protease, pancreatin fulfills the functions of these three enzymes
  9. Papain: Helps break down protein
  10. Pepsin: Breaks down protein (Needs hydrochloric acid to work)
  11. Protease: Breaks down protein into amino acids

[edit] Enzyme Effects on the Body

Enzymes fulfill their functions (listed above) but may have effects on the body. They act as a catalyst, so once they have broken down food, they can still be used to break down more. Almost always, their effects on the body are for the positive. The main effects are shown and described below.

[edit] Effects on Cells

Enzymes slow down the aging process within cells and certain enzymes, called metabolic enzymes, assist in starting chemical reactions within cells, tissues, and organs. These reactions are necessary to live.

[edit] Effects on the Digestive System

Enzymes assist the digestive system by breaking down fat, fiber, protein, acids, lactose, and other products, which makes it easier for the body to digest and absorb nutrients.

[edit] Lack of Enzymes

A lack of enzymes has profound negative effects on the body. These include food allergies, and stomach (bowel) irritation.

[edit] Enzyme Destruction

Enzymes are destroyed when the foods that they are within are cooked. When the temperature of a food reaches 118 degrees fahrenheit, it starts to break down. Enzymes that reach this temperature are destroyed in much the same way as they break down foods; Their structure is permanently altered. At this point they no longer produce the effects they would if they were not cooked.

Hegemonic peace is a term in political science. It is a kind of peace between two actors who's relative power is unbalanced, but the gap in the power still allows both actors to accept or reject the agreement conditions. Hegemonic peace lies in between "equal peace" between two relatively equal partners who agree on the settlement terms and "domination peace" imposed by an overwelming power of one of the actors on another.

    Pecom 32 was home computer designed and produced in 1985 by company EI NiÅ¡. The characteristics of the computer were: CDP 1802B CPU (5MHz), 16 K ROM BASIC, 36 K RAM (32 K available for user), plastic keyboard with 55 keys, textual screen of 40 x 24 characters in 8 colors. It had 8 octave tone generator with 10 volume levels, and connectors for TV, monitor, tape recorder and RS232. The dimensions of the computer were: 25.2 cm x 19.6 cm x 5.6 cm

    The computer was planed to be produced at a low price, to be a good domestic Yugoslavian alternative to the (often illegaly) imported ZX Spectrum and Commodore 64.

CPU was considered obsolete and not much known by local programmers. The only way to get high resolution graphic was in character redefinition of 32 characters. There was also complaint to the slowness of the mathematical functions.

    It was purchased by many schools, to teach introduction to BASIC programming, but very few home users buy this computer. The computer was later expanded to 64 K of RAM and renamed to Pecom 64.

    [edit] Josephine Shaw Lowell (December 16,1843-October 12, 1905)

    This request for creation has been reviewed. The result is below.
    If your request was declined and you want to make changes and try again, copy and paste your article into a new submission and make your changes there.


    Josephine Shaw Lowell was a Progressive Reform leader in the United States in the Nineteenth century. She was born into a wealthy New England family in 1843. Her parents, Francis George Shaw and Sarah Sturgis, were both philanthropists and intellectuals who encouraged their five children to study, learn and become involved in their communities. Josephine was a Unitarian.

    Josephine married Charles Russell Lowell, a business man, in 1863. She followed him to Virginia when he was called into service during the Civil War. Josephine helped wounded men on the battlefield. Charles died in battle, only a few years after they were married and only one month before their daughter was born.

    A young widow, Josephine moved to Staten Island with her daughter, Carlotta, and lived with her parents. She became a businesswoman and a reformer. Josephine was active in the Anti-Imperialist League where she met Jane Addams and other prominent Progressives. She served as Vice-President of the League from 1901-1905 and was a great advocate of Philippine independence.

    Josephine was committed to social justice and reform and seized the opportunity to become involved in Progressive reform and the eradication of poverty. She once said,â If the working people had all they ought to have, we should not have the paupers and criminals. It is better to save them before they go under, than to spend your life fishing them out afterward."

    In 1876, Governor Samuel Tilden of New York State appointed Josephine to Commissioner of the New York State Board of Charities. She was the first woman to ever hold this position. She served actively on the Board until 1889.

    Throughout her lifetime, she also founded many charitable organizations including: the New York Charity Organization in 1882, the House of Refuge for Women (later known as the State Training School for Girls) in 1886, the Woman's Municipal League in 1894, and the Civil Service Reform Association of New York State in 1895.

    Perhaps her most wide-ranging and effective organization was the New York Consumers' League which she established in 1890. This organization strove to improve the wages and the working conditions of women workers in New York City. The League was particularly concerned with retail clerks. Josephine published a "White List" that contained a list of stores known to treat women workers well. Initially, the list was very short.

    The New York Consumer's League was adopted in many other cities as chapters opened across the country. The umbrella organization, the National Consumer's League, became a powerful lobbying group.

    She died in 1905 in New York City. The Fountain Terrace in Bryant Park, which is behind the New York Public Library, is dedicated to her. The fountain is New York City's first public memorial dedicated to a woman.
