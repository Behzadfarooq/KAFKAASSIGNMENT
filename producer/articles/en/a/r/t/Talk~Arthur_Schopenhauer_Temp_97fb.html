[edit] Comment

I used the "Introduction" section written by David Berman in the following source for a few comments below:

 "The World as Will and Idea, Abridged in One Volume"
 Edited by: David Berman
 Translated by: Jill Berman
 Publisher: Everyman Library, Orion Publishing group, London, U.K.
 Date: 1997
 ISBN: 0 460 87505 1

This souce says he was named Arthur so the name would be recognizable in many European languages, and also because his father had hoped that he would be born in England (during a visit there) so he would enjoy 'immunities' valuable to a merchant. ([1] p. xvii)

The first paragraph of the WikiPedia entry says "...that saw life as being essentially evil and futile...". [1], p.xxxii says that Shopenhauer saw the "Life, in short, (1) is morally wrong, (2) has no meaning or purpose, and (3) will always have more pain than pleasure." I think a statement like the latter might be clearer. Morally wrong, since "To exist is to be unjust, since it is impossible not to encroach on the will of another." ([1], p. xxxi). Also the wikipedia definition of evil doesn't seem to fit well to me. A brief explanation in the first paragraph on why he felt life is morally wrong, as in the referenced quote, would probably add value and clarity.

In the section on Philosophy, I think the link between "desire", "Will" and the "noumenon" thing-in-itself could be stated more clearly? i.e. The Will (to life, to exist) is desire, and both, being equivalent, are the thing-in-itself. All things that exist are therefore objectified desire. And because existence is desire, there must always be more unhappiness than happiness in the world, which seems to be the root of his philosophy's pessimism. ([1] p. xxv, xxxii)

I'm a novice at Wikipedia. My comments are _only_ meant in the spirit of constructive criticism. I was recently reading the above mentioned source, so these comments were fresh in my mind.
