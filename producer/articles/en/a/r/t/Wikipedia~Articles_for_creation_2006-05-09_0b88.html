[edit] [Saifa]

Saifa, translated from japanese as rolling wave. Often used as a Kata or display in many martial art styles. Saifa is a kata used in karate-do and in volves usually a movement of 20 steps, including grabs and strikes, all performed in different motions. It is a mixture of slow and graceful moves with strong sharp movements. Its Bunkai(or practical application) involves the compressing of the vertebrae in the neck and the full breaking of the neck. This kata can have variations from martial arts style to style and can appear in a variety of places in the karate-ka's(students) training.

[edit] Bhongir

Bhongir is a town in Nalgonda district , 47kms from Hyderabad .

Bhongir is famous for its fort.

Bhongir fort was built on an isolated rock by the western Chalukya ruler Tribhuvanamalla Vikramaditya VI and was thus named after him as Tribhuvanagiri. This name gradually became Bhuvanagiri and subsequently Bhongir. At the foot of the fortified rocks 609.6 meters above the sea level stands the town of Bhongir,it has a unique egg-shaped construction with two entry points protected by huge rocks, so the fort was considered practically impregnable by invading armies. The splendid historical fort with the awe-inspiring rock and the aesthetically fortified courts which have stood the ravages of time stir the imagination of the tourists.A moat that encircles the fort, a vast underground chamber, trap doors, an armoury, stables, ponds, wells etc., make for fascinating viewing. The view from top of the surrounding countryside is simply breathtaking. The Bala Hisar or citadel on the top of the hill gives a bird's eye view of the neighbouring area. The fort is associated with the herioc queen Rudramadevi and her grandson Prataparudra's rule.

[edit] Region Rock

Region rock is a subgenre of punk music originating from the southeast United States. It is generally melodic and energetic, often with more personal rather than political lyrics than other punk subgenres. Most, if not all, region rock bands, record labels, and music venues are diy and non-profit. Some prominent cities for this style include Miami, Pensacola, and Gainesville in Florida, Athens, GA, Chattanooga, TN, Cleveland, MS, and Asheville, NC. Most of the bands play mainly in their town or nearby towns in the region, mostly in houses, basements, and small bars or coffee shops, and therefore may not be well known outside of their own town despite being local favorites. Some bands that have received more widespread recognition include the Stun Guns, Sexy, Hickey, ADD/C, Jack Palance Band, Queer Wulf, and Allergic to Bullshit. Many of the region rock bands put out 7" records on Chattanooga's This here Records, which is now being joined by the more recently established Plan-It-X South record label and Onion Flavored Records.

[edit] USS Winnemucca (PC-1145)

PC-1145 was laid down on 2 June 1943 at the Defoe Shipbuilding Company in Bay City, Michigan; launched on 27 October 1943; and commissioned at New Orleans, Louisiana, on 1 June 1944, with Lieutenant J. L. Houget, USNR, in command.

PC-1145 completed shakedown training in the Gulf of Mexico on 11 June and reported for duty to the Commander, Gulf Sea Frontier. For the next year, the submarine chaser plied the waters of the Gulf of Mexico and the Atlantic seaboard conducting anti-submarine patrols and escorting coastwise convoys, In the performance of those duties, the little warship ranged as far north as New York City and as far south as the Panama Canal Zone.

Following the victory in Europe, PC-1145 received orders assigning her to the Pacific Fleet. Early in June 1945, she departed her base at Miami, Florida, and headed via the Panama Canal for the California coast and underwent repairs at San Diego, California, before continuing west to Hawaii. She departed San Diego on 15 July and arrived in Pearl Harbor on the 21st. She did not resume her westward voyage toward the Marshall Islands until 7 August. The submarine chaser reported for duty upon her arrival at Eniwetok Atoll on the 13th, but hostilities ceased the following day.

Nevertheless, the submarine chaser remained in the western Pacific more than a year. For about a month following her arrival, she performed patrol and escort duty in the Marshalls and the Carolines. On 7 September, she departed Eniwetok for a round-trip voyage to Japan, returning almost a month later, on 5 October. The ship then resumed her patrol and escort duties in the Marshalls and Carolines. After a round-trip voyage to Wake Island late in October, she reported to the atoll commander at Majuro in November for duty as station search and rescue ship. That assignment lasted until mid-February 1946 when she departed Majuro for extensive maintenance at Guam.

She completed repairs on 16 June and departed Guam on an inspection tour of the Western Carolines carrying representatives of the United States Commercial Company, a public corporation charged with responsibility for the economic rehabilitation of the Central Pacific region. For the next four months, PC-1145 served the United States Military Government administering the Central Pacific islands and worked hand-in-hand with officials of the United States Commercial Company surveying the region in preparation for efforts to reestablish a viable local economy.

In November, she departed the Central Pacific for her first complete overhaul since commissioning. She entered the Pearl Harbor Naval Shipyard on 21 November and remained there until 23 December. On that date, she reported to the Commander, Fleet Training Center, Pearl Harbor, under whose auspices she conducted refresher training and made a cruise to Hilo. PC-1145 departed Pearl Harbor on 27 February 1947, and on 4 March, arrived at Midway Island where she reported for duty to the Commander, Naval Operating Base, Midway. There, she served as an air search and rescue ship until the end of the second week in May. Relieved by PC-1172 on the 13th, PC-1145 headed back to Pearl Harbor. The warship operated out of the Oahu base conducting upkeep and repairs and serving as a training ship until late July. She then returned to Midway and resumed duty as search and rescue vessel. On 15 November, PCS-1399 relieved her at Midway; and PC-1145 returned to Pearl Harbor for a restricted availability.

For the remainder of her active career, PC-1145 alternated between duty out of Pearl Harbor and service as an air search and rescue ship, at first based at Midway but later at Kwajalein, Johnston Island, Guam, and Samoa. After 1950, however, Guam and Samoa ceased to be duty stations for the submarine chaser and her sister ships.

Duty in and out of Pearl Harbor consisted of repairs, training, target towing, and anti-submarine warfare operations. PC-1145 departed Pearl Harbor for the last time on 19 February 1955 and arrived in San Francisco, California, a week later. F ollowing nearly four months of repairs at Treasure Island, she stood out of San Francisco Bay on her way to Astoria, Oregon, and inactivation. On 15 August 1955, the ship was placed out of commission at Astoria and was berthed with the Columbia River Group, Pacific Reserve Fleet. On 15 February 1956, the submarine chaser--still inactive--was named Winnemucca, after a city in northwestern Nevada situated on the Humboldt River, and the seat of government for Humboldt County. She remained with the reserve fleet until 1 June 1960 when her name was struck from the Navy list. On 1 November, she was transferred to the Navy of the Republic of Korea.

[edit] USS Susanville (PC--1149)

USS Susanville (PC-1149) was laid down as PC-1149 on 6 November 1943 at the Defoe Shipbuilding Company in Bay City, Michigan; launched on 11 January 1944; and commissioned on 22 June 1944.

She sailed, via the Mississippi River and New Orleans, Louisiana, to the Gulf of Mexico and conducted shakedown training out of Miami, Florida. During her first year of service, PC-1149 cruised the waters along the east coast of the United States and in the Caribbean Sea. She patrolled for German U-boats and escorted coastal and Caribbean convoys from port to port. The submarine chaser ranged as far north as New York and as far south as Guantanamo Bay, Cuba.

On 13 June 1945, about a week before the first anniversary of her active service, she transited the Panama Canal and joined the Pacific Fleet. After stopping at San Diego, California, and at Pearl Harbor, she arrived at Eniwetok in the Central Pacific on 28 July; then sailed the following day for the Marianas. She reached Saipan on 1 August and remained there through the end of the war and into September. After a brief voyage to Iwo Jima early that month, PC-1149 returned to the Marianas and operated in that island group for a little more than a year. In November 1946, she returned, via Kwajalein and Pearl Harbor, to the United States, arriving at the Columbia River Reserve Fleet berthing area in Oregon in mid-December. On 20 February 1947, PC-1149 was placed out of commission and berthed at Columbia River.

On 15 February 1956, while still out of commission, she was named Susanville, after a town in Lassen County in northeastern California. Sixteen months later, on 16 July 1957, Susanville was transferred, on loan, to the Nationalist Chinese Navy and was commissioned as Hsi Kiang. She served the Taiwan Navy until 30 June 1972 when she was transferred to the Taiwan Customs Service.

[edit] Century Break

In the sport snooker, century break refers to scoring 100 points without missing a shot. The player does this by potting red balls and coloured balls alternately.

Scoring 100 points over the course of a whole frame is not a century. It must be done during one break (turn at the table). Points for a foul shot by the opponent do not count in the player's break.

The highest possible century in snooker is 147, composed of 15 reds, 15 blacks and the six remaining colours; yellow, green, brown, blue, pink and black.

Under exceptional circumstances, it is possible to score a break of 155. if a player comes to the table after a foul shot by the opponent and he is unable to hit both sides of any red ball, he may nominate one of the colours as a red. Under these circumstances, it is possible to pot 16 reds and blacks, plus 27 points for the six remaining colours, totalling 155 points.

To score a century, there must be at least ten reds on the table when the player comes to play. If there are nine red, then he can only score 99 (9x8 + 27 = 99)

The seven-time World Champion Stephen Hendry holds the record for century breaks in tournament play. He has made over 700 in tournament play.

The quickest recorded century break in tournament play was by Tony Drago. In the 1996 UK Championship, he took just 3 minutes 31 seconds (241 seconds) to score a hundred.

The youngest player to score a professional century break was Judd Trump at 14 years 206 days old.

[edit] Moulin Rouge, Las Vegas

The Moulin Rouge opened on May 24, 1955, and was the first integrated hotel/casino in Las Vegas if not the nation. Until that time most all strip-casinos were totally segregated, off-limits to blacks unless they were the entertainment or low-paying labor. On top of this, discrimination helped to hem the black population into living on the "west side" of town:

  • In the North by Washington Avenue;
  • In the South by Bonanza Avenue;
  • In the West by H Street;
  • In the East by A Street

It was during this era that a caucasian man by the name of Will Max Schwartz saw the need for an integrated hotel. Will, along with other investors (including boxing-great Joe Lewis), built and opened the Moulin Rouge at 900 W. Bonanza Road.. This placed it comfortably in a prime location between the predominately white area of the strip and the largely black west side. The complex itself was two stuccoed buildings that contained the hotel, casino, and a theater. The exterior had the hotel's name in stylized cursive writing, and murals depicting dancing and fancy cars.

When it opened, the Moulin Rouge was fully integrated top to bottom from employees to patrons to entertainers.


[edit] A Short History

The popularity of the Moulin Rouge blew up almost overnight. The hotel made the June 20th, 1955 cover of Life magazine, with a photo of two female showgirls. A veritable A-List of 50-60's era performers regularly showed to party until dawn. Great black singers and musicians such as Sammy Davis, Jr., Nat King Cole, Pearl Bailey, and Louis Armstrong would perform often. These men were banned from gambling or staying at the hotels on the strip. In addition, white performers including George Burns, Jack Benny, and Frank Sinatra would drop in after their shows to gamble and perform. Eventually management added a 2:30am "Third Show" to accomodate the popularity and crowds.


[edit] A Sudden Closing & Lasting Impact

In November of 1955 the Moulin Rouge closed it's doors. Some say it was a victim of a casino oversaturation (the Moulin Rouge was one of 4 new hotels that ran into major financial issues that year). Some say it was poor management. The exact cause will probably never be known.

The short but brief life of the Moulin Rouge helped the civil-rights movement in Las Vegas. Many of those who enjoyed and were employed by the hotel became activists and supporters. The hotel was also the spark needed to bring an end to segregation on the strip.

Under threat of a protest down the Las Vegas Strip in March of 1960 against racial discrimination from Las Vegas casinos, a meeting was hurridly arranged by then-Govenor Grant Sawyer between hotel owners, city and state officials; local black leaders and then-NAACP president Dr. James McMillan. The meeting was held on March 26, 1960 at the closed Moulin Rouge, and lead to an agreement to desegregate all strip casinos.

The Moulin Rouge has been entered into the National Register of Historic Places.


[edit] An Ignoble End

Shuttered for decades, many plans had been hatched to rebuild and reopen the cultural landmark. However on May 29th, 2003 an fire ripped through the buildings, almost entirely gutting the complex. No witnesses have ever been found, no one has come forward with information leading to the cause of the fire, and to this day all that remains is the front visage with it's signature stylized name.

[edit] right to communicate

A right to communicate was conceptualized by Jean d‚ÄôArcy, a French TV pioneer and founder of Eurovision. In 1969, while serving as Director of Radio and Visual Services in the United Nations Office of Public Information, he published an article on ‚ÄúDirect Broadcast Satellites and the Right to Communicate‚Äù in the EBU Review (Vol. 118:14-18, 1969). In the that article he made the bold assertion that ‚ÄúThe time will come when the [UN] Universal Declaration of Human Rights will have to encompass a more extensive right than man‚Äôs right to information, first laid down twenty-one years ago in Article 19. This is the right to communicate.‚Äù

As envisioned by d‚ÄôArcy, a right to communicate is planetary in that it was conceived at a time when the global implications of satellite communication, combined with computing networking, were becoming apparent. He foresaw that information and communication technologies provided an alternative to the traditional vertical, top down, one-way communication dominated modern mass media. ICT made possible the personal, horizontal, global communication that would in time evolve into the Internet and World Wide Web. Both individuals and groups could directly participate in multi-way communication on the global network. Thus, a right to communicate is a right belonging not only to individuals but to groups as well. It is a strong positive right in that the state has the responsibility to insure its citizens have the resources to exercise the right. Finally, it is an extension or redefinition of earlier rights and freedoms, in particular freedom of opinion and expression. However, like other third generation rights the debate of what constitutes a right to communicate is yet to be resolved.

Jean d‚ÄôArcy himself never provided a concise definition of the right to communicate, however, his idea was taken up within the United Nations community. The 1970s was a period of intense debate within the United Nations about cultural diversity, media dominance, and the ‚Äúfree flow of information‚Äù. During this time the Non-Aligned nations were advancing the proposal of a New World Information and Communication Order (NWICO). As well, Cold War politics established further fault lines. Nonetheless, efforts began within UNESCO in the 1970s to formulate a right to communicate. However, the right to communicate fell victim to international North-South and East West ideological and power politics in the 1980s. The United States and Great Britain withdrew from UNESCO, the idea of a NWICO collapsed, and UNESCO abandoned it efforts to formulate a right to communicate. While UNESCO dropped the effort to promote a right to communicate, various non-governmental organizations (NGOs) remained committed to and preserved d‚ÄôArcy‚Äôs vision.

With the collapse of the Soviet Union and the end of the cold war, combined with the emergence of the Internet, there is renewed interest in communication rights. Efforts were made, unfortunately unsuccessfully, to promote a right to communicate during the United Nations World Summit on the Information Society. Nonetheless, scholars and communication rights activists continue to explore the concept and its implications in the context of rapidly expanding global communication. As citizens around the world increasingly take advantage of global electronic communications but see their privacy, intellectual freedom, right to information, and cultural autonomy threatened, they will recognize the need for a comprehensive right to communicate.

A right to communicate can be conceived as a human right that is the apex of a cluster and hierarchy of rights, freedoms, entitlements, and responsibilities. A right to communicate includes at a minimum, according to J. Richstad and M. H. Anderson, ‚Äúthe right to inform and be informed, the right to active participation in the communication process, the right of equitable access to information resources and information, and the right of cultural and individual privacy from communication‚Äù. A right to communicate encompasses interconnected freedoms such as the freedom of opinion, expression, and information. These freedoms in turn are exercised through such entitlements as freedom of the press, the lack of censorship, the rights of journalists, the right to access to information, and so forth.

A right to communicate also embodies the right of individuals and groups not to communicate, a right relevant to cultural rights and national and group identity. A right to communicate also embodies the concept of citizen participation and responsibility for the formulation of communication policy and rights. It provides a framework for addressing rights, freedoms and entitlements related to access including literacy, language, intellectual freedom, cultural diversity, language right, and intellectual property rights.

While its precise definition and application requires further research and debate, the pioneers promoting a right to communicate provided the basic framework for developing a strategy to advance democracy and electronic networking. That framework consists of participatory, interactive, horizontal, and multi-way communication. Further information and analysis of this framework and its implication for communication rights can be found at www.righttocommunicate.org.

[edit] Tournament of Champions (Debate)

The Tournament of Champions, sometimes called the TOC, is the coordinating organization for national-circuit high school debate. The Tournament of Champions itself is held every May in Lexington, Kentucky and is hosted by the University of Kentucky.

Currently, the Tournament of Champions features four events: Policy Debate, Lincoln-Douglas Debate, Public Forum Debate, and Student Congress. Although each of these events originated in the National Forensic League, the TOC is considered more competitive than the NFL National Tournament. Indeed, qualifying to even attend the TOC is more difficult and more selective than qualifying for NFL Nationals. Further, the judging at the TOC is more consistent, if not better on the whole, than the judging at NFL District Qualifiers and Nationals. Finally, qualification to the TOC is based on multiple performances at nationally-designated invitationals which earn their "bid" status, while qualification to the NFL is based on a one-time performance at a local chapter's district qualifier. For these reasons, the Tournament of Champions is considered by some as the better-respected national tournament of the two.

[edit] Qualifying for the Tournament of Champions

Admission to the Tournament of Champions is a difficult task. In Policy and Lincoln-Douglas debate, certain tournaments are awarded "bid status" for different levels of advancement. The largest and most competititive tournaments (such as Greenhill, the Glenbrooks, and the like) award bids to those competitors who reach Octafinals. Other tournaments are authorized to give bids at other levels (Quarterfinals, Semifinals, and Finals). These tournaments earn their bid status by virtue of their size, the scope of their competition, the quality of the tournament, and the historical value of the tournament. To fully qualify to the Tournament of Champions, competitors must earn two such bids from two bid-designated tournaments.

In Student Congress, bids are earned according to the level of competition that the representative reaches (semifinals, finals, or placing in the top six). Unlike qualifying in Policy or LD, however, a congressperson who places in the top six at their state forensic associaton's tournament of who wins their NFL district qualifier can count either of these achievements as one of their two required bids.

In Public Forum, there is not yet a bid system. The event is still in its formative stages and not as many higher-level national invitationals offer a full-fledged Public Forum division as they do for Policy or Lincoln-Douglas. Accordingly, the Public Forum division of the Tournament of Champions is structured as a round robin. The TOC Public Forum Round Robin is only in its second year. To qualify for this competitition, prospective two-person Public Forum teams must apply on an individual basis.

[edit] Wikipedia:Walkabout

This page is a proposed Wikipedia policy, guideline, or process. The proposal may still be in development, under discussion, or in the process of gathering consensus for adoption. References or links to this page should not describe it as "policy".

Category:Wikipedia proposals

This proposal seeks to establish an extension to WP:BITE, that users might consider trying to be new again for a while. This will require some amendment to WP:SOCK. All users are welcome to comment on the talk page or improve the proposal. However, significant changes should be discussed before implementing them.

[edit] Overview

You've been around for a while here, and you're comfortable. You have read the guideline page stating please don't bite the newcomers -- perhaps you've been politely referred to it -- but you don't think you are guilty of that kind of behaviour. Still, you might feel up to a wikivacation. But gosh, you still want to participate in your favourite hobby (which is Wikipedia, of course). If this is so, then you might consider participating in, shall we say, an altered state: you're going to take a Walkabout.

[edit] Oh yeah, I seen that on TV, that's that Aussie thingie, eh?

Well, sure, a walkabout is that. You can think of it as leaving your vitrual community and entering the vast wilderness of anonymity to walk among the wandering unregistered users and the wind-blown help pages. Although another analogy might be the practice of certain rulers who, to be sure that the people were being treated fairly, sometimes left to walk about in disguise among their subjects.

[edit] Alright, I'm listening. What do I do?

The idea isn't hard. Go away, try to forget everything you know about this place, then come back and walk about. Here are some suggested steps to follow:

  1. You'll want to write yourself a wikivacation note and finish up some of your wikiwork. Well, if you must. But for full effect, don't.
  2. Log out.
  3. Go to the Main page.
  4. Don't to anything unless you've been told that you can do it, and how, in some sort of help page, or the like, or on a talk page by another user, or stumbling across it some other way, not just because you already know how.
    1. If you do read a help page, or the like, try to read without preconception, and not read into it what you already know. If you see an unintended but reasonable interpretation, try to take it.
    2. And did you really read all help pages when you were new? Most important things are stated multiple times. Some are stated only once, and not prominently. You might try to miss those things. For instance, you didn't know that Wikipedia was editable by anyone except that, just now, you saw it on the main page. Or maybe you didn't see it: you missed it, so you won't think about editing Wikipedia till you find it later.
    3. No cheating.
  5. Do not refer to the fact that you've been around a while. Let people treat you as a newcomer.
  6. Don't even think about your usual account for at least a few days.


You might also consider:

  • Logging in, but without making a user page.
  • Not logging in at all.
  • Contribute to an area you don't normally contribute to.
  • Contribute to an area you do normally contribute to, but without disclosing your identity. (You can give your usual account credit for your contributions when you're done your walkabout, and not before.)
  • All of the above at once. Why not?

[edit] Are you nuts? Why would I do any of that?

  • To see if help pages, and the like, accurately reflect the current state of affairs. This can uncover problems in the "user interface" of Wikipedia, and this is good practice for any user interface, really.
  • To see how newcomers are actually treated. This can uncover problems in the community.
  • To be reminded about what it's like to be new. This can uncover problems in yourself.
  • To actually watch and participate without guilt as Wikipedia functions without you executing any special role as RC patroller, administrator, resident expert, mediator, wikiproject leader, or the like, preferably even having dropped these roles unannounced. This can uncover something you knew on a rational level already.

[edit] Caveat

The sockpuppet rules do explicitly allow this sort of thing, and since your usual account is now dormant, there is little to worry about. Still, one possible exception is if you weighed in on an active discussion just before your walkabout, and then wandered back into it during your walkabout while it was still active. Use common sense.

[edit] Proposal specifics

Of course anyone can do this right now anyway, so the above text can stand on its own, as a suggestion, in the project space. However, further action is actually required:

  • Amend WP:SOCK to explicity allow this! As it stands, this is frowned upon. There is a one-line out for prominent users buried in there, but the overall tone is really quite negative towards this sort of thing. There would need be a more emphatic exception, referring to this page, and prominence should not be a precondition.
  • Amend WP:BITE to suggest this, as an addendum to admonitions to remember that you were new once.

Rotrud (775-810) was the eldest daughter of Charlemagne and Hildegard, a Swabian princess.

Rotrud's life comes down to us pieced together from a patchwork of sources. We know that she was educated in the Palace School by Alcuin of York, who affectionately calls her Columba in his letters to her.

When she was six, her father betrothed her to Constantine IV of Byzantium, whose mother Irene was ruling as regent. The Greeks called her Erythro, and sent a scholar monk called Elisaeus to educate her in Greek language and manners. However, the alliance fell apart by 786 when she was eleven. (See Runciman 106, 110) Although she never married, she did bear an illegitimate son, Louis, with her lover Count Rorico of Maine; their son went on to become Abbot of St. Denis.

We do know, however, that Rotrud also eventually took the veil and joined her aunt Gisela, abbess of Chelles. The two women authored a letter to the aging Alcuin, who was at Tours at the time, requesting that he write a commentary explaining the Gospel of John. The two women writers seem to have been erudite and versed especially in the conventions of religious women writing to churchmen (as in the case of St. Jerome and his lady correspondents, Eustochium in particular). As a result, Alcuin eventually produced his seven-book Commentaria in Johannem Evangelistam, a more accessible companion to the gospel than St. Augustine's massive and challenging Tractates on St. John, whose depths the two women had been trying to plumb in the first place. Commentators have dated the letter to the spring of 800, four years before Alcuin's death and ten before Rotrud's. (See Gaskoin 119, who disagrees with D√ºmmler, MGH 196 on the sequence of Alcuin's letters.)

Strangely enough, these two aspects of Rotrud are seldom considered together. Political histories of Charlemagne her father discuss her as a princess who was potentially a pawn and a woman of questionable morals (see, for instance, the German Wikipedia entry: de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotrud); religious histories discuss her as the second nun in the letter from Chelles (monasticmatrix.usc.edu/commentaria/article.php?textId=65). These small contradictions show us a portrait of a woman who was perhaps more fascinating than what little we have left behind.

[edit] Heinrich Josef Baermann

Heinrich Josef Baermann, also spelled Barmann (with umlaut above the initial "a") was a clarinetist born in 1784 in Germany, for whom Felix Mendelssohn and Carl Maria von Weber wrote concerted clarinet pieces. His son, Karl Baermann or Barmann, was also a distinguished clarinetist.

In addition to being a fine performer (responsible for the advance of clarinet technique) Baermann composed his own original works for clarinet solo, and clarinet with orchestra. These include a pair of clarinet concertinos (one of them, in c minor, beginning with the unusual pairing of tympani and solo clarinet, as a sort of introduction to the first movement). Their style is very much in keeping with the concertos of Weber and Louis Spohr.

In the 1830s, Mendelssohn wrote a pair of double concertos for Baermann and his son. These are scored for clarinet, bassett-horn, and orchestra, and exist in alternative versions with piano accompaniment.

Greeks began to believe that the gods resided in a heaven, called Olympus, in the sky. The poets of the day supported this belief.
