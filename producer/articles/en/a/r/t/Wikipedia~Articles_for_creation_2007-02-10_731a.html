Daniel Frederick George Henderson (born February 9, 1943, Manhattan, New York - died December 16, 2006, Queens, New York) is an African American Emmy Award winning television character actor, Nine-Time Grammy Award winner, songwriter, and R&B singer.

[edit] Life

Woods was born in Manhattan in 1943, and was raised there, along with his eight siblings, his mother, Lucinda Henderson, worked as a cashier at a local department store on the Upper East Side of Manhattan, while his kind, eccentric father, Thomas Henderson, worked as a famous author. Daniel started to learn how to play the Piano at 5 Years old, and became very smart with it, unlike his older siblings, Daniel went to the Elvin Rockefeller Elementary School in Brooklyn, he was very smart at Math, Reading and Science. In 1951, 8-Year Old, Woods decided to have a acting and singing career (both at different times), and was discovered by His late Uncle, Robin King, who died in 1950, when Daniel turned Eight Years old. Daniel went to show business success, and was signed to a label named Candy Records, Daniel recorded his first album, Songs of Love which was released on January 27, 1951, the Album scored big time on The Top 100 Billboard Music Charts, the hit single "Come and Fly Me" won Daniel Three Grammy Awards including one for Best New Recording Artist and Album of The Year.

Daniel went back to school after his first album was a success, and at 14 Years Old, In 1957, Daniel Graduated from Spellman College in Atlanta, Georgia, and recieved his Bachelor Degree, and Diploma. In 1961, Daniel Woods made his acting debut on Television, starring in the comedy series, Rich Man, Poor Man which aired on the CBS television network from 1961 until 1964, the show only last 65 episodes and three seasons, the show became a top-rated show on CBS and on the Nielsen Ratings, it did good throughout the entire series' run (#30 in 1961-62, #27 in 1962-63, and #2 in 1963-64).

After the cancellation of Rich Man, Poor Man, CBS Executive Producers saw Woods' work, and strongly decided to give him his own sitcom, The Gang, which Woods sung the theme song, and co-wrote the song. The Gang aired from September 9, 1965 until August 8, 1970.

Cesare Cremonini (22 December 1550 in Cento in the then Papal States - 19 July 1631 in Padua) was an Italian philosopher and scientist. Considered one of the greatest philosophers of his time, he is now remembered as an infamous side actor of the Galileo affair, one of the two scholars who refused to look through Galileo's telescope. Galileo used him as the main prototype for the character Simplicio in his Dialogue Concerning the Two Chief World Systems.

[edit] Overview

Cesare Cremonini teached philosophy for 30 years, first at the University of Ferrara, then from 1590 at the University of Padua where he was a colleague and rival of Galileo Galilei. He teached the doctrines of Aristotle, Alexander of Aphrodisias, and especially Averroes. Having teached that reason alone cannot demonstrate the immortality of the soul, some accused him of materialism and atheism.

When Galileo claimed he had discovered mountains of the Moon, Cremonini was one of the scholars who sternly refused to even check through the telescope, because Aristotle had definitely demonstrated it impossible.

His students included English doctor William Harvey (who first correctly described the properties of blood being pumped around the body by the heart), and eminent Greek scholar Ioannis Kottounios (his successor to the chair of philosophy at Padua).

[edit] Holy Innocents' Episcopal School

Holy Innocents' Episcopal School, founded in 1959, is an independent coeducational preparatory school located in Atlanta, Georgia. It is the third largest Episcopal School in the United States, educating approximately 1,400 boys and girls from grades Pre-Kindergarten through 12th. Its highly competitive academic curriculum and sports programs, combined with strong Christian teachings, enables its graduates to attend the finest universities in the United States. In recent years, the school has expanded dramatically, adding a new middle school, a gymnasium, a weight room, sports fields and many other state of the art facilities, with more expansion planned over the next several years. It is one of the most prestigious prep schools in the nation and educates the children of some of Atlanta's most affluent residents. The campus is located on 10 leafy acres in the beautiful northern suburb of Sandy Springs. The school's mascot is the 'Golden Bear' and the school colors are maroon and gold.

Chung's activism began in 1992, shortly after tested positive for HIV. Cecilia was amongst the transgender advocates who sat on the Transgender Discrimination Task Force in 1994 when the Human Rights Commission wrote the report on transgender discrimination.

In 1998, Chung joined a group of HIV positive writers on HIVlife Channel on Gay.com. In 2001, Chung was elected President of San Francisco Lesbian, Gay, Bisexual Pride Celebration Committee.

In 2005, Chung joined the Transgender Law Center as their Deputy Director.

In 2006, Cecilia led a group of volunteers in the celebration of the 40th Anniversary of the Compton's cafeteria riot. The same year, she was elected Grand Marshal for SF Pride and honored as Local Heroes by KQED

Cecilia sits of many non-profit boards which include Asian and Pacific Islander Wellness Center, Stop Prisoners Rape and Harvey Milk LGBT Democratic Club.

[edit] Dental Amalgam Panics

Dental Amalgam Panics have erupted frequently since the early 1800's and are caused be a fear of amalgams, which contain mercury, as a dental filling. Belief in the safety of amalgams started officially in 1859 with the formation of the American Dental Assocation, and today over 90% of practising USA dentists are ADA members. ADA bylaws, designed to prevent further panics, currently state that any dentist who advises the removal of amalgam fillings for health reasons is unethical and can be expelled from the ADA. Only a minority of dentists have ever been publicly opposed to the use of amalgam in dental fillings for these reasons. When the ADA reversed itself in 1983 and said mercury leaks out of fillings, this eventually led to widespread panic culminating in 1990 with the 60 Minutes Television investigative report "Is There Poison In Your Mouth". Millions of Americans and patients worldwide rushed to dental offices demanding to have silver amalgam fillings removed. In order the quell the panic, the ADA introduced the following bylaw:

5.A.1. DENTAL AMALGAM AND OTHER RESTORATIVE MATERIALS. Based on current scientific data, the ADA has determined that the removal of amalgam restorations from the non-allergic patient for the alleged purpose of removing toxic substances from the body, when such treatment is performed solely at the recommendation or suggestion of the dentist, is improper and unethical. The same principle of veracity applies to the dentistâs recommendation concerning the removal of any dental restorative material.

This large panic subsided by 1992 and any dentists who continued to remove amalgam for health reasons were expelled. The belief that amalgam fillings are safe, first publicly announced by the ADA in 1859, pre-dates the discovery of bacteria in 1863. The discovery of bacteria is considered to be the formal start of modern medicine, and hence the ADA's belief in the safety of amalgam pre-dates modern medicine itself. All of the science of the past 150 years has not changed the ADA's belief, regardless of whether the science is pro or anti-amalgam in nature. and many panics have resulted. The ADA is adamant that amalgam is safe, even though in September 2006 an independent panel of experts convened by the Food and Drug Administration voted 13:7 to not endorse amalgam as safe/unsafe. The panel stated there are too many unanswered questions about amalgam, and was also mindful of the panic that had happened in 1990. A small panic has started again because of this finding.

There are many people who claim to see health improvements from removing dental amalgam, and alternative dental assocations like the International Academy of Oral Medicine and Toxicology (IAOMT) have formed out of the belief that amalgam is unsafe. For these patients and dentists, seeing is believing and the results of amalgam removal and health improvement have been documented and presented to government agencies. The ADA calls reports of recoveries "anecdotal" and states they are causing panic.

The current state of Dental Amalgam Panic is that the ADA believes it is safe, the FDA panel of experts voted 13:7 that they cannot say it is either safe or unsafe, and the IAOMT says it is unsafe. The American Medical Assocation has no official position on the safety of amalgam fillings. The Food and Drug Administration has never tested amalgam for safety, but has a webpage which says that amalgam fillings are safe "except for a small minority of patients who are allergic". The amalgam panics will continue, historians have divided the controversy into 3 "Amalgam Wars", covering the 150 years of the widespread use of this material.

Marine Corps Air Station Camp Pendleton (MCAS Camp Pendleton) (ICAO: KNFG, FAA LID: NFG) is a United States Marine Corps airfield located within Marine Corps Base Camp Pendleton, California 33.30Â° N 117.35Â° W. Its ICAO airport code is NFG. It was commissioned in 1942 and is currently home to Marine Aircraft Group 39. The airfield is also known as "Munn Field" in honor of Lieutenant General John C. "Toby" Munn - the first Marine Aviator to serve as the Commanding General of Marine Corps Base Camp Pendleton.

[edit] History

On September 25, 1942, the area presently known as Marine Corps Air Station, Camp Pendleton, California was designated an auxiliary landing field and served as a sub-unit of Marine Corps Air Station El Toro. The airfield consisted of little more than a dirt runway and provided landing facilities for such aircraft as the Beech C-45 and the Sikorsky HRS-1 & 2. During the early 1950âs, the airfield was used for the filming of The Flying Leathernecks starring John Wayne. The first permanent squadron stationed at the airfield was formed in the late 1960âs. This became a composite squadron consisting of OV-10âs and UH-1s.

Through the years, aviation began to play an increasing role in Marine Corps tactics, creating a need for modern facilities. To meet this need, the auxiliary landing field was re-designated a Marine Corps Air Facility (MCAF) on September 1, 1978 serving as home to Marine Aircraft Group 39 (MAG-39). Since 1978, the Group expanded to a strength of four tactical helicopter squadrons, one helicopter training squadron, one observation squadron, and an aviation logistics squadron. This increase in aircraft and personnel established once again the need for improved facilities.

On March 13, 1985, MCAF Pendleton was re-designated as Marine Corps Air Station effective April 1, 1985. Today, the Air Station supports over 180 helicopters assigned to MAG-39, Marine Aircraft Group 46 Detachment A, and a wide variety of other Marine Corps units and visiting aircraft from other branches of the Armed Forces. The closure of MCAS Tustin and MCAS El Toro were a result of Base Realignment and Closure legislation which saw MCAS Camp Pendleton expand its facilities again to support three additional helicopter squadrons. The first medium lift helicopter squadron joined MAG-39 in January 1999, and the final one came in June of that same year.

[edit] Saltwater and Freshwater Sport Fishing in Pakistan

Hidden away in the northernmost tip of the Arabian Sea lies one of the worlds last few undiscovered angling destinations.

In the South, Pakistan's vast coastline is even today; largely unexplored and sport anglers have only scratched the surface of this pristine sea. Saltwater game fishing in Pakistan has developed in its own unique way over the last 20 years in terms of tackle, techniques and boats. A century old Pakistani boatbuilding and seafaring tradition, with the new generation of conservation minded anglers, technology and tackle has combined to make angling in Pakistan a truly unforgettable experience.

The variety of saltwater gamefish is astounding and may favourably be compared to Australia or New Zealand. In fact Pakistan has just beaten Australia's record of Talang Queenfish (16.5kg) to become the world record holding country for this species. The snubnose pompano world record has also been broken in Pakistan. Two world records in as many years! The unique feature of our offshore fisheries are the sheer number of species of potential world record size that can be regularly caught. Cobia and narrowbarred mackeral are two species of note which are on the verge of being broken in Pakistan. Cobia of near world record size are caught each year in Pakistan and the record has unofficially been broken a few times. The same applies for narrow barred mackeral (or tanguige as it is known in Australia), with the Pakistan record falling mere pounds below the current world record. A glance at the list of species available to the saltwater angler makes one relaize why comparisons may be made to the great Australian and New Zealand fisheries. All three marlin species plus swordfish, yellowfin, bigeye, dogtooth, longtail and a host of the smaller tuna species abound. Mako, hammerheads, tiger, bull, lemon, and white sharks also feature prominently. Offshore waters are also full of the mandatory dorado. In addition to this the near shore waters hold cobia, tanguige, groupers, amberjack, snappers, rainbow runners, barracuda, and four species of trevally (including GTS) to complete all the requirements of a world class fishery. All this in the tiny secton of our extensive coastline where sportfishing activities are currently being conducted.

As one travels north from the coastline the terrain changes to extensive mangroves and saltwater estuaries. Barramundi, giant threadfin salmon, red and black drum, mangrove snappers, flathead and a host of other inshore species abound here and reports of massive barramundi and threadfin salmon are commonplace. The saltwater environment then gives way to Pakistanâs fantastic floodplains, and finally into freshwater lakes and rivers. The extend of flora and fauna in this region is now becomes apparent as this region is one of the worlds seven major migration routes for waterfowl. Thus, heavily fertilized, world famous lakes and dams such as Keenjhar lake, Haleji lake, Ranwal dam, Hab dam, Tarbela dam and a host of others provide exciting freshwater game fish. Topping the quarry list are the mighty mahseer, elusive snakeheads, numerous large catfish species and of course many carp species. These provide the course angler and freshwater plug caster with all the action he can possibly ask for.

The floodplains are finally interrupted in the extreme north of Pakistan where the mighty stretches of the Karakoram and Hindukush mountains boast thousands of streams and lakes. Here lies some of the worldâs most exciting wild trout and Mahseer action. Given the remoteness of the best of the hot spots freshwater fishing in Pakistan is an adventure that involves not only world class fishing, it guaranteeâs to âtake your breath awayâ. Itâs a time portal, which grants every angler the opportunity to once again revisit his real reason he started fishing.

However these unique and relatively un-exploited destinations do not exist without their setbacks. Without any active regulatory body, sport fishing in Pakistan is rapidly becoming a meat collection race, with no bag limits, undefined seasons and no concept of catch and release. Fishing continues without a thought for sustenance, no thought for the future.

In order to preserve the essence of sport fishing and ensure development, not degradation, it is pivotal that we, the anglers, develop and practice ethical parameters within which we conduct our sport.

[edit] Fly Fishing in Pakistan

The Indus river and its tributaries are the lifeline of Pakistan. Running from the highest mountain ranges in the world in the Himalayas down to the vast Arabian sea. Along the course of this mighty river lies some of the most exciting freswater angling in the world - both course and game angling.

AS the glacier laden mountains give way to the blistering subcontinent heat, the months from may to september provide one of the best brown trout fisheries in the world. Loch Leven trout, the eggs of which were brought over in ships more than a century ago have thrived in northern Pakistan's rich rivers and deep, crystal clear lakes. Here the story of brown trout is much the same as that of the rainbow trout in New Zealand. Often compared to Chile and Germany in terms of the average size of the brown trout caught, the difference lies in the altitude and scenery one an angler casts his fly in. The Himalayas are the tallest mountains in the world and flyfishing against such a backdrop is truly an experience to remember. While rivers provide lighter tackle fun, the hidden lakes are the true treasure. Wild stock, brilliantly coloured, and in fantastic sizes are waiting to be fought. Reports of 5 to 10lb fish are commonplace and a confirmed 18lb brownie has been caught! Rainbow trout in Pakistan are restricted to farms and have somehow not adapted to our local waters. This is perhaps just as well. Just about all fishing is done with spinners and the trout are completely unaware of flies. This makes flyfishing in Pakistan one of the last true frontiers in the gameanglers "must fish" list of locations.

Saltwater fly fishing is still virgin territory in Pakistan. It is safe to say that gamefish have hardly ever seen a fly and are simply waiting for an adventurous angler to try his hand at this pinnacle of sportsfishing. All the sought after saltwater fly fishing gamefish are present in our waters. Giant trevally, barracuda, mackerel, cobia, tuna, milkfish, bonefish, barramundi, threadfin salmon and a host of others are available in the various inshore and offshore fisheries. [edit] Miscellaneous Organic NESHAP

On August 25, 2003, the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) issued a final rule to reduce hazardous air emissions from miscellaneous organic chemical manufacturing facilities . The final rule requires facilities to install emissions controls known as "maximum achievable control technology" or MACT for process vents, storage tanks, equipment leaks, wastewater systems, and transfer operations at facilities covered by this rule. The rule also includes provisions that allow industry to comply through an alternative, pollution prevention-based standard. The alternative standard requires significant reductions in the amounts of toxic air pollutants used during the manufacturing process. The rule allows facilities to focus on improving processes by reducing solvent use. The final rule will remove 16,800 tons per year of air toxics. The annualized cost of this rule is estimates by USEPA to be approximately $75 million.

BACKGROUND The Clean Air Act requires EPA to identify industrial categories that emit one or more of the 188 toxic air pollutants listed in the Act. For major sources within each source category, the Act requires USEPA to develop standards that restrict emissions to levels consistent with the lowest emitting plants. Major sources are those sources that emit 10 tons per year or more of a single air toxic or 25 tons per year or more of a combination of air toxics. The "Miscellaneous Organic Chemical Manufacturing" MON source category covers 22 categories on EPA's initial list of source categories. It includes source categories generally known as miscellaneous chemical production and polymers and resins. Chlorinated paraffin production, rubber chemical production, polyester resin production and alkyd resin production are examples of processes covered by this rule.

The CHCRS is an all-volunteer rescue service with approximately 100 certified and credentialled members providing emergency and disaster rescue and support services to the public, as well as to governmental and volunteer law enforcement, fire, and medic agencies.

Hamilton County Emergency Services provides the Rescue Service with 24 hour-per day dispatch services from the Chattanooga 911 Center, and the Service respond to calls from three geographically disbursed sites serving the County from East Brainerd, Lupton City, and Lookout Valley.

Over 300,000 citizens in the Chattanooga-Hamilton County area depend on the Rescue Service to answer their calls for help. CACRS also responds in the 11 county area surrounding Hamilton County through mutual aid agreements.

In the 70 year history of the Rescue Service its trained responders have answered thousands upon thousands of calls for assistance, including plane crashes, cave and cliff rescues, body recoveries, search and rescue support, wildland and brush fires, extrication calls and standbys, medical calls and standbys, hazardous materials incidents, and during many other emergency and disaster and public event situations and incidents. Hundreds of victims have been treated and dozens of lives saved by CHCRS volunteers.

Through the use of professionally trained and credentialed volunteers, the Chattanooga-Hamilton County Rescue Service has saved taxpayers tens of millions of dollars over the years in personnel, training, and equipment costs.

The CACRS is an IRS 501(c)(3) nonprofit charitable organization. Contributions to the Chattanooga-Hamilton County Rescue Service, Inc. are tax-deductible as allowable by federal and state law.

Chattanooga Hamilton County Rescue Service has been a nationally respected leader in the recruitment, retention, and training of volunteer personnel, and is a charter member of the Tennessee Association of Rescue Squads.

The Rapid Response Fleet includes 16 heavy, medium, and light rescue response units, including specialized cave and cliff rescue, mass casualty, wildland and brush fire, extrication, generator and light trucks, emergency incident rehabilation units, four-wheel drive, ATV, and crew transport units, specialized mobile air unit to refill fire department breathing air and scuba tanks, foam trailers, Homeland Security Community Emergency Response Team equipment trailer, and a specialized collapse and trench rescue vehicle.

[edit] Nancy Argenta

Nancy Maureen Argenta, (b. Herbison, January 17, 1957) is a soprano singer, best known for performing music from the pre-classical era. She has won international acclaim, and is considered by many as the leading Handel soprano of her time.

She was born in Nelson, British Columbia, Canada. She spent her early years in the settlement of Argenta, from which she later took her professional name (to avoid being mistaken for another Canadian soprano, Nancy Hermiston.)

After graduation from high school in 1975, she was a student of Jacob Hann in Vancouver and of Martin Chambers at the University of Western Ontario, graduating in 1980. [edit] OHLC Bar Chart

An OHLC bar chart is a type of bar-chart typically used to illustrate price movements of an financial instrument over time. Each bar on the chart represents the price movement in one unit of time, e.g. one day or one hour. OHLC stands for Open, High, Low, Close, referring to the opening price (e.g. for a daily bar chart this would be the starting price for that day), the highest price reached during that time, the lowest price reached, and finally the closing price for that time period.

The Japanese candlestick chart is simply another way of visualizing an OHLC bar chart. Both charts show the exact same data, i.e. the opening, high, low, and closing prices during a particular time frame. Some traders find the candlestick chart easier to read.

[edit] Research Process Automation (RPA) and Research Development Environment (RDE) software.

Research Process Automation (RPA) is the automation of tasks in the research and writing lifecycle. This automation is typically accomplished through the use of information technology and can encompass many types of tools including plagiarism detection, search engines, internet encyclopedia, e-Learning software, research databases (such as those provided by EBSCO Industries, LexisNexis and Science Citation Index), word processors, and reference management software.

The term was coined by James D. Beasley in 2003 while he and a colleague, Jeffery D. Reynolds, developed a software tool they called a Research Development Environment (RDE) (Beasley, 2003). RDE was described as an âintegrated, multi-function software that enables or facilitates the creation of research work productsâ across the research process phases âeducation, research, writing, citation, submission, review, and publicationâ (Beasley, 2004). An RDE has similarities to an Integrated Development Environment (IDE) used in software development.

Benefits from RPA and RDE tools may include increased productivity, enhanced focus on higher-level tasks such as concept development and critical analysis (as opposed to repetitive, administrative tasks), and easier communication and sharing of information between research team members or students and their instructors. Another key anticipated benefit is the reduction of internet plagiarism. This was described in Beasleyâs paper entitled âThe Impact of Technology on Plagiarism Prevention and Detection: Research Process Automation - A New Approach for Preventionâ.

A similar approach to combating plagiarism is used by Peter Vamplew and Julian Dermoudy of the University of Tasmania with their Anti-Plagiarism Editor (APE) and Gorilla audit software combination. This is a two-tiered approach which âmonitors the studentâs actions during the development of their submitted work, and one which audits the work after its submissionâ (Vamplew & Dermoudy, 2005).

This âmonitor and auditâ approach is in contrast to software tools that only attempt to detect plagiarism in submitted works. The 'post-submission' approach has several limitations and, at best, can only detect potential instances of plagiarism after they have occurred (Beasley, 2004). Most 'post-submission' approaches utilize content-matching software to detect potential plagiarism. Content-matching software, such as the popular TurnItIn, have several limitations including the inability to access all possible sources (Weinstein & Dobkin, 2002, p. 26) particularly 'deep web' sources in password protected, limited access, databases. Accurate detection of plagiarism is particularly problematic in technical content where similar words are expected to be more prevalent (Huges, Brown, Jakobson, Phipot, Dwight-Moore, Jarret, Grainger and Short, 2002).

The approaches described by Beasley and Vamplew & Dermoudy indicate that a more effective, preventative, path to plagiarism reduction can be realized by applying RPA and using RDE software.

Some of the types of functionality described in this article can be found in the APE/Gorilla software and Thomson ResearchSoftâs EndnoteÂ® product.

[edit] Matthew Lock (Hawkesbury Settler)

Matthew Lock (1763-1836) was born in London, Middlesex, England around 1763. He was an educated craftsman who worked as a plasterer around the London area. In 1787 he was convicted of theft and sent to Australia on the Second Fleet where he went on to become one of the first settlers placed on the banks of the Hawkesbury River.

[edit] Crime & Punishment

In 1787 Matthew Lock was changing his lodgings to be more conveniently located nearer his work when he was accused of stealing from his landlord. Lock was convicted on the charge of theft at the Old Bailey on 12 September 1787 and sentenced to 7 years transportation to the colony of New South Wales. He spent the next 7 months in the overcrowed Newgate Gaol before being moved to the hulk Stanislaus for another 18 months. On 16 November 1789 Lock was again moved, this time to the transportation ship Surprize, to await transportation as part of the Second Fleet.

[edit] Hawkesbury Settlement

The December of 1792 saw the departure of the Govenor, Captain Arthur Phillip, back to England. His replacement, Captain John Hunter, would not arrive until 1795 during which time the colony was run by Lieutenant-Governor, Major Francis Grose. Within a year of taking up the post Grose had placed settlers on the banks of the Hawkesbury River, among which was Matthew Lock. With the settlers firmly established on their farms Grose made out grants of 30 acres in the district of Mulgrave Place. The first twenty of these grants were registered on 3 November 1794, and two, eight days later on 11 November. Lock received a grant of 30 acres at Mulgrave Place on 19 November 1794, which lay isolated on the left bank below Green Hills. Close by lay the farm of John Cobcroft and a little downstream that of Thomas Reibey. On 22 July 1795 a grant of 30 acres was given to John Francis Molloy, a bush doctor who trained under Thomas Arndell, for the land lying between the farms of Matthew Lock and Thomas Reibey.

[edit] Family Life

Two years after Lock arrived in the colony came the arrival of the convict transportation ship, Pitt, carrying 410 convicts of which 58 were women. Among the 58 women on board was 19 year old Scotland native Eupham Graham. Not long in the colony, Eupham settled with Matthew Lock and although they never married they lived as man and wife. on 2 June 1794, while living on the banks of the Hawkesbury River, Eupham gave birth to twins Thomas Graham and Elizabeth Graham. The following year saw the birth of a daughter, Mary Graham, on 16 September 1795. The children always went by their mother's surname due to the fact their parents weren't married. Two years later, on 27 April 1797, Eupham Graham died at the age of 25 leaving twins Thomas and Elizabeth, aged 2 years 10 months, and daughter Mary, aged 1 year 7 months, without a mother. The problem of raising the children was short lived when on 17 July 1797, less than 3 months after the death of Eupham, Matthew Lock married Alice Burrows at St John's Church in Parramatta. No children were born from this marrige.

[edit] The 1800's

By 1802 Matthew Lock was the privileged owner of a horse and had received a further land grant of 50 acres at Mulgrave Place on Robinson's Lagoon. In 1803 he aided his good friend Constable Andrew Thompson in the round up of 15 Irish escapees from Castle Hill. Sadly that year also saw the death of his daughter, Elizabeth Graham, at the age of 9. Another land grant of 30 acres at Mulgrave Place was given to Lock in 1804.
