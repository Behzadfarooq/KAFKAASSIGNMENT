[edit] Latin pronounciation of suffix -ang

In the original spoken Latin palette, the suffix -ang traditionally ends in a soft "g" sound much like as in "sigh" and "laugh." Today's pronunciations tend to attempt to vary the sound of this suffix by over pronouncing the "g" at the end. In words such as "sing" the sound is cut short on the "g" furthermore, the -an before the "G" takes much of the pronouncement on itself. Therefore, the word "sing" ,while likely to be pronounced with a hard consonant "g" at its end, instead uses, in the traditional way of Latin and anglo-saxon speech, a softer, less pronounced "g" sound to cut the palate's resonance short, hence words like "rain" and "rang" sound almost alike except for a more annunciated "a" in the ending "-ang" This takes the longest sound in the word and cuts the "g" at the end short. As in "gang." Rather, most modern language speakers would pronounce the "g" hard at the end. This change is taken from the anglo-saxon tradition of ending a word with a longer, almost nasal pronunciation indicating that the speaker has ended his word and is going to a new one. Modern English has tried to differentiate between words like "bang" and "bane" by elongating the "g" and making it harder than traditional. In reality the ending "-ang" could be considered an entire accent due to the long sounding "a" and the following "n" which takes most of the hard "g" sound away.

Manuel B. Gonnet is a region of La Plata department in the province of Buenos Aires, Argentina.

It is located approximately 6 km northwest of downtown La Plata, locality head of the party homonymous.

Form leaves from the set of localities that were developed in the line of the then South Railroad (soon General Railroad Roca) that ties to La Plata with Buenos Aires, like Tolosa, Ringuelet, City Bell and Villa Elisa.

[edit] History

Manuel B. Gonnet takes its name from Manuel Benito Gonnet, who was the prime minister of Public Works of the province (after the federalization of the city of Buenos Aires) and outstanding provincial legislating.

On July 13, 1882 the provincial government began the construction of new branches of the railroad that communicated to the new provincial capital with the West Railroad and the South Railroad. The La Plata line- Empalme Pereyra (soon Villa Elisa) was inaugurated the first of January of 1889.

In 1889, soon to be inaugurated the railway stations Adolfo Alsina in that layout, the proprietors of the small farms of the zone decided to conform a society, to divide the lots and to create a town. The station is released on February 15th and 24th of that month the inauguration of Villa announces Maximum Peace, names of the then governor.

Nevertheless, the undertaking was not awaited and many years were required to obtain a stable population nucleus. Initially, the constructions were in their majority aim houses week of inhabitants of the near city of La Plata.

When the population began to settle down, in 1909, Luis Castells, proprietor of the earth that were “across of the routes” (to the north of the station), decide to divide his property and to create a new nucleus, denominated at the present time Villa Castells.

In 1931 the name change of the station by Manuel B. Gonnet took place and it gave the new town his definitive name, including so much to Villa Maximum Peace as to Villa Castells, to whom new districts were added as was growing the population and extending territorially.

Introduction

Poloc is a family-centred club with its own ground in the picturesque Pollok Country Park estate on Glasgow's south-side.

Members come from all backgrounds and from across the ethnic and cultural spectrums with all ages taking an active part in club life. As well as competitive and social cricket for juniors and adults, there are all-weather tennis facilities, a unique golf section, spectator seating, dedicated junior pavilion and well-appointed clubhouse with spacious bar area, pool table, darts and catering facilities.

Competitively, the club's cricket sides play under the names "NVT Poloc" and "NVT Shawholm", NVT being the club's main sponsor - more about it and the club's other funders and supporters can be found on the club's website (www.poloc.com). NVT Poloc play in the Scottish National Cricket League Premier Division whilst NVT Shawholm play in the Western District Cricket Union First Division.


The beginnings

Founded in 1878, Poloc Cricket Club spent its first season on the site of the old Pollokshaws Race Course and in 1880 moved to our unrivalled location at Shawholm where play has continued for the last 129 years! The editor of a local newspaper wrote in 1887; "their ground is a beautiful one hardly to be matched in Scotland for its surroundings".

A pavilion was acquired by the club from the now-defunct Pollokshields Athletic Club, also located on Pollok Estates, and the building was moved to Shawholm on rollers! In the same year, the main avenue from Pollokshaws Road to Pollok House was opened, running past the north side of the ground in what is now Pollok Park. This avenue still provides a beautiful tree-lined approach to Shawholm to this day with the River Cart to the left.

Our club's golf section was formed in 1889 and with bowls, putting and archery, Poloc became a sporting club possibly unique amongst cricket clubs in Scotland.


Growth and development

Full tenancy of the six-acre site was granted to the club in 1888 by Sir John Stirling Maxwell who continued to be involved in Poloc's affairs until his death in 1956. He is remembered by older members as an outstanding and generous laird and landlord.

By 1890 Poloc sides had become strong enough to match their cricketing adversaries, in particular the club's nearest and fiercest local rivals, Clydesdale. The Titwood side play only a mile or so away from Shawholm and our rivalry continues to this day as both sides battle for supremacy in the Scottish National Cricket League. Home or away, "derby" matches remain highlights of any season's fixture card.

Interestingly, in 1908 the Pollokshaws Working Lads Club decided to form a football team from amongst their ranks. When their ambitious plans reached the ears of Sir John Stirling Maxwell he allotted that club an area of land on the estate, rent-free. The footballers, in turn, to show their appreciation, took the name of Pollok Juniors FC, and Sir John became their first Honorary President. The ground allocated became known as Haggs Park and was adjacent to Poloc Cricket Club; in fact, the different spellings of the two names "Pollok" and "Poloc" were adopted at this time, deliberately, to avoid confusion.

Membership at Poloc reached a peak of 600 in the late 1940's, including a healthy waiting list. And, in 1955, Shawholm played host to the first televised cricket match in Scotland when Poloc entertained the club's other principal Glasgow rivals, West of Scotland, who play on the city's north side at Hamilton Crescent.

Membership has come down a bit since the heady days of the 1940's and 1950's but numbers remain healthy and new members are always welcome! Members need not be serious sportsmen or women with many gladly falling into the category of "social" members. With its idyllic and extremely safe setting as well as facilities and well appointed clubhouse, membership of Poloc remains a hugely enjoyable proposition.


Historic moments

Shawholm played host to Poloc versus the MCC in June 1965. The ground was fully decked out with marquee and commissionaires.

The clubhouse and its gardens had never looked better! The game was a three-day match played between the 23rd and 25th.

Scotland's first televised cricket match took place at Shawholm on 7 May 1955 when West of Scotland were Poloc's visitors. Poloc batted first making 110 for 9 in brilliant sunshine. In reply, west made 120 for 6. Unfortunately Poloc's own historical record of the day notes that "the viewing was tedious"!

Notwithstanding the cricket on view however the day was enjoyable with, again, a marquee being erected in front of the tennis courts for teas.

Bringing things right up to date, the ground was invited to host three internationals as part of the 2005 ECC European Championships. The matches (The Netherlands versus Italy, Jersey versus Germany and The Netherlands versus Denmark) saw white ball/black sightscreen cricket at Shawholm for the first time and yet again demonstrated club members' willingness and ability to host such matches.


Junior cricket

Poloc runs junior cricket teams at five age-group levels: under 11, 13, 13b, 15 and 18 and compete in West Junior Cricket Union- as well as Cricket Scotland-organised competitions. Under 10 players also compete in "Kwik Cricket" competitions from time to time.


The Community Coaching Initiative

Poloc Cricket Club has been providing cricket coaching in its local community under the auspices of the "Community Coaching Initiative" since 2002. Working with Glasgow Council a pilot scheme was run in two local schools and, since then, the Initiative has grown each year so that, in 2006, eight local schools took part. The core part of the coaching carried out in schools is weekly sessions carried out by the club's coaches at the schools.

The culmination of each year's primary schools coaching is a festival day at Shawholm that sees the schools competing against each other. In addition, the club, under the umbrella of the Initiative and in partnership with the WJCU, regularly hosts "Kwik Cricket" festivals.

In addition to the work in local schools, the club's Community Coaching Initiative also works with other community groups in providing sports coaching in and around the club's locality. Most often this work is part of a larger initiative being carried out in the community.

All the work of the Initiative is carried out by appropriately qualified coaching staff with the assistance of appropriately supervised coaching assistants. All coaches have been appropriately "Disclosure Scotland" checked and the Initiative's work is carried on as an integral part of the club's youth activities, recognised by Cricket Scotland's "TOPClub" accreditation

The Initiative was granted charitable status in early 2007 and is registered with the OSCR under number SC037774. The registered body itself is CCI Glasgow Limited, a private company limited by guarantee and registered in Scotland under number SC315322, whose registered address is Shawholm, Pollok Park, 2060 Pollokshaws Road, Glasgow G43 1AT.


Golf

Uniquely, Poloc Cricket Club also plays host to the UK’s only R&A-recognised six-hole golf course.

Founded in 1889, the Golf Section's centenary was marked by a letter of congratulations from the custodians of the game's rules based at the home of golf.

With the cricket bats, helmets and pads all safely locked away in attics and back bedrooms for at least a few months, attention for many turns to golf at Shawholm in the early autumn. Taking part in golf over the autumn and winter months - a round at "wee poloc" - is a unique challenge, with monthly medals with club competitions every fortnight throughout the season.

Some English County grounds have trees within their boundary ropes; at Shawholm the greens and tee boxes make unique features on the cricket ground in the summer months!


Tennis

Poloc Cricket Club also boasts six tennis courts: three all-weather tennis courts and three clay courts, both adjacent to the clubhouse building.

[edit] Simpson Compound Gear Set

The simpson planetary gearset uses a compound planetary gearset with common sun gears. Note just because the sun gears are tied together does not mean they have to have the same number of teeth. The front carrier is physically tied to the rear ring gear as is the output. In neutral the simpson planetary gear set does not have a locked member i.e. there isn’t a reactionary member. Reverse is accomplished by clutching the sun assembly to the input and locking the rear carrier assembly to the case, i.e. making it the reactionary member. The gear ratio is – Nrr/Nsr where Nrr is the rear ring gear tooth number and Nsr is the rear sun gear tooth number. First gear is accomplished by making the forward ring gear the input member by clutching it to the input shaft. The common sun gears rotate backward. This causes the rear planetary carrier assembly to attempt to rotate backwards. However an overrunning clutch locks the carrier assembly making it a reactionary member. The gear ratio is rather complex: define X = (Nrr/Nsr) and Y = ( Nsf / Nsf) then the gear ratio is X(Y) + second gear ratio where Nsr and Nsf are the number of teeth on the forward and rear sun gears and Nrf and Nrr are the number of teeth on the forward and rear sun gears respectively.

Second gear is accomplished by locking the common sun gears to the case while the forward ring gear is still clutched to the input shaft. The gear ratio is (Nrf + Nsf)/ Nrf where Nrf is the number of teeth on the forward ring gear and Nsf is the number of teeth on the forward sun gear. The gear ratio is exactly the same as that of a simple planetary gear set. Notice that the rear carrier is spinning at over speed in the same direction. Third gear locks both the common sun gears and forward ring gear to the input shaft with clutches. When two members of a planetary gear set are locked together the output is locked in a 1 to 1 ratio.

Example with Nrr = 31 T, Nrf = 29T, Nsf = Nsr = 13T

Then rear carrier pinion gear (Ncr) has 9T, and forward carrier pinion gear (Ncf) has 8T. Ncr = (Nrr – Nsr) / 2 and Ncf = (Nrf – Nsf)/ 2

Note : Nrr – Nsr and Nrf – Nsf must be even

Reverse gear ratio = -Nrr/Nsr = -31/13 = -2.38

First gear ratio = X(Y) + second gear ratio = 31/29 + 1.419 = 2.49

Second gear ratio = (Nrf + Nsf) / Nrf = (13 + 31)/ 31 = 1.419

Third gear is 1 i.e. direct


These gear ratios are derived from the following Planetary gear equation:

Ns(Ws) + Nr(Wr) = (Ns + Nr) Wc

Where Ns = number of teeth on the sun gear, Nr = number of the teeth on ring gear, Ws = speed of sun gear, Wr = speed of ring gear, and Wc = speed of carrier assembly.
