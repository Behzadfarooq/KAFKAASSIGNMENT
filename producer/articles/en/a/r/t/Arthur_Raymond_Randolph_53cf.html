Arthur Raymond Randolph (born 1943) is a judge on the United States Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit.

He earned a B.S. from Drexel University in 1966 and a J.D. from the University of Pennsylvania Law School in 1969, where he also served on the law review. He then clerked for 2nd Circuit Judge Henry Friendly, which began a career in law in Washington, D.C., moving between private practice, government, and academia.

He started as the Assistant to the United States Solicitor General for three years, went into to private practice briefly, and returned as the Deputy U.S. Solicitor General from 1975 to 1977. He also taught at Georgetown University Law Center from 1974 to 1978. He then stayed in private practice until he moved to the bench in 1990 when President George H.W. Bush nominated him to replace the seat vacated by Spottswood William Robinson III.

[edit] Cases

In February of 2007 ruled: "Guantanamo Bay inmates cannot challenge their detention in US courts".

This highly controversial case concerns foreign nationals held without charge by the US at Guantanamo Bay, Cuba. It also concerns Congress's authority to make laws governing review of cases by Federal Courts, and a provision of the US Constitution known as the Suspension Clause, which declares “The Privilege of the Writ of Habeas Corpus shall not be suspended, unless when in Cases of Rebellion or Invasion the public Safety may require it.” (U.S. CONST. art. I, § 9, cl. 2).
Following the US Supreme Court decisions in Rasul v. Bush, 542 U.S. 466 (2004) and Hamdan v. Rumsfeld, 126 S. Ct. 2749 (2006) detainees filed numerous petitions for writs of habeas corpus challenging their detention as unlawful and alleging violations of the US Constitution, treaties, statutes, regulations, the common law, and the law of nations. In response Congress passed the "Military Commissions Act of 2006" (MCA), signed by President G. W. Bush in October 2006, which sought to overrule Hamdan and strip US Courts of jurisdiction over ALL detainee cases, including habeas cases.
Meanwhile, detainee cases found their way to the Circuit Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia, where Circuit Judge A. RAYMOND RANDOLPH, writing the majority opinion, concluded that the MCA had effectively revoked all statutory authority for hearing detainee cases. The majority further expressly rejected every premise that habeas was otherwise applicable to the detainees. He concluded: "Federal courts have no jurisdiction in these cases.", and ordered the dismissal of all detainee cases. [Opinion: http://pacer.cadc.uscourts.gov/docs/common/opinions/200702/05-5062b.pdf ]
In dissent, Circuit Judge JUDITH W. ROGERS disagreed that habeas was unavailable to the detainees, explaining that the suspension clause was more appropriately viewed as a general limitation on the power of government, rather than as an individual right. She agreed that MCA purported to withdraw a right (of habeas) but that it "does so in a manner that offends the constitutional constraint on suspension."
The majority opinion appears to leave no means for a foreign national held abroad by the US to complain concerning, for example, torture by the US, and also calls into doubt whether US citizens held abroad by the US government have any right of habeas. Alternatively, the dissenting view appears to offer that, subject to exigency or reasonable laws of administration, all persons held by the US would have some manner of the right.

Appeal to the US Supreme Court appears likely. [1]
