Six Strong Guys (original title: Luk jong si) is a comedic drama made in 2004 by director Chun-Chun Wong and starring Ekin Cheng and Lawrence Cheng.

Produced and filmed on location in Hong Kong, the movie focuses on a group of men have committed to a suicide pact but constantly fail to jump from the agreed rooftop. Though the premise may seem dark, the movie makes a clear point of using light moods and exploring each character's motivations for such an extreme act. The film's overall tone is life-affirming and positive, as the characters indvidually determine that a full and rich life, even when difficult, is worth living.

On March 2, 2007, the residents of State College, Pennsylvania celebrated St Patrick's Day on the wrong day. During the Spring 2007 semester at Pennsylvania State University, the school decided to schedule spring break to include St. Patrick's Day. The movement was successful despite hurdles from the university and the township and March 2nd 2007 became State Patty's Day.

A Convicted Killer who denied any involvement in the murder of a woman Pedro Cruz Muniz, convicted by a Williamson County jury of killing a Southwestern University student in 1976, is scheduled to be executed at 6 p.m. Tuesday in Huntsville.


Muniz, 41, will the be the 1st person from Williamson County to be executed since the 1940s, authorities say.


Muniz was sentenced to death in 1986 for the rape and bludgeoning of Janis Bickham, a 19-year-old freshman from Van Vleck in Matagorda County. Muniz, a Georgetown resident, told police that he grabbed Bickham from behind as she walked across a bridge that spans the North Fork of the San Gabriel River on the night of Dec. 20, 1976.


A survey crew found Bickham's wallet, poncho shoes and Bible near the bridge the next morning. The discovery triggered a search that ended Dec. 22 when Bickham's nude body was discovered underneath a pile of limbs and leaves.


"It was a cold, calculated crime -- the kind that is behind the reason we have the death penalty," said District Attorney Ken Anderson, who prosecuted Muniz in 1986. "This wasn't a matter of a fit of rage that lasted 30 seconds. It was an attack on a totally innocent stranger that lasted at least 30 minutes. And he had many opportunities to back out at any time."


In his written confession, Muniz said he forced Bickham down to the river bank and into an abandoned tin building where he raped her and beat her until she was unconscious. He dragged her from the shed about 150 yards down the river bank, through thick brush, and pushed her against a fence.


Bickham died from a fractured skull, caused by 5 blows to her head by someone wielding a large rock, an autopsy showed.


Shoe prints found near Bickham's body matched the pattern on the bottom of Muniz's tennis shoes, and material scraped from her fingernails appeared to match the blue jeans Muniz was wearing that night. 1 witness said he saw Muniz following Bickham on the bridge, and another saw him walking out of the woods near the Blue Hole swimming area where her clothes were found.


Lt. Robert Hernandez is the only officer still working for the Georgetown Police Department who was involved in the murder investigation. Muniz also had been charged with raping a Georgetown woman and was out on bond when he killed Bickham, Hernandez said.


The rape was similar to the attack on Bickham in that Muniz grabbed a woman off the street and pulled her into some tall weeds, Hernandez said.


"He beat up that girl real bad," Hernandez said. "She said she played dead and that's when he stopped beating her."


Hernandez said he feels sorry for the family members Muniz leave behind. "But for what he did, I think he made his choice."


Muniz's family did not return phone calls seeking comment.


Former Georgetown officer Mike Loney said he is going to Huntsville to see Muniz's execution.


"It's closure," Loney said. "I've seen what happened to (the Bickham) family and to other officers involved with this case. This will close it."


Loney said Bickham's younger brother, Paul, who lives in Van Vleck, and her aunt and uncle are expected to be at the execution. Her father died a few years ago, and her mother is in a nursing home in poor health, Loney said.


Muniz was first convicted of Bickham's killing in July 1977, but the U.S. Supreme Court struck down that verdict because Muniz was not warned that results of a psychiatric exam could be used against him. Anderson said the evidence, and brutality, led to his decision to pursue the death penalty.


"You can't hear this evidence and not realize Pedro Muniz should not be put to death," Anderson said. "The facts were so horrible."


(source: Austin American-Statesman)





WASHINGTON, D.C.:


A federal law intended to speed the pace of executions does not restrict some claims by death-row inmates that they are too insane to be put to death, the Supreme Court said today.


The court's 7-2 ruling in an Arizona case said inmates whose previous claims of insanity were dismissed as premature can raise the issue again when their execution is imminent.


The Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act generally limits state inmates to one federal appeal. But the court said Ramon Martinez- Villareal's insanity claim should not be considered a 2nd appeal and therefore, is not barred by the federal law.


"To hold otherwise would mean that a dismissal of a 1st...petition for technical procedural reasons would bar the prisoner from ever obtaining federal...review," Chief Justice William H. Rehnquist wrote for the court.


Today's ruling upheld a decision by the 9th U.S. Circuit Court of Appeals that halted Martinez-Villareal's execution hours before it was to occur in May 1997 and gave him a chance to pursue his insanity claim.


The Supreme Court ruled in 1986 that the execution of an insane person would violate the Constitution's ban on cruel and unusual punishment.


Martinez-Villareal, a Mexican citizen, was convicted in Arizona of killing James Thomas McGrew and Fernando Estrada-Babichi in 1982. Prosecutors said he shot the 2 to prove his manhood.


Other federal appeals by Martinez-Villareal had been rejected previously. His 1st federal appeal had tried to raise the insanity issue but it was dismissed as premature because no execution date had been set.


When the claim was renewed, a federal judge ruled that under the new death penalty law, Martinez-Villareal must get approval from a federal appeals court to file a second appeal on the insanity issue.


But the 9th Circuit court ruled that the federal law did not bar Martinez-Villareal from raising the insanity issue again, and the Supreme Court agreed.


The inmate's 1st claim was dismissed as premature "because his execution was not imminent and therefore his competency to be executed could not be determined at that time," Rehnquist wrote.


Because Martinez-Villareal did not get a ruling the 1st time he raised the insanity issue, his new claim cannot be considered a 2nd petition under federal law, Rehnquist said.


His opinion was joined by Justices John Paul Stevens, Sandra Day O'Connor, Anthony M. Kennedy, David H. Souter, Ruth Bader Ginsburg and Stephen G. Breyer.


Justices Antonin Scalia and Clarence Thomas dissented, contending the law bars inmates from filing claims that previously had been presented to a federal court.


The case is Stewart vs. Martinez-Villareal, 97-300.

[edit] Quarter Mile bridge

The bridge was built in 1928 and crosses the Maribyrnong River from Keilor East on the North side to Sunshine on the South side of the river. It is 54.6 metres above the river and 400 metres long, hence the Quarter Mile name. The girders were sunk up to 20 metres below ground level to obtain firm foundations for the concrete pedestals, onto which the big steel towers were erected. The bridge is noteworthy for its unusual K frame bracing. In its construction, 1,600 tons of steel and 6,000 cubic metres of concrete were used. Since standardisation of the Victoria/N.S.W. line, all Sydney trains cross the bridge. It is located near the E.J. Whitten Bridge on the Western Ring Road.

[edit] St Mary's Church, Billingshurst

St Mary's Church, Billingshurst is an Anglican church in Billingshurst, West Sussex, England. It is part of the Diocese of Chichester.

The church building has been a focal point for Christian worship in the village for more than 800 years. The earliest parts of the church date back to the 12th century but it is likely that there was earlier occupation of the site. Over the centuries, there has been extensive development. The main feature of the nave is the fine 15th/16th century wagon roof with over 30 different designs of carved oak bosses, including the arms of the Percy, Beaufort and Neville families. The two tie beams may date from the 13th century. The spire, 120 feet high, was added to the tower in the 15th century. The eight sides are covered with wooden shingles.

Janice630 23:44, 4 March 2007 (UTC) Evan Weiner (born April 2, 1956 in New York, NY) is an American journalist who covers the business and politics of sports. He is the author of "The Business and Politics of Sports."

Weiner is a columnist for the New York Sun, "The Business of Sport." He also is a contributor to msnbc.com and numerous newspapers and magazines around the United States. Between June 14, 1999 and June 23, 2006, he did a daily commentary for radio's Westwood One's Metro Network called "the Business of Sports." He is also a regular television and international radio guest discussing the connection between the United States Federal Government, state and local governments and sports owners. In 2005, Weiner was a recurring guest on Sam Donaldson's ABC News Now Internet program, "Politics Live." He is recognized as one of America's foremost authorities on sports business and is a regular on the college lecture circuit.


The book, "The Business & Politics of Sports" contains over 100 of Weiner's columns written between 1998 and 2005 that describe the power plays, media and political (federal, state and local) connections which rule the 19 billion dollar a year business of sports. At the heart of Evan Weiner's writing lies his belief that "it's great to watch a game and report on it, but democracy deserves more than a box score when it comes to scrutinizing the business of sports." "The public," Weiner suggests, "needs to be aware of the relationship between politics, government, the media and sports ownership. Americans generally view sports as fans and do not realize how many hundreds of millions of their tax dollars go to big sports." He calls upon editors and TV progrmmers to look at sports as more than an entertainment forum and opines that journalists also need to examine the sports industry because, "in the end, just about every American citizen has some money directly or indirectly invested in the sports industry." In The Business & Politics of Sports Mr. Weiner's columns are divided into ten thematic chapters entitled Business, Colleges, Endorsement/Sponsorship, Labor, Events, Politics, Media, Stadiums, Olympics, and Fans and the Public. Laid out in an easy to read format, they progress in chronological order from 1998 to the present, showing the fascinating evolution of sports business. (1)


Weiner contends that the mindset of newspaper and magazine editors, along with radio and TV programmers, is that sports are merely games, the toy store of life.
