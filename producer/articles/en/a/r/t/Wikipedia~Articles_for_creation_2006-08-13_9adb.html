[edit] Mount Pisgah (Oregon)

Mount Pisgah is a hill in Lane County, Oregon, rising 1060 feet above the surrounding Willamette Valley to a maximum elevation of 1531 feet. It is situated between the Coast Fork and Middle Fork of the Willamette River, two miles SE of their confluence. Springfield is immediately north of the Mount Pisgah, and the city of Eugene is a few miles west. It hosts the 2363 acre (956 hectare) Howard Buford Recreation Area as well as the non-profit Mount Pisgah Arboretum at its base.

[edit] Recreation

Several hiking trails are maintained by the Lane County Parks Department on Mount Pisgah. The summit is accessible by a steep 1.4 mile trail from a parking area near the base of the hill. Several other trails make their way through the adjacent arboretum and up the slopes.

The summit offers a panoramic view of the Southern Willamette Valley and includes a bronze relief sculpture illustrating the surrounding topography and identifying many nearby geographic features.

[edit] Arboretum

The Mount Pisgah Arboretum covers 209 acres at the base of Mount Pisgah and offers visitors 7 miles of trails through natural riverside habitat and hundreds of catalogued plant species. Admission is free.

[edit] Parking

The Lane County Parks Department manages a gravel parking area at the base of Mt. Pisgah and adjacent to the arboretum. The Parks Departments charges $2.00 for all day parking served from a self pay station. In 2006 fines for parking violations are $94, down from $141 in years past.

[edit] Geology

The hill consists of basalt or its intrusive equivalent diabase. Small crystals of calcite and various zeolite minerals are often seen where the rock outcrops, especially near the summit.

[edit] Controversy

Wildish Land Company owns 1400 acres of undisturbed land adjacent to the park and has filed an Oregon Measure 37 claim seeking 15 million dollars in compensation in lieue of developing a large subdivision. The land was previously zoned for gravel production with an estimated value around five million dollars. Opponents of the subdivision assert that it would dramatically alter the character of the park. As an alternative to development or compensation, some have suggested the controversy represents a historic opportunity to purchase the land, integrate it with the existing park, and eventually link the expanded Howard Buford Recreation Area with other undeveloped land as part of a greater Eugene greenway.

[edit] Sligo Way

The Sligo Way is a 75km long distance trail that goes through the county Sligo. Starting at Lough Talt (where the Western Way ends and the Foxford Way starts as well) it goes through the Ox Mountains, besides Lough Easky to 1km south of Templeboy (where B&B accomodation is availlable). From Templeboy it leads further through Coolaney, Collooney (train station and B&B availlable). Then leading besides the shore of Lough Gill it ends in the village of Dromahair (county Leitrim, B&B and hostel accomodation availlable). The Way is very scenic, overlooking Sligo Bay and various lakes. The part between Collooney and Dromahair is especially scenic for its views over the valley where Collooney and Ballintogher lay. The signposting is very good.

[edit] Diamond -Water Paradox - referenced in Pricing article in Wik..

In "Wealth of Nations," Adam Smith uses the paradox in dicussion of "value." In explaining "USE," he suggests water as a commodity which has tremedous value - it can be used in washing, cleaning, cooking, and as a drink. However, water is plentiful and the cost negligible and therefore has no "value in EXCHANGE." A diamond, however, has almost no "value in USE" - it has (had) no specific function in manufacuring or food preparation - but a diamond has great "Value in EXCHANGE." It can be used to purchase other items. The more contemporary excersize in the topic might be the gift one might bring to a host of a dinner party. One could bring a $20 bottle of wine or one might bring a $20 bill. The BILL has greater value in EXCHANGE than the bottle of wine - the host might use it to purchase a bottle of wine, or food or other goods as the host sees fit. The botle of wine has greater value in USE - it can be consumed at once while the bill is, in itself, useless.

[edit] History

Union Council Koral Was established with the establishment of District Islamabad in 1979. before the establishment of union council the village Koral was the part of union council Chaklala distt Rawalpindi and its other villages were part of union council Kirpa. in 1986 some more villages were included in union council Koral.

[edit] Geography

union council Koral is about 15 Km from Islamabad city, on left side of Islamabad highway and about 3 Km from Islamabad International Air Port. Uc Sihala is on its eastern side, UC Kirpa and tarlai on its western side, Tehsil Kahuta on northern side, and distt Rawalpindi on southern side.

[edit] Culture

[edit] Races and Tribes

The important races and tribes living in the area are Rajput, amongst whom the important sub-divisions are Bhatti, Rawal, Janjua, and Chohan etc.Other main castes are Awan, Mughal, Qureshi, Syed, Dhunds and Sattis who inhabit the Murree and Kotli Sattian Hills and the Ghakkars who are descendants of a fine race of warriors. Some of the other tribes of the district are the Jat, Malyar and Pathan. The people in general are not dark and are of a lighter complexion than in the rest of Punjab. People of area are well built and of medium height, sturdy and rugged looking and are among best soldiers in the Pakistan Army. There are few scattered Kashmiris, who are distinguished by their fair complexion and good looks. The people in the Murree hills resemble the people of Hazara and Azad Kashmir and are more rugged than the people of the plains.

Due to unfavorable agricultural conditions, villagers have little time for the pursuit of cultural activities or recreation. Whatever cultural activity and forms of entertainment exist is related to fairs and occupations.

The birth of a child is welcomed and Azan is recited in the ears of the newborn. The Aqiqa ceremony is held when the head of the child is shaved and goat or sheep is slaughtered. The common games in villages are Chhun Chhuan, Chaunji Trap. Changhan and tip-cat (Guli Danda). Young people prefer Kabaddi, wrestling, tent pegging and Bini. The elder folk usually pay a visit to the village Chawpal while they sit around the Huqqa and gossip about the day‚Äôs events. Music and dancing are also common. Bhangra, Summi, Luddi Jhummal and Giddi in the fairs and festivals are offered.

[edit] Dress

Males usually wear Shalwar, Kameez (Shirt) and Chaddar (Cloth sheet). The Chaddar is a multipurpose dress. The colour of the men's dress is generally white, khaki or grey, in the hilly region a long coat is worn in winter, while in the plains they generally carry a blanket.

Females wear coloured Shalwar and long shirt and a dopatta/chadar (head cover). Women wear the usual ornaments like earrings, bangles, necklaces, on festive occasion or marriages.

[edit] Food and Health

Wheat and maize are the staple food grains white Bajara is also used to a lesser extent. Meat and vegetables are also consumed by well to do families. Tea is popular all over the district. Bread is generally backed in a Tandoor.

Due to good weather conditions in the area the health of population is generally better

[edit] Betrothals and Marriages

Marriage is generally started by a betrothals ceremony in which women folk along with some made members of the bridgegroom's family go to the house of the girl's parents with suitable gifts in the form of clothes, sweet and ornaments etc. and the bride's parents host the party. Marriage ceremony is observed in different fashions all over the UC Relatives and friends and members of the biradari (tribe) are invited in marriages. Relatives and family friends offer gifts to the parents of the bridegroom.

[edit] Superstitions/Beliefs

Most of the people in the UC and in all district are Muslims. Influence of the Pir is quite strong. Shrines of the Pir of Golara Shrif, Dewal Shrif, Bari Imam, and Mohra Shrif are visited by a large number of devotees at the annual Urs of these saints. The people generally have a conservative outlook on life and are particular in the observance of religious ceremonies. There are many shrines of respected saints in different parts of the Pothohar where people come to pay homage from all over the country. The most famous of these shrines are those of Pir Mehr Ali Shah Golra Sharif, Bari Imam, Pir Mohammad Qasim in Mohra Shrif and Mian Fazal Rabbani of Kalyam Awan and Malik Siraj of Potha

[edit] Agriculture

There are generally two crops in a year, Rabi and Kharif. Rabi crop is followed by Kharif crop. This system is locally known as Do-Sala Wheat, Bajra Aand Maize are the common Rabi and Kharif crops. Wheat is grown in all the12 union councils subject to weather conditions.

Agriculture depends entirely on rainfall and at some places on the mountain streams. People living there generally have extremely small land holdings. Maize is the staple kharif crop; potato cultivation has also made considerable progress. In areas where water from the hills is available vegetable are also grown.The main Rabi crops are wheat and barley, while maize is grown as Kharif crops.

Due to unfavourable conditions for agriculture, people of this area do not take interest in crop husbandry. The cultivators generally content themselves with sowing the seeds after shallow ploughing and bare rest to nature. The use of latest agriculture implements and fertilizers is not common except for cash crops like potato and orchards. Fruits grown in this region include groundnuts, apples, citrus, and apricots.

[edit] Irrigation

There is no proper source of irrigation in area. Most of the land is irrigated with rainy water. No canal system is available. The rain is the main natural source of irrigation. The physical nature of the area does not permit irrigation by canals. Out of 14685 acres only 5,000 acres is irrigated by wells.

[edit] Brian Ulrich (Photographer, Artist)

Brian Ulrich is a photographer artist born in 1971 in Northport, New York, now based in Chicago, Illinois.

In 2001 as a response to the U.S government's call to citizens to bolster the economy through shopping, Brian began a long standing photographic project titled, Copia. Copia examines the pecularities and complexities of consumer and contemporary culture, through large scale photographs of shoppers, retail spaces and surreal displays of goods. Intially focusing on big box retails spaces, Brian expands Copia to thrift stores, and behind-the-scenes backrooms.

The Game of Washers is a popular southern version of Horseshoes. It is an outdoor lawn game. [edit] Royce Hall

After [Walt Disney Concert Hall] and [Los Angeles International Airport]'s [Theme Building], [UCLA]'s Royce Hall is perhaps the most recognizable building in [Los Angeles]. Built in the [Romanesque Revival] style, its signature pair of [brick] and [sandstone] towers have inspired numerous of the university's logos.

Designed by David Allison and named after philosopher [Josiah Royce], Royce Hall's exterior design loosely follows that of the Church of San Ambrogio in [Milan], [Italy]. Comprising a large [concert hall], classrooms, and faculty offices, it was completed in 1929 as one of the four original buildings of the [University of California, Los Angeles]. Severely damaged in the 1994 [Northridge Earthquake], it underwent a $71 million restoration that was completed in 1997.

Due to its acclaimed acoustics and 6,600 pipe Skinner [pipe organ], the 1,833 [concert hall] has often been used for recording sessions by the [Los Angeles Philharmonic]. Individuals who have appeared on its stage include [George Gershwin], [Albert Einstein], [Leonard Bernstein], [Ella Fitzgerald], and [John F. Kennedy].
