Bio Dr. Esogbue is a Professor in the School of Industrial and Systems Engineering at the Georgia Institute of Technology. Augustine came to Tech in June 1972 as an Associate Professor of Industrial and Systems Engineering with joint appointment with the Health Systems Research Center. In June of 1977, he was promoted to Full Professor with tenure. His research interests include dynamic programming, fuzzy sets, decision making and control in a fuzzy environment, and operations research with applications to socio-technical systems such as health care, water resource management and disaster control planning. He is also the Director of the Intelligent Systems and Controls Laboratory which is currently investigating a hybrid approach to intelligent control via fuzzy sets, neural networks, and reinforcement learning theories as well as its application to various large-scale, nonlinear and uncertain dynamical systems.

In addition to his research, Dr. Esogbue teaches courses in dynamic programming, stochastic operations research, engineering design, and neuro-fuzzy control. He is also considerably involved in a number of civic activities in Atlanta such as the Atlanta Sister Cities Commission (Vice-Chair), Leadership Atlanta Development Corporation, and the 100 Black Men of Atlanta, Inc.

The office block at Moorfoot Sheffield was designed as the headquarters of the Manpower Services Commission (MSC), the UK government body created in 1973 to oversee employment and training matters. Built in the late 1970s, it was established as part of the process, started in the 1960s, of dispersing civil servants out of London. James Prior, the Tory Secretary of State for Employment following Margaret Thatcher's general election victory in May 1979, visited the site while it was under construction in November 1979; a Derbyshire stone commemorative plaque in the entrance lobby commemorates the visit.

It was a Tory government, though, that in 1987 abolished the MSC, moving its training functions to the Training Agency within the main Department of Employment, with Jobcentres coming under the newly created Employment Service.

The MSC HQ building is now occupied in part by the Department for Work and Pensions (DWP) and by the Department for Education and Skills (DFES).

[edit] Zuchetto

A Zuchetto (Italian for head) is the small, round skullcap worn by a bishop, cardinal or pope in the Catholic religion or of the ecclesiastic. The official name of the Zuchetto is pileolus. Other various names used are berettino, calotte, subbiretum (because worn under the biretta), submitrale (because worn under the mitre), soli-deo. The zucchetto worn by the popeis white while that worn by a cardinal is red, even when the cardinal is a member of an order. Cardinals who had been secular priests received the red zucchetto and also the red biretta in 1464 from Paul II; the cardinals taken from the regulars were granted both in 1591. If the newly-appointed cardinal is at Rome he receives the zucchetto from the Sotto-guardaroba as he leaves the throne room where he has received the mozetta, and biretta from the pope; otherwise the zucchetto is brought to him, along with the decree of appointment, by one of the pope's Noble Guard. The pileolus of the bishops is violet, that of other ecclesiastics, including the prelates, unless a special privilege to wear violet is granted, black. Bishops and cardinals wear it at Mass, except during the Canon; other ecclesiastics may not wear it at Mass without special papal permission. However, according to a decision of the Sacred Congregation of Rites (23 September, 1837), a bishop also may not wear it while giving Benediction.

It cannot be said positively when the zucchetto became customary, but it was probably not before the thirteenth century. It appears on the cardinals in the fresco, "St. Francis before Honorius III", painted about 1290 in the upper church of St. Francis at Assisi. It is seen also under the tiara in the effigy on the tomb of Clement VI (d. 1352) at La Chaise-Dieu. The figures on the several tombs of bishops of the fifteenth century in the Roman churches show the zucchetto under the mitre. In the "Ordo" of Jacobus Gajetanus (about 1311) the zucchetto is mentioned in connection with the hat of the cardinals (cap. cxviii), and with the mitre in the "Ordo" of Petrus Amelii (cap. cxliv.), which appeared about 1400. It is shown in the pictures and sculpture of the late Middle Ages sometimes as a round skullcap, sometimes as a cap that covers the back of the head and the ears. In this shape it was called camauro; this designation was given especially to the red velvet cap of this kind bordered with ermin that was peculiar to the pope. There was great confusion as to the proper use of the zucchetto and hence the Sacred Congregation of Rites has delivered several decisions on the Subject ("Decr. auth. Congr. SS. Rit.", V, Rome 1901, 382).

[edit] sputter coating

Sputter coating is a process of covering a specimen with a very thin layer of heavy metal, generally a gold/palladium (Au/Pd) mixture. This coating increases the ability of a specimen to conduct electricity and emit secondary electrons when in a scanning electron microsope, acting as a "stain" for electron microscopy. Biological specimens, composed largely of carbon compounds, are usually poor emitters of secondary electrons due to the low atomic number of carbon. Rather than absorbing electrons from the electron source of the microscope and then emitting electrons for detection, carbon compounds tend to collect a charge.

[edit] {[Talbot Duckmanton}}

Sir Talbot Duckmanton was the General Manager of the Australian Broadcasting Commission - now the Australian Broadcasting Corporation - both known as 'the ABC'- from 1965 to 1982.

One of the more notable things about his tenure with the ABC was the Post Office Box addresses for the ABC in each of the capital cities of the Australian States - PO Box 9994.

In his earlier days with the ABC, Talbot Duckmanton was a sports broadcaster, having a particular interest in cricket. He created the first 'live' broadcast of a cricket test match from overseas by reading telegraph reports of each part of the game (each bowler's approach and the batsman's response) and broadcasting it as if he were there watching the match, including a sound effect generated by striking a piece of wood with a pencil to simulate the sound of the bat striking the ball - crowd sounds were supplied by sound effects men (sic, sports broadcasting in the 1930s was particularly male) using phonograh records of applause, cheers, etc.

Sir Donald Bradman's batting average in Test cricket was 99.94. What better number to have as the PO Box address of the ABC? Sir Talbot couldn't think of another.

At age 13,Kickerillo, took his first job at Western Auto in Bryan, Texas, followed by a job at Hughes Tool Company in Houston, making ammunition for World War II. At age 16, he joined the Merchant Marines and traveled around the world for the next thirteen years.

Upon his return to Houston in 1957, Kickerillo's entrepreneurial endeavors led him to build several new homes and within one year, the Kickerillo Company was in real estate development and home construction. Since those early days, Kickerillo has had a profound impact on the growth of Houston and the trends of the upscale housing market. The Kickerillo Companies have developed or built in more than 28 communities that provide quality custom-built homes, shopping centers, churches, schools, apartments and recreational facilities. In 1964, Kickerillo purchased his first bank, and his financial holdings grew to over 20 Banks, Savings & Loans and Insurance Companies.

Five decades later, Vincent is a multi-millionaire and the chief executive officer of a substantial real estate empire that has developed over 15,000 lots and built more than 10,000 homes in the premium price range. In addition, he has developed more than 1,000 acres of commercial property all over the city of Houston.

The highly successful builder/developer/banker/businessman has proven that a strong commitment to making a dream a reality can elevate a person who starts with very little into a realm of remarkable success. As a response to his extraordinary good fortune in Houston, he has strived to better himself and the community. The Kickerillo family commits itself to playing active roles in a variety of local and national benefits for others.

His impressive record of accomplishments, strong character, and solid beliefs have made Vincent Kickerillo successful in business and finance. Vincent shares his good fortune with his wife Mary and his daughter Kelli.
