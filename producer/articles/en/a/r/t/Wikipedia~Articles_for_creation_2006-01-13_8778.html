[edit] Minyan Shivyoni Hilchati

A Minyan Shivyoni Hilchati (shivyoni means 'equitable' and hilchati means "in accordance with Jewish law") is a Jewish prayer service in which women have a significantly expanded liturgical opportunities within the context of Orthodox practice, including a separation between men and women during prayer. Such a service represents an attempt to readdress the role of women within the framework of Orthodox halakhah, Jewish law as represented by traditional Jewish sources including the Talmud, the Shulhan Arukh, and responsa of Orthodox rabbis.

[edit] Theoretical Grounding

The theoretical underpinning of such a service is a series of articles in the journal of EDAH, a Modern Orthodox organization, beginning in 2001. These articles argued that under certain conditions it is acceptable under Orthodox jewish law for women to read from the Torah, one of the most central acts of traditional Jewish public prayer, during Shabbat (sabbath) prayer services. The seminal articles were Rabbi Mendel Shapiro’s article “Qeri’at ha-Torah by Women: A Halakhic Analysis” (EDAH Journal 1:2 2001.) and Rabbi Daniel Sperber’s article, “Congregational Dignity and Human Dignity: Women and Public Torah Reading.” (EDAH Journal 3:2, 2002)

[edit] Status within Orthodoxy

The articles permitting these practices were by Orthodox rabbis, and the journal they appeared in, EDAH Journal, is the journal of an established Modern Orthodox organization. The leadership of minyanim shivyoni hilchati take great care to keep within the boundaries of Orthodox Jewish law so interpreted. Nonetheless, the interpretation was and is a controversial one. EDAH Journal published the Shapiro article only for discussion purposes, and published it along with a counterargument by Rabbi Yehudah Henkin, " Qeri'at Ha-Torah by Women: Where We Stand Today" (EDAH Journal 1:2 2001) summarizing the arguments against such an interpretation. A minyan shivyoni hilchati currently represents an experimental innovation in Orthodoxy that has not gained the offical approval of any major Orthodox organization or rabbinical association, and hence cannot be said to have full general acceptance within mainstream Orthodox practice.

[edit] Innovations

Key innovations of a minyan shivyoni hilchati include:

1. While retaining the Orthodox requirement that public parts of the service can be conducted only with ten men present, a minyan shivyoni hilchati voluntarily undertakes not to conduct public prayer unless ten women are also present, thus creating a de facto egalitarianism.

2. While retaining the Orthodox requirement of separate seating for men and women and a mechitza (partition) between the men's and women's sections, in a minyan shivyoni hilchati the mechitzah runs down the middle of the room, so that the men’s and women’s sections are side by side. The bimah (dias) is located in the center between the men’s and women’s sections and a shtender (reader's lectern) is located on each side. When the Torah is led in procession around the congregation, it passes through both the men's and women's sections. It is carried through the women's section by a woman, passed over the mechitza, and carried through the men's section by a man.

3. Women can lead certain parts of the prayer service: kabbalat Shabbat, Pesukei de-Zimra, and the removal and replacing of the Torah in the Ark. Certain parts (Maariv, Shacharit and Mussaf) remain reserved for men. The result is that women and men each alternate leading parts of the service, each leading approximately a half, creating a service with an egalitarian feel.

4. Women are called to and read from the Torah.

5. Women can give D'Vrei Torah (sermons or lessons), a practice that is also accepted in a larger range of Orthodox congregations.

[edit] Continuity With Halacha

The services nonetheless have, and take great care to retain, a distinctly Orthodox flavor which is different in character from Conservative, Reconstructionist, and Reform services, which have been egalitarian for decades. Key differences between a minyan shivyoni hilchati and a Conservative service include:

1. The service is not fully egalitarian. While it creates an atmosphere of men and women alternating leadership roles in a more-or-less even fashion, there are some parts of the service only men can do even within the liberal interpretation of Orthodoxy a minyan shivyoni hilchati follows. These parts (e.g. Shacharit and Mussaf) are always done by men. The Orthodox idea that men and women have roles which are complementary, not interchangable, is retained.

2. The Orthodox requirement of separation between men and women during prayer is fully and carefully retained. In addition to the mechitzah separating the men's and women's sections, men and women are never present on the bima at the same time during prayer. Men lead from the shtender on the men's side and women from the schtender on the women's side. The Torah procession is carefully orchestrated so that men and women stay in their respective sections.

3. The complete Orthodox prayer service is done, including parts eliminated by the Conservative movement, such as prayers recalling temple sacrifices and petitioning for their resumption.

4. The permission for women to read from the Torah is based on a passage in the Talmud that concerns Shabbat (the Jewish sabbath). Accordingly, these special services are often conducted only on Shabbat, and services with women reading from the Torah are not available on weekdays.

5. Because the original Shapiro article suggested that there may be greater halachic support for women reading from the Torah in a minyan in a private home rather than a synagogue, some organizations conducting such services take care to call themselves a "minyan" or "kehilla" rather than a "synagogue," to meet in settings, such as private homes or rented halls, which are not reserved full-time for prayer, and to remain informal rather than formally incorporated organizations.

[edit] Universalization

This request for creation has been reviewed. The result is below.


In Social Work practice and psychotherapy, universalization is a supportive intervention utilized by the therapist to reassure and encourage his/her client. Universalization places the client’s experience in the context of other individuals who are experiencing the same, or similar challenges, and seeks to help the client grasp that his/her feelings and experiences are not uncommon given the circumstances. The therapist or social worker utilizing this supportive intervention intends to “normalize” the client’s experience of his/her emotions and reactions to the presenting challenge. By normalizing the client’s experience the therapist is attempting to help avert the client’s natural feelings of “being alone”, or that “no one understands me”.

For example, a therapist working with a 21 year old client who is experiencing rejection from her family and friends after admitting that she is a lesbian will use universalization. The therapist will explain that many other young adults who have revealed their sexual orientation undergo the same rejection and scrutiny from peers and parents. The therapist will follow this up by telling her that her emotional response to the rejection is likewise normal and not at all uncommon given the circumstances.

          [edit] Quebec Gatineau Railway technical, structural overview

          This request for creation has been reviewed. The result is below.
          If your request was declined and you want to make changes and try again, copy and paste your article into a new submission and make your changes there.


          The Quebec Gatineau Railway (QGRY) came to existence when Canadian Pacific Railway got rid of most of its eastern Canada operations in the early nineties. QGRY consists of that portion of former CP trackage included between Hull and Montreal, QC on the north shore of the Ottawa River, and between Laval and Quebec City on the north shore of the Saint Lawrence River.

          Although originally built to the highest exploitation standards in the early 20th century by CP, most actual QGRY trackage nowadays stands as a good example of Class B railroading. Average freight speed on the QGRY network dropped from 43 miles per hour in 1959 to a meager 25 mph in 2004. In the face of rising competition from trucking in the 1960s and 1970s, CP Rail was content with letting its eastern Canada network fall to pieces and simply gave up on competitioning against road transport. Today, QGRY is trying to pick up on its predecessor by focusing on efficient operation and low freight tarrifs, while at the same time maintaining its infra- and superstructures in minimally good condition.

          QGRY has a valuable, decisive structural advantage over its south-shore CN competitor for the future of railroading between Montreal and Quebec City. The QGRY right of way is straighter than CN's Saint-Hyacinthe and Drummondville subdivisions; it features far less curves. Building a Montreal-to-Quebec High Speed Train over the QGRY would involve less curve-redesigning than with the CN line on the St. Lawrence South shore. However, QGRY is handicapped on only one aspect: right of way gradients between Three Rivers and Quebec City are substantially steeper than any portion of CN's Montreal-to-Quebec line.

          As for the Hull to Montreal QGRY line, although it is mounted on modern 120-lbs-per-yard rail, it is of little interest in regard of modern railroading, as curves and gradients are frequently prohibitive, especially on the portion between Gatineau and Grenville, QC. This part of QGRY operations bears very little promise for the future, as it relies mostly on forest and paper products as its chief transportation staple. In Quebec, this sector of industrial activity has revealed patently unstable in the last few years. Increasing pressure from ecology pressure groups and an equally increasing approbative greater public's reaction to these pressures don't help either.

          Lately, QGRY was able to capitalize on the renaissance of commuter train traffic in the Greater Montreal area. QGRY is successfully exploiting a commuter train line between Montreal and St. Therese, a third crown suburban area. Other similar projects currently involve QGRY trackage: extending the St. Therese service over to St. Jerome, and establishing a new line from Montreal to Mascouche, also in Montreal's third crown suburban area.

                    [edit] Access Control Facility (ACF2) an Introduction

                    This request for creation has been reviewed. The result is below.

                    ACF2 operates on mainframes under the MVS operating system. It provides the functionality for access control to computer systems and data residing on disks and tapes in this environment. Organisations use ACF2 to control access to its IBM mainframe systems and business applications, often in conjunction with other software products, such as ESP, and internal application controls.

                    ACF2 is set up in such a way that all data is protected by default. Access to data is controlled according to ownership and rules. By default each user has access to their ‘home’ area. This is defined in the prefix field of his logon ID information. Users get access to other data only if there is a rule that allows it. ACF2 distinguishes four types of access to data, namely, READ, EXECUTE, WRITE and ALLOCATE. Through rules a user may be allowed none, all, or any combination of these types of access.

                                        A letterform, letter-form or letter form, is a term used especially in typography, paleography, calligraphy and epigraphy to mean a letter's shape.

                                        In one sense letterform applies strictly to the design of individual letters. In typography, "letterforms" is often used to describe the study and design of indiviudual letters while typography applies to the design and use with letterforms. As such, "letterform" applies not only to letters but to any graphic elements of a script, typeface or font (including numbers, symbols and punctuation).

                                        In another sense letterform applies to the individual shapes of letters that gives a text an aesthetic. In this way, medieval scholars, for instance, may discuss the particular features of a script that give it distinction and definition among other scripts. [2]

                                        Depending on context, similar terms are sometimes used interchangeably - especially character but also sign, glyph and grapheme.

                                        The history of letterforms is discussed in fields of study relating to materials used in writing. Epigraphy includes the study of letterforms carved in stone or other permanent materials. Paleography is the study of writing in ancient and medieval manuscripts. Calligraphy treats the letterforms of decorative writing, usually in ink. Typography includes the arrangement of letterforms designed for metal print or computer. More broadly letterforms may be discussed wherever letters appear stylistically - in graffiti for example.
