[edit] Contextual Objectivity

Contextual Objectivity is a principle with roots in quantum mechanics that was adapted and applied to the explain and describe the operations of news media organizations during times of war. Proposed by Adel Iskandar and Mohammed El-Nawawy in their analysis of Al-Jazeera as a case study, the term expresses the attempt "to reflect all sides of any story while retaining the values, beliefs and sentiments of the target audience." The concept has been applied by some scholars to explain FOX News Channel's news programming in the run-up to the Iraq war in 2004. Other studies used contextual objectivity to describe differences between mainstream media and alternative ethnic media's coverage of post-Hurricane Katrina relief efforts.

[edit] Heartilage - pulled a heartilage

The Australian term "heartilage" whilst commonly used locally, is not well documented or known internationally. It is used to describe someone who has given a poor excuse for a poor performance. Often it describes someone who doesn't have the guts to do something or complete something. Eg, "John says he has hurt his knee but I reckon hes just pulled a heartilage".

Also used to describe someone who leaves a party early, or pulls out of an organised event. Eg, "Wheres John? hes pulled a heartilage and gone home", or "John couldn't make it, hes pulled a heartilage".

The origin of the term is unknown, however links to common terms like "a strong heart" and "a heart like Phar Lap" can be assumed. The reverse is a small heart, or no heart, or a pea heart. And when combined with a sporting injury like weak cartilage you get heartilage.

Google search the term for dozens of references including sporting club newsletters and forums. Although poorly documented it is widely used in Australia.

[edit] Grossman, Aubrey (1911-1999)

American Labor attorney and noted Communist, born in California 29 March 1911. He was the son of David "Dave" Grossman (1888-1967) and his wife Emma (née Silverstein) Grossman (?-?). Aubrey had an older brother, Joseph Grossman (1907-1973). He attended UCLA, and was a football star for the school's team.

From earfly in his life, Aubrey became involved with numerous social causes, most notably in defending Harry Bridges, the Australian-born labor leader who was investigated by the US government for his ties to Communists.

An obituary written soon after his death noted:

"But Aubrey's influence extended far beyond his family and into the courtrooms of the United States. As a civil rights lawyer, he argued on behalf of Native Americans, war resisters, African Americans, labour leaders and Chinese acupuncturists. For more than 50 years, he put his commitment to his clients and his beliefs before his personal safety or security. He was beaten, jailed for contempt, and threatened with disbarment, but he didn't let these temporary setbacks stand in the way of his values. Aubrey was born in California, attended UCLA, and graduated from the Boalt Hall law school at UC Berkeley, where he met his wife of 61 years, Hazel Grossman, at a student rights rally. He was threatened with expulsion from college for participating in free speech causes, and in 1936 he overcame an attempt by the American Legion to deny him a law license on the basis of his `pernicious radical activity.' But the radicalism didn't end there. He successfully defended San Francisco labor leader Harry Bridges at two deportation hearings. In the 1960s and 1970s, he defended Vietnam War draft resisters and the Pitt River Indian nations in their widely publicized attempts to occupy and win back the ancestral lands they claimed had been taken by the American government. After this trial, Aubrey was jailed for five days for contempt. In the 1950s, Aubrey defended Willie McGee, a black man in Jackson, Mississippi, accused of rape for having a consensual affair with a white woman. Aubrey was beaten by the state police in Jackson after demanding a stay of execution for Mr. McGee, who was ultimately executed."

He died in San Francisco, California, on 8 December 1999.

[edit] Richard Outten

[edit] Barre, Jean Auguste

Jean Auguste Barre was a French sculptor and medalist. Born in Paris in 1811, he was trained by his father Jean Jacques Barre (1793-1855), a medalist. Barre studied at the Ecole des Beaux-Arts in Paris, and he is mainly known as a portrait sculptor.

Exhibiting at the French Salon from 1831 to 1886, he first showings were of medallions and medals. Barre is known to be one of the first sculptors to make miniatures of famous contemporaries, such as Napoleon III, Queen Victoria, dancer Marie Taglioni, and Susan B. Anthony. His bronze works are on display in such places as the Louvre and the Cleveland Art Museum. One of his stone works is found in the cemetery of Pere-Lachaise, where he did a bust for the tomb of his friend Alfred de Musset.

He died in Paris in 1896.

[edit] Joe Hutton, Sr.

Legendary head coach Joe Hutton, Sr., the and his Hamline, MN Pipers won National Association of Intercollegiate Athletics (NAIA) Championships in 1942, 1949, and 1951. Hutton’s career spanned 50 years, 34 of which were spent at Hamline (1931-1965). During that time, Hutton won more games than any other Hamline coach. His overall record stands at 588-186.

He coached the Pipers to 12 NAIA tournament appearances and 19 MIAC Championships (won or shared). Under Hutton, Hamline’s total MIAC record was 347-99.

Seven “Huttonmen” went on to professional basketball careers after their collegiate days. Hutton’s seven pro players were John Norlander, Howie Schultz, Rollie Seltz, Hal Haskins, Vern Mikkelsen, Jim Fritsche, and Joe Hutton, Jr. Hutton also had the opportunity for a professional career, but in 1949, Hutton turned down a chance to coach the Minneapolis Lakers to stay at Hamline and coach his older son.

Hutton retired in 1965 and in 1986, the fieldhouse was renamed the Joe W. Hutton Arena in honor of his phenomenal career at Hamline.

Joe Hutton, Sr. was the first coach selected to the Helms Foundation Hall of Basketball Immortals, an honor which three of his players later shared.

In 1986, he was named to the National Association of Basketball Coaches Hall of Fame and in 1987 to the NAIA Hall of Fame.

[edit] The Chalk

In Terry Pratchett's Discworld books, specifically those books featuring Tiffany Aching, the Chalk is a region of the Discworld known for its fields and sheep-based agriculture. The general climate seems to parallel that of Wales and the British Midlans.(1)

The name of this area is derived from the chalk that is the most common mineral, the only other rock being mentioned as found in the Chalk is flint. This is observed by Granny Weatherwax, who believes (or claimed to believe) that magic prowess is in some way linked to the hardness of the adjacent bedrock, as evident by the large number of magic user native to the Ramtop Mountains.(1)

It seems likely, based on geography presented in other Discworld books, that the Chalk is either a common or poetic name for the Sto Plains, or else a subset of that blanket term. This is because the Sto Plains are described as the geographically uninteresting area that is the continental mass between the Ramtops and the Port cities of Quirm, Psuedopolis, and Ankh-Morpork.(2) This is also the area where the Chalk is described as being, and shares the lack of immediately interesting features or urban development.(1) This is further shown by the fact that the residents of the Chalk, as is the standard throughout the Sto plains, the Ramtops, and other areas, speak the modern Morporkian language.(1) The largest apparent reason to doubt that the terms are interchangeable is the lack of large-scale cabbage growing, which is identified throughout the Discworld series as the defining characteristic of the Sto Plains.(3)

[edit] DuckU

DuckU is a weekly, thirty-minute television variety-program produced entirely by students at the University of Oregon. Most of the students involved are from the School of Journalism and Communication, but students from other majors also participate. Students take DuckU for variable upper-division journalism credit, which can be repeated.

Producers arrange shooting schedules, locations and turn in their three-minute segment on a mini DV tape by a Sunday night deadline. The segments are then edited together, printed to a master mini DV tape and delivered to the local cable television provider.

The show is supervised by faculty adviser and adjunct professor Rebecca Force, and executive produed by students Daniel Epstein, Eric Marley and Matt Rose. DuckU airs Wednesdays at 10:00p.m. on channel 23 (Oregon Public Broadcasting) and again in the University of Oregon dorms on Thursdays at 9:00p.m. on channel 14.
