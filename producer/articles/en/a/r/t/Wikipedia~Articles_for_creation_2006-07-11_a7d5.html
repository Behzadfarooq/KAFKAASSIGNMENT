[edit] Orivirran saarto

Orivirran saarto is a place of 16th century fortress located in Savonranta, Finland. It's believed to be built around 1540-1550 and was used until the beginning of the 17th century. During 16th century Finland was a part of The Swedish Kingdom which tried to push the borders eastward, and that led to wars of varying success with Novgorod. The expansion was halted by the unification of Russia and was eventually rolled back later. Purpose of the Orivirran saarto was to be early warning for the St. Olaf's Castle which is still located 50km to south. Four cornered layout (60*20m) build from stones and outer fortress around 65*75m, with 300-400 soldiers and 5-10 cannons. October 1592 Russians, Cossacks and Carelians destroyed fortress by doing surprise attack from north-east. Finnish defenders of fortress lost 200 soldiers as a dead or prisoners of war, and Russians won 8 cannons, light weapons and one ship. Fortress didn't survive and today there exists only very few ruins of fortress with memorial stone (1965, H. Havas).

INTRODUCTION

This article will discuss energy from the point of view of the worldâ€™s and Indiaâ€™s demand for energy for heating, transport and manufacturing industry, etc, in the light of available resources and increasing demand, and the difficulties created as oil and gas supplies run out over the next few years.


FORMS OF ENERGY

The forms of energy include mechanical, electrical, chemical, heat, light and sound. Energy is usually experienced when it is converted from one form to another or when it is transferred from one body to another. E.g. a torch battery converts chemical energy into electrical energy, which is then converted by the bulb into light and heat energy.

One of the fundamental laws of physics is that energy is always conserved; it is either converted from one form into another, or transferred from one body to another. It never disappears nor is it created from nothing. However, some forms of energy are more useful than others through being storable (e.g. petrol) or particularly versatile (e.g. electricity), and are therefore regarded as high-grade forms of energy. The conversion of heat from burning coal or oil into other forms (e.g. electricity or mechanical energy) for operating machines or motor cars is unfortunately inefficient. The laws of thermodynamics show that only a limited fraction can be converted, and that the rest must be ejected (wasted) at a lower temperature. Thus, even in the most efficient electricity power stations, only 30-40 per cent of the heat from the coal or oil or nuclear reactor is converted to electricity. The remaining 70-60 per cent is ejected uselessly into the atmosphere or into rivers, because it emerges at too low a temperature to be put economically to useful purposes.

The basic unit of energy is the joule. Energy must be distinguished from power, which is the rate at which energy is used, and which is measured in watts, i.e. 1 watt equals 1 joule per second. RESOURCES AND DEMAND

Man used wood, peat, and coal for about 3000 years before the industrial revolution for heating, cooking and metal working. Coal fuelled the worldâ€™s industrial revolution, and only in the last fifty years has oil come into significant use and displaced coal as the principal fuel source.

These fossil fuel resources have taken 350 million years to accumulate. However, the continually increasing demand for fuel has in the case of oil led to the rate of consumption doubling approximately every 12 years during the 20th century. Such is the level of consumption now that there is a real possibility that 80 per cent of the known reserves will have been consumed between 1960 and 2020, even assuming a corresponding exponential decrease in consumption after around 2000.(Hubbert 1969)

Since oil is not only a source of heat energy, but represents a rich source of large organic molecules- the raw material of the chemical industry- exhausting this non-renewable resource in one generation is extremely wasteful, especially if 90 per cent of it is literally burnt for heat. However the decline and inadequacy of supply of a major source of fuel over the recent years is posing serious problems of how to find and switch to alternative fuels sufficiently quickly.

That the demand for energy will continue to grow may be seen by considering the correlation between annual energy consumption per capita and gross national product per capita of different countries. This suggests that increasing standards of living in developing countries will further increase the demand for energy for ore production, manufacture of materials and goods, electricity supply, transport, and agricultural developments.

Closer home, coal takes care of more than half of Indiaâ€™s commercial energy needs while oil and natural gas supply 40 per cent. Coal is primarily used for power generation (70 per cent) and industrial processes, whereas petroleum is the mainstay of transportation. Some quantities of petroleum derivatives such as naphtha, fuel oil and diesel are also used in power generation. Natural gas is the main feedstock for fertilisers. An equal quantity of natural gas is also consumed in combined cycle gas turbines that generate electricity and this essentially displaces fuel oil and naphtha.

ENERGY CONVERSION EFFICIENCY

A variety of means of converting energy from one form into another is essential if the limited range of primary fuels is to meet the wide ranging needs of modern society. Conversion from heat (thermal energy) to mechanical or electrical energy is unfortunately intrinsically inefficient, so that even a good modern electricity-generating power station has an efficiency of only about 40 per cent. This is not due to poor design, but due to theoretical limitations arising fro the nature of heat energy. Conversion of heat into work involves conversion of disordered motion into ordered motion, and is only partially achievable.

The transmission and distribution of energy also involves energy loss, e.g. heat losses in electricity cables, frictional energy loss in mechanical systems, energy used up in transporting coal, oil, gas etc. the overall efficiency of the electricity supply industry is thus only 27 per cent, due primarily to the thermodynamic inefficiency of the conversion process and to transmission losses.


MEETING FUTURE ENERGY NEEDS

Faced with a serious decline in oil supply from the 1990s onwards, coal and nuclear power are being widely looked to, to fill the gap. Some countries, notably Japan, France and Germany, have either very restricted coal deposits or traditionally small coal industries, or have run their coal reserves down. They and other countries are installing nuclear reactors very rapidly, despite public misgivings about disposal of long-lived radioactive wastes, possible hazards of reactor accidents releasing radioactivity etc.

However, uranium resources themselves are limited, and it is not clear whether the uranium-mining industry will be able to grow fast enough to keep pace with the demands of a rapidly expanding nuclear power industry. Uranium fuel costs are expected to increase rapidly in the near future, partly through demand, but also because as the richer deposits become exhausted, poorer grade ores would have to be used.

Energy supply based on fossil fuels and uranium suffers from two grave disadvantages: (1) the known resources are finite and non-renewable (2) because they produce their energy as heat, which can be converted only inefficiently into electricity, to achieve suitable versatility far more energy is used than is really necessary. With growing use of energy world-wide, this may produce serious climatic changes, as all this energy ultimately ends up as heat in the atmosphere.

The intolerable heat, temperature inversions, prolonged fogs and smogs around some cities, even at todayâ€™s rate of consumption, indicates the real possibilities of this.

Both these disadvantages can be avoided by utilising renewable resources, i.e, hydroelectric power, solar heating, conversion of sunlight directly into electricity via photovoltaic cells ( solar cells), energy from windmills, waves at sea, hydrogen gas produced by sunlight acting on suitable electrolytic cells, or natural gas produced from the decay of animal or vegetable waste. All these sources take energy, provided by the sun, from the atmosphere, and ultimately return it there so that they do not alter the overall energy flow into the atmosphere. The capital cost per unit of power produced tends to be higher than for fossil-based systems, but because the energy itself is supplied free by the sun, they are rapidly becoming competitive with these sources as fossil fuel costs escalate.

Owing to the variability of solar, tidal, and wind power, energy storage is needed to compensate for periods of inadequate power. This may be by batteries, pumped hydropower, flywheels, heat storage, hydrogen gas, or access to other sources. The cost of newly emerging renewable resources is quite high. The research and development (R& D) investments and activities will bring the costs down over time. The date of non-conventional backstop technologies overtaking the conventional fossil fuel based ones would depend on the price movements of fossil fuel based energy supplies which should reflect its scarcity premium. However, economy can have cheaper energy supply along conventional route over a longer horizon if the efficiency of use of energy is upgraded. In an era of renewable energy supply, too, the efficiency of energy use will determine the carrying capacity of renewable resources in terms of providing support to growing population and energy activities.


THE QUEST FOR ENERGY SECURITY

The quest for energy security is an important one in the emerging global order because of the skewed regional distribution of fossil fuels resources in the biosphere in the given context of technology and resource options as of present.

Energy security has finally moved up front for India, after having been on the backburner for decades. The country still doesnâ€™t have a national energy policy but for some time now, it has been pursuing a series of measures that should reduce vulnerability. The need for setting up a strategic petroleum reserve has been accepted as a policy imperative. India has also been earnestly pursuing overseas oil equity. The oil and natural gas major, ONGC Videsh has been on a shopping spree acquiring oil acreages in many parts of the world.

However, a lot of ground still needs to be covered. Home to a sixth of the global population , India has a mere 0.5 per cent of the global oil reserves. This makes import dependence ineluctable. A cardinal imperative of nations in search of energy security is diversification of supply sources, fuels and even types of energy.

The versatile refining capacity added by the private sector in the post reform era, has given the country a degree of flexibility that makes it possible to source a range of crudes. Acquiring overseas oil acreages can therefore actually enhance energy security through physical deliveries as long as distances over which they need to travel are not too huge. With the oil import bill amounting to staggering $25 billion a year, every extra cent spent on the transport will have to be reckoned with.

Energy security is a daunting challenge that needs imaginative solutions. If piping gas from the neighbourhood is proving to be elusive for geo-political reasons, perhaps India could explore the possibility of setting up power plants in neighbouring countries and transporting electricity through high voltage transmission lines. Such arrangements work successfully elsewhere in the world. The national transmission utility has demonstrated its capability in laying and operating high-voltage transmission lines in difficult geographic terrain.

There can be no unique solutions for ensuring energy security. All options for diversification- of supply sources, fuels, types of energy- need to be pursued vigorously and purposively if India is to achieve its goals of development that would raise living standards for its billion plus population.

[edit] Princess Xenia Georgievna Romanova of Russia

Born 22 August, 1903 in Mikhailovskoe, Xenia Georgievna is the daughter of Grand Duke Georgi Mikhailovitch of Russia and Princess Maria Georgievna of Greece. Her older sister was Nina Georgievna, born in 1901. She and her sister left Russia in 1914 to spend the war years in England with their mother.

Xenia married twice, first to William Bateman Leeds (b. 19 September, 1902) at the age of eighteen, the son and heir of the American tin power. They wed in Paris on 9 October, 1921. Thiers was seen as a splendid match and the couple was an influential one in New York's Long Island North Shore society, where they lived in their estate, Kenwood, at Oyster Bay. Xenia and William had a daughter on 25 February, 1925, Nancy Helen Marie Leeds. The couple divorced in 1930.

Her second marriage was with Herman Jud (b. 14 February, 1911). The wedding took place at Glen Cove, New York on 10 August, 1946. Xenia and Herman's child was Alexandra Leeds Jud, born 30 March, 1959.

In the summer of 1927, Xenia involved herself in the Anna Anderson/Anastasia Tchaikovsky affair by telephoning Gleb Botkin with an invitation for Anna to live as a guest at Kenwood. Xenia explains her hospitality: "I had heard that Botkin was arranging to bring 'the invalid' to the United States through a newspaper organization. This bothered me because I had heard so many conflicting stories. It then occured to me that I should take her myself and avoid all this proposed publicity. For if she were indeed an imposter it would save much unpleasantness for my family, and if she were the real Anastasia it was ghastly to think that nothing was being done for her.... This solution would be simple, so it seemed to me."

As children, Xenia and her sister Nina had played frequently with the two youngest daughters of Tsar Nicholas II, Maria Nicholaievna and Anastasia Nicholaievna, as well as the youngest child and only boy, Tsarevitch Alexei. Both sisters possessed vivid memories of their second cousin Anastasia, who they described as "frightfully temperamental" and "wild and rough". According to Xenia, Anastasia "cheated at games, kicked, scratched, pulled hair, and generally knew how to make herself obnoxious."

Princess Nina even said, "Anastasia was madly jealous of me because I was taller than she was. As the daughter of the Emperor she thought she ought to tower over everyone."

Xenia was on a cruise with her husband William in the West Indies at the time of Anna's arrival in New York. She had arranged for Anna to stay with Annie Burr Jennings, a friend of Xenia's who lived in a Park Avenue townhouse. Upon her return, Xenia sneaked unannounced into Annie Jennings's crowded salon to observe Anna. After watching Anna offer her hand to Gleb Botkin, Xenia declared that she knew she was watching an equal. She stated, "It was so matter-of-course, so unforced--in no way a theatrical gesture. With it she radiated a natural grandeur and I was impressed on the spot."

Xenia recognized Anna Anderson as the Grand Duchess Anastasia at once, asserting that Anna was herself at all times, never giving the slightest impression of playing a part. The two remained great friends for life.

Xenia died in Glen Cove, New York on 17 September, 1965.

[edit] Combat (juggling)

Combat is a game played by jugglers. The basic rules are quite simple:

  • At a signal everyone starts juggling.
  • The last person juggling a full set of balls wins.
  • Holding one ball while juggling the others in one had is not permitted.

The combative aspect is that players are allowed to interfere with each other, as long at there is no actual physical contact between players.

Combat is most often played with three balls per player. Very experienced jugglers may play combat with four or five balls (but the games are shorter), and brave jugglers may play with clubs.

Basic strategies include:

  • Moving face-to-face with an opponent so the opponent gets confused about which balls to catch. (Only works on novice players.)
  • Throwing one ball high, and using the time to wave a hand in front of an opponent.
  • Grabbing an opponent's ball out of the air, and either dropping one out your own balls, or continuing with four balls.
  • Grabbing a ball from one opponent and throwing it at another opponent. (Not allowed when playing with clubs.)
  • If the game is played with clubs, then it is possible to hit an opponent's club out of the air with one of your own.
  • Throwing one ball high and far away, so it is possible to safely hold the two remaining balls while running away from an aggressive opponent.
  • And of course, turning away from all opponents and hoping that they will drop a ball before you do.

[edit] John Shillito Co. (Shillito's Department Store)

Cincinnati's first and oldest department store was founded by John Shillito, who worked as a clerk for Cincinnati's leading dry-goods merchants, Blanchly and Simpson. In 1832, Shillito bought the business and merged it with a store owned by William McLaughlin, and known as McLaughlin & Shillito. The following year, Shillito left the partnership and joined with Robert W. Burnet and James Pullen to form Shillito, Burnet and Pullen. In 1837, Shillito bought his partners' interests and started John Shillito and Co., billed as the most commodious dry-goods store west of the Alleghenies.

Shillito's built a new store in 1857, on Fourth Street between Vine and Race. Shillito's moved to Seventh and Race streets in August 1878 in a store designed by architect James McLaughlin, who also designed other landmark Cincinnati buildings, including the Cincinnati Art Museum. Modeled after Le Bon Marche, a famous Paris department store, the new building's steel skeleton was a new innovation that inspired the Chicago style of modern commercial architecture. The interior was spectacular, with a six-story atrium flanked with elaborate Victorian iron railings, with a grand staircase and topped with a 60-foot-diameter hexagonal glass dome, which, though covered, still tops the building.

The city's retail center did not gravitate to Shillito's new location as they had hoped, and competition slowed the store's growth until 1928 when the the company was absorbed by the F&R Lazarus & Company of Columbus. A year later, Lazarus (along with its Shillito's subsidiary) joined with Filene's of Boston and Abraham & Strauss in Brooklyn to create Federated Department Stores, which initially had its headquarters in Columbus, but moved to Cincinnati in 1945.

Federated merged Shillito's with Rikes of Dayton in 1982, changing the name to Shillito-Rikes. Four years later, the chain merged with Lazarus in Columbus, and the Shillito name was dropped.

[edit] Baltimore Orioles (1901-1902)

The New York Yankees began play as the Baltimore Orioles in 1901. This team is unrelated to today's Baltimore Orioles, who were moved from St. Louis to Baltimore in 1954.

The early Baltimore Orioles were created as a strategic venture by Ban Johnson and the American League. At the time, the then-minor league was attempting to become a major league, but its agreement with the National League prevented it from placing a team in New York. When the agreement ended, the American League began reorganizing its franchises to target key markets previously controlled by the National League. In January 1903, the American and National Leagues held a "peace conference" to settle conflicts over player contract disputes and to agree on future cooperation. The NL also agreed that the American League could establish a franchise in New York. The Baltimore Orioles moved to Hilltop Park stadium at 165th St. and Broadway in Manhattan and became the New York Highlanders.

The Highlanders were later renamed the New York Yankees in 1913.

    [edit] LA Freewaves

    LA Freewaves was founded in 1989 by Anne Bray. The organization is dedicated to the creative exhibition of the most innovative and culturally relevant independent new media from around the world. LA Freewaves facilitates cross-cultural dialogues by inventing dynamic new media exhibition forms at experimental and established venues throughout Los Angeles, and recently, by planning and organizaing film festivals that are assembled by curators located all over the world. LA Freewaves is building one of the largest online archives and Internet new media resources. LA Freewaves also presents local workshops and develops educational material, advocating creation and access to ground breaking alternative media.     R.K. Baliga (Ram Krishna Baliga) (born 29 Dec, 1929, died Oct 26, 1988)-Father of the Electronics City in Bangalore India.

    According to the Indian Express (1), in the early 1970's Mr Baliga envisoned making Bangalore the "Silicon Valley of India" and it was met with skepticism. However, the then Chief Minister Devaraj Urs of Karnataka decided to support him and made him the Chairman of the Karnataka State Electronics Development Corporation [Keonics] in 1976 (1). In 1978 Keonics established Electronics City on 332 acres in Konappana Agrahara and Doddathogur Village, near Bangalore (4). The development of the Electronic City gained substantial momentum after India liberalized its economy in early 1990's. According to the Keonics website the Electronics City today houses over 100 information technology and electronics companies who employ over 30,000 personnel (4).

    He was also president of the Indian Institute of Plant Engineers which comprised of 4000 members according to the Deccan Herald (2), president of International Club at Westinghouse Electric Corporation (USA) (3), and president of Karnataka Productivity Council (1).
