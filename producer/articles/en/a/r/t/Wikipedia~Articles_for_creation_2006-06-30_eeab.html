[edit] Amanda Patricia Koster

Amanda Koster is an internationally acclaimed photographer who has made it her mission to document some of the world’s more compelling issues. Koster is committed to the advancement of human rights and cultural diversity in the arts. She has successfully combined her anthropology background with photographic and videography skills to create media content as a means for effective communication and learning.

Amanda holds a bachelor’s degree from Southern Connecticut State University in Anthropology with a concentration in religious studies, art history and women’s studies. Koster also studied at Creative Arts Workshop, in New Haven and then at the International Center of Photography in New York City, concentrating on portrait, photojournalism and documentary photography.

She worked as a staff photographer for Yale University Biomedical Communications before launching her own freelance career. She was a stringer for The New Haven Register Newspaper (circulation 500,000) for 2 years. Now she regularly photographs for major international magazines such as Newsweek, Fortune, Sunset, Fast Company and Metropolis.

Amanda has worked as a photojournalist documenting the work of several non-profit organizations including Doctors Without Borders, Action Against Hunger, United Way, and Rabuor Village Project. Koster has since expended her skills with filmmaking and writing and has produced still and motion photography content for an NGO Rabuor Village Project.

Her longer-term still and motion projects include AIDS orphans and widows of the Luo tribe in Kenya, "AIDS Is Knocking." (aidsisknocking.org) The project was recently awarded honorable mention in the Santa Fe Center for Photography int’l competition and 2nd place in the international 2004 LUCIE Awards (all 5 entries), and most recently, 1st place: photo essay in the 2005 Society of Professional Journalists competition. Aids is Knocking is a sponsored project of SEDRAT Arts and will be featured on the cover of their 2006 Calendar.

Other projects include“This Is Beautiful," (thisisbeautiful.org) an ongoing multiple award-winning project that redefines the innate beauty of women and a very personal project about her grandfather“"Andrew’s Roots," an immigrant from Romania. Click here to learn about her upcoming project in Morocco: "Before Harmony: Moroccan Women's Song Project." Grants from Kodak Professional support all of these projects.

Amanda has worked extensively with youth media projects in The United Sates and Kenya. She has worked with youth-at-risk adolescents in New Haven CT, New York City and Seattle WA teaching photography as a tool for documentary storytelling creating a voice and outlet for underrepresented youth.

Amanda spearheaded the first African site in Kenya for Bridges To Understanding, an international youth media project connecting children worldwide via the Internet with digital storytelling using still photography, video and journalism. She chose the coast of Kenya because of its predominantly Muslim population and believes that now if ever, is the right time for Americans to open their minds to Islam. Her work has taken her to several countries in Africa including Ethiopia, Eritrea, Kenya, and Tanzania and in addition, United Arab Emirates, Turkey, India, Brazil and Romania. She is on the faculty of the Photographic Center Northwest.

[edit] Grand Puba - Reel to Reel

The album Reel to Reel was released by the freshly solo Grand Puba in 1992, following group projects with the likes of Brand Nubian and the short lived duo Masters of Ceremony. Both of the group’s albums were critically acclaimed but failed to duplicate that success in terms of album sales. Puba’s previous works did nothing if to help to establish himself as one of Hip-Hop’s (still fairly new) strongest lyricist. Grand Puba’s Reel to Reel spawned a style lyricism and word play that had never been witnessed by the likes of Hip-Hop. Puba’s wordplay was reminiscent of Slick Rick’s fairy tale rhymes (e.g. The Great Adventures of Slick Rick) yet, they had a more socially conscious undertone (e.g. “Proper Education”) but also still keeping a playful attitude about them (e.g. “Honey Don't Front”). Puba’s style on and off the microphone would later play a major roll in the development of rapper/producer Kanye West. Everything from West’s polo, yacht club fashion sense, to his rhymes boasting of spending four-hundred dollars on Gucci loafers and being the “freshest” rapper alive was from major influences by Puba among others.

[edit] Steak Diane

Steak Diane is a recipe that originated in a New York City restaurant, with filet mignon steaks of beef in a cream, mushroom, and brandy sauce. The recipe is noted for using flambeed brandy, serving as an entertaining display for the diner.

Generally the recipe is made with garlic, cream, worcestershire sauce, mushrooms, lemon, cream and flambeed brandy.

The steak is sauteed in the pan with the cream, and in restaurants when the dish is ready to be finished, it is brought to the diner and flambeed to burn off the brandy.

Franks distinguished himself in action at Yazoo City on 6 March 1864 when he and his shipmates from the Marmora landed a 12-pound howitzer to defend the city, which was threatened by strong Confederate forces. [edit] Professional Agrologist

Introduction

The term Profession Agrologist refers to members of the self regulated Agrology profession in Canada. A Professional Agrologist (P.Ag.) is an individual dedicated to contributing to the prosperity of Canada’s agri-food industry by providing unbiased and professional expertise. Profession Agrologists are guided by a strict code of ethics to ensure that Canada’s agri-food and health products are of the utmost quality. P.Ags come from many diverse fields such as soil science, genetic research, animal science and agriculture business. The P.Ag. protects the interest of the public by licensing and safeguarding the competence of Agrologists. Professional Agrologists are committed to growing the industry and the business. Their purpose is also to enhance the quality of professional development for colleagues within the industry. Agrology is a self regulated profession and each province has a Provincial Agrologist Act which defines the scope of the professional and the practice of agrology. The act also defines the qualifications required for the right to practice.

Becoming a Professional Agrologist

While the process varies slightly between provinces becoming a Professional Agrologist requires a formal education in agriculture followed by an articling process. The articling process lasts approximately two years and is meant to develop a better understanding of the rights, responsibilities and obligations of a Professional Agrologist. While working with a mentor the Articling Agrologist (A.Ag.) gains experience and opportunities that will help integrate them into the profession. After completing the articling period and being evaluated by their peers the title of P.Ag. is bestowed granting with it the rights and responsibilities of a Professional Agrologist. Professional Agrologists have a continuing commitment to professional development a constantly seeks to improve upon their skills and education.

Internationally Educated Agrologists

Recently the Ontario Institute of Agrology (OIA) in Guelph launched a new initiative to attract and develop international Agrologists. Through this program newcomers to Ontario or current residents with agricultural degrees (or their equivalent) from Colleges, University or other educational institutes abroad can receive assistance from the OIA and Ontario’s professional Agrologists to get registered to practice the profession in Ontario. [edit] MORRISSEY - MULLEN (M&M)

Morrissey - Mullen, aka M&M, Britain’s most outstanding jazz-funk/fusion group of the Seventies and Eighties, was formed by Dick Morrissey (tenor and soprano saxes and flute) and Jim Mullen (guitar), who joined forces in 1976 and played together for some 16 years with various regular line-ups featuring British jazz musicians such as Martin Drew on drums, and John Critchinson or John Burch on piano (with whom Dick Morrissey formed an octet in 1984), although none of the three appear on any of the albums.

They also used the band as a springboard for a new generation of young British musicians, including Chris Fletcher on percussion, Gary Husband on drums and Carol Kenyon as vocalist among others, until Dick Morrissey’s ill health required too many visits to hospital. When M&M finally dissolved in 1986, Jim Mullen and Dick Morrissey continued meeting up for what would in effect be jam sessions with what they called, in their characteristically easy-going style, “Our Band”, usually featuring the same musicians that had accompanied them in M&M.

The band actually came into being in New York where Dick Morrissey and Jim Mullen were recording and touring with their mutual friends in the Average White Band.

Coinciding with the recording and release of the first of seven Morrissey-Mullen albums (plus two EPs), UP (Atlantic, 1976), which featured the guys from AWB, plus Luther Vandross and Cissy Houston on vocals, together with some of the New York scene’s top session musicians, the Morrissey-Mullen Band spent eight months in New York, including a six-week residency at New York’s famed Mikell’s, which attracted the attention of many of the top musicians of the day, with Boz Scaggs, Dave Sanborn, Steve Gadd, Richard Tee, Michael & Randy Brecker and many others dropping by for a jam.

On their return to the UK, and shunning the gruelling road, Morrissey-Mullen concentrated on the small-club circuit. In 1980, EMI persuaded them to enter the Abbey Road recording studios to make the first-ever digital recording of a non-classical music piece, a cover of the Rose Royce hit Love doesn’t live here anymore. Their 1981 album Badness reached number one in the UK disco charts.

Monolith, California is roughly 4 miles east of Tehachapi, California.

Monolith began in the early 1900s as a location secured by the Los Angeles Board of Public Works. The location was a good source of limestone and clay needed for high quality cement to bring water from the Owens Valley to Los Angeles, a project finished in 1914.

In 1920 the U.S. Potash Company, renamed Monolith Portland Cement company the next year, set up at the site and was a major player for the cement needs for Southern Califoria. During WWII, it's output was used to construct a naval base on Guam.

[edit] Central Corridor Coordinating Committee

The Central Corridor Coordinating Committee is a workgroup in the Minneapolis-St. Paul area set up to explore transit options for the Central Corridor, the interurban corridor roughly following University Avenue in the Twin Cities area. After a long period of examination, this committee narrowed transit options for the corridor from a broad universe of choices to just three: a no-build option where services are only incrementally improved, a Bus Rapid Transit (BRT) option, wherein buses run on semi-dedicated transitway, and a Light Rail Transit (LRT) option using Light Rail Vehicles (LRVs). In May of 2006 the CCCC concluded its study by publishing a Draft Environmental Impact Study, which concluded that LRT was the best option for the corridor. After a period of public meetings and public comment, the CCCC recommended on June 6, 2006 that the Metropolitan Council select LRT as the locally-preferred alternative for the corridor and apply for Preliminary Engineering to begin.[1] On June 28, 2006, the Metropolitan Council concurred with the CCCC's decision and officially selected LRT as the locally-preferred alternative.[2]

Metropolitan Council manages all transit, waterway, and other public resources for the seven-county area surrounding the Twin Cities, and as such, the Central Corridor project will now transition to Metropolitan Council. The work of the Central Corridor Coordinating Committee is now complete.

[edit] Michael Champion

Michael Maurice Champion is a former President of King's College London Students' Union (2004-05) and former member of the Conservative Future National Management Executive. He was also a candidate in the 2005 election for National Union of Students of the United Kingdom National President.

Champion attended the prestigious Nottingham High School for Boys where he chaired the student Conservative group and won the mock election. He went on to King's College London to study War Studies before being elected Societies Officer in 2003. This paved the way for his bid for the Presidency in 2004 where he won against six other candidates.


[edit] KCLSU Presidency

Champion's Presidency was notable for being the one to initiate the KCLSU Governance Review and spawn a process that is being repeated in student unions across the UK. Controversially, though, it was also the year that the historic medical students' bar at St Thomas's Hospital was closed, due to declining trade. Champion, though a War Studies student, enjoyed a large following from the medical school and hence this move was unpopular, though it was made on Health and Safety grounds.

His Presidency was also the first time in recent years that the student newspaper, "Roar", was permitted a greater degree of editorial independence. Subsequently, articles that questioned his regular enjoyment of alcohol in the KCLSU bars were published, polarising student opinion about the appropriateness of his indulgence. His former Deputy President, Rajesh Joshi, often openly criticised Champion's actions outside the political arena, leading even to an outright war of words in the student paper.

However, Champion did institute the tradition of the Macadam Cup, named for Sir Ivison Macadam, a fellow former President of KCLSU. The day of sporting rivalry between the medical students and non-medical students is now accepted as a regular sporting fixture and is considered by many to be his most well-known achievement.

In the 2005 elections, Champion stood for re-election to the part-time role of Equal Opportunities Officer, which he lost. He also faced two motions of censure at KCLSU Executive Committee meetings, one at Student Representative Council (SRC) and was threatened with a motion of no confidence at the final SRC of the year, which was withdrawn before the vote. He was, however, awarded Honourary Life Membership of KCLSU, along with the majority of the other full-time officers that year. This is the highest award that KCLSU can present.


[edit] NUS and Conservative Future

On the back of his election to KCLSU, he stood and won a seat on the National Management Executive of Conservative Future. Part of his manifesto included the belief that "there's nothing wrong with a bit of lefty-bashing". He also promised more social events, one of which was held at KCLSU as a private function.

That year, Champion was selected as the official candidate for NUS President by Conservative Future. As Kat Fletcher was widely-expected to win a second (and final) term in office, a low-profile campaign was run with Champion openly admitting he expected to lose. In a widely reported misjudgement, he attended the informal hustings at NUS National Conference in a state of inebrieation, earning him notoriety across national student politics. To his credit, he gained 76 votes, a few dozen more than the fall-back option of "Re-open Nomination", but an order of magnitude less than the incumbent President.


[edit] Final year

Champion re-stood for election to the Student Representative Council of KCLSU and won as a representative for his school of study.

Carnoustie Theatre Club, is a small amateur theatre club based in Carnoustie Angus Scotland

It owns its own theatre the curiously named Dibble Tree Theatre situated in the towns high street.

The clubs original constitution urged it to 'own and run its own theatre'. Over the years many attempts were made to do this. One hopeful but ultimately hopeless effort was the lease of the then defunct Dominion Cinema. It looked good during that beautifully dry summer. When the Autumn rains came, so did the internal floods. There weren't enough buckets and basins in Carnoustie to keep our beautifully decorated and much loved clubrooms a viable prospect.

But in 1991 we acquired our present property and had it redesigned and rebuilt it in its present form as a comfortable 48 seat theatre. The club is a member of both the National Operatic and Dramatic Association and the Scottish Community Drama Association

Our annual programme of productions includes a pantomime in December at Carnoustie High School Theatre, full length plays in May, June and October and one-act plays in February, all in Dibble Tree Theatre.

How did the theatre get it's peculiar name? For this we have to understand a bit of Carnoustie folklore. In Napoleonic times Carnoustie was a much smaller coastal farming village and Thomas Lousen, a Sea Fencible (coastguard) and small time farmer was trying to plant out a relatively infertile plot of land close to the village. Thomas was using a willow 'dibble' (planting stick) and in the planting process grew tired, fell asleep and when he awoke forgot where he had left his dibble. Some months later he found it again but as is the wont of willow, it had rooted.

This became Carnoustie's Dibble Tree. David Lowson, Thomas's great, great grandson, now a man in his seventies, lives close to the site of the Dibble Tree which grows just on the edge of the theatre grounds. Over the years he tended the tree which has suffererd lightning strikes and other injuries and now shows it's great age.

The Executive Committee of the Club, in seeking to find a suitable name for the theatre wished to link it strongly to the town and so it wasn't difficult to choose 'Dibble Tree' Theatre, as the name. David Lowson and his wife performed the official opening of the theatre at a Grand Gala Fete 18 months after the start of work.

[edit] European Union Youth Orchestra

The European Union Youth Orchestra is one of the world’s most prestigious and dynamic Orchestras. It unites Europe’s most talented young musicians from all 25 EU countries, under some of the world’s most famous conductors, in an Orchestra which transcends cultural boundaries and performs all over the world. The level demanded of the players, combined with the renowned musical leadership of its conductors, has won the EUYO an outstanding musical and educational reputation and regular comparisons with the world’s finest Orchestras.

The Foundation of the EUYO The European Community Youth Orchestra (later to become the European Union Youth Orchestra) was founded in 1978 by Lionel and Joy Bryer, Chairman and Secretary General of the International Youth Foundation of Great Britain, with a view to creating an Orchestra which would represent the European ideal of a community working together to achieve peace and social understanding. At the same time it was also designed to provide an invaluable professional development experience for young orchestral musicians.

The proposal for the creation of the Orchestra was first put to the Committee on Cultural Affairs and Youth of the European Parliament in 1974. As a result of an all-party effort, a resolution was adopted by the European Parliament by an overwhelming majority on 8 March 1976 during its plenary session. The European Commission officially confirmed the Patronage of the Orchestra in April 1976. The EUYO's Honorary President is the President of the European Parliament and its Honorary Patrons the Prime Ministers of each of the EU member states, headed by the President of the European Commission.

“This Orchestra has an outstanding reputation for excellence and for fostering a new kind of creative co-operation across borders with all the energy and potential that brings.” Josep Borrell Fontelles, President of the European Parliament and Honorary President of the EUYO


The Musical Pedigree of the EUYO Lionel and Joy Bryer then began to work with Maestro Claudio Abbado, the Orchestra’s Founding Music Director, and its first President, the Rt. Hon. Sir Edward Heath KG MBE, to help establish the Orchestra as a world-class institution. Claudio Abbado was succeeded as Music Director by Bernard Haitink KBE in 1994, who in turn was succeeded by the EUYO’s present Music Director, Maestro Vladimir Ashkenazy in 2000.

[edit] JKD Concepts

Description

Jeet Kune Do Concepts is a complete self defense process for the streets. It covers components from (CSW) Combat Submission Wrestling, Kali, Muay Thai boxing, Savate, Boxing, Jun Fan Gung Fu, Wing Chun and Defensive Tactics from the Law Enforcement field. The purpose of this art is to integrate different techniques and tactics from many sources to produce a style that is suitable for an individual, allowing them to defend themselves in kicking, boxing, clinch and grappling/groundfighting ranges, with or without weapons, against one or many opponents.

History

JKD means the way of the intercepting fist and was developed by Bruce Lee. His thinking was to combine techniques taken from some other martial arts such as the trapping and short-range punches of Wing Chun, the kicks of Savate, and footwork and rhythm of Western Boxing. More importantly JKD not only combines aspects of different styles, it also simplifies many of those aspects that it adopts by stripping what is unnecessary. Ultimately JKD preaches that a fighter should do whatever is necessary to defend him or herself. It should not be a specific set of styles, but rather the fighter should choose the tools necessary and disregard what is not useful to them in a fight. Many proclaim Bruce Lee and his JKD philosophy as the beginning of modern mixed martial arts.


Bruce Lee

The JKD Concepts branch, was initiated by Dan Inosanto, Larry Hartsell, Paul Vunak and Burton Richardson. They have continued to teach JKD under the philosophy that it was never meant to be a static art but an ongoing evolution.
