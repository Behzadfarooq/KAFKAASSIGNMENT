Madhu Koda (Born January 6, 1971 at Patahatu in West Singhbhum district, Jharkhand) is a Member of the Legislative Assembly (MLA) in Jharkhand, a state of India. On Monday, September 18th, he will be sworn in as the fifth Chief Minister of Jharkhand.

35 year-old Madhu Koda began his political career as an activist with the All Jharkhand Students Union, and eventually became associated with the Rashtriya Swayamsevak Sangh (RSS). He won in the 2000 Assembly elections from Jaganathpur as a Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP) candidate. In the government headed by Chief Minister Babulal Marandi, Madhu became the Panchayati Raj Minister. He subsequently held this post when Arjun Munda took over the reins in 2003.

During the 2005 Assembly Elections in Jharkhand, the BJP denied Koda a ticket. He contested as an Independent candidate and won from Jaganathpur once again, defeating his nearest rival from the Indian National Congress by over ten thousand votes. With a fractured mandate in the state, Koda agreed to support a BJP-led government led by Arjun Munda, taking over as the Minister of Mines and Geology.

In September 2006, Madhu and three other independent legislator's withdrew support to the Munda government, bringing it into the minority. In the subsequent period, the opposition United Progressive Alliance decided on him as consensus candidate to become Chief Minister. His government will include the participation of the Jharkhand Mukti Morcha, Rashtriya Janata Dal, United Goans Democratic Party, Nationalist Congress Party, All-India Forward Bloc, 3 independent MLA's (besides himself), and the outside support of the Indian National Congress and Communist Party of India - Marxist Leninist. He will be sworn in on September 18th, 2006.

Oxley residences is a university accommodation owned by the University of Leeds. It is located approximately 3.5 miles from the university, and is near the university playing fields. It houses up to 460 students. Oxley Hall and Bardon Grange were constructed in the 1850s, and the hall has been restored to it's original victorian appearance. The main accommodation buildings were purpose-built as student accommodation.

Oxley mostly offers en-suite single accomodation in three-storey blocks with two flats per floor, each with five rooms and a kitchen. Shared-bathroom facilities, along with some shared rooms, are available in Oxley House and Bardon Grange.

Facilities at Oxley Residences include a bar, a music practice room with a piano available, a pool room, and two laundrettes. It is a short distance from Headingley, which is a popular student area around Leeds.

Accommodation is offered at Oxley Hall during the summer vacation; 98 rooms are available, of which 30 are en-suite.

Agyrios N. Theofilopoulos was born in Patras, Greece on November 23, 1942. He received his MD from the University of Athens Medical School and subsequently trained in Internal Medicine. After a required two year stint in the Greek army as a medical officer, he went to the U.S. in 1970 on a Rotary International Fellowship as a research fellow in rheumatology at the University of Texas Southwestern Medical School. Following training in immunopathology at the Scripps Research Institute, he became a professor in the department of immunology.

The central theme of Dr. Theofilopoulos' research is the definition of the mechanistic and etiologic aspects of systemic lupus erythematosus and other autoimmune diseases utilizing unique mouse models. His early work on the biology of immune complexes led to the identification of receptors of soluble complement components on lymphoblastoid cells. He used cells displaying these receptors to develop the “Raji Cell Assay” to characterize immune complexes, used to establish diagnostic markers for many disorders. This was followed by work on lupus in mouse models and his models became the primary experimental vehicle worldwide for the study of the pathogenesis of this disease. Italian classical pianist Loredana Brigandì is among the few pianists in the world who researches, records and helps popularize the works of important but lesser known authors of classical music of the past.

In 1996 her name became known internationally with the issue of the complete edition of Ignaz Moscheles' piano works, op.70, in a world premiere recording for Nuova Era, which won outstanding praise from international critics and continues to receive abundant playing time on the most important Radio stations in Europe and the United States of America.

Towards the end of 1997, dedicating herself with increasing passion to the task of studying and researching great forgotten composers, Loredana decided to withdraw from her didactic activities which saw her, at an extremely young age, holder of a chair of Principal Pianoforte at the Conservatory of Reggio Calabria, Italy since 1984.

In 1999 the international music scene welcomed another world premiere from Loredana on the Nuova Era label. On this occasion she released the complete sonatinas of Friedrich Kuhlau, which, as with the Moscheles disc, involved works which were already available in printed form.

The search for unpublished works developed a few years later from the very impossibility of finding works which were not already recorded and published. In this context, driven by the desire to bring new life to the piano repertoire (somewhat lacking in the whole area of works from forgotten composers) and by the thought of the quantity of splendid music waiting to be discovered, she decided that she should devote herself to the rediscovery of all those works that merited a return to prominence and which had caught her interest not only for what they offered to a researcher, but also for what they offered to a pianist.

In June 2000 she went to Denmark, where she found the Sonatas, Variations and the Rondos of Friedrich Kuhlau in the Royal Library of Copenhagen. In July 2002 she recorded these new works at the Swiss Radio Auditorium in Lugano in two cds, released a few years later on the Nuova Era Records label.

Shortly after, she rediscovered other significant works of the Hungarian composer Ignaz Moscheles ( 1794-1870 ) including Sonatas and Fantasies from her recording in June 2006 to be released shortly.

[edit] Saskatchewan Isotope Laboratory

The Saskatchewan Isotope Laboratory (SIL) in the Department of Geological Sciences, University of Saskatchewan has facilities for the analysis of a wide range of stable and radiogenic isotopes for use in environmental, palaeoclimate and geological investigations.

The SIL is fortunate to have a range of modern equipment, supported by an active group of staff and students. The SIL provides analyses in many isotope systems, including Rb-Sr, Sm-Nd and Pb-Pb analyses of rocks, minerals, ores and other samples; H, C, O and/or N analyses of water, carbonates, phosphates and organic materials. Analytical capabilities in non-traditional isotopes such as Cr, Ca and Mg as well as in U-series dating are also being developed.

Projects undertaken at the SIL regularly range from local, provincial, national to international in scope and are performed in collaboration with academics, government agencies, exploration and mining companies or with environmental consultants.

The SIL also hosts a number of databases for international use, including the DateView geochronology and Ion Exchange Resin databases. Various software packages for the processing of isotope and geochemical data have been developed by SIL staff and may be downloaded from this site.

[edit] Saxton, Bradford County, Florida - A Partial History

Saxton, Bradford County, Florida - A Partial History Written by Kenneth Walter Martin, August 2006

Saxton is an area 2 miles south of Lawtey and 5 miles north of Starke. The center was about a quarter mile east of Florida 13. Florida 13 later became US 301. I don’t know the beginnings of Saxton, but US Genweb shows William Bray born 23 May 1890 in Saxton, Bradford County, Florida, In the early 1900s there was a sawmill and commissary at Saxton by the railroad. That sawmill closed before 1926. I don’t know when the sawmill was started. The area was mostly small farms. The 1930s were depression years. Strawberries were the main money crop. Most people raised vegetables for home and market. Some people worked with timber and some had cattle that roamed freely in the woods. The Walter Martin family moved to the Saxton area in April, 1938. They were Walter Martin, his wife Esther and their children Kenneth, Phyllis, Donald, Douglas and Clifford. Florence and Ralph were born in Saxton. Mr. Martin bought his 40 acre farm from Walter Swanbom. Walter Swanbom had worked at the Saxton sawmill. He lost his left arm in an accident at that sawmill. The Swanboms lived across the highway (FL 13) from the farm they sold to the Martins. When the Saxton sawmill closed, Walter Swanbom went to work at a sawmill in Slater, FL near Fort Myers and he seldom came home to Saxton, but his family lived in the Saxton home until they died. His wife was Agnes. His son was Arthur (Buster), and his daughter was Dorothy. Arthur was in the Navy and Dorothy taught school. The nearest store was Griffis Grocery about a mile south of Saxton. Most of the people went to Starke to do their shopping. When World War 2 came the depression ended. Many Saxton residents worked at Camp Blanding. There was a Church of God at Saxton. It was a simple building with gas lights that had to be pumped up and lit with a match. It had no electricity or running water. There was a hand water pump in front of the church and an out house behind the church for a rest room. This church was fully Pentecostal. About 1940 they tore that church down and hauled the lumber in to Starke and used the lumber to build the Starke Church of God.


The Pastors of the Saxton Church of God were as follows: R.F. Kirkland 1932-1934 E.L. Hicks 1934-1936 Felix Hudson 1936-1937 C.H. Shaw 1937-1938 Rev. Bass 1938 B.E. Chesser 1938-1939 R.F. Kirkland 1939-1942

The A.C. Sumner family lived just across the railroad tracks from the church. They moved to Saxton from Lawtey in 1926. Nancy (Granny) Bell lived with them. Mr. Sumner’s wife was named Nancy, also. Their children were Arvin, Aaron, Ruby, Crockett, Winton, Loca Bell and Doris. Oxel and Mollie Nelson lived near the Swanboms. They were related to the Swanboms. Oxel was from Chicago and from Sweden before that.. Milo Lee was a black man who lived near Saxton. There was a crazy black woman who walked up and down the highway constantly talking to herself. Carl and Pearl Hernwall lived on FL 13. He was an unemployed banker, but later he became employed at a bank in Starke. The Nelson Green family lived across the highway from the Martins. They were Nelson and wife, Winifred, and children Oleita, Ruby, Harry and Phoebe. Adam Green and wife, Rosa Mae and children Robert, Grace, Dorothy, Herbert and James lived east of the railroad. All the school children in that area had to ride the Lawtey bus to the school in Starke. Louie Lee Wainwright was a high school student from Lawtey and he drove the school bus. He later became the head of the whole Florida prison system. The “Silver Meteor” and the “Orange Blossom Special” were some of the trains that came through Saxton. Florida Motor Lines buses came through Saxton on highway FL 13.(later FL 200, now US 301). FL 13 was a narrow 2 lane road. We called it “the hard road”. Now US 301 is a wide 4 lane highway. In recent years the Saxton area has become more populated. Part of it is called “Strawberry Acres”. The area is quite prosperous now. Few depend on farming for their living.

The Copper River Record is a small independent bi-monthly newspaper in Glennallen, Alaska. It is published on the first and third Wednesdays of the month. A typical issue runs 20 pages.

[edit] Richard Djerf

Richard Djerf committed what could arguably be the worst murders in Phoenix Arizona.

On September 14, 1993, Djerf forced himself into the Luna residence at gunpoint. Djerf believed Albert Luna, Jr., the Luna's son, had burglarized his house. Patricia Luna and her 5-year-old son Damien were at home. Djerf secured Mrs. Luna and her son by tying their arms and legs and gagging them. When Rochelle Luna arrived several hours later, Djerf took her to her bedroom where he raped and killed her. When Albert Luna, Sr. arrived at home, Djerf forced him into his bedroom at gunpoint. Djerf handcuffed Mr. Luna to a bed and smashed his head with a baseball bat, and then removed the handcuffs because he believed Mr. Luna was dead. Djerf then returned to the kitchen where Mrs. Luna and Damien were. Mr. Luna regained consciousness and charged Djerf. Djerf stabbed and shot Mr. Luna killing him then turned the gun on Mrs. Luna and Damien.

In the end, Djerf went on a seven-hour frenzy of violence, experimenting with various methods of killing before he resorted to shooting and stabbing to death four members of a former friend's family.

The judge who sentenced him to death in 1996 said Djerf "relished" the time he spent killing the Luna family to get revenge against his friend for burglarizing his apartment.

Djerf admitted killing Albert Luna Sr., 46; his wife, Patricia, 40; and their two children, 17-year-old Rochelle, whom he also raped; and 5-year-old Damien on Sept. 14, 1993. The only surviving member of the family was Albert Jr., Djerf's former friend.

"It was all my fault, all my fault," Djerf told a reporter in 1995 before he was sentenced to death.

"I'm not crazy. I don't hear voices. I didn't have a terrible childhood. I just did what I did."

On the day of the killings, Djerf showed up at the Luna home with flowers, then forced himself in at gunpoint. He told The Republic he hadn't planned the killings.

"Then something took over. I'm not saying I got possessed, I guess I just lost control. Everything was, like, speeded up. It wasn't drugs, it was just me."

He tied up Patricia and her son, tying their arms and legs and gagging them. When Rochelle arrived home hours later, Djerf raped and killed her in her bedroom. When Albert Sr. arrived home, Djerf handcuffed him to his bed and smashed his head with a baseball bat, then removed the cuffs. Albert Sr. regained consciousness and charged Djerf. Before he left, Djerf spread gasoline around the house and turned on the stove, leaving a pizza box on the burner. But the house didn't burn.

"Of course I regret it," Djerf said in 1995. "I wish I'd taken someone with me that day and they'd have talked me out of it."

At his sentencing in 1996, Djerf scoffed at the multiple death sentences, saying, "They can only kill me once."

The Arizona Supreme Court rejected Djerf's appeal, and the U.S. Supreme Court has refused to hear it.

A warrant of execution was issued by the Arizona Supreme Court in February 2002. The U.S. District Court issued a stay one month later.

[edit] Digifant

DIGIFANT - The Electronic Motor Management System

Digifant is the name Volkswagen gave to its in-house derivative of the Bosch L-Jetronic computerized engine control system.

The system contains mixture control with lambda closed loop, automatic idle stabilization, feedback control of the charge air pressure and spark advance angle control with knock control. There is also an on-board diagnostic system.

The Digifant electronic control unit incorporates all the functions of the fuel system and ignition system and provides both the actuation signal for the fuel injectors and optimum ignition timing point for all engine operating conditions.

Injection duration opening signals are provided based on the following inputs:

• Engine speed

• Intake air volume

• Coolant temperature

• Oxygen content in the exhaust gas

• Battery voltage

• Throttle position

• Intake air temperature

The injector opening time is taken from a program in the control unit at 16 points for RPM and 16 points for load for a total of 256 operational points. Injection times can be determined between these fixed points for a total of 65,000 theoretical different opening duration points.

[edit] Hole Accumulation Diode (HAD)

This request for creation has been reviewed. The result is below.


Hole Accumulation Diode (HAD) is a patented technique of the SONY Corporation to reduce electronic noise in a CCD or CMOS imaging sensor by reducing the so-called "dark" current that occur in the absence of light falling on the imager for noise reduction and enhanced image quality.

The "hole" refers to places in a semiconductor where an electron has been dislodged, thus creating a positive charge. These "holes" or positive charges can be created by heat or imperfections in the creation of the imaging chip. The "holes" are accumulated, or trapped, in a separate semiconductor layer that acts as a diode that prevents them from returning or creating noise. "HAD" technology suppresses the fixed pattern noise that results from "dark" current that occurs regardless of the amount of absorbed light. By fabricating a hole-accumulation layer below the surface of the CCD, "dark" current can be suppressed at the source.

HAD CCDs are used in consumer and professional single and 3-chip video cameras.

[edit] Mission Netaji

Mission Netaji (http://www.missionnetaji.org) is an Indian non-profit trust established in 2005. Based in Delhi, Mission Netaji concentrates its work chiefly on unraveling one of the long-standing mysteries in world history, surrounding the disappearance of the Indian freedom fighter Netaji Subhash Chandra Bose. According to official lines, Netaji Bose was killed in an airplane crash in Taihoku in the erstwhile Formosa (now Taiwan). But based on the various testimonials by the Japanese officers of the war time and the subsequent inquiries held by many foreign governments, there never occured such a plane crash. There are no official records pertaining to such an accident, nor was there an official Japanese probe. (Formosa was under Japan then). Mission Netaji hit fame by reaching the conclusion that Netaji Bose didn't die in any plane crash in Taihoku, even before the commission could infer the same. Mission Netaji tries to prove the conventional crash theories wrong and collect and preserve all important documents that substantiate the existence of Bose even after 1945.

Probes and Results

The Indian government installed three commissions on three different occasions to probe into the alleged death of Netaji in plane crash [[3]]. The first was the Shah Nawaz commission in 1956, under Prime Minister Jawaharlal Nehru; the second was the GD Khosla commission from 1970-74 under Prime Minister Indira Gandhi and the third was the Manoj Kumar Mukherjee commission [[4]] from 1999-2005 under Prime Minister Atal Behari Vajpayee and later Manmohan Singh. The first commission reached the result that Netaji was killed in the airplane crash as said before. But since the commission was not allowed to visit the site of accident, the report lost its fidelity. The second commission also reached the same result, but that also lacked credibility so that Prime Minister Morarji Desai later rejected the findings of both the commissions. But the Mukherjee commission reached the conclusion that there was no plane crash in Taihoku on the said date or nearby dates, and concluded that Bose had plans to escape to the Soviet Russia. The commission also indicated that the ashes, said to be of Bose, kept in the Renkoji temple in Japan are not his. The commission was helped in this regard by author Anuj Dhar, a Mission Netaji associate.

But the Indian government rejected the findings of the Mukherjee commission saying that Bose in fact died in the plane crash. Under the Right to Information act, Mission Netaji appealed for the certified copies of the records based on which the first two commissions corroborated the death of Netaji. The appeal was rejected by the Indian government saying the disclosure of evidences will wreck India's foreign relations, security and strategies. The same was brought before the Central Information Council (CIC) by Mission Netaji and the CIC's directive in this regard was also turned down by the government. Still the matter is pending with the CIC.

(b) To form a world wide network of individuals, groups and organisations, having a common vision.

(c) To publish materials pertaining toh the mystery surrounding Netaji mystery.

(d) To help and provide medical relief to the weaker section of the society.

(e) To work for the eradication of illiteracy.

(f) To support the efforts aimed at the eradication of discrimination based on caste, religion and gender.

(g) To provide aid and assistance to the people affected by natural cataclysms or withering accidents.

(h) To organize appropriate schemes, activities and ventures for the welfare and benefit of the people in general, and for charitable purposes.

[edit] Theory of Human Sciences

Framework of reference, which demonstrates the associations between disciplines. In this framework interdisciplinary research, discussions and teaching can be structured. In the following anthropological inquiry, methods and results are focussed (after Medicus G. 2005; with the author’s permission):


[edit] Issues:

● Can we structure interdisciplinarity in the human sciences?

● What knowledge is the foundation for which speciality?


[edit] Method:

In terms of theoretical methodology, (A) complexity or reference levels (e.g. molecule, cell, organ, individual, group) and their “interaction” with each other play an important role in the human sciences. This aspect is partly circumscribed by the term “bio-psycho-social” (e.g. Riedl 1984). In addition, (B) the four central questions of biological research are of particular importance: Two of them are taken into consideration in all of the human sciences, namely: (1) the question regarding the immediate cause-effect relationships in functional processes (causation), and (2) the question regarding ontogeny. In order to better understand life phenomena in all their complexity, it is also necessary to question (3) their adaptive value (adaptation), and (4) their phylogeny.


Theoretical aspects of the central questions (after Tinbergen 1963):

(1) Question about Causation i.e.: Cause-Effect-Relationships in Behavioural Processes

How do behaviour and psyche "function" on the chemical, physiological, neuropsychological, mental and social levels and what are the relations between the levels?

e.g.: Friendly behavior patterns are adversaries of aggression, they can be furthered culturally (Physiology: Endorphine levels rise during grooming in enactor and recipient). Unattractive behavior patterns such as wanton aggression can be culturally inhibited.

(2) The Question of Ontogenesis (cf. Medicus 1992)

Which inner developmental steps and which environmental factors have which effect and when?

e.g.: Children recognize themselves in a mirror at 20 months of age. This is one of the foundations of social cognition, for example of being able to take another's perspective as a prerequisite for cognitive altruism and cognitive cooperation.

(3) The Question of Adaptation Questions regarding the adaptive value relate to the function and purpose of a behaviour.

(a) Question about the adaptive Value to the Extra-Species Environment i.e. to the kind of ecological niche created by the individuals: What are the costs, what are the benefits of a form of behaviour, e.g. in terms of energy intake and consumption?

e.g.: Social bonding is advantageous for protection against predators and collective hunting.

(b) Adaptation Value within a Species: What are the costs, what the benefit of a behaviour pattern, e.g. in relation to familial proximity (cf. brood care) and social or/and sexual attraction?

e.g.: Friendly behavior helps to develop and maintain bonds as a basis for reciprocal support, e.g. during parental care and during aggressive interactions.

(4) The Question of Phylogenesis Why did structural associations evolve "this way and not otherwise" phylogenetically? (Behavior can be seen as "space - time - structures". (e.g. Lorenz 1977))

e.g.: Parental care and mother-child bond were phylogenetic preconditions for social bonds. Within this development in addition to their original function, elements of brood behavior became elements of social behavior, e.g. kissing and grooming.

------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Theoretical aspects of the levels of inquiry (after Hartmann 1964):

(1) Law of Recurrence: Lower categories recur in the higher levels as a subaspects of higher categories, ... but never vice versa.

(2) Law of Modification: The categorial elements modify during their recurrence in the higher levels (they are shaped by the characterstics of the higher levels).

(3) Law of the Novum: ... [the] higher category ... [is] composed of a diversity of lower elements, [it] contains a specific novum, ... which is ... [not] ... included in the lower levels... .

(4) Law of Distance between Levels: The different levels do not develop continuously, but in leaps. [The levels can be clearly differentiated.]

In terms of empirical methodology, the comparison between animal and man and intercultural comparisons are emphasized when investigating the central questions, on all the reference levels, if possible – for example on the biochemical level (e.g. pharmacological research), cellular and organ plane (e.g. physiology), as well as on the individual and group level (e.g. ethology).

• The concepts of the basic questions and of the levels of inquiry are at least 150 years old.


[edit] Results:

An orientation matrix can be prepared based on the central questions (Table / Columns: Causation, Ontogeny, Adaptation, Phylogeny) and reference levels (Table / Lines: Molecule, Cell, Organ, Individual, Group, Society), which comprises those disciplines that have a reference to the system layers of the living world:

In the table the questions and reference levels in italics are also the subject of the humanities (after Medicus 2005).


In this “structural model of interdisciplinarity”, disciplines with a reference to the performance of the nervous system, their questions and results can be allocated and intertwined with each other. Especially when studying the proximate causes (causation, ontogeny), the “basal” reference levels are a prerequisite for understanding the “higher” levels. However, knowledge of the laws of the basal levels (e.g. of cell physiology) is insufficient for understanding complex behavioral patterns or a personal experience. The whole is more than just the sum of its parts. Every reference level is equally important. Many concepts and terms are only useful within specific levels of reference and cause confusion, if they are used on the wrong level of reference.


The areas of the four central questions are very closely intertwined with each other. An “either/or” through the formation of contrasting theories is much further away from reality than a “both/and”. If one studies only the proximate causes and underestimates or ignores the ultimate causes (phylogeny, adaptation), many life phenomena remain inexplicable and many theories remain inadequate. For example: The question of whether “infantile sexuality” is a part of parental care (Freud) [e.g. hugging, cuddling, kissing], or behavior patterns of parental care are part of the social behavior and adult sexuality, can be decided with the help of the fourth central question: in terms of phylogeny, parental care developed before the evolution of social societies (with individuals, who recognize members individually or/and as being affiliated to their group). The elements of parental care were a phylogenetic prerequisite for social ties in some genus of animals, and in the course of evolution they became a part of the (friendly) social and sexual behavior of adults (Eibl-Eibesfeldt).


[edit] Summary:

The reference framework for those disciplines that deal with the functions of the nervous system has a very simple basic structure: It becomes clear when, based on the matrix with the four central questions of biological research (causation, ontogeny, adaptation, phylogeny), one asks and at the same time takes the reference levels (e.g. cell, organ, individual, group) at which the questions are aimed into account. This “bio-psycho-social” orientation framework is the basis for the development of an interdisciplinary consensus: It is the starting point for a systematical order for those disciplines, and also the basis for a consistent networking and structuring of their results. In terms of epistemology: Since the answers to the reference planes and to all four central questions must fit together without contradictions, misconceptions can thus be revealed by inconsistencies.

[edit] The Function of Water in Relation to Food Processing

Food Science is an applied science that deals with all aspects of food beginning at harvest or slaughter and ending at its consumption. One very important part of food science is the techniques used to turn raw ingredients into food products known as food processing. Food processing can be as simple as peeling a potato or more complex such as dehydrating fruit. Either way, the food is being altered. One critical factor in the success of food processing is the knowledge of water.

Almost any food substance contains water unless specific steps have been taken to remove it. Even a slice of whole grain bread has a water concentration of 38 percent or .4 ounces (DeMan, 1999). Water affects many aspects of food such as texture. The affect that water can have on a food’s texture can easily be seen when comparing fresh and dried fruits and vegetables. Water also affects tenderness, especially in meat products (Vaclacik and Christian, 2003). Water has a great influence in the overall quality of a food product, affecting its shelf life. The overall quality of a food can be affected by water loss or water gain in a product. Stale chips are an example of undesirable water gain while if lettuce loses water content, its structure loses its turgor and becomes limp. Almost every food processing technique alters the water found in foods in some manner; freezing is a good example (Vaclacik and Christian, 2003). The knowledge of how water acts in relation with food processing will determine the final quality of the food product.

Water, referred to chemically as H2O, is composed of two hydrogen atoms and one oxygen atom held together by covalent bonds. The atoms arrange themselves in a pattern of HOH. The shape that water takes is a tetrahedron with a bond angle of 105 degrees in the liquid state and an angle of 109.6 degrees in solid form (ice) (Vaclacik and Christian,2003). The importance of the expansion of water’s bond angle will be discussed later in this paper.

Water is able to form hydrogen bonds with other molecules containing hydrogen, oxygen and nitrogen due to its partial ionic properties. Oxygen possesses a partially negative charge while hydrogen has a partially positive charge. Hydrogen bonds are important because they are able to occur in high numbers and have a significant impact on the properties of the substance in which it is found. Water has the ability to form up to four hydrogen bonds, one for each hydrogen atom and the possibility of two forming from the oxygen (Vaclacik and Christian, 2003). When water is frozen, the hydrogen bonds arrange themselves in a three dimensional lattice formation and increase the angle of the bond causing an overall expansion in volume of 9% (Vaclacik and Christian, 2003). This expansion is a great example of the importance of understanding water dealing with food processing. For example, this expansion must be accounted for when preparing the packaging for foods to be frozen. This expansion can also cause damage to the structure of some foods such as fruits resulting in a mushy texture in the product (Vaclacik and Christian ,2003).

Ice structure is determined by crystal growth and nucleation. Ice grows in a crystal formation. Crystalline structures can be coarse or fine depending on the rate of cooling. Slow cooling allows for few nuclei to grow to a large size while faster cooling results in more nuclei formation with smaller ice crystals (DeMan, 1999). Crystal growth directly correlates with decreasing temperatures and ice crystal size directly correlates to the number of nuclei as described above. Crystal type will affect food quality for example; fine crystals are desirable to have in ice cream.

It is important for a food scientist to understand the physical properties of water because they play a huge role in food processing. In comparison with other substances, water has unusually high values for certain physical constants, such as melting point, boiling point, heat capacity, latent heat of fusion, latent heat of vaporization and surface tension (DeMan, 1999). This implies that water has the ability to withstand large amounts of heat. Understanding this in relation to the food product the water is contained in may play a role in the way that the food is processed.

It is also important for food scientists to understand the affects that solutes have on water. Solutes such as salts and sugars found in water affect the physical properties of water. The boiling and freezing points of water is affected by solutes. One mole of sucrose (sugar) raises the boiling point of water by 0.52 degrees C, and one mole of salt raises the boiling point by 1.04 degrees while lowering the freezing point of water in a similar way (Vaclacik and Christian, 2003). Solutes in water also affect water activity which affects many chemical reactions and the growth of microbes in food (DeMan, 1999). Water activity can be described as a ratio of the vapor pressure of water in a solution to the vapor pressure of pure water (Vaclacik and Christian, 2003). Solutes in water lower water activity. This is important to know because most bacterial growth ceases at low levels of water activity (DeMan, 1999). Not only does microbial growth affect the safety of food but also the preservation and shelf life of food.

There are three different types of water found in food. The different types can have a profound impact of food products in relation to food processing. If water can be extracted easily from a food by squeezing or cutting this is known as free water. If the water cannot be removed from the food easily it is known as bound water. The third type of water is known as entrapped water. This type of water is immobilized in capillaries or cells, but flows freely if released by cutting or damage (Vaclacik and Christian, 2003). It is important to know the different types of water because they have different characteristics. For example bound water will only freeze at exceptionally low temperatures while free water is not as difficult to freeze (Vaclacik and Christian, 2003). They also behave differently as vapors and have different densities. These differences must be accounted for during food processing so that the highest quality of food product can be achieved.

Water hardness is also a critical factor in food processing. It can dramatically affect the quality of a product as well as playing a role in sanitation. Water hardness is classified based on the amounts of removable calcium carbonate salt it contains per gallon. Water hardness is measured in grains; 0.064 gm calcium carbonate is equivalent to one grain of hardness (Vaclacik and Christian, 2003). Water is classified as soft if it contains 1 to 4 grains, medium if it contains 5 to 10 grains and hard if it contains 11 to 20 grains (Vaclacik and Christian, 2003). The hardness of water may be altered or treated by using a chemical ion exchange system. The hardness of water also affects its pH balance which plays a critical role in food processing. For example, hard water prevents successful production of clear beverages. Water hardness also affects sanitation; with increasing hardness, there is a loss of effectiveness for its use as a sanitizer (Vaclacik and Christian, 2003).

Water plays a huge role in everyday life. Not only is it essential for life, it also plays a critical role in food and food processing. The chemical make up of water is different in its different states of matter. Environmental factors will affect the quality of water. The physical characteristics, type and quality of water all play a big role in the success of food processing. It is essential for a food scientist or for a person in the field of food science to have an understanding of water as to keep the standards for quality food processing high.

[edit] 5 S's

The use of the 5 S’s in an organization is to simplify a system. By having a simple foundation it makes it easier to maintain a quality environment in the workplace. Although implementation of the 5 S’s could be a very difficult process, implementation of my processes is difficult. • Sort- The first thing to do in a workplace is to sort through a work area and remove anything that isn’t needed to perform that operation. The goal is to reduce the number of items in the work area. The items that are used in the area then need to be organized in appropriate locations according to the frequency of use. Objects used more often are kept closer to the worker where they are handier and objects that are not used as much can be put farther from the operator or in other storage areas. • Shine- Clean the workplace. This means removing any dirt, dust, or other debris. When done cleaning the area it is then possible to locate the source of contamination and work on eliminating it. While cleaning it would also be a good idea to inspect the equipment used in a work area. • Set in Order- During this operation look at the processes and movements taken by the worker and map them out. Then look for wasted movements or processes taken by the worker that could be eliminated. Analyze the current map and create a new map moving items around to see if the area can be more efficient. • Standardize- It is important that everyone in the company is on the same page. Make sure all workers understand where locations of items are, how delivery of items is going to take place and where the items are going to be delivered to, how many items are going to be produced at a single time and when they are due, and the procedures that are going to be taken to produce the items. • Sustain- Reevaluate the work areas to ensure that the steps above are sustained. Forms can be used to help the evaluation process and to document anything that is not sustained (MacInnes). The theory of the 5 S’s was created by the Japanese. The name of the 5 S’s comes from five Japanese words: Seiri, Seiton, Seiso, Seiketsu, and Shitsuke (Saferpak). They used the techniques for not just improving the physical environment of the company but also for improving the everyday thinking process. By practicing the 5 S’s the workplace becomes more organized, efficient and cleaner.

[edit] Greater Prairie Chickens (Tympanuchus cupido pinnatus)

General Biology The greater prairie chicken stands about 18 inches tall and has a wingspan of about 28 inches. Prairie chickens diets mainly consist of leaves, seeds, berries, and some acorns. Sexual Behavior The greater prairie chicken is a medium-sized grouse that was once found all throughout the middle United States. Greater prairie chickens are territorial birds. They often defend their territory, which is related to their mating success. Male greater prairie chickens do not usually venture far from their booming grounds, and they are typically solitary until the breeding season. Their booming grounds are the area in which they perform their displays in hopes of attracting a female. Their displays consist of inflating air sacs located on the side of their neck and also snapping their tails. These booming ground usually have very short or no vegetation. The male prairie chickens will stay on this ground displaying for almost two months. The breeding season usually begins in the United States in April or Early May. During this time the males establish booming sites where they will be displaying for the females. After mating has taken place, the females will move about one mile from the booming grounds and begin to build their nests. The average female will have between five and seventeen eggs with an average near twelve (Illinois Natural Resources Information Network (INRIN, 2005). The incubation of these eggs is done by the female and usually lasts about 21 to 24 days. There are between five and 9.3 young per brood. (INRIN, 2005). The young, are raised by the female and fledge in one to four weeks, are completely independent by the tenth to twelfth week and reach sexual maturity by age one (Ammann, 1957). One problem facing prairie chickens is the ring-neck pheasants. Pheasants will lay their eggs in prairie chicken nest. The pheasant eggs hatch first; this causes the prairie chickens to leave the nest thinking that the young have hatched. In reality the eggs did not hatch and the young usually die because the mother is not there to incubate the eggs.

[edit] MisdiagnosedMiscarriage.com

MisdiagnosedMiscarriage.com is the first site of its kind challenging the idea that a diagnosed miscarriage always ends in miscarriage.

The Misdiagnosed Miscarriage began in 2004 as a result of one author penning an editorial at Epinions.com. She shared her story of misdiagnosed blighted ovum. Over the course of the next year, she received emails in which other stories of misdiagnoses were shared or women just wondered if they could be misdiagnosed.

As a result The Misdiagnosed Miscarriage site was born. The site supports women dealing with a diagnosis of miscarriage and gives them the information needed to make an educated decision regarding when to end a pregnancy.

One link has been found in women who visit the site and end up with a misdiagnosed blighted ovum. The vast majority of these women have a retroverted uterus. They often look one to two weeks behind schedule while the uterus is retroverted early in pregnancy and then later in pregnancy, their dates look more accurate. The stories on the site challenge the ultrasound literature in which state a baby is always seen if the mean gestational sac diameter (MGD) is more than 13 mm with no yolk sac and no heartbeat is seen with Human Chorionic Gonadotropin (hCG) levels of 10,000 or more. In a number of women the measurements and levels are much higher before they have a confirmation of a viable pregnancy.

To date, no studies have been completed examining transvaginal ultrasound use in pregnant women with tilted uteri during the first trimester.

[edit] SREEKANTESWARAM TEMPLE

SREEKANTESWARAM IS known for the temple of Lord Shiva. There is another temple by the same name at old Puthenchantha (the area near Overbridge), which has over the years acquired the name Pazhaya Sreekanteswaram.

The Pazhaya Sreekanteswaram temple is located at an inconspicuous place, at the end of a lane opposite the SMV School for Boys on M.G. Road. The lane is flanked on both sides by buildings. The temple has two main deities - Lord Shiva and Lord Vishnu. There are a few eateries in the vicinity of the temple. The fragrance of camphor and incense sticks wafting across the compound of the temple is smothered by the smell of meat being roasted in spices at the eateries. The importance of this temple began to wane with the rise of the new Sreekanteswaram, but one can still find references to it in the Mathilakam records (documents relating to the administration of the Padmanabhaswamy Temple), dated 1587. The Chirikandeswaram mentioned in the record could bear reference to the old Sreekanteswaram. The old Sreekanteswaram was on the route taken by the King during Pallivetta, a ritual connected with the festivities of the Padmanabhaswamy Temple. The new Sreekanteswaram is located to the southwest of the old temple. It is bounded by Punnapuram and Kaithamukku on the west, Puthenchantha on the east, the Fort on the south. The railway line bisects Chettikulangara, which is to the north of Sreekanteswaram. Among the old topographical features of Sreekanteswaram, one would find that two aristocratic, old Brahmin houses - the Koopakkara madom and Kallanthattil madom - had some historic importance. Koopakkara madom once overlooked the vast lands, Pandara vilakom, belonging to the King. The Koopakara Pottis constituted one of the nine hereditary trustees of the ettara yogam of the Padmanabhaswamy Temple. These Brahmins occupied a pride of place in the history of Venad before the rise of Maharaja Marthanda Varma. The ettara yogam also included the King of Travancore. The Koopakara madom owned a priceless collection of very old palm-leaf manuscripts. Many came here to seek knowledge and refer to the manuscripts, including Chattambi Swamikal. The Kallanthattil Namboothiris belonged originally to Kolathunadu in Kannur. One of them came to Padmanabhapuram during the reign of Maharaja Marthanda Varma seeking to train the heir-apparent in martial arts. But, prior to entrusting him with the training, the King wished to test the trainer's skill and agility. gurukkal, After becoming convinced of his martial skills, the King invited the gurukkal to stay at Sreekanteswaram, which was close to the royal kalari at Sree Paadam palace. This was during the 18th century, when the capital of Travancore was shifted to Thiruvananthapuram. The story of how the gurukkal succeeded in impressing the King is narrated in Kottarathil Sankunni's `Aithihyamala' (`The Legends of Kerala'). Many years later, some of the members of the Kallanthattil family shifted to Thiruvalla. Poet Vishnu Narayanan Namboothiri's mother is a descendant of this family. Sreekanteswaram was also home to Sreekanteswaram Padmanabha Pillai (1864-1945), lexicographer and scholar, who compiled the `Sabdatharavali', an authentic Malayalam dictionary. An effort that bore fruit after 20 long years of relentless research, the `Sabdatharavali' was the first of its kind to be compiled by a Malayali. Old timers also recall another interesting figure, Bhaktan Parameswaran, who used to frequent the temple. A writer and publisher, Bhaktan Parameswaran used to promote low-priced religious books. Many a poet - right from Irayimman Thampi to Tulasivanam (R. Ramachandran Nair) - has sung the glory of the Lord of Sreekanteswaram.
