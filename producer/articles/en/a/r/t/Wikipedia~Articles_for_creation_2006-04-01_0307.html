[edit] Bruce Logan (1946- )

Calibanu 15:22, 1 April 2006 (UTC)Calibanu

Bruce Logan (1946- ) is a New Zealand conservative Christian political activist who has been involved in opposition to liberal social policies within his country for over a decade.

Logan was originally principal of Orewa College in Auckland, until he moved southwards to Christchurch in the mid-nineties. He became curriculum director at Middleton Grange School, New Zealand's largest fundamentalist Protestant private school, at the same time. While employed there, Logan also acted as Director for the New Zealand Education Development Foundation, which attempted to interest centre-right political parties in fundamentalist Christian-related education concerns, like 'school choice,' school bulk funding, the abolition of outcome-based education strategies and a deregulated post-compulsory tertiary education sector.

In Cutting Edge, Logan often reprinted articles from the Institute for Economic Affairs (United Kingdom), British tabloid journalist Melanie Phillips, First Things (United States, edited by Richard John Neuhaus and other Anglo-American and Australian social conservative publications. Apart from this, he also became a list candidate for the short-lived New Zealand Christian Coalition political party (see Christian Politics in New Zealand.)

In addition, he wrote several booklets for NZEDF, including A Questionable Conception (1996), which opposed comphrensive sex education; A Level Playing Field? (1998), which advocated for school 'choice' for financially challenged conservative Christian parents; and Marriage: Do We Need It? (1998)which encouraged discrimination against unmarried same-sex and lesbian/gay couples.

In 2000, the New Zealand Education Development Foundation metamorphosed into the Maxim Institute. Logan served as its first director and also operated out of Christchurch-based offices, as the Institute had offices in Christchurch and Auckland. Middleton Grange provided premises for their Christchurch offices. Logan also published Evidence, the Institute's 'policy journal,' which ran for fifteen issues (2001-2005).

After the formation of the Institute, Logan wrote Same Sex Marriage? (2000) for Affirm, a Tauranga-based fundamentalist New Zealand Presbyterian organisation opposed to civil unions,gay rights in New Zealand and lesbian and gay ordination within their own denomination. As well as this, he authored Waking Up to Marriage (2004), which repeated and supplemented earlier conservative social scientific research which encouraged promotion of heterosexual marriage against heterosexual cohabitation, solo parenthood and legal recognition of lesbian/gay couples, for the Maxim Institute.

During the lifetime of the Institute, Logan campaigned against prostitution law reform, voluntary euthanasia, lesbian and gay civil unions in New Zealand and other contentious conservative Christian issues related to feminism, lesbian/gay rights, family policy and bioethics.

On October 17, 2005, Paul Litterick of the New Zealand Association of Rationalists and Humanists used Copyscan, an innovative software package, to analyse Logan's published newspaper work, and published the results in Fundy Post (Issue 18), an online chronicle of the excesses of New Zealand conservative Christians and other faith-based illiberal elements. Litterick found that much of Logan's work was plagiarised from its original Anglo-American social conservative sources, which include the Mackinac Institute for Public Policy, Heritage Foundation, Institute for American Values and National Fatherhood Institute, Maggie Gallagher (a US social conservative journalist), Melanie Phillips, The Spectator (UK), Conservative Christian Fellowship (UK) and Digby Anderson.These revelations have meant that many New Zealand newspapers no longer accept contributed news items from the Maxim Institute as a consequence, as can be seen from the Institute's media columns on their website.

The Institute has erased all Logan's previous articles from their website, but many can still be referenced from the Index New Zealand database, which contains references to author and article or published paper details from many New Zealand newspapers, magazines and specialist journals.

In November 2005, Logan retired from the Maxim Institute, but is currently serving as a journalist for the New Zealand fundamentalist Challenge Weekly newspaper.

[edit] Azizul Haque

AKA Azizul Haque, Azizul Hacque, or Khan Bahadur Azizul Huq. Born in the village of Paigam Kasba in Khulna district of Bengal (now Bangladesh), was with the Calcutta (Kolkata) Police service, when Sir Richard Henry, had him and another Indian police officer, Hem Chandra Bose , involved in fingerprint classification project in 1897. Haque devised a mathematical formula to supplement Henry’s idea of sorting slips in 1024 pigeon holes, based on finger print patterns, and Bose, who besides working with Haque and Henry on the project, subsequently worked on the development of telegraphic code system for fingerprints. Henry, however, initially did not acknowledge openly contributions of the two Indian police officers to the development of fingerprint classification that he presented later in England, and for which he was recognized and the classification system was named Henry’s System of Fingerprint Classification. But years later, when Haque requested recognition and compensation from the British government for his contribution to fingerprint classification work, Henry then acknowledged publicly first Haque’s contribution, and he also did the same, when the issue of compensation for Bose also came up. The classification bearing Henry’s name became the most widely used classification system worldwide. Sodhi and Kaur published an extensive research paper on the issue of two Indian police officers contributions in their recent article in Current Science (Vol. 88, NO 1, 10 January 2005): ‘The forgotten Indian pioneers of finger print science. In their paper’, they quoted The Statesman, the eminent newspaper in India publishing an article dated 28 February 1925 entitled, ‘Indian affairs in London,’ which stated, “A Muhammadan Sub-Inspector played an important and still insufficiently acknowledged part (in fingerprint classification). Then they went to quote several sources to support the contention of Haque’s contribution to fingerprint classification. J.D. Sifton, Officiating Chief Secretary to the Government of Bihar and Oissa, wrote a letter (letter no. 761 PR, dated 15 June 1925): “Azizul Haque…allowed to start research work upon a method of classifying finger prints, and after months of experiment he evolved his primary classification which convinced Sir E.R. Henry that the problem of providing an effective method of classifying fingerprints could be solved. Thereafter the secondary and other classifications were evolved and the Khan Bahadur (Haque) played an important role in their conception.” Sir Henry, when contacted to endorse a grant of honorarium to Haque, wrote in a letter dated 10 May 1926 to P.H. Dumbel, the then Secretary of the Services and General Department, India Office, wrote, “ …I wish to make clear that, in my opinion, he (Haque) contributed more than any other member of my staff and contributed in a conspicuous degree to bringing about the perfecting of a system of classification that has stood the test of time and has been accepted by most countries.” At the time of final approval of the honorarium, the Home Department (Government of India) noted, “It appears from the information now received that he (Haque) was Sir Edward Henry’s principal helper in perfecting the scheme and the he actually himself devised the method of classification which is in universal use. He thus contributed most materially to a discovery which is of world-wide importance and has brought a great credit to the police of India.” On subsequent request to comment on Haque’s fellow Indian police officer who also worked on the project with Henry, Rai Hem Chandra Bose (Bose), Sir Henry wrote in 1930, “The Rai Bahadur (Bose)…has devoted the whole of his official life to perfecting the methods by which search is facilitated and as his labours have contributed materially to great credit.” Sodhi and Kaur, based on the evidence they gathered, is proposing that Henry’s System of Fingerprint Classification should be renamed Henry-Haque-Bose System of Fingerprint Classification.

[edit] tundish

A Tundish is a recepticle used in metal foundaries to hold molten metal, typically in a continuous flow process. I can also be used in filling moulds.

The tundish allows a reservoir of metal to feed the casting machine while ladles are switched, thus acting as a buffer of hot metal, as well as smoothing out flow, regulating metal feed to the moulds and cleaning the metal.

Metallic remains left inside a tundish are known as tundish skulls and need to be removed, typically by mechanical means (scraping, cutting)

Historically, a tundish was a flat funnel used to fill a tun, or barrel.

[edit] Crescent Town [CT]

Crescent Town - Crescent Town is a neighbourhood in East York, Toronto, Ontario, Canada. It is located near Victoria Park ave. and Danforth ave.

Crescent Town can trace its roots back to 1887 when Walter Massey - the son of Hart Massey, Canada's first major industrialist - purchased a 240 acre (1 km²) country property centered around Dawes Road and Victoria Park Avenue. The Massey farm was named Detonia after Mrs. Massey's family whose surname was Denton. The Massey farm sold fresh eggs and poultry as well as fresh trout which was caught in the many streams and rivulets that criss-crossed the farm. The Massey farm was also the home of the City Dairy Company which produced the first pasteurized milk in Canada.

In 1933, Susan Massey generously gave 40 acres (160,000 m²) of Detonia to Crescent School where her grandsons were educated. Crescent School operated at Detonia until 1969 when this property was sold to the developers who built the present day Crescent Town neighbourhood.

Hollywood actor Kiefer Sutherland from the hit fox television show 24 was among the first Crescent Town residents. He spent part of his childhood in Crescent Town and attended the Crescent Town Elementary School.

[edit] El Quinto Real (The Royal Fifth)

The Quinto Real was the policy used by the Spanish and Portuguese conquistadors in their adventures in the America's, Africa, and India by which one-fifth of all the riches and revenue exacted from those areas would go straight to the Crown (i.e. the State Treasury). This may have originated during the days of the Reconquista of the Iberian Peninsula from the Moors.

Generally as long as the Crowns of either country received their quinto (fifth) share from the new conquests they let the adventurers do as they pleased with the new lands. The titles and grants they received from the Crown merely served as appearances that the new lands were under royal prerogative.

[edit] Electron analyser

The main purpose envisaged is for the simultaneous detection of electrons scattered from surfaces and having energies between a few tens of eV to greater than 2000 eV. A prototype has been constructed that approximates a hyperbolic deflection field for the electrons entering an entrance slit. It exhibits an energy resolution of a few eV and a collection efficiency of 0.05% of 2 sr. Useful Auger spectra can be acquired in a time of less than 2 s. The significant improvement in spectrum acquisition time that this represents offers many possibilities to further Auger and photoelectron spectroscopy.

[edit] TETSUHIRO HOKAMA

Tetsuhiro Hokama 9th Dan Hanshi, President of the Okinawa Goju-Ryu Kenshi- Kai Karate-do, Kobudo Association and founder of the first Karate museum in the World. Although not as well known in North America compared to other Okinawan Goju-Ryu teachers, Hanshi Hokama is one of the most knowledgeable Goju-Ryu Karate masters. He has a deep understanding of the "Old Ways" of Karate.

Born in Taiwan in 1944 of Okinawan parents. Hanshi Hokama began Karate as a child in 1952. His grandfather Seiken Tokuyama taught him the fundamentals of Shuri-te Karate. In 1961 his formal training began at the Naha Commercial High School Karate club. That same year he began training with the legendary Seiko Higa (1898 -1966) a student of Kanryo Higaonna (1853-1915) and Chojun Miyagi (1888-1953). It was at Higa's Dojo where he met Shinpo Matayoshi (1922-1997 Kobudo teacher) and began learning Kobudo, Hakutsuru ken (White Crane fist) and Kingai-Ryu (a martial art that his father, Shinko Matayoshi 1888- 1947 learned in Manchuria). Upon the death of Seiko Higa in 1966 Hanshi Hokama continues his training with one of Higa's top student Seiko Fukuchi (1919-1975) who was Seiko Higa's assistant instructor. It was both of these great masters that taught Hokama the Kakushite (hidden hand) referring to the secret techniques in Kata. A young Tetsuhiro Hokama was not convinced with the bunkai he was first being taught, he kept asking questions and even doubting the Kata applications. They saw the young Hokama eagerness to learn that they began to teach him the "Old Ways". Higa eventually gave a copy of the Bubishi, an ancient martial arts text and told him to study it deeply.

January 1987 - Founded the Okinawa Prefecture Karate and Kobudo museum. 1989 - Chairman of the Okinawa Karate Gojuryu-kai. 1990 - Conducted a study of Chinese Kenpo (Shokakuken) in Taiwan. President - Nishihara Machi cultural Association. 1991 - Appointed researcher for Kobudo by the Okinawan Government. 1992 - Published a calligraphy book on Okinawan proverbs. Awarded a testimonial by the Okinawa Karate-do Association. 1993 - The Congress of The United States of America gave a Services award for Karate instruction. 1996 - Erected a monument to celebrate the birthplace of Karate. 1999 - A candidate to be nominated as an intangible cultural treasure person. 2001 - Currently working on a Karate textbook. 2002 - Appeared in The Learning Channel documentary "Ultimate Top Ten Martial Arts" 2002 - Visits New York where he taught a series of Karate and Kobudo seminars at Sensei Luis Morales U.S. Kenshikai Dojo. 2005 - (May) Personally Trained (6 hrs a day) Kyoshi Chris Doyle (7th Dan) and Renshi Brian Cyr (6th dan) during their 18 day Okinawan Martial Arts Research Tour. 2006 - (June) Hokama Hanshi wil be teaching a twelve day Okinawa Karatedo & Kobudo intensive in Canada. Grandmaster Tetsuhiro Hokama set up a Karate museum in his Dojo in 1987 for people to understand the history, culture, and philosophy of Okinawan Karate and Kobudo. More than 300 items are displayed. Hokama Sensei comments that Karate is one aspect of Okinawan cultural pride. He puts great effort in teaching and building his museum collection, helping to spread the Okinawan Karate way throughout the world. His vision is to for the museum to have its own building. Hokama Sensei recently appeared in the Learning Channel documentary "Top Ten Martial Arts". He has been featured in many foreign magazines - proof of a very good international reputation.
