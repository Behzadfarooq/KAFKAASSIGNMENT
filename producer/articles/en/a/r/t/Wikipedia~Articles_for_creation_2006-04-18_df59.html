[edit] "Chartism"

In “Chartism,” Carlyle deals with Chartism in the abstract. Even if Chartism per se is dead, he claims (which it was not), “the living essence of Chartism has not been put down.” What he means is that Chartism is not a one-time insurrection, but rather a manifestation of working-class discontent as well as of social and economic instability. Because this discontent is a reaction to laissez-faire by a starving and uneducated populace, it is likely to remain as long as conditions persist and to occasionally explode in violent uprisings (references to the French Revolution are frequent in “Chartism”). The nation needs to respond to Chartism by developing a national purpose (it’s as vague as it sounds) that provides work and food for the working class. Although he largely refuses to discuss legislation, Carlyle does recommend a dual program of Education and Emigration (led by pioneers from the upper classes)

Carlyle blames working-class discontent on laissez-faire. His use of the term is questionable, since he is referring to the New Poor Law as a piece of laissez-faire legislation. The New Poor Law was a Benthamite measure, and the Benthamite Radicals were not entirely advocates of laissez-faire. Since for Carlyle, “Work is the mission of man in this Earth,” the New Poor Law seems fair enough to him. However, he ironically suggests extending the compulsion to work to all strata of society. In a more frank tone, he claims that ruling classes need to take charge of the governance of the working class. He is particularly referring to the middle class, since it is more involved in the nation’s “work” than the aristocracy; however, Carlyle is not as vehement about the demise of the aristocracy here as he is in Past and Present.

Taking charge of the working class means, not Parliamentary relief in this case, but rather developing some vaguely defined Ideal on the part of a “real Aristocracy.” This “real” aristocracy must replace “the sole cash nexus” with more meaningful bonds of love, faith, and common interest. The new rulers must be “strong men.”

Over the course of “Chartism” Carlyle makes reference to the revolutionizing of work by the steam-engine and to the global economy. He is mainly interested in the tendency of machines and the global market to create devastating ups and downs in labor demands, as well as to level down wages (or more accurately standards of living). Regarding the global labor market he uses the example of the Irish (or “Sanspotatoes”). The Irish problem is at the same time a parallel to and a part of the Chartism problem. He claims that the invasion of Irish workers has become such a problem that soon England will either have to arrive at a solution or be completely overrun. According to Carlyle, the globalized, mechanized economy reduces the majority of workers to the state of being of reckless gamblers, though at no fault of their own.

Carlyle employs incendiary rhetoric, but is anti-revolutionary and anti-democratic. He associates the latter with “the sole cash nexus” and leveling and “the national Palaver.” For him, it is more important that “the great dumb, deep-buried class” be governed and, through education, rendered articulate (and perhaps gradually enfranchised?). Carlyle sees Ideals rather than Revolutions as the forces that drive history. Sometimes, though, revolutions become necessary to awaken the nation to the necessity of striving towards the new Ideal. “Events are written lessons,” and are to be ignored at the peril of the entire nation. Carlyle hopes that England can learn from the French Revolution and from Chartism, and avoid a violent English Revolution. Change must come. But through the recognition of the Ideal on one hand, and the awareness of the consequences of tardiness on the other, Carlyle hopes the English “may be able to transact it through argument alone!”

[edit] Seat Pitch

Seat pitch is a measure used to show the amount of space between seats in a cabin such as on an aircraft or in a train. To measure seat pitch, one must measure from any point of a seat to a seat behind at the same measurement location. For example, it you measured from the back of the seat in row seven, measuring from the back of the seat in row seven to the back of the seat in row eight would give the seat pitch in a given cabin. Seat pitch doesn't exactly mean legroom, but the more seat pitch, the more roomy the seat will feel.

  • To obtain high surface hardness
  • To increase wear resistance
  • To improve fatigue life

[edit] Antioch Chamber Ensemble

The Antioch Chamber Ensemble debuted at the gala opening of the New Jersey Performing Arts Center in 1997, and quickly emerged as one of the New York Metropolitan area’s finest vocal ensembles. In recent seasons, critics have called Antioch “stellar,” “flawless,” and “an exceptional group.” Seeking to present as diverse a program as possible of the world’s greatest choral literature, both sacred and secular, Antioch has performed works ranging from William Byrd’s Mass for Four Voices to Brahms’ Liebeslieder Waltzes to Frank Martin’s Mass for Double Choir, with a core group of only eleven singers.

Since their debut as the ensemble-in-residence at Trinity Church, Wall Street in 2001, Antioch’s performance highlights have included feature performances for Carnegie Hall’s Neighborhood Concert Series and the Trinity Wall Street “Noon-Day” Concert Series in New York. They will appear for a fifth time in May of 2006 at the renowned Piccolo Spoleto Festival in Charleston, South Carolina for the “Spotlight on Choral Music” series. They have performed feature concerts for numerous venues, including the Hayes Mansion in San Jose, California. As part of their commitment to music education, Antioch also conducts master classes for young choral artists, such as the Bernardsville High School Madrigal Ensemble in Bernardsville, New Jersey.

The 2005-2006 season will mark Antioch’s third season as ensemble-in-residence at Calvary Church in Summit, New Jersey. The debut of their independent “Concerts at Calvary” series was met with acclaim by Classical New Jersey, which raved, “The intonation was pure, the blend exquisite, the voices ethereal in the church's sympathetic acoustics.” Further, Antioch’s first full-length recording, Winter Songs, featuring the Mid-Winter Songs by contemporary American composer Morten Lauridsen, was released in December of 2003. Mr. Lauridsen has said of the recording, “Antioch is certainly in the top rank of a cappella choirs in the United States… I recommend this CD most highly, especially to choral aficionados". In June of 2005, Antioch toured for the first time, in beautiful Nova Scotia.

The eleven exceptional musicians who comprise Antioch are music educators and singers who have performed with ensembles such as the Choir of Trinity Wall Street, Vox Vocal Ensemble, Cantori New York, Philadelphia’s Bridge Ensemble, and the world-renowned Westminster Choir. They are led by artistic director Joshua Copeland.

Torvea Castle

He was only ten years old when the “Angel of Death” made his first visit to the Castle. Freddie Tovrea, the son of rancher E.A. Tovrea, former owner of The Tovrea Castle died of appendicitis. His parents were devastated and heart-broken as they delivered his body to the Pioneer Cemetery for burial.

A 20-year old former caretaker at Tovrea Castle, also called the wedding cake house is hardly skeptical when it comes to ghostly apparitions. The wedding cake shaped structure sits silently on a hilltop between Washington and Van Buren Streets, just west of the Red Mountain Freeway watching the thousands of cars that stream by daily unaware of the ghosts that watch their passing.

He was hired as caretaker and housekeeper for the 1931 vintage castle that had its share of sadness, a feeling which many who visit the house experiences. He was sweeping the floor on the second story when he experienced something that almost stopped his heart. As he looked down on the dusty floor he saw footprints moving toward him. He threw down the broom and ran for the door. Unsure of what to do, he called his father who came immediately with a friend and met the young man outside the castle.

He took them inside and there in the dust were the footprints, just as he described them. They were obviously a woman’s step and they concluded that they probably belonged to Della Tovrea Stuart, the reclusive mistress of the castle who lived in the basement for many years and believed to reside in the castle even after her death.

The castle began with a dream of Alessio Carraro, an Italian immigrant, a sheet metal merchant. After selling his business in California in 1925, he moved to Phoenix on the insistence of one of his friends who told him Phoenix was the next hot spot in the nation and that a lot of money could be make, buying property before the tourist boom. Believing his friend’s prediction, he checked out an area that stretched eastward to the outskirts of Phoenix.

The city of Phoenix was already working on the tourist boom with the The Arizona Biltmore Hotel, Hotel Westward Ho, the San Marcos Hotel in Chandler and the Wigwam Resort in Litchfield Park. Hotel rooms were going to be at a premium when the snowy, cold weather in the northeast would bring tourist by the thousands to the Valley of the Sun.

The owner of the property he wanted was a widow, Lillie Warner Smith. She inherited the land and had a frame house on the corner of the property. Carraro bought the land for $250 an acre that went from Van Buren Street to the middle of the Salt River bed, a nice legacy for his young 15-year old son, O.Lee. His legacy was to be a spectacular hotel called “El Castillo (the castle). He not only planned to make money on his hotel but also by selling off all his excess land at a hefty profit.

Carraro and his son moved into her old house as they built their dream. It was here when they met a man from Russia who offered to build a cacti garden to landscape his hotel. Believing that this might be just the tourist draw he need, he hired him. The Carraro Cacti garden was the design and labor of love of M. Moktatchey. The garden had 300 varieties of cacti while Carraro began building his dream hotel in 1929.

Being a thrifty Italian builder, he crushed his own granite, blasted from his own land to make the cement used to create a full basement with vaulted tunnels, a system of cooling the air in the house. With the help of 18 workmen, he framed the castle in wood and then covered it with stucco.

The castle rose higher and higher as they completed the second and third floors and crowned the castle with a cupola surrounded by windows reached only by a narrow inside stairway that gave the residents and the ghosts, a magnificent view of the valley.

The ground floor held a large lobby, the kitchen with cobalt blue sink. Teller cages, he salvaged from a closed bank, served as the kitchen cabinets. From the bank, he also bought a large vault, which he built into the basement wall to keep valuable for himself and his guests, and late used as a wine cellar.

A sitting room and dining room surrounded by windows include a fireplace decorated with a replica of the “Dancing Woman” featured in the Orpheum Theater. This gives rise to the idea that the same workmen also built the Phoenix Theater. The second floor has six odd-sized bedrooms with one bathroom and the third floor has two large bedrooms with one bath. Salvaged wood flooring covers most of the floors.

Unfortunately, although Carraro and his son loved the house, his wife hated the heat and dust of the desert and refused to leave California where she lived with her youngest son, leaving room for rumors that being Italian with money, he must have been connected with the Chicago gangsters and that Mafia money really built the house for Al Capone.

Carraro wanted to add property to his castle and attempted to buy forty acres of adjourning property but lost the property to Edward A. Torvrea. He wanted the land to pen up his sheep close to his slaughterhouse, which was nearby. The smells and sounds forced Carraro to put his castle up for sale, which was promptly bought by Tovrea and his second wife Della, twenty-seven years his junior.

In June of 1931, he sold the castle and 43 surrounding acres sold for about $22,000 and after selling or donating the rest of his acreage, Carraro, divorced and died in 1974 as he lived in an small trailer in Yarnell. His dreams of the future, lost because of some sheep.

The joy of owning the castle didn’t last long for Edward died the following year. Della eventually remarried. Her new husband was William P. Stuart, publisher of the Prescott Courier. They spent half their time in Prescott and half in the castle until Stuart died in 1960. Della was seventy-two years old and lived alone in the castle as a recluse. She was a little fearful, perhaps a bit intuitive and kept big English Mastiff as guard dogs and some 100 plus peacocks to serve as watch cocks.

On a cold winder November night in 1958, two men entered the castle and according to reports, they climbed in through the cupola. They came upon Della asleep in the kitchen. Their footsteps awakened her. They overpowered her and tied her to a chair and beat her severely. Della lost over $20,000 in silver, jewelry and cash that night but more important she lost her will to live. She died in a nursing home two months later but most people still she still resides in her “dream castle” as she dreams an unending dream.

[edit] Chapter 15 United States Bankruptcy Code on Cross Border Insolvency

It happens with increasing frequency that a bankruptcy proceeding in one country has a connection to assets or information located in another. Because of the involvement of multiple jurisdictions unique problems arise. In order to solve some of these problems, the United States enacted Section 304 of the US Bankruptcy Code in 1978. Section 304 has recently been repealed and replaced with Chapter 15, titled "Ancillary and Othe Cross Border Cases". This section has increased the range of options available in the United States in support of foreign bankruptcy proceedings.

Chapter 15 incorporates the Model Law on Cross Border Insolvency drafted by the United Nations Commission on International Trade and Law. The law provides solutions to problems which arise in connection with cross-border bankuptcy, allowing US courts to issue subpoeanas, orders to turn over assets, the issuabnce of stays on pending actions, and orders of other types as circumstances dictate. The ancillary proceeding permitted under Chapter 15 is often a more efficient and less costly alternative to initiating an independent bankruptcy proceeding in the United States. It also avoids the conflicts which could arise between the jurisdictions involved in two independent bankruptcy proceedings initiated in connection with the same debtor.

Chapter 15 also establishes mechanisms for the cooperation bwteen US and foreign courts and representatives regarding proceedings which involve the same debtor.

Whether the US courts will extend the "additional assistance": sought in connection with a foreign proceeding under Chapter 15 is a matter of discretion.

The main consideration the US courts will take into account in making this decision is whether the laws of the foreign jurisdiction in connection with which the additional assistance is sought violate the laws or public policy of the United States and if the foreign courts conduct their proceedings according to basic rules of procedural fairness.

Among other factors the US courts will consider how the foreign jurisdiction treats creditors, whether it prevents the fraudulent transfer of debtor's property, if US creditors are protected against prejudice in the processing of their claims there, and the manner in which assets are distributed.

The court may conduct an evidentiary hearing, including the hearing of expert testimony, to determine whether the proceedings of the foreign forum are sufficiently just to grant the assistance requested.

[edit] CPZ

CPZ is an abbreviation for Criminal Protection Zone.

CPZ refers to a business that has banned firearms from their premises, usually by displaying a "No Guns" sign at the entrance.

The CPZ name is a result of the signs having the effect of disarming all law-abiding gun owners. While having no effect on criminals who wish to carry guns. Thus creating a safer environment for criminals who can be assured that they will not be met with armed resistance.

The term likely originated in Ohio after the state passed it's Concealed Carry law and many businesses put up "No Guns" signs.

[edit] Pillnitz Declaration

The Pillnitz Declaration, or more commonly The Declaration of Pillnitz, was a carefully worded statement issued by the monarchs of Austria and Prussia in June 1791. It declared that the two nations would be willing to intervene in France in certain circumstances, but was not intended to provoke war but to mearly have a sobering effect on revolutionary France.

The Declaration was made because while at first the absolute rulers of continental Europe were pleased with the revolution as undermining a competing power, they became frightened when they listened to the tales of the French nobles who fled France during the early days of the French Revolution. When Louis XVI and Marie Antoinette were arrested during their attempt to flee France in June 1791, Austria and Prussia decided it was time to act, producing the Pillnitz Declaration
