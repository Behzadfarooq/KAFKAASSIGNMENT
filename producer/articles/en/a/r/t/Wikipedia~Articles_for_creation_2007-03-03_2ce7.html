The Asian CounterStrike:Source League (ACSL) was started in Fall of 2006 with the goal of uniting CounterStrike:Source (CSS) communities from countries and regions across Asia. This was the first time that teams and players from different Asian countries were able to compete with each other on an international and regular basis. The competition currently involves competitors from Korea, China, Taiwan, Hong Kong, India, Singapore and Malaysia.

CounterStrike, the original game, enjoys large support by e-sports organisations, but its younger brother, CounterStrike:Source, has largely been forgotten about despite a vibrant and growing community. The ACSL is providing a competitive structure for CSS in Asia, much like what already exists in the U.S. (CPL and CEVO) and Europe (Enemy Down).

[edit] Chicago Journal of International Law

Archived discussion follows below. Please do not modify it.
This request for creation has been reviewed. The reviewer comments appear below the article text.


The Chicago Journal of International Law is a biannual, student-edited law review published by the University of Chicago Law School. CJIL, as the journal officially is abreviated, contains articles concerning issues of international law and policy written by legal scholars. CJIL is one of the three student edited journals published at the [University of Chicago].[1]

CJIL began publication with it first issue in the spring of 2002.[2] Since then, the journal has become the eleventh most-cited journal focusing on international or comparative law based on citations by law journals archived in Westlaw on a trialing seven year basis (with CJIL severly handicapped in the latest ranking by having published in only the last six years), and within the top 100 of all law journals.[3]

The journal's impact has been heightened because its articles are covered by several notable academic abstrating services, including the Journal of Economic Literature, and its articles are available via LexisNexis, Westlaw, ProQuest, and HeinOnline. Beyond citations in court opinions and other legal journals, CJIL articles have been referenced in newspaper articles, as well.[4][5]

Law students are selected to join CJIL in a given year by that year's editors (also students) through a blind writing competition held after the participants' first year of law school.[6] The outgoing editoprial board (generally made-up of third year law students) most often selects the next year's editorial board from among the current staff.[7]

Anders Osborne is a Grammy winning songwriter and singer.

Anders was born in 1966 in Uddevalla, Sweden, left home at age sixteen, and hitchhiked and played music throughout Europe, North Africa, the Middle East, Asia, and the U.S. He settled in New Orleans in 1985 at still called Louisiana his home.

During his first decade in New Orleans, Anders lived in the in the French Quarter and played regularly in local clubs. After gaining a following, Anders received an independent record deal with Rabadash Records out of New Orleans, and released "Doin’ Fine" in 1989.

Anders and his band toured the U.S. during these years and in 1995, he was signed to Okeh Records. He released "Which Way to Here", and album that dealt with spirituality and tolerance. The record became Osborne’s first commercial break with two top-five singles “Favorite Son” and “Pleasin’ You.” Both were featured in several Hollywood movies, and the latter was later recorded by Johnny Lang.

Shanachie Entertainment signed Osborne in 1998 and in 1999 released his fourth album, "Living Room", a very personal record that signified some new directions in Anders’ music after the breakup with his longtime partner Theresa, drug use, and a family death. This album also features guest appearances by Keb Mo, Kick Joseph and Tommy Malone.

Keb' Mo's 1998 Grammy award winning album "Slow Down", featured two songs co-written with Osborne.

Since 1994, Anders has been working as a professional songwriter in Nashville, first for Polygram and now for Universal where he writes and produces for other artists.

His song, “Watch the Wind Blow By,” was recorded by country music star Tim McGraw, hitting No. 1 on the country charts for 2 weeks and selling over 3 million albums.

A thrust engine is one that produces thurst out of a nozzle as its form of work. They are usually used to propell a aircraft of some nature. These are disinguished from a mechanical output engine, which is one that converts some form of energy into a mechanical work. Thust engines are a large category that includes all manner of jet engine and rocketrys, as well as ion thrusters. Propeller engines in some air or water craft can be said to produce thrust in a manner of speaking, but the actual thrust is produced by intermediate mechanical action; therfore, the engines themselves, regardless of their particular output, are of the mechanical output rather than thrust type.

A thrust engine's efficiency at converting is energy source into work is called specific impulse.

Tayari Jones is an African American author and winner of the Hurston/Wright Award for Debut Fiction. Born in 1970, she was born and raised in Atlanta, Georgia and educated at both Spelman College, the University of Iowa and Arizona State University.

Jean Renoir said he was "astounded" by this film and saw it at least ten times when it first came out, crediting it with inspring him to make a career as a film director. He said, later in life, that it impressed him with "the possiblity of creating within a film a world that might differ greatly from reality but still would be experienced as having a wholeness and coherence like that of the world we live in."

Submarine Fleet is a post punk/shoegaze band from Portland OR. Lead by Ashkelon Sain from Trance To The Sun and Blade Fetish, has produced albums for such bands as Cinema Strange. Submarine Fleet has had music featured in the films Stanley Cuba and Atomic Vixens.

Current Members
