Articulated vehicle

An articulated vehicle is a vehicle which has a permanent or semi-permanent pivoting joint in its construction, allowing the vehicle to turn more sharply. There are many kinds of articulated vehicles, from heavy equipment to buses and trains. Steam locomotives were sometimes articulated in that the driving wheels could pivot around turns.

In a broader sense, any vehicle towing a trailer could be described as articulated (the word articulated comes from the Latin articulus : small joint). In the UK, the term "articulated lorry" refers to the combination of a tractor and a semi-trailer. In the U.S., this is called a semi-trailer truck, "tractor-trailer" or "semi-truck" and would not necessarily be called articulated.

[edit] Type of vehicles

[edit] Buses

A Volvo articulated bus
Volvo front loader on which the front portion pivots for turning
Siemens LRV
Main article: Articulated bus

Buses are articulated to allow for a much longer bus which can still navigate with the [[turning radius]] of a normal bus.

[edit] Trucks

Main article: Semi-trailer truck

In the UK, semi-trailers on tractor units are referred to as "articulated lorries."

[edit] Trains and rail

Main article: Articulated car

Many train cars are made with articulated connections, sharing a common wheel truck between two cars. This reduces costs, weight, vibration and noise.

Trams and Light Rail Vehicles have been made with articulated designs since the 1950s. This was instead of using trailers or multiple units, which had been attempted in the early 1900s. The articulated design allows passengers (and fare inspectors) to move the entire length of the vehicle.

See also: Articulated locomotive

[edit] Heavy equipment

Main article: Engineering vehicle

Front loaders, graders, dump trucks and other vehicles have been made with articulated joints to greatly reduce their turning radius.

[edit] Military vehicles

Some military vehicles, like for instance the US military M561 Gama Goat, were engineered with an articulated body for maximum rough terrain capability.
