[edit] Victoria Lautman

Victoria Lautman is a commentator in Chicagos world of arts, culture, and the humanities. As founder, host, and executive-producer of Writers on the Record with Victoria Lautman, Victoria created a popular author-interview series, which is also a three-pronged, multi-media brand unique to Chicago, comprising a monthly column in Chicago magazine, a free, audience-attended interview at the Lookingglass Theatre, and a simultaneous broadcast on 98.7WFMT Radio. The series was inaugurated in September 2004, and has drawn major national and international authors to Chicago, including Edward P. Jones, Louise Erdrich, Frank McCourt, Michael Cunningham, and Susan Orlean, among many others.

Prior to conceiving and founding this free, public series, Victoria was a featured host and contributor on Chicago Public Radio for two decades, beginning as a free-lance arts reviewer while in her twenties. These accessible, humorous, and insightful reviews proved popular and eventually led to Victorias own arts and culture magazine program, "Artistic License", which aired for eight years. When a new daily magazine, "848", started up, Victoria signed on as a regular interviewer and contributor on a wide variety of subjects, including not only arts and culture but also medicine, education, and other complex issues. Her personal essays on day-to-day life were a regular feature of the program.

Victoria has been Chicago Editor for Metropolitan Home Magazine, Art & Auction, Architectural Record, and HG. Shes also been a frequent contributor to the Chicago Tribune Sunday Magazine, and has written for Interview, Elle Dcor, Architectural Digest, USA Today, The Chicago Sun-Times, and the Chicago Reader. Her best-selling art book, "The New Tattoo," from Abbeville Press, was first published in 1994.

Victoria has also been a regular contributor to Chicago PBS affiliate WTTW-Ch. 11's Emmy award-winning programs "Artbeat Chicago" and "Chicago Tonight"; a segment host and producer on WBBM-Ch. 2s award-winning magazine show, "Eye On Chicago"; and, a regular culture correspondent for WMAQ-Ch. 5s afternoon news report.

Victoria has an M.A. in Art History from George Washington University, and a B.A. in Anthropology and Art History from the University of New Mexico. She also attended Merton College at Oxford University for archeological field training, and spent one summer digging through Bronze Age garbage in the Scottish Hebrides.

[edit] Steam Biplane: Hiram Maxim's Aeroplane

In 1894 machine gun inventor Sir Hiram Maxim turned his hand to heavier than air flight. Near Bexley, south of London, England, he built a temporary aerodrome in which he constructed a large steam powered biplane. Complete with a thousand pound water tube boiler and 300 horsepower engine, this incongruous monster was trundled out onto guideways and launched into flight.

While the guideways prevented free flight, the aircraft did take to its wings with three passengers for one thousand feet and as much as its guides would let it. The machine crashed at the end of its journey.

[edit] Frederick Macaulay

File Edit Options Buffers Tools Help

Frederick Robertson Macaulay (August 12, 1882 - March, 1970) was an economist of the Institutionalist School. He is known for introducing the concept of bond duration. Macaulay's contributions also include a mammoth empirical study of the time series behavior of interest rates published in 1938 and a study of short selling on the New York Stock Exchange (Macaulay and Durand, 1951).

Macaulay was born in Montreal to a family influential in Montreal business. He obtained his Bachelor's and Master's degrees from the University of Colorado in 1909 and 1920, respectively. He also obtained a law degree in 1911. In 1924, he obtained a PhD from Columbia University.

Macaulay worked at the National Bureau of Economic Research from 1921 until 1938. He also taught at the New School of Social Research (1921-1826). In 1938, Macaulay became research director of the Twentieth Century Fund.

[edit] Four Vertex Theorem

Every simple convex closed curve in R^2 has at least four vertices.

In other words, every convex curve, has at least 4 points where the rate of change of local curvature, Ks reaches zero.

i.e. for a simple closed convex paramaterized curve, gamma(t), dKs/dt=0 at at least 4 points on the curve

Two examples: In the case of a circle, which has constant curvature at every point, dKs/dt= 0 at every point and every point is a vertex, and thus the curve has an infinite number of vertices. thus the number of vertices > 4

In the case of an ellipse of the form x^2/(a^2)+ y^2/(b^2)=1, there are only 4 vertices, occuring at the points of maximum and minimum curvature, ie at (x,y)= (a,0), (-a,0), (0,b) and (0,-b). thus the number of vertices is =4

[edit] Bellevue House

Bellevue House is a National Historic Site of Canada, located in Kingston, Ontario, which was occupied for a brief period by Canada's first Prime Minister, the Right Honourable Sir John A. MacDonald. The site is maintained and funded by Parks Canada.

Bellevue House was built in the early 1840s for Charles Hales, a successful Kingston grocer. Asymmetrical in shape, it is an outstanding example of Italianate Villa architecture, constrasting greatly with the more prominent Georgian limestone buildings of Kingston.

In letters, MacDonald nicknamed the house "Tea Caddy Castle" and "Pekoe Pagoda." Macdonald rented the house, motivated by the chronic ill-health of his wife, Isabella, which is now thought to have been Tuberculosis. He wrote of the "...complete quiet and seclusion of the house, which is completely surrounded by trees and has a fresh breeze ever blowing on it from Lake Ontario..." that, he hoped, would bring about an improvement in her condition. However, their stay, beginning in 1848, was brief, and Isabella's health showed no great signs of improvement. Macdonald himself was increasingly troubled by financial concerns and in September 1849, the couple moved again to more modest accomodation in downtown Kingston.

The property itself is quite large and picturesque, boasting a magnificent garden as well as an apple orchard. Although the property is still the same width, it is considerably smaller in length than in the 1840s; originally the property extended from what is now Union Street down to the shore of Lake Ontario. In addition, it used to provide a much more genuine belle vue (French for beautiful view) of the lake.

Today, as a National Historic Site of Canada since 1967 (it was occupied privately until this time), Bellevue House is a popular tourist destination. A modern reconstruction of the carriage house (which was destroyed in the 1950s) serves as a visitor's centre, as well as the location for all administration of the site. The house itself is furnished with 1840s period items, although only a few items in the house actually belonged to Sir John A. MacDonald; these items include a baby cradle and a set of books. During the summer, the house itself is staffed by several employees dressed in 1840s period costume, and the grounds are maintained by gardeners in period costume, using solely tools of the era (such as using only a scythe to cut the grass, etc.). Tours and information are offered in both French and English.

[edit] snooping protocol

It is a two state protocol for maintaining cache coherency in a multiprocessor environment, In a snooping system, all caches on the bus monitor (or snoop) the bus to determine if they have a copy of the block of data that is requested on the bus. Every cache has a copy of the sharing status of every block of physical memory it has. Multiple copies of a document in a multiprocessing environment typically can be read without any coherence problems; however, a processor must have exclusive access to the bus in order to write.

There are two types of snooping protocol:

  • write-invalidate: the processor that is writing data causes copies in the caches of all other processors in the system to be rendered invalid before it changes its local copy. The local machine does this by sending an invalidation signal over the bus, which causes all of the other caches to check for a copy of the invalidated file. Once the cache copies have been invalidated, the data on the local machine can be updated until another processor requests it.
  • write-update: the processor that is writing the data broadcasts the new data over the bus (without issuing the invalidation signal). All caches that contain copies of the data are then updated. This scheme differs from write-invalidate in that it does not create only one local copy for writes.

[edit] Working Class Movement Library

By Michael Herbert,

The Working Class Movement Library is a national collection of the history of labour movement in Britain, founded in the mid-1950s by Ruth and Edmund Frow, whose personal and political partnership lasted for over 40 years and led to the creation of this unique and wonderful archive.

[edit] How The Library Began

Eddie Frow was born in Lincolnshire in 1906, the son of tenant farmer. After leaving school he became a toolmaker in the engineering industry. In 1924 he joined the Communist Party of Great Britain and in subsequent years lost many jobs because of his political and trade union activity. During the Depression he was active in the unemployed workers movement in Salford and served four months in Strangeways prison after being badly beaten up by police after leading a march to Salford Town Hall in October 1931, an event that Walter Greenwood included in his novel Love On The Dole and which also featured in the film (somewhat differently from the reality) He found work again on his release, became a leading member of the engineering union and was eventually elected as the full-time Secretary for the Manchester District, a position he held for just under ten years, retiring in 1971.

Ruth Frow was born in 1922 and served in the RAF during the war, going into teaching afterwards. She joined the Communist Party of Great Britain in 1945 whilst canvassing amongst Kent miners during the general election. In the 1950s and 1960s she was active in the peace movement as well as the National Union of Teachers. Ruth finished her teaching career in 1980 as deputy head of a large comprehensive school in Manchester.

Ruth and Eddie first met at a Communist Party school in 1953 and soon merged their lives - and their respective book collections. They began collecting material on the history of the trade union, radical and labour movement, travelling the country in their holidays in a 1937 Morris van and later a caravan, which on each trip they filled with books and pamphlets barely leaving room for themselves to get in.

The result of these journeys was that their house in Old Trafford became treasure trove , with bookshelves in every room as well as banners, emblems, prints and much else all meticulously catalogued by Ruth and Eddie, although they could often locate a volume with bothering to look it up so well acquainted were they . They also began writing books, pamphlets, and articles and were in great demand as lecturers, as well as being active in the Society for the Study of Labour History. News of their library spread and many researchers made their way to Old Trafford, their studies fuelled by regular cups of coffee and Ruths home-made buns.

[edit] The Library Moves to Salford

By 1987 the house was full to overflowing. Fortunately at this point Salford City Council offered to provide a new home for the library, together with full-time library staff, and later that year the entire collection moved to Jubilee House, (a former nurses home opened in 1901) which is situated on the Crescent opposite Salford University. In the seventeen years since the collection has continued to grow and rarely a week goes by without some new material being donated. Often a donor will arrive unannounced with a bag full of wonderful archive material that may have been in a family for several generations. The ceramics trade union CATU has recently agreed to house some of its important archive at the library.

Eddie died in May 1997, just short of his 91st birthday, and he is still much loved and greatly missed. His obituary appeared in the Morning Star, The Guardian, The Times, The Independent and even (this would have amused him) the Daily Telegraph.

[edit] The Collection

The library collection begins in the 1790s, a fascinating period when, in the wake of the American and French revolutions, ideas of democracy and liberty reverberated across Europe. The library has a whole room devoted to Tom Paine, whose book The Rights of Man is one of the founding documents of the British radical tradition.

The rest of the collection covers the two hundred years since and includes collections on trade unions, Chartism, the suffrage movement of the early 1900s and the second wave of feminism in the 1970s, Socialist organisations of every hue, the Co-operative movement, Ireland and the Irish in Britain, anti-fascism, peace movements such as the No Conscription Fellowship and CND as well international issues such as the Spanish Civil War and Vietnam War.

As well as tens of thousands of books and pamphlets some of them unique to the library - the collection also includes banners, posters, pottery, leaflets, newspapers, journals, prints, photographs, videos, LPs and CDs, furniture, union emblems and many other items which document and illuminate the story of the struggle of working people over many decades for social, economic and political justice.

Whilst there are other labour movement historical collection in Britain the library is unique both for the broad range of the collection and the scarce nature of some of the holdings, some of which cannot even be found in the British Library. The library is particular strong in ephemeral material such as tens of thousands of radical pamphlets from the 1790s onwards. Jonathan Miller was filmed in the library for instance for his recent BBC TV series A Brief History of Disbelief, examining rare secularist pamphlets by Thomas Paine, Richard Carlile, George Jacob Holyoake, and Charles Bradlaugh.

[edit] Friends, Volunteers and Trustees

The library has an expanding Friends organisation comprising individuals and trade unions who give financial help to support the librarys activities. We have been very fortunate in attracting a growing band of Volunteers who give up some of their own time to carry out work in the library. There are many tasks still to be done in the collection, which far exceed the time available to the two full-time members of staff. The Volunteers are carrying out vital work as sorting, listing and cataloguing material and writing material for the website, which is a key source of reference information on the collection. and includes an online book catalogue.

The library has been a Trust since the early 1970s. The Trustees comprise both academics and those from a trade union or educational background. The Management Committee meets regularly to plan and implement the strategic development of the library as a resource. We have started producing a quarterly newsletter Shelf Life which is sent to Friends and others and secured funding to produce a CDRom aimed at schools entitled Children of the Industrial Revolution (a free copy was sent to every school in Salford) We also hold regular events such a drama production by Mikron Theatre on the history of Clarion Cycling Club and Open Days for the public.

[edit] DVD

Friends of the library have filmed and edited a 25 minute DVD about the library, made by volunteers who work in the TV industry , which tells the story of how the library was founded by Ruth and Eddie. As well as including archive footage of interviews with Ruth and Eddie from 1987 and 1991 it also features interviews with staff, volunteers and readers. The DVD has been professionally filmed and edited while the commentary has been done by Christopher Eccleston, the Salford-born actor who has worked extensively in film and TV in the past decade, including taking the role of Dr Who in the BBC TV series. It wa smade by John Crumpton and Faisal Quereshi. The DVD is used publicise the library and help raise money.

The library welcomes researchers and visitors. Please phone first to book an appointment as staff time is limited. We also welcome donations of printed material and archive collections.

[edit] Organocatalysis

Organocatalysis is catalysis of a chemical reaction by the use of a small organic molecule (typically <500 Daltons). It is an emerging field within synthetic chemical science, currently under intensive study.

Traditionally, catalysis has been achieved by either enzymes (macromolecular proteinaceous substances, often naturally occurring), transition metals or transition metal complexes. Many such substances are known to catalyse reactions of many types, for many different substrates. The use of small organic molecules (organocatalysts) to facillitate synthetic chemical steps is only just beginning to be exploited, despite being known for many years.

Examples of organocatalysts include; the naturally occuring amino acid, (L)-proline; cinchona alkaloids ; various synthetic imidazolidinones.

Organocatalysis is 'greener' than traditional catalysis as no heavy metal wastes must be purified from chemical products or disposed. They are also more simple to manipulate than transition metal complexes, which tend to be unstable to air or moisture.

Current notable researchers in the field of organocatalysis include Profs. D. MacMillan (CalTech/Princeton, US), B. List (Max-Planck-Institut, Muelheim, Germany) and K.A. Jrgensen (rhus, Denmark).

[edit] Large Vestibular Aqueduct Syndrome

ENLARGED VESTIBULAR AQUEDUCT SYNDROME (Large Vestibular Aqueduct Syndrome)

Name

Enlarged Vestibular Aqueduct Syndrome (EVAS or EVA) may also be known as Large Vestibular Aqueduct Syndrome (LVAS) or just plain Vestibular Aqueduct Syndrome (VAS). Some researchers call it Large Endolymphatic Duct and Sac Syndrome (LEDS). Still others believe it is a milder version of Pendred Syndrome (PDS).

Anatomy

With LVAS the bony channel or aqueduct connecting the cranial space and the inner ear (vestibule) is larger than normal (greater than 1.5mm or roughly 1/17 of an inch in diameter at the midpoint). The normal diameter for the aqueduct averages 0.8 mm, but with LVAS it can be as large as 8.0 mm.

Within this channel is the tube-like endolymphatic duct, filled with a fluid called endolymph. Endolymph naturally flows into the endolymphatic sac situated between the skull and the brain, but with LVAS, it may reverse direction and flow backwards into parts of the inner ear involving hearing and balance causing damage to the inner ear.

The vestibular aqueduct changes shape throughout a childs life from birth to its mature shape at about 3 or 4 years old, going from straight and broad to a J-shape. It can suffer damage over this period, leading to hearing loss. As a result, LVAS is not congenital.

It is not clear to doctors how LVAS or the enlarged endolymphatic sac and duct cause hearing loss but theories abound.

How to Detect It

Evidence of hearing loss as a child is one of the first signs to look for to detect this condition, especially if hearing loss follows any head trauma. CT Scans or MRI Imaging can be used to confirm this condition. The CT Scan can see the aqueduct, while the MRI has the capability of seeing the endolymphatic duct and sac.

Implications

LVAS is estimated to present itself in between 1% and 1.5% of the population with sensorineural hearing loss. This proportion is several percentage points higher among those with sensorineural loss of unknown sensorineural hearing loss. Bilateral LVAS is much more common than unilateral LVAS.

LVAS results from having a specific gene in an overlapping area to that for Pendred Syndrome. The results in one study suggest that around 39% of LVAS cases with sensorineural hearing loss had family members with the same problem. This suggests that the gene for LVAS is an autosomal recessive gene (has to be received from both copies of the gene).

Hearing loss usually occurs after birth as the vestibular aqueduct shapes itself into a more mature form. Most LVAS patients lose their hearing as children, fewer as adolescents and yet fewer as adults. Sensorineural hearing loss is the most common result though conductive hearing loss is also possible (or both). In addition, other deformities such as cochlear deformities often accompany LVAS.

In cases where there is LVAS without other cochlear deformities, the likelihood of hearing loss is much more significant, especially in the higher frequencies. Also, sudden drops in hearing are much more likely. The presence of cochlear deformities in effect safeguards the hearing in the case of LVAS.

It is a bad idea to have LVAS and to have an injury to the head or neck or to be subjected to activities that increase inter-cranial pressure. This may result in post-traumatic Menieres Disease and/or sudden hearing loss. It is suggested that people with LVAS refrain from contact sports, unless the ears that have LVAS are not salvageable or the persons previous history shows no resulting hearing loss from such activities.

Hearing loss may be in sudden bouts or may be gradually progressive. Sudden bouts may occur after minor head injuries or after increased inter-cranial pressure. There is a chance the hearing will recover to normal after a sudden bout, but often in recovers to a new normal at a lower level than before. A sudden hearing loss with LVAS may damage the balance-related part of your ear region, and may result in vertigo and imbalance.

How to Handle LVAS

To control LVAS, take precautions to prevent head injuries, and sudden changes in intra-cranial pressure. In the event that hearing drops to a low level, people with LVAS are generally good candidates for cochlear implants.

[edit] Ask the Pilot

ASK THE PILOT is the title of a long running air travel column at Salon.com, an online news, politics, and arts magazine based in San Francisco. The column is authored by Patrick Smith (born Patrick Santosuosso) a furloughed airline pilot and freelance contributor who lives near Boston.

Smith began writing occasional, unsolicited pieces for Salon in November, 2001, after losing his job in the post-September 11th industry downturn. In June, 2002, he submitted a questions-and-answers format article covering several common queries about air travel. It was well received, and Salon published several follow-ups under the banner "Ask the Pilot," a name coined by Andrew Leonard, the editor at Salon who first discovered and promoted Smith's writing.

Marked by an edgy, literate, and occasionally provocative style, ASK THE PILOT gained a cult following and became a regular fixture on Salon. It has appeared weekly there since 2002, usually running on Fridays, and is read around the world. Topics range beyond technical aspects of flying to encompass everything from airport architecture to global travel. The author has a particular fondness for issues pertaining to terrorism, and for exploring the histories and cultures of the world's airlines. The early Q&A structure was abandoned for a more essay-style format, though he occasionally tackles reader-submitted questions.

A full, regularly updated index of columns can be viewed here: http://dir.salon.com/topics/p_smith/index.html (Salon is a subscription site, but Access to Salon.com is free by watching a short advertisement)

"Salon.com tends to bury the work of Patrick Smith under a pile of political diatribes and pop-culture effluvia," wrote Dan Mitchell in the New York Times. That's too bad, because more people should see his ASK THE PILOT column, in which he shares his expertise on the airline industry, with mild snark and delightfully gratuitous references to 80's alternative rock."

The New York Times' Joe Sharkey calls ASK THE PILOT "Trenchant and insightful."

An adaptation of the column's first two years was published in book form in June, 2004, by Riverhead Books, an imprint of Penguin. Amazon.com awarded ASK THE PILOT its "Best Travel Book of 2004" honors. It sold reasonably well and remains in print, but has suffered from lack of promotion and extremely poor placement in stores. An Italian version, "Chiedio al pilota," has sold over 10,000 copies in Italy.

Patrick Smith hosts a home page with additional information about the column and the book. http://www.askthepilot.com

[edit] Northwest Classen High School

History Over Northwest Classen

  • Northwest Classen was built in 1955 to meet the growing need for educational facilities in northwest Oklahoma City. It was designed after a committee of architects and administrators visited and studied forty schools in seven states. The final architechure plan was made by Hudgins, Thompson, Ball and Associates, and construction was performed by the Builders Company. The building was first opened on September 3, 1955 with 10th, 11th and 12th grades. When the second wing was completed in 1956, 9th graders were also admitted. Enrollment in 1956 was 2002. At the time, Northwest was the premier school facility in Oklahoma City.

The social scene in the early years of the school were dominated by fraternaties (Delta Sigma, Alpha Omega, Kappa Alpha Pi, Phi Lamda Chi, and Phi Sigma Alpha), the O-Club, and the Lancers and Falcons, both of which were pep clubs. Each group had its own car "honks", designated parking places in the school lot, and favorite hang outs. The fraternity boys liked the "Carousel" drive in (now a What-a-burger at 34th & May) while the O-Club boys stayed across the street at the local Dairy Queen (now a car dealership). They are a record of all of the quantities of materials, preliminary works and specifications that are required to complete a building as designed. These documents may be sent, along other tender documents, such as contracts, drawings, etc, to various construction contractors who will, if interested, return a price bid based on the information contained in the bills.

[edit] Florida County Road 535

Florida County Road 535 (also known as CR535 and Winter Garden - Vineland Road is a county maintained highway that generally runs north-south through a rapidly growing area of western Orange County, Florida. The highway begins in the City of Winter Garden, Florida at Florida State Road 50 as a two lane highway with many twists and turns. There is a full intersection with Florida State Road 429, the Western Expressway, at Stoneybrook West Parkway. The road makes a 90-degree turn east at Summerport Village, continues for two more miles as a two lane road and then turns south again and widens to become a four-lane divided highway at Chase Road in Windermere, Florida. Many motorists pull onto the shoulder of County Road 535 late at night to watch the firworks show taking place at the Magic Kingdom of Walt Disney World, which is just to the west of the highway near the intersection with Reams Road. The road runs along the northern property line of Walt Disney World and ends in Lake Buena Vista, Florida at the intersection of Apopka - Vineland Road. At this point the road reverts to state ownership and is renamed Florida State Road 535 as it continues south past Interstate 4.

During 2007 there are plans to shift the northern most section of County Road 535 approximately one-half mile to the east. Just north of the intersection with State Road 429 (Western Expressway)a to-be-built 4-lane highway would route traffic through a planned shopping center called Winter Garden Village at Fowler Groves and connect to a recently-built 4-lane highway currently called Daniels Road. Daniels Road continues north to the intersection of Florida State Road 50. A widening of the 2-lane bridge over Florida's Turnpike will be needed. North of this intersection Daniels Road becomes Dillard Street (Florida State Road 537) and continues into downtown Winter Garden, Florida.
