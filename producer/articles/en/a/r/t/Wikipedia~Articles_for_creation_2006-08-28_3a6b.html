[edit] Odds of winning the lottery

A phrase commonly used to describe the odds of winning the lottery is, "you have a better chance of getting struck by lighting."

Is this true?

Yes. The odds of winning the jackpot in the Lotto 6/49 are 1 in 13,983,816. The odds of getting struck by lighting are about 1 in 2,000,000. Therefore, the statement is mathematically true.


So why is the lottery so popular in the world of gambling? Quick, easy, and cheap. No skill necessary. Whereas blackjack and poker require knowledge of the game, patience, and good math.

"I think the entire music industry is in a seven year slump. Nothing is happening, no one is taking chances. But it doesn’t really bother me because there is so much good music out there if you look for it. It’s really up to the listeners to change things. If there’s any true statement it’s this: 'Repetition is the death of art.'" ~Mark Martucci +++++++++++++++++++++++++++++++++++++++++++++++++++++++++++ Mark Martucci is a songwriter from New Jersey. I actually discovered his music by accident. I was on another musician's message board and randomly came across a link to his website. With the internet now a breeding ground for young musicians, it is pretty overwhelming at times. With Mark Martucci, I got lucky. He is talented and full of promise.

Martucci's music is diverse and charming. If you want a sad song, Mark can provide it. How about a little ambience? Done. In search of catchy tracks with originality? Yup, he can do that too. Who is this guy? Keep reading... +++++++++++++++++++++++++++++++++++++++++++++++++++++++++++ Hugh: I know that you currently live in New Jersey. Were you born there? Was Garden State an accurate portrayal of your home? I think it would have been better with some fresh Martucci jams.

Mark: Well first off, you should know that I'm a huge film buff. I would say that my love of music and film are about on the same level. I try to give my music a kind of narrative and presence of a film, the characters and moods. Strangely enough, i haven't seen Garden State as of yet. I hear it's a great film, but not the best representation of NJ.

I live in the nature-ish area of NJ, so it's not the stinkhole that most people make it out to be. I always need access to nature or I go a little crazy. I lived in Philly for a few years while I was attending the University of the Arts for Multimedia. Cities can get really depressing and on top of it I was going through some tough personal times. But living in this kind of gray microcosm led to the biggest musical inspiration of my life that I continue to draw from even to this day.

As for soundtracks, that is something that would be perfect for me. I work fast and with theory. I recently made a soundtrack for a friend's film. It’s a very black & white album, spectrum wise. Half of it is very melodic and beautiful. The other half is atonal and dissonant, almost unlistenable. But that’s the design of the project. Actually, I’m also the character in the film. I’m jumping into a lake this Saturday to do some underwater photography. The film will be available on my website when it’s finished.

H: When did you start making music on your own? Prior to that, were you a member of any bands?

M: I probably picked up a guitar when I was about 13. I was writing songs before I knew how to play. I still don’t know how to play, so not much has changed. I had a few early bands with names like Willis, Mom’s Plate, and The Phantom Of The Paradise. It was this kind of power pop thing with a moog. We still really didn’t fit in with whatever the local music was…mostly loud, fast, hormone driven music.

I’ve been solo since the high school days, but every once in a while I do projects with friends. Like The Great Britians with Justin Rauschkolb, and most currently a project with Brian Mietz. An interesting thing about that project is that we’ve never been in the same room at any point doing those songs. We just exchange files online, and go back and forth with it. You can hear all these songs on the site.

H: You do a fantastic cover of "Waltz #1," so you are obviously an avid Elliott Smith fan. What other artists are you a big follower of?

M: I’m not really a fan of any new music. There are a few projects I follow like Jim O’Rourke, Wilco, The Frames, Flaming Lips, Elliott and so on. I mostly listen to music from about 40 years ago like Nick Drake, Beach Boys, Beatles, Zombies, The Move, The Bonzo Dog Band, Bob Dylan…back when music was good.

Personally, I think the entire music industry is in a seven year slump. Nothing is happening, no one is taking chances. But it doesn’t really bother me because there is soo much good music out there if you look for it. It’s really up to the listeners to change things. If there’s any true statement it’s this: 'Repetition is the death of art.'

H: It seems that every week or so you post a new song on your online journal where people can discover and comment on your music. I admire your support of sharing free tunes. You seem to be quite a prolific songwriter. What does your recording environment consist of?

M: I’m all for sharing music online. Most bands that think this make most of their money off touring and not from music sales, but for me, I don’t make money from either. I just like people to hear what I’m doing, so they can see a kind of process and can grow along with me. Maybe I could put out a more dissonant experimental record, and people would be right there on the same level and accept, maybe even embrace it. I would love to broaden people’s outlook on music as an artform, not entertainment.

By entertainment, I mean big budget, explosion, aggressive, in your face music that people who don’t really care about music buy. That’s really what it comes down to. Some people actually don’t like music, but everyone needs to own cds. So, what will they buy? Whatever’s easiest to like and what’s designed to make you tap your foot. You can hear the worst song ever on the radio and still drum on the steering wheel. That’s just pop music design at it’s finest, but it works, so why bother to change it? Thus, the slump wins again.

I guess I’m prolific because I need to be to stay interested and excited in what I do. If I was doing the same thing all the time it wouldn’t evolve. It would just be the same thing over and over. I have all these other side projects to inspire my main stuff. Before The Pillows, there wasn’t much ambience in my music. It’s just a string of fountains pouring into the same pool. Plus, it’s just fun to do stuff like The Haunted Houses and The Fellows Employment Agency.

My entire studio consists of a mic, a four channel mixing board, and a computer. Most people think that you need to record in a studio to make a decent product, but I think it has more to do with a knowledge of tone and a decent technique. I’ve been doing this for so long that I understand all my instruments and how to combine them. Layering is also key. Most of my songs range from 80 to 120 tracks. Most of it is subtle stuff, but it needs to be there nonetheless. It really is the little things that you can’t hear, but enrich the product.

H: One of your aliases, The Pillows, consists of your more ambient material, while your self-titled outlet seeks a happy medium between gentle, acoustic numbers and widespread experimentation. Does the nature of your songs depend on what mood you are in? How would you describe your songwriting process?

M: Well, mood is key when I’m writing sad songs. I really need to be upset to write something like that, but most of the time it’s a feeling, an urge to just go downstairs and mess around until I have something. My actual process is always changing. I used to sit down with a guitar and really map everything out, but lately I just set-up the keyboard, hit record, and just play…and build and build. It all just kind of happens. You have a beginning, when it comes to a chord change, it just happens naturally. It’s kind of hard to explain. It’s just all so spontaneous.

H: I know that you have been writing a new album. When are you hoping to have it out by? Will listeners familiar with your work recognize some of the tracks or will the album consist mainly of unreleased material?

M: I’m actually making two totally different albums at once now. One is a rock opera that some people are familiar with. There are songs online from it like “The Flight” and “The Dream.” That has turned into a full album that I’m working on. It’s all related and has a kind of sad, but uplifting sound to it.

The other album is more of a random mix of old and new songs. Some songs have been released before like “Glass” and “Before You Go To Sleep,” but they have very different sounds. [edit] Arturo Belleza Rotor

Arturo Belleza Rotor (born June 7, 1907, died April 9, 1988): Filipino medical doctor, civil servant, musician, writer.

Arturo B. Rotor was born in the Philippines, and attended the University of the Philippines. He graduated simultaneously from the Conservatory of Music and the College of Medicine. He trained further at Johns Hopkins University's medical school, publishing a paper on a rare form of hyperbilirubinaemia now known as "Rotor's syndrome". During World War II, Rotor served as Executive Secretary of the Philippine Commonwealth government-in-exile under Manuel L. Quezon, the Philippine President in exile. In the immediate post-WWII period, he was appointed Secretary of the Department of Health and Welfare. Later, Rotor was director of the University of the Philippines' Postgraduate School of Medicine and was a practising physician through the early 1980s.

Rotor was an internationally respected writer of fiction and non-fiction in English. He is widely considered among the best Filipino short story writers of the twentieth century. He was a charter member of the Philippine Book Guild; its initial publication (1937) was Rotor's "The Wound And The Scar", despite his protests that someone else should have been selected. In 1966, the Philippine government recognized his literary accomplishments by awarding him the Republic Cultural Heritage Award. Arturo B. Rotor's best-known literary works are: The Wound and the Scar (1937), Confidentially, Doctor (1965), Selected Stories from the Wound and the Scar (1973), and The Men Who Play God (1983)

Rotor was also a highly accomplished musician and published music critic. He was an orchid fancier and breeder, a long-time member of the Philippine Orchid Society, and is the namesake of a Vanda orchid species (Vanda merillii var. rotorii).

Rotor died in 1988, survived by his wife Emma Unson, who taught college mathematics and physics. They had no children.

Arturo B. Rotor shared an interest in orchids with his younger brother, Dr. Gavino B. Rotor, Jr. Gavino Rotor took this interest even farther, receiving his Ph.D. from Cornell University on orchid biology and becoming an authority on orchid propagation. The orchid species Rotorara is named after Gavino.

[edit] Long Island is not an Island (US supreme court case 469 U.S. 504 (1985)

In 1985 the U.S. Supreme Court Ruled that Long Island is not really an Island. The courts opinion was that Long Island is simply an extension of the mainland.

This decision came in the RHODE ISLAND AND NEW YORK BOUNDARY CASE, (469 U.S. 504 (1985)). The federal government and states couldn’t agree who controlled the Long Island and Block Island sounds. The states wanted control to regulate shipping and commerce on the sounds. The Key to the case was if Long Island was really an Island. If it was simply an extension of the mainland as the states argued, then under law the sounds are inland bays controlled by the states. If it was truly an island then they would be considered open waters under federal control.

In the end the court ruled in favor of the states. The Court came to this decision by determining that the East River, which separates Long Island from the mainland, was too shallow for safe ship passage until humans widened it. Therefore Long Island is not a Natural Island. Also, Long Island and the adjacent shore also share a common geological history. These are the reasons the court ruled that long island is just an extension of the mainland.

[edit] Spiccato

Spiccato is a bowing technique for string instruments in which the bow bounces lightly upon the string. It is often performed with the tip of the bow. Spiccato was a frequent adornment used during the Baroque period.

The bow is held a short distance above the string and allowed to bounce. This occurs because of the elasticity of the string. It is very important to refrain from squeezing the bow with the right thumb since this will hinder the bounce. The speed with which the spiccato is performed can be changed by bow placement. A the balance point the spiccato will be slow and above the middle of the bow the speed will pick up. Another method of varying the speed is by varying the height of the bow above the string (the higher the bow jumps the longer the time required for the bow to return to the string and therefore the slower will be the resulting spiccato). The character of the spiccato is influenced by the tilt of the bow. When using full hair the bow bounces more and sill have shorter character whereas when the bow is angled the character of the spiccato become more mellow and longer. The ability to perform Spiccato was made much easier after the development of a concave bow (bending toward the hairs) by Francois Tourte (1747-1835)On the Kinematics of Spiccato Bowing..

The etymology of the word includes the Itlaian spiccare, meaning to separate. Also see piccare, Latin for pick.

    Most computer languages are one-dimensional. You start at Point A (the beginning of the program) and simply work your way down the list, doing each operation in turn. Sure, you might skip a few operations once in a while, but you're still just operating in one dimension.

    In 1993, Chris Pressey (recently loony operator of the Esolang list) graced the world with Befunge, the world's first (to my knowledge) two-dimensional programming language. In Befunge, your code is laid out on a page, and execution can move in any direction across the page. This transforms programming into a very visual process. I like.

    But two dimensions just weren't enough for me, now, were they? No. So I created 4DL, the world's first (and I can say that with some confidence) four-dimensional programming language. Why, you ask? Well, I know how much trouble most people have thinking visually in three dimensions (or even two). 4DL turns programming into not only a visual task, but an exercise in visualizing higher-order dimensional spaces--which everyone does in their free time, right? The 4DL Language

    4DL is a very simple language based around a stack of bytes. All operators are single ASCII characters appearing in a four-dimensional grid of arbitrary size. (The size along each dimension must be at least one.)

    In most languages, the instruction pointer always moves in the same direction. In 4DL, the instruction pointer--the flow of execution--can be steered along any axis. Execution begins at coordinate (0,0,0,0), moving in the positive direction along the X axis (that is, to the right within the slice). This direction is referred to below as X+. The full set of directions includes X+, X-, Y+, Y-, Z+, Z-, T+, and T- -- that is, moving "forward" or "backward" along any axis.

    [edit] Pamela Omidyar

    Pamelma Omidyar earned a BS in biology from Tufts University in 1989. After pursuing a masters in molecular biology at University of California, Santa Cruz, she decided to leave the lab for work that provided more immediate impact on people's health and communities. She now works with the Omidyar Network, HopeLab, SFIC and the Jonathan M. Tisch College for Citizenship and Public Service at Tufts University.

    Pam is devoted to alleviating human suffering, focusing specifically on ending human trafficking and slavery, and promoting early childhood development. Inspired by her life-long passion to help children with chronic illness, Pam founded HopeLab, a nonprofit organization that combines the highest standards of scientific research with innovative solutions to improve the health and quality of life for young people with chronic illness. The Network's stated mission is to "enable individual self-empowerment on a global scale" and employ "business as a tool for social good" (inspired by 18th century economic philosopher Adam Smith). Drawing from a $200 million for-profit fund and a $200 million nonprofit fund, the Network invests in areas such as microfinance, technology and community-based initiatives expecting risk-appropriate returns on all for-profit investments. He cites "strangers connecting over shared interests" as the social impact tied to Meetup's ability to be profitable.

    The Omidyars recently donated $100 million to Tufts University to launch the Omidyar-Tufts Microfinance Fund. The fund is intended to spur economic self-empowerment for the poor in developing countries through microfinancing, while demonstrating to other institutional investors the potential of microfinance as commercially viable.     [edit] Tennuvian

    The Tennuvian is a breed of horse derived from the cross between a Tennessee Walking Horse and a Peruvian Paso. It is a fairly recent breed with its official registry, the Tennuvian Horse Registry and Breeders Association, being founded in 1991. According to the THRBA, there are currently less than 200 registered Tennuvian horses in the United States, but that number is increasing at the breed gains in popularity.

    The Tennuvian is a gaited horse, exhibiting a smooth lateral gait that is said to be smoother than the Tennessee Walking Horse, yet lacking much of the termino found in Peruvian Pasos. The Tennuvian is well suited for trail and endurance riding, and makes a spectacular edition to the show ring. The THRBA registers Tennuvians of all colors, and accepts Tennessee Walking Horse and Peruvian Paso cross-breeds that have at least 25% of either breed’s blood. The typical height of the Tennuvian is between 14 and 15 hands.

    According to Paula Bonset, founder of the THRBA, Tennuvians “are graceful yet hard-working horses that have a pleasant disposition and eagerness to do what they are taught.” Tina Ashford, owner of a Tennuvian gelding, also states "The quality and endurance of the Tennuvian makes it an ideal companion horse, as well as a trusted trail or show mount."
