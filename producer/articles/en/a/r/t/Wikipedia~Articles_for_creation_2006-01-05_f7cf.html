[edit] Paleolinguistics

Paleolinguistics is the study of human prehistory through linguistic evidence. It is identical in goals and methods with historical linguistics, differing only in application by concentrating on deeper and older relationships between languages. (The difference between paleolinguistics and historical linguistics is analogous to the difference between paleoanthropology and anthropology in general.) Some applications of paleolinguistics are classification (the organization of languages into language families, and subsequently the organization of families into macrofamilies), reconstruction (reconstituting the lexicon, grammar, and phonology of hypothetical proto-languages), and glottochronology (the estimation of linguistic time-depths on the basis of preserved vocabulary, analogous to the radiocarbon dating of archeology). Some eminent paleolinguists of the past were Karl Bouda, Joseph Greenberg, Karl-Heinrich Menges, V.M. Illich-Svitych, Edward Sapir, Robert Shafer, Sergei Starostin (†2005), Morris Swadesh, Alfredo Trombetti.

[edit] Bio

Stolen Youth is best described as a 5 piece fast, hard Punk band reigning from Adelaide, South Australia. Stolen Youth formed in the year 2000, and didn't play until the first show in 2001. Since then Stolen Youth have been playing shows non-stop and has managed to build a very good reputation in the music scene locally and Australia wide. As a band there is one thing Stolen Youth has always focused on and that's the "DIY" ethic. Stolen Youth play shows from clubs and pubs, to halls and alleyways. Stolen Youth are really passionate about its music and have worked extremely hard to get where they are today.

As a guide, some of Stolen Youth's main influences include bands like Propagandhi, MYC, Good Riddance, Strike Anywhere, Strung Out and many others. Stolen Youth have three releases, all through independent record label Two Bucks Records. The first one is an EP that was released in 2003 called "Smurfer", which is a 5-track cd that was recorded in two days. This release stayed in the independent music charts for a number of weeks, debuting at number 2 and remaining there for weeks to come. The next release was a Demo Tape called "Demo 2004" which was a limited release to show the punters where the band was heading with their music. The latest release has been the best to date. The title of this EP is "When Spiders Unite" and has done really well since its release. "When Spiders Unite" has sat at number 1 for the first 12 weeks and looks to remain in the charts for a while to come.

As well as playing with numerous local and interstate bands, Stolen Youth has also had the privilege of supporting international acts such as Good Riddance, Lagwagon, Ignite, Champion, Madball, The Bleeders, Anti-Flag, Lawrence Arms, Suicide Machines, No Use For A Name and Rambo. Stolen Youth has also had the privilege of being on the road and touring around Australia and playing a number of cities such as Melbourne, Sydney, Newcastle, Canberra and Tasmania plus other big shows like Resist Records show case, All Systems Down Fest and more.

Stolen Youth is a very politically charged band. As a band, Stolen Youth have very strong beliefs and it is a main focus when the band writes music. Because of this, Stolen Youth has managed to gain a lot of respect from a large crowed which has made them a strong driving force in the Adelaide music scene.

[edit] National Playlist

I'd like to use my first post to introduce myself, and to comment on a CBC Radio One show called The National Playlist hosted by Jian Ghomeshi.

First of all, my name is Stacey. I'm from Fort McMurray, Alberta (lucky me, I know) and plan to leave very shortly. I hope to attend the University of Toronto in the fall, if I am accepted. Some of my interests are music, writing, reading, and more recently, politics. (Thanks in part to The Hour, on CBC news world, hosted by George Stroumboulopoulos) Oh, and the CBC, though I'm sure you've noticed that by now.

The topic that I'd like to talk about in this post is the National Playlist. http://www.cbc.ca/nationalplaylist/ (Known as NP from now on)

I started listening to NP sometime in the fall of 2005, and was immediately hooked. Jian's knowledge and cleverness, combined with the opinions of his panelists is constantly entertaining and interesting, and the music is phenomenal. This broadcast has opened my eyes to bands and musicians that I may never had heard otherwise. And it made Apple some money too. My Christmas wish list this year? About 65 books and an iPod nano.

The NP is the top ten songs for this great nation (Canada) updated every week. Each week, Jian brings in three new panelists, and they each bring two songs to the platform. They present their songs, and argue why it deserves a spot on the list, and I believe that Jian has begun presenting a viewer pick each week in lieu of one of his own picks. The listeners go onto the NP website, and vote on which songs simply have to stay. By the end of the week, four songs have to go, and new ones are added in.

I can say that I'm truly grateful for this program. And while I don't always agree with Jian or the panelists, I can send in my rant through the programs website. Songs that I had already known were given new life when argued by their representative on the show. Jian and the panelists are so knowledgeable when it comes to music, and they notice the smallest, most minute details, things that I may never have picked up on my own. It feels like my birthday when I discover something new about a song I've loved for years.

The only thing that I would change about the NP, is, in addition to airing on CBC Radio One, have it as a podcast! Then, it's ok if I miss it. I can simply download it from iTunes, throw it on my nano, and listen in delight any time I like; on the plane, driving in my car, while reading. It would be a thing of pure beauty.

Either way, the NP is a quality show, and would highly recommend it to any music lover, regardless of age.

[edit] Awakening Generation

In [[Generations(book}|Generations]] Strauss and Howe identify the group of people born in the first quarter of the 18th Century (1701-1824) as the Awakening Generation, a group that had the signficance of being the first generation within the British colonies of North America to be mostly born in America, the first to describe themselves as Americans, and the oldest to live in significant numbers in the new United States of America.

Born largely to late-wave Glorious and to early-wave Enlightener parents. they grew up in the relatively stable, unthreatening and prosperous times of the early 18th century as an Idealist archetype. Like other Idealist generations, they tended to develop tendencies of religious and political radicalism early in life, but eventually became the principled leaders of the secular world at the time of the great Crisis Era that they would know late in life -- in their example the American War of Independence.

[edit] United States Indian Police

The United States Indian Police (USIP) were organized in 1880 by the Union Agency in Muskogee, Indian Territory, to police the Five Civilized Tribes. The USIP recruited many of their policemen from the ranks of the existing Indian Lighthorsemen. Unlike the Lighthorse who were under the direction of the individual tribe, the USIP was under the direction of the the Indian Agent assigned to the Union Agency. Many of the US Indian policemen were given Deputy US Marshal commissions that allowed them to cross juridictional boundaries and also to arrest non-Indians.

In 1886 two Indians killed Sam Sixkiller who was the popular Captain of the US Indian Police and a Deputy US Marshall comissiond by the Judicial District of Western Arkansas. After the killers escaped indictment by the tribes, Congress passed a law (24 Stat., 463.) giving the district Federal courts juridiction over any Indian who commited a crime against a federally appointed Indian policeman or United States deputy marshal.

==Other Indian police==

There were basically two other types of policemen on the reservations:

  • Tribal police.
Several Indian tribes replaced hereditary chiefs with constitutional governments. These tribes hired policemen under a number of different titles—sheriffs, constables, regulators, lighthorsemen, etc.—to enforce tribal laws.
  • Indian agency police.
Many tribes had no recognizable governments and therefore no tribal laws. On these reservations, the Indian agent assigned to the tribe hired Indian police from among tribal members to effect law and order according to Federal, agency, and treaty rules. These were considered federally appointed policemen. The Indian police that killed Sitting Bull were of this kind.

[edit] Trimble (nautical)

The term "trimble" is believed to be a phonetic condensation of the Saxon phrase "Terembel", referring to the mythical artifact of Galatian lore (Terem's Bell or The Terem Bell). Through an unknown path of transmission, English mariners of the colonial period began to employ this term to describe the brief comic outbursts amidst otherwise unbroken periods of doom or despair. When the First Vicar of H.H.R.N, Thomas Waddington, introduced the mystic tradition of Trimblism in his tome "Kaluma! Upanishad of War" he interpreted explicitly as " ...a divine chortle. A moment of vision and disgrumblance. Such as a long awaited bloviation or alieviation of the bowels or loins." Waddington's demise after attempting to retake the island of Anatahan from the British in whose service he had previously helped conquer led to the forbidden status of the practice in the Royal Navy,\. American privateers, however, some of whom had previously been held prisoner by the Royal Navy, began using the term as a perjoritive reference to British officers.

Take for example the following excerpt from the rutters of American Captain Johannes Jornmeinnes of the frigate Locust Eater:

The prisoners we had ta'en that day we good men and true and put the back into the rope. Ho'ever the trimble who had the honour and the freedom of my own Cabin and Company did suffer me and my men with the most rank abuses unbecoming a gentleman of his stature. I had previously been under the mista'em impression that trimbles were of the landed class of gentry, yet this man's tongue had clearly seen the darkest corners of the darkest tavern wenches darkest folds. And so we see how a trimble truly a trimble in his heart will always be.

The term is all but forgotten today but is used in some regions of the Southern United States as a slang term for the salt-water taffey popular at many seaside resorts, perhaps alluding to British officers alleged fondness of sweets.

[edit] The Power running play

Power requires a great deal of timing in the backfield and a lot of physical play by the linemen. Power is only run to the strongside of an offense, usually on the side of the formation where the TE is lined up. On the playside (strongside in this case), the center, guard, tackle and tight end will all block down, that is to block the defender closest to them on the inside. For the center, this generally means blocking back on a DT or making a combo block on the NT to one of the linebackers. This "blocking down" creates great angles for the offensive linemen and allows them to make their blocks relatively easily. However, it creates a problem at the end of the LOS, where the last man is left unblocked...but not for long.

At the snap, the FB will attack the defensive man on the end of the line, usually a DE or LB, with the intention of sealing him to the outside, creating a crease between himself and where the TE was originally lined up at the snap. The weakside guard will pull through this hole and act as a lead blocker for the RB who will be reading the guard's block.

Power is generally good for a few yards, but it can be defeated if the FB makes a poor block or the guard cannot get in position quickly enough to make a block for the RB.

[edit] Mahoosuc Mountains

The Mahoosuc Mountains, a northern range of the White Mountains, straddle the border between New Hampshire and Maine. Their highest peak, 4,180 foot Old Speck Mountain, is the 4th highest peak in Maine. Substantial parts of the range are publicly owned as parts of the National Parks Service Appalachian Trail corridor and Grafton Notch State Park in Maine.

The Appalachian Trail traverses the main ridge of the Mahoosucs between Berlin, NH and Grafton Notch. Mahoosuc Notch, considered one of the most difficult sections of the Appalachian Trail, cuts a deep cleft in the middle of the range between Mahoosuc and Fulling Mill Mountains.

[edit] Ben Grauer

Ben Grauer was born in Staten Island, N.Y. in 1908. Grauer was a child actor in the 1920s, both in film and on Broadway. Among his credits were roles in films directed by D.W. Griffith. After graduating from Townsend Harris High School, he received his BA from City College in 1930. Ben Grauer started as an actor in radio, but soon became part of the broadcasting staff at the National Broadcasting Company. He was the main announcer for the NBC Symphony Broadcasts on radio and TV from 1937 till 1954. Arturo Toscanini said he was his favorite announcer. Grauer's other close personal associations in broadcasting included Walter Winchell, Eleanor Roosevelt and of course, Arturo Toscanini. Starting in 1932, Grauer covered the Olympic Games, presidential inaugurations, and international events. He married interior designer Melanie Kahane in 1954 and retired from NBC in 1973. For most, Ben Grauer is best remembered as the NBC radio and TV host of the annual New Year's Eve broadcasts Live from Times Square.

For decades, these broadcasts were part of the NBC Tonight Show, where he worked not only with Johnny Carson, but his predecessors. During his 40 year broadcast career, he hosted over half a dozen TV programs on NBC including game shows, quiz shows, concerts and news programs. His career at NBC ended in 1973. In the decade before his death, Grauer also collected material for a projected history of prices and pricing, with special attention to book prices. He was active in several professional journalistic organizations as well as the Grolier Club. He died in New York City in 1977.

[edit] Corliss Engine

The Corliss engine os a type of steam engine developed by George H. Corliss. Prior to the development of the steam turbine, Corliss engines were the most efficient type of steam engine and the preferred type of engine for large constant-speed stationary power applications. The most famous Corliss engine of the 19th century was the central exhibit in Machinery Hall at the great Centennial Exposition of 1876.

A double-acting single cylinder Corliss engine was characterized by the use of four rotary valves,one exhaust valve and one admission valve at each end of the cylinder. An ingenious cam in the linkage for the admission valves controlled the cut-off of the admission valves. This allowed the admission valves to admit only a brief puff of high pressure steam when the engine was operating at low load, or a long blast of steam at high load, with automatic regulation by a Governor.

Large Corliss engines are preserved today at a number of museums, including the Henry Ford Museum.

Dennis Hall is one of the United States most decorated athletes. Dennis is a 10 time US National Champion, World Champion, and 3 time USA Olympian. This includes a Silver Medal at the 1996 Atlanta Games. In addition, he is an assistant coach at the University of Wisconsin-Stevens Point.

During his high school years he was also a three-time Junior National Greco Champion and World Team member. He was Wisconsin's first Dream Team All-American.

After a brilliant prep career, Dennis Hall attended the University of Wisconsin on a full scholarship for one year. He realized that in order for him to reach his full potential as an international Greco wrestler he would need to train differently than the collegiate athlete. He trained, traveled and competed with the US Greco team. He was named USA Wrestling Man of the Year in 1995! [edit] Locking (dance)

A form of underground dance started by Don Campbellock during the early 70s in California. Its style is easily recognizable because of the signature "funky chicken" inspired elbow flapping, pointing, and wrist rolls. The uninitiated often confuse this style with popping or pop n'lock or poplocking. The latter two words do not refer to any particular form of dance but were used because popping and locking originated so closely to one another in time and space that the public had trouble discerning the two.

[edit] 145th Street (IND Eighth Avenue Line station)

Seven tracks on two levels. The upper level is used by the A and C trains and has four tracks and two island platforms. Tile band is gold with black border. The station had a full mezzanine but the central portion is now used as a police precinct.

The lower level is used by the B and D trains has two island platforms and three tracks. The tile border here is gold with black and the name tablets are black with gold border. The northbound platform is extra wide, such that the three trackways line up directly underneath ones above. Escalators lead up from this level to the mezzanine, bypassing the upper level platforms. This is the last stop for the B during midday and early evenings; at this time, it lays up on the middle track. During rush hours, D trains stop on that track since it runs express on the IND Concourse Line in peak direction and local stations are served by the B

This station is useful for reaching the northern parts of the City College campus. The station and the streets leading to the college are well policed.
