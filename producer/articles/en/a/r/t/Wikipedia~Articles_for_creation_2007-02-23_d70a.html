“Everything Happens To Me” (original Italian title: Chissà perché... capitano tutte a me) is a children's comedy movie starring Bud Spencer and child actor Cary Guffey that was released in cinemas in 1980. It is a direct sequel of the 1979 movie The Sheriff and the Satellite Kid.

[edit] Plot

Following the events of The Sheriff and the Satellite Kid, Sheriff Hall and H-725 (here named Charlie Warren) still get no rest from the military: because the little alien has not yet grasped the meaning of keeping a low profile, they are constantly on the move, and H-725’s father has to pick them out of a tight spot too many times already.

Hall and Charlie finally arrive at the city of Munroe (probably Munroe Fall, Ohio), where petty crime, from vandalism to armed robberies, is running rampant, moreso because the town currently has no sheriff. Charlie pushes Hall into staying in this place, and somewhat reluctantly Hall takes up the job in this chaotic place, with the help of the local radio jock and City Mayor Howard (Ferruccio Amendola). Soon the riff-raff in town learns not to underestimate the new sheriff as he metes out hard-hitting arguments to stay out of trouble.

However, the local thugs are the smallest of Hall’s problems. A task force of hostile alien invaders are preparing to enslave mankind with the use of hypno-wave devices and advanced androids; already they control the local authorities and military installations. Charlie immediately senses the true nature of these individuals, however, and after much persuasion convinces his big friend to look into the matter. But this arouses the suspicion of the alien leader (Claudio Undali); he has Hall arrested for snooping into matters of national security and Charlie kidnapped. Hall manages to sneak out of prison, and after Howard has told him what happened to Charlie, the sheriff decides to take up the fight. Through the public announcement Howard applies to the citizens of Munroe for help, and the thugs and hooligans whom Hall has previously thrashed join forces with the sheriff, just as the alien leader executes the final stage of his plan to take over Munroe by sending the local state police force to the El Dorado festival and round up the townspeople. Hall and his helpers engage the officers in a massive fist-fight, though, and begin to win the upper hand until the alien chief decides to use his hypno-wave machine. The rioters are quickly subjugated – except for the sheriff, who wanders as if in trance to the military base which serves as the alien’s headquarters.

As Hall arrives there, the alien leader and his androids discover that their hypno-waves have no effect on the sheriff (due to an earlier treatment by Charlie’s wonder device. The androids are sent against Hall, but the sheriff’s fists prove to be tougher than they are, and soon the androids’ neural-nets end up completely scrambled. The alien leader tries to activate a self-destruct device, but the sheriff takes a swing at him and he shatters (probably to avoid censorship). Charlie is released from captivity and happily rejoins the sheriff.

However, as the town celebrates their rescuers, once again the military arrives to try and apprehend Charlie once more. Fed up with the constant pestering, Hall and Charlie take off into space with the old-timer automobile to join Charlie’s family. But as soon as they have left the atmosphere, a small inconvenience makes Hall grumble once more: “Why does everything happen to me!”

[edit] Wally the Very Fast Warthog

Archived discussion follows below. Please do not modify it.
This request for creation has been reviewed. The reviewer comments appear below the article text.
If your request was declined and you want to make changes and try again, copy and paste your article into a new submission and make your changes there.

A little known fact about the classic Sega video game series, Sonic the Hedgehog, is its predecessor, "Wally the Very Fast Warthog". The idea was coined by a man named Thomas Golosquer in the year 1867. The concept for "Wally" was slightly different from the final product, but the essentials were all there: A small mammal that can curl up in a ball and travel at incredible speeds while attempting to roll through golden rings. Golosquer's concept was attempted prior to the invention of the television, but simultaneous to the discovery of photoconductivity, made by Golosquer's contemporary and close friend, Willoughby Smith, whose name was partial inspiration for "Wally".

Golosquer's concept was enacted without the use of any interactive media and was instead done with an actual warthog, whose actual name was ironically Stanley, and metal hoops that had been painted gold. The game was intended to teach children the finer points of training a wild animal to roll through hoops as fast as possible. Golosquer's idea was later adapted by Sega to create Sonic the Hedgehog. They opted to change from warthog to hedgehog because warthogs don't do summersaults very well (a problem that was found commonplace in Golosquer's original design).

Ever since the debut of Sonic, Golosquer's ancestors have been trying to fight for some form of compensation, as his ideas were never even so much as credited by Sega. As of yet, the courts have not found enough proof to honor the family's demands.

[edit] Vlisco Real Dutch Wax Prints

Vlisco was founded on 15 August 1846 in Helmond. Over the years the textile factory has been extended on a regular basis. The products were sold on the domestic market and abroad. In 1852 the company started exporting batik prints to the former Dutch East Indies. Vlisco started to export to Africa in 1909. The Africans admired the batik pieces called Real Dutch Wax Prints for their bright colours, appealing designs and special crackling effects. Today Vlisco is omni-present in Africa with its label Vlisco Real Dutch Wax Prints.

Diastema refers to dental charecteristics in hominids and hominoids. When looking downward at a hominoid jaw bone, it is easy to see a sqaured off shape. This sharply contrasts hominid jaw bones which have a more rounded appearence, almost looking like a parabola. Monkeys exhibit diastema as well, with a V-shaped jaw. These differences show scientists the differences in lifestyle with relation to food sources as well as the abundance of food. It can also charecterise social patterns.

[edit] Ken Herndon

Ken Herndon, 49, is the Jefferson County (KY) Judge Executive, a Democrat, and the highest constitutional officer in Kentucky's most populous county, home to Louisville. He was first elected in 2002 and reelected in 2006. He succeeded Rebecca Jackson, a Republican, who went on to run (unsuccessfully) for governor of Kentucky in 2003. Herndon is a native of Paducah (McCracken County), Kentucky. He is a graduate of Lone Oak High School and Murray State University. In 1991, he helped organize the Fairness Campaign in Louisville, which supported passage of several civil rights initiatives in Louisville and Jefferson County. Those efforts were successful, as both former governments (prior to Merger) and the current government, the Louisville-Jefferson County Metro, have passed such laws. Herndon has also been an unsuccessful candidate for 34th District State Representative, 5th Ward Alderman, and 35th District State Senator. He is also involved in other human and civil rights issues, the Old Louisville Neighborhood Council, and works as Executive Director of the Louisville Downtown Management District. His current term expires on December 31, 2010.

Carlo Alessandro Pierogli (1720-1801) was the eldest son of the composer Giovanni-Battista Pierogli and was a composer in his own right. He was born in Venice, but spent several years of his life in England before returning to Venice with his mother and younger brother.

Life

Carlo Alessandro moved to his mother's home in Venice in 1729. He became a choirboy at San Marco and had violin lessons as well, reputedly with Vivaldi. Though he is usually remembered as a composer, he wrote relatively little music, all of which has luckily survived. Carlo entered the priesthood in 1737, and was for some time the director at several small churches. However, the priesthood did not suit him, and he left in 1741, seeking for posts in Rome, Florence, and Vienna. He eventually ended up in London, where he eventually became good friends with J.C. Bach. He left London in 1768, going to visit his father in Germany before seeking a post in Vienna. He found one, working for the Count Josef von Stuszen, the ambassador to Switzerland. However, the count died in 1771, and his successor did not like music. Carlo was once again without employment. Carlo eventually returned to Venice, only to find his mother deathly ill. She died three days later. Carlo was thus forced to take guardianship of his younger brother, Lucio Amadeo. He decided to sell their house in Venice, and moved to Florence. There, Carlo was able to find employment writing church music. He visited Vienna again in 1783, where he met the young Mozart and the great Haydn. He returned to Florence yet again without a post. He died in 1800 of old age.

[edit] Spear SAT Solver

{{lowercase}}

Spear is a fast SAT solver. It inherits some features from HyperSAT as well as from Minisat. Spear exposes numerous search parameters on the command line, which makes it easy to tweak the parameters according to the problem being solved. Several predefined sets of search parameters are available. Some sets were found by automatic parameter optimization based on iterated local search. Spear is developed and maintained by Domagoj Babić at University of British Columbia. Automatic parameter optimization was done by Frank Hutter. The solver was written in C++ and it is available as binary on Linux and Cygwin. Other platforms might be supported later as well. Spear is free for non-commercial use.

[edit] Design for Manufacturability (PCB)

Design for manufacturability (DFM for short. Often referred to as "design for manufacturing") is a design methodology intended to ease the manufacturing process of a given product. In the PCB design process DFM leads to a set design guidelines that attempt to ensure manufacturability. By doing so, probable production problems may be addressed during the design stage.

Ideally, DFM guidelines take into account the processes and capabilities of the manufacturing industry. Therefore, DFM is constantly evolving.

As manufacturing companies evolve and automatize more and more stages of the processes, these processes tend to become cheaper. DFM is usually used to reduce these costs. For example, if a process may be done automatically by machines (ie: SMT component placement and soldering), such process is likely to be cheaper than doing so by hand.

[edit] Apothecary Hymns

Founded by New York City native Alex Stimmel in 2004, Apothecary Hymns began life as a one-man band. Stimmel played all the instruments on his debut single, a limited-edition picture sleeve entitled "Half of What is Seen." Released on Jugendstil Records, Stimmel began distributing the 7" single to labels, and found interest from the Chicago-based independent Locust Music (Josephine Foster, Sir Richard Bishop, Espers, &c). The debut Apothecary Hymns album, Trowel & Era was released in 2005 as Locust 69. Trowel & Era, too, was recorded and produced by Stimmel, who again handled all the playing and singing. All songs were written by Stimmel, except for "All True Love Is Happiness" (co-written with Nick Ackerman) and "The Human Abstract", which is a setting of the William Blake poem.

The music of Apothecary Hymns is best categorized as psychedelic. The lyrics are poetic and thoughtful, and often relate tales rather than relying on first-person narratives. There are connections both to acid-folk and harder-edged psychedelia, with more overt fuzz-toned guitars and rock execution.

Live, Apothecary Hymns varies between solo performances by Stimmel (often on electric guitar) and full-band shows, usually as a rock quartet (piano, guitar, bass, & drums). Members currently include Rob Fellman (bass), Dan Lubell (Keyboards) and Mark Mickelthwaite (drums).

A second album is being recorded in 2007 for release on Locust later in the year.
