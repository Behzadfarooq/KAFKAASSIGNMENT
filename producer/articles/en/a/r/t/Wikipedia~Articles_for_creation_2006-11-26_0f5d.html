Colour Revolt is an Oxford, Mississippi-based indie rock band. They first called their group Fletcher, but later changed it to Colour Revolt, a name taken from Edwin Abbott's 19th century social commentary Flatland. The band's plans to record its self-titled debut EP in a friend's basement in Jackson, Mississippi in August 2005 were disrupted by Hurricane Katrina, which filled the space with evacuees. Instead, they worked in the living room of the owner of the local Esperanza Plantation record label, which issued the self-titled disc on a small-scale basis in December 2005. Colour Revolt undertook a five-week national tour opening for Brand New in the summer of 2006. Tiny Evil, an imprint of Interscope Records, picked up the EP and gave it a national release on October 3, 2006. Members include lead singer, guitarist, and keyboard player Jesse Coppenbarger; guitarist Jimmy Cajoleas; guitarist, keyboard player, and singer Sean Kirkpatrick; drummer and singer Len Clark; and bassist Drew Mellon.

[edit] James O'Brien (journalist, writer, musician)

This request for creation has been reviewed. The result is below.


Boston's James O'Brien began writing early in life. In 1994 he left college for a year and lived in Framingham, Mass., where he worked in a gargoyle studio three days a week and wrote four days a week. During that time, he published his first short story "Jesus and May Ellen Frost" in Haunts magazine (issue #33).

In 1995, O'Brien returned to school, attending Clark University in Worcester, Mass. as a screen studies/philosopy major. He left the university in 1996 when finances were exhausted. That fall, he re-enrolled at Framingham State College and finished his Bachelor's of Arts in English by spring 1997.

Boston-based sculptor Jason Karakehian introduced O'Brien to acoustic singer/songwriter environs in 1996, at which point O'Brien switched his writing focus from fiction to song. In 1997, O'Brien moved to Mission Hill in Boston and has lived in the city since.

O'Brien was a touring songwriter from 1998 to 2004. He shared stages with underground luminaries such as Dan Bern, Freedy Johnston and Howie Day ... and with cult heroes like Tanya Donnelly, Kay Hanley and Juliana Hatfield. In those six years he released three albums and logged hundreds of thousands of miles on the road.

O'Brien's third album, "Church of the Kitchen Sink" garnered critical praise and his prominence on the national political folk scene prompted rock journalists to start asking quesitons, post 9/11. O'Brien played a concert in New York City at the Sidewalk Cafe on Avenue A on Sept. 12, 2001. Following the concert, he walked through lower Manhattan, observing the aftermath of the terrorist attacks of the previous day. O'Brien's identity as a political songwriter was the focus of interviews about post-9/11 songwriting in the Boston Globe, the Boston Phoenix and National Public Radio station WBUR 90.9 in Boston.

Political performance was not always a boon for O'Brien's career. From 1999 to 2001, O'Brien appeared frequently on WERS 88.9 at Emerson College in Boston. In 2001, prior to an interview, an on-air DJ informed O'Brien that he could not to discuss politics during the program. O'Brien did not comply with instructions. It was his last interview with the station's morning program staff.

In other arenas, O'Brien was tapped to review Bob Dylan's return to the Newport Folk Festival for Voice of America in 2002. In 2003, O'Brien organized a showcase concert, "Songs Not Bombs," in Nashville, Tenn. at the 15th Annual Folk Alliance conference. During that showcase, O'Brien joined singer/songwriter Karaugh Brown and Grammy award winner Bill Morrissey on stage. The three musicians collaboratively performed Dylan's "The Times They Are A' Changing," each singing a verse. In the audience: Newport Folk Festival organizers and Harvard Square's Club Passim alumnae who'd worked with Dylan in the early 1960s.

Back in Boston, in 2003, O'Brien continued to work with the songwriting scene as a documentarian, writing for Club Passim's mini-magazine, The Listening Room. He interviewed 1960s Folk Revival icon Jim Kweskin upon the release of Kweskin's first album in 20 years. The interview was published in The Listening Room in February and Kweskin's label, Blix Street, asked for and was granted to use the interview as part of their promotional campaign for the new record, titled "Now and Again."

From 1998 to 2005, O'Brien hosted the open stage at Club Passim.

During that time he watched a small group of Boston songwriters emerge to form the Counterfolk scene. Later, O'Brien would connect Counterfolk artist Michael Epstein with Boston music producer Jordan Tishler (who produced O'Brien's albums in 1999 and 2002). Tishler and Epstein would go on to record "Her Brilliant Fifteen," under Epstein's new moniker The Motion Sick. With that album, Boston's first Counterfolk band would receive national attention in spring 2006, in Spin Magazine. Additonally, O'Brien's writing in 2001 in The Listening Room about Counterfolk and its precedent, Antifolk in New York City, prompted public response from New York-based Antifolk founder Lach. Later in 2001, Lach was booked to play Club Passim as a representative of the Antifolk sound.

In the winter of 2005, O'Brien brought his touring career to a close. He now plays the occasional solo concert, the most recent of these was Sept. 1, 2006.

Journalism became a supplemental income for O'Brien in the winter of 2005 and 2006. After freelancing for several online sports and music magazines, he was hired to write for the Norwood Times in Norwood, Mass.

O'Brien became editor of the Norwood Times and the Canton Times in spring 2005.

James O'Brien is currently editor at Boston's Bulletin Newspapers. He works specifically with the West Roxbury Bulletin, the Hyde Park Bulletin and the City Edition of the newspaper which covers the neighborhoods of Jamaica Plain, Allston-Brighton and the North End. O'Brien also reports for all of the Bulletin newspapers.

On Oct. 31, 2006, James O'Brien opened www.Cinescare.com, an academic/film criticism site devoted to the study and promotion of horror cinema as a valuable drama.

James O'Brien is currently writing two books: A ghost story, Ghosts Upstairs, and biography of civil rights lawyer, Christopher King, titled American Lawyer.

He is married to singer/songwriter Karaugh Brown.

[edit] Snowball Derby

70.191.197.61 06:16, 25 November 2006 (UTC) The Snowball Derby is a 300-lap super late model stock car automobile race held annually at the half-mile Five Flags Speedway in Pensacola, Florida. The race has been contested every year since 1968 and is always run on the first weekend in December. The race has a reputation for attracting some of the bigger names in stock car racing due to the fact that it is run after most tracks and touring series have concluded their seasons. Due to the all-star nature of the race, the Snowball Derby is considered by many to be the most prestigious race in late model racing.

Owgei outlines the nursing care to be provided to a patient. It is a set of actions the nurse will implement to resolve nursing problems identified by assessment. The creation of the plan is an intermediate stage of the nursing process. It guides in the ongoing provision of nursing care and assists in the evaluation of that care.


Characteristics of the Owgei It focuses on actions which are designed to solve or minimize the existing problem. It is a product of a deliberate systematic process. It relates to the future. It is based upon identifiable health and nursing problems. Its focus is holistic.

Elements of the plan In the USA, the nursing care plan consists of a NANDA nursing diagnosis with related factors and subjective and objective data that support the diagnosis, nursing outcome classifications with specified outcomes (or goals) to be achieved including deadlines, and nursing intervention classifications with specified interventions.


The nursing process Care plans are formed using the nursing process. First the nurse collects subjective data and objective data, then organizes the data into a systematic pattern, such as Marjory Gordon's functional health patterns. This step helps identify the areas in which the client needs nursing care. Based on this, the nurse makes a nursing diagnosis. As mentioned above, the full nursing diagnosis also includes the relating factors and the evidence that supports the diagnosis. For example, a nurse may give the following diagnosis to a patient with pneumonia that has difficulty breathing: Ineffective Airway Clearance related to tracheobronchial infection (pneumonia) and excess thick secretions as evidenced by abnormal breath sounds; crackles, wheezes; change in rate and depth of respiration; and effective cough with sputum.

After determining the nursing diagnosis, the nurse must state the expected outcomes, or goals. A common method of formulating the expected outcomes is to reverse the nursing diagnosis, stating what evidence should be present in the absence of the problem. The expected outcomes must also contain a goal date. Following the example above, the expected out come would be: Effective airway clearance as evidenced by normal breath sounds; no crackles or wheezes; respiration rate 14-18/min; and no cough by 01/01/01.

After the goal is set, the nursing interventions must be established. This is the plan of nursing care to be followed to assist the client in recovery. The interventions must be specific, noting how often it is to be performed, so that any nurse or appropriate faculty can read and understand the care plan easily and follow the directions exactly. An example for the patient above would be: Instruct and assist client to TCDB (turn, cough, deep breathe) to assist in loosening and expectoration of mucous every 2 hours.

The evaluation is made on the goal date set. It is stated whether or not the client has met the goal, the evidence of whether or not the goal was met, and if the care plan is to be continued, discontinued or modified. If the care plan is problem-based and the client has recovered, the plan would be discontinued. If the client has not recovered, or if the care plan was written for a chronic illness or ongoing problem, it may be continued. If certain interventions are not helping or other interventions are to be added, the care plan is modified and continued.

There are also care plans written for "at risk" problems, as well as "wellness" care plans. These follow a similar format, only designed to prevent problems from happening and continue or promote healthy behavior.

[edit] Uses


The Contraharmonic mean is useful in signal processing. For example, a contraharmonic filter can remove Salt and pepper noise from a picture.
In addition it is a useful descriptive statistic since it is sensitive to the larger values in a set.
An analysis of the arithmetic, harmonic and contraharmonic means can yield information about the relative importance of very large or very small values.

[edit] Portal Bridge

Portal Bridge is on bridge on Amtrak's Northeast Corridor over the Hackensack River in New Jersey. As it is only double-tracked, it currently operates at capacity. Consequently, along with the Hudson River Tunnels, it is one of the largest chokepoints for New Jersey Transit and Amtrak trains in their respective systems. New Jersey Transit is currently studying how to increase capacity on Portal Bridge.
