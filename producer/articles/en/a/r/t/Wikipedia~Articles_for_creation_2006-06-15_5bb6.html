[edit] Equidistant Letter Sequence

Equidistant Letter Sequence (ELS)is a method by which purportedly meaningful messages have been extracted from written text. To obtain an ELS from a text, choose a starting point (in principle, any letter) and a skip number, also freely and possibly negative. Then, beginning at the starting point, select letters from the text at equal spacing as given by the skip number. For example, the bold letters in this sentence form an ELS form the word SAFEST. (The skip is -4. Spaces and punctuation are ignored.)

Often more than one ELS related to some topic can be displayed simultaneously in an ELS letter array. This is produced by writing out the text in a regular grid, with exactly the same number of letters in each line, then cutting out a rectangle.

[edit] Agnolim

Agnolim was formed in Ankara - Turkey in 2001 by some dedicated amateur musicians of whom most of were tango dancers. The band plays classical Argentine Tango tunes as well as the modern day tango songs. The band members are friends among the first tango dancers of Ankara.

The group made several live performances in Istanbul and Ankara. They did not release an official studio album. However, some of their studio and live recordings have reached many audiences.

When Agnolim was in search for a name, the group members came up with different ideas all including the word "tango". They looked for a simple, easy to pronounce and remember name that would always be kept in mind of the audiences. So, eventually "Agnolim" was chosen. The origin of the name comes from the word "milonga", which is a sub-style of tango music.

Agnolim's repertoire include tangos, valses and milongas.

[edit] Irving L. Horowitz

Horowitz was born in New York City on September 25, 1929, to Louis and Esther Tepper Horowitz and educated at the College of the City of New York (now city College of the City University of New York, or CUNY), B.S.S., 1951; Columbia University, New York City, M.A., 1952; and the University of Buenos Aires, Argentina, Ph.D., 1957. In 1950, he married Ruth Narowlansky; they were divorced in 1963. He married Mary Ellen Curtis in 1979 with whom he has two children, Carl and David. From the beginning of his career as an assistant professor of social theory at the University of Buenos Aires, 1956-1958, Horowitz spent the next forty-plus years at various academic institutions nationwide and abroad in India, Tokyo, Mexico, and Canada. In addition to his teaching positions, he was an advisory staff member of the Latin American Research Center, 1964-1970; consultant to the International Education Division, Ford Foundation, 1959-1960; a member of the advisory board of the Institute for Scientific Information, From 1962 to 1969, Horowitz was professor of sociology at Washington University in St. Louis. He has also been a visiting professor at Stanford University, the University of Wisconsin, Queen’s University in Canada, and the University of California, and a Fulbright Lecturer in Argentina, Israel, and India; a member of the advisory board of the Institute for Scientific Information, 1969-1973; consulting editor for Oxford University Press, 1974-1969, and for Aldine-Atherton Publishers, 1969-1972; the founding president of Transaction Society; an external board member of the Radio Marti and Television Marti Programs of the U.S. Information Agency, beginning in 1985; chair of the board of the Hubert Humphrey Center, Ben Gurion University, Israel, 1990-1992; and has served as an external board member of the methodology section of the research division, U.S. General Accounting Office. As the author of more than twenty-five books and editor of numerous other titles, Horowitz has analyzed such diverse topics as the influence of Sun Myung Moon and the Unification Church on American politics, the future of book publishing, and politics in Cuba. In 1990, he published his autobiography, rather a brief "sociological biography" than one that is intellectual of intimate. This was Daydreams and Nightmares: Reflections on a Harlem Childhood (London: Jackson Publ., 1990, 104 p.), for which he received the National Jewish Book Award. It is an unromanticized look at growing up as the son of Russian-Jewish immigrants in the streets of predominantly black Harlem, New York City, in the 1930s. Throughout his academic career, Horowitz received many awards, including a special citation from the Carnegie Endowment for International Peace, 1957, for Idea of War and Peace in Contemporary Philosophy;; recognition by Time as the Man of the Year in Behavioral Science, 1970; the Centennial medallion from St. Peters College, Jersey City, New Jersey, 1971, for outstanding contribution to a humanistic social science; and a presidential outstanding achievement award, 1985, from Rutgers University. He is a member of the Carnegie Council, American Association of Publishers, American Political Science Association, American Association for the Advancement of Science, and past president (1961-1962) of the New York State Sociological Society. Horowitz latest academic post was Hannah Arendt University Professor Emeritus of Sociology and Political Science at Rutgers University, since 1992. Over the past several decades Horowitz has worked to develop a political sociology that can measure the extent of a society's personal freedom and State-sanctioned violence. As a result of his work, a standard for the quality of life in any particular nation or social system has been constructed based on the number of people arbitrarily killed, maimed, injured, incarcerated, or deprived of basic civil liberties. Horowitz has tried to build a bridge between his current analysis of state power and authority and his earlier studies of comparative international stratification and development. He is recognized as the individual who introduced the phrase "Third World" into the lexicon of social research. Horowitz is the founder of Studies in Comparative International Development—now in its 40th year. He is also chairman of Transaction-Aldine Publishers. From 1962 to 1969, Horowitz was professor of sociology at Washington University in St. Louis. He has also been a visiting professor at Stanford University, the University of Wisconsin, Queen’s University in Canada, and the University of California, and a Fulbright Lecturer in Argentina, Israel, and India. He is a prolific author. Among his most recent books are Tributes: An Informal History of Social Science in the Twentieth Century; Behemoth: The History and Theory of Political Sociology. [edit] American Board of Family Medicine

The American Board of Family Medicine (ABFM) is the second largest medical specialty board in the United States. Founded in 1969, it is a voluntary, not-for-profit, private organization whose purposes include:

  • Improve the quality of medical care available to the public
  • Establish and maintain standards of excellence in the specialty of Family Medicine
  • Improve the standards of medical education for training in Family Medicine
  • Determine by evaluation the fitness of specialists in Family Medicine who apply for and hold certificates

The ABFM certifies physicians in the area of family medicine once they pass a written examination. Certification periods range from 6-10 years. It has a rotating Board of Directors and is located in Lexington, KY.

The Leather Man legend, as researched by Alex Gallrdo. The Leather Man was something of a mystery. He was a singular and curious character that roamed about westchester and Connecticut for some 30 years. People said that if you saw him from a distance, that he looked like a bear walking on its hind legs. As he got closer, you could see that it was a man covered in lether from head to foot. For more than 30 years, from 1858 to 1889, the Leather Man regularlly traveled a 300 mile clockwise circut from Brewster, in Putman county, to as far east as Saybrook, Connecticut. He averaged some 9 miles a day on his trek, and usually covered the entire route in a little over a month. He rarely varied from his chosen path,and people used to say that they knew the day of the month when the Leather Man appeared. The Leather Man seemed to care for horses, as he would often be seen reparing holes in the country roads that he traveled. In this way, the horses would not step into the holes, and injure themselve. Because he wore an Angus Dei, with a small crucifix, and carried a French prayer book, dated 1844, he was presumed to be Catholic. Over the years, stories began to develop about him. Piecing these stories together, from interviews of people that knew him, the following tale evolved. The Leather Man was born Jules Bourglay, in Lyon, France, in 1830. Although he was the son of a woodcutter, he managed to get a liberal education. When he finished his studies in Paris, he met and fell in love with Lillian Larone. He invested much of Larone's funds in purchasing large quantities of hides and raw leather to be processed and resold. Jules was certain that the price of leather would go up. Instead the revese occurred; the price of leather fell 40%. Many leather businesses failed. Larone went bankrupt, and Jules was disgraced. He was forced to leave Paris, and Lillian forever, in 1856. The shock of causing the loss of the Larone family fortune, as well as losing Lillian was too much him to bear. A short time after, Jules was found wandering about the countryside in a dazed and incoherent condition. He was committed to an insane asylum, and he was there for two years before he escpaed. Some sympathetic neighbors helped him to get to the cost, and they payed his passage to America. He arrived in New York in 1858. His friends had hoped that by going to America, and getting a fresh start, that his mental condition would improve. That he would get over his obession with leather, and the past. It was a vain hope. Thus, the saga of the Leather Man began. He lived by doing odd jobs, usually acception only food, or scraps of leather for payment. Sometimes he would accept coins, and buy goods from a general store, by pointing things out.If he did not need any supplies, he would refuse the money for work done, or leave it on the doorstep of whoever had given it to him. Yet, at times he would accept pennies from children, probably because hedid not want to hurt their feelings. The Lather Man's all leather outfit was made of scraps of leather that he had sewn together, with laces cut from the same material. His knee-length coast was large, and hung loosely about him. His trousers were more like fishermen boots, as they extended to his armpits. He wore a large leather hat, with a big crown, and carried an oversize leather bag. In this bage, he carried pieces of leather, food, tobacco, some tools,and a bowl from lwhich he ate and drank. The coat had sizable pockets, where other supplies were kept. These included a smoking pipe, with a tin bowl, that he had made himsself. When he walked, he made the kind of creaking sound that one would associate with horses in harness. A Connecticut tanner once oiled his complete attire for him, and this made him happy. He always tried to get soft leather, the type used in the high tops of boots. In winter, he wore a woolen knit shirt under his clothing. His shoes had two-inch soles, and were made of layers of wood and leather. They were held together with hobnails, and wire, and weighed about ten pounds. His entire leather covering weighed about 90 pounds, not counting his leather bag, tools, and supplies. The Leather Man wore a short beard that he would clip with a pair of heavy scissors. This gave him a shaggy, unkept appearance. He was known to bathe by immersing simself in a stream or pond, leather garments and all. Once, when a farmer's wife offered him a basin of water to wash with before eating, he became insulted. He never returned to that farm again. In winter he would forego bathing altogether. He generally slept in natural caves,orin huts of his own making,occasionally he would spend the night in a barn. He always refused an offer to sleep in a house, or a bed. In the caves and huts, the Leather Man would keep a supply of firewood, and made a neat bed of leavves and branches, held together with stakes and side rails. The floors of these huts and caves were swept clean. Tin cans that were used for cooking would be washed clean, and put bottom up on stakes to dry. In his own way, he was clean and tidy. A Mr. Beach, of Forestville, Connecticut, said that the Leather Man visited his farm for dinner every 34 days for ten years. Another family, the Colts, stated that he visited them regularly for 27 years. While the Leather Man traveled a great deal across country, he also walked on dirt roads. He always carried a big stick with him to protect himself from vicious dogs, or cruel boys, that occasionally woould bother him. When this happened he was know to change his route a bit. The last part was a fairly straight route, except for a detour to New Canaan. This itinerary covered some 200 miles in Connecticut, and 100 miles in New York. He would spend 22 days in Connecticut, and 12 in New York. There appeared to be no rhythm or reason for following this route, other than people were friendly, and they seemed to be caves available for housing. As the Leather Man grew older, his movements slowed, and his visits became irregular. He stopped more often, and stayed in one place longer. He replaced his heavy leather bag with a carpetbag. Some memebers of the Connecticut Humane Society noted the Leather Man's weakened condition, and had him arrested in December, 1888. He was taken to Hartford Hospital, and given the choice of staying in the hospital or returning to France. He declined both options, and the hospital authrities had to release him, because he had committed no crime. Consequently he continued his wanderings. While the Leather Man survived the winter of '88,'the end was not far off. He developed cancer of the mouth, and a year later, one Henry Miller found the Leather Man in a cave in Ossining. He was dead. They buried him in Sparta Cememtery, near Ossining in pauper's row. Some people alleged that robbers, who thought he had a hidden fortune in his cave, had murdered him. At the unveiling of the marker,both the French and American flags were flown, and some people dressed in 19th century coustumes. The national anthems of both countries were played and sung. Thus, 64 years after his death, the Leather Man had finally found a home. Photgraphs of the Leather Man can be seen in some local historical societies. Except for his all leather outfit, he appears not unlike the many unfortunate, homeless peole found today in big cities. [edit] lm13700

The LM13700 in an operational transconductance amplifier, or OTA for short, that is almost identical to the LM13600 (the darlington output buffer is the only difference). The LM13700 is like a standar OP amp, but it is voltage in and current out and is programmable via the IABC pin. This chip is very useful in audio electronics especially in analog synthesizer circuits like voltage controlled oscillators, filters, and amplifiers!! (VCO, VCF, VCA)

[edit] Oil Cleansing Method

The Oil Cleansing Method is a system for cleaning your face using oils. Beginners often start out with mixture of half Extra Virgin Olive Oil (EVOO) and half Castor Oil (CO). Once experience is gained, the mixture can be tweaked based on skin type and personal preference. Other oils that are commonly used are Jojoba oil and Coconut Oil.

The oil is rubbed into skin for approx. 2 min. Next, a warm, damp washcloth is drapped over face until it cools. The cooled washcloth is then used to wipe off excess oil. Continue wipeing until the oil is gone. If face feels tight, one or two drops of EVOO or another oil of your choice may be used to moisturize.

Many users have experienced fewer blackheads, softer skin, and less acne.

Stephanie Strauss has written an excellent article on the subject that can be found here.

[edit] Steruphilia

If you go to the opera or a movie and sit next to a person determined to sneeze or cough as loudly as possible, you could be sitting next to a steruphiliac. Steruphilia is the love of sneezing or coughing. The term originated when people had limited means to determine if an inert human body was alive or dead. If it sneezed or coughed - a welcome sound to bereaving relatives - it was detemined to be alive. The waiting relatives in this case were said to be in a state of steruphilia.

[edit] External Compression Headache

External Compression Headache is caused by any type of head wear. This includes headwear that places pressure on the head — including tight hats, helmets, headbands and goggles. It is not known why some people are more sensitive than others to this type of pressure. External compression headaches can affect anyone who uses head wear. The headaches are more common among people who have migraines. It is believed to affect about four percent of the population. It is also referd to as "swim goggle headache".

[edit] IAANS

2-(4'-(iodoacetamido)anilino)naphthalene-6-sulfonic acid

An environment-sensitive fluorescent probe commonly used in biophysical studies of proteins. IAANS can be attached to proteins via naturally occuring or engineered amino acid Cystine. The fluorescence intensity and the emission wavelength of the probe is very sensitive to substrate binding, folding and unfolding of the protein, as well as changes in ionic strength of the environment and association of the labeled protein with other biomolecules.

[edit] Tiebout Competition

Tiebout competition is an application of free-market principles to the creation and maintenance of public goods. An incentive to provide better services is created by forcing local authorities to compete for tax-paying households.

This idea has been applied in a host of different ways (see link below).

The idea comes from a 1956 paper by Charles Tiebout, a geographer from the University of Washington.
