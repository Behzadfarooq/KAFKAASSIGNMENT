[edit] The first camera phone and who invented it

At Jon Peddie Research we decided to investigate who was the person, or group who came up with the idea of adding a camera to a mobile phone.

We surveyed over 4,000 engineers and managers in the mobile phone and camera market and got over 2,000 replies and a lot with references to (http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camera_phone)., http://www.w2forum.com/dictionary/Camera_Phones, http://www.answers.com/topic/j-sh04, http://sharp-world.com/corporate/info/his/chronology/p14.html, which all said “The world's first camera phone was the J-SH04 made by Sharp Corporation and went on sale in Japan in November 2000.”

Dave Cooper from Imagination Technology found an interesting patent filed by Alcatel in European Patent office in July 1992. (http://v3.espacenet.com/textdoc?DB=EPODOC&IDX=EP1028577&QPN=EP1028577&CY=ep&LG=en) Source European Patent Office Figure 1: Radio telephone with video player recorder The radio telephone has a hand-held housing with electroacoustic transducers (2,3) behind sound inlets (9,10) and a video recorder (7), a video player (6) and an electronic circuit (5) for image and a sound communications by radio within the housing. The telephone also has a hands-free device. The video recorder has a wide-angle objective (8) and is mounted in the front of the housing with the video player.

And former JPA employee Tommy McClain found a US Patent filled in February 2002 by Miyake; Hiroyuki at Mitsubishi. [1] which was originally disclosed in the 1998 Japanese Patent Publication (unexamined) No. 336498/1998. Source: US Patent Office Figure 2: Cellular phone with imaging device Hitherto, in the cellular phone equipped with an imaging device in which the cellular phone and the imaging device are integrally accommodated, for the purpose of taking a picture while visually recognizing a photographing screen on a monitor, a camera of the imaging device faces in opposite direction of the monitor. Therefore, to take a picture of a user while watching on the screen a person to whom the user is speaking, it is necessary to change the direction of the imaging device every time. In a cellular phone equipped with an imaging device 1 according to the invention, on the front side 1a provided with a monitor 4 of a housing 100 of the cellular phone, a lens 2 of the imaging device is disposed facing outwardly. Further, a viewfinder 3a is provided facing in the same direction as that of the lens 2.

This patent was filled in the US in 2002, and probably was filled in Japan much earlier (Patent Abstracts of Japan, 11-146034, May 28, 1999.) Notice the similarity to the shape and locations of elements to the Kyocera VP-210 in Figure 33. The real first camera phone But the first commercially available phone was correctly identified by Eisaku Ohbuchi-san at DMP Digital Media Professionals, Dr. Peter Weigand at Micronas, and Tony Henning, Managing Editor, The Mobile Imaging Report form Future Image Inc. as being the Kyocera visual phone (VP-210) which was introduced in Japan in May 1999, over a year and half before the Sharp J-SH04

Kyocera Corporation’s President: Kensuke Itoh, showed the VP-210 with a CMOS image sensor with approximately 110,000 pixels and a 2.0-inch reflective TFT color LCD at the Business Show '99 Tokyo.

The phone could transmits and receives images in real time at a rate of approximately two frames per second, making it the world's mobile first color videophone. In addition, users can send and receive e-mail with image attachments by using the P-Mail DX (deluxe) service offered by the DDI Pocket Telephone Group

Source Kyocera Figure 3: First camera phone, Kyocera VP-210 The VP-210 had a 65k color 2.0-inch reflective TFT color LCD (size: approximately 40 mm x 30 mm. The phone was featured in a UK magazine article (PC World) in May 2000 with the SRP of £36.19.

Kyocera Corporation, headquartered in Kyoto, Japan, has been developing high-tech products, high-performance materials and electronic components since 1959. The second camera phone Don Lee at NexusChips had a totally different idea, and said, “As far as I know the world’s fist camera phone was the Samsung Anycall CDMA camera phone, SCH-V200 in June, 2000. The SCH-V200 had a 1.5 inch color TFT-LCD, 350K pixel and 20 pictures storage. It was released at "KIECO 2000 exhibition" in April, 2000. The SCH-V200 was not a successful product. Then, Japanese camera phone, Sharp's SH04 was released in November, 2000.” Source Samsung? Figure 4: Samsung’s SCH-V200 Chris.Turner at Business-Advantage got some data from his friend Tony Henning Managing Editor, The Mobile Imaging Report form Future Image. The Camera Phone was the fifth multi-function hand-held phone developed by Samsung, after the Internet Phone, MP3 Phone, Watch Phone, and TV Phone. The unit featured a fixed lens located on the upper backside of the phone, and the subject captured by the lens is displayed on the TFT-LCD. Once the subject has been framed correctly, the user presses the shutter button on the bottom of the TFT-LCD. The Camera Phone can take 20 pictures at 350,000-pixel resolution and included a CD-ROM for picture editing and a data cable to connect the phone to a PC

We couldn’t find any of references on this phone in English, but found a dozen in Russian, and did see things in the text that suggested it was indeed an April 2000 event. Kudos to Don for finding this obscure little beauty. And the third camera phone How Sharp got credit for being first is a mystery, except that Sharp make such a claim in a 2000 document (http://sharp-world.com/corporate/info/his/h_company/2000/)... “The J-SH04 was the industry's first mobile phone to feature an integrated 110,000-pixel CMOS image sensor for taking digital photos.” Now it is interesting that the Kyocera and the Sharp have the same resolution, but the Sharp’s camera is on the back side, so it was intended as a camera and not a video conference phone like the Kyocera. Vodafone echoes the claim at http://www.vodafone.jp/english/release_detail/e20030326_02/e20030326_03.html, and even the history of photography’s timeline (http://www.marcine.com/photohistorytimelinefr.html ) gives them credit. Source Sharp Figure 5: Sharp’s J-SH04 camera phone Like the Kyocera VP-210, the Sharp J-SH04 had a 65k color 2.0-inch screen and could show up 12 lines of text.

It gets a little confusing because also in October 2000, Sharp is credited with the J-Phone J-SH04 and it is shown as a clam-shell.

Epilog – The inventor After we completed the survey and published our results in two places ("Jon Peddie's Tech Watch," July 18, 2005, Volume 5, Number 14, Jon Peddie Research, Tiburon Ca., "Handheld Multimedia Devices: Semiconductor and IP enabling multimedia on handhelds and phones," copyright 2004, Jon Peddie Research, Tiburon Ca.) which got world wide distribution, some diligent readers kept digging, and in November of 2005, Wolfgang Gruener of Tom's Guide Publishing found THE inventor of the camera phone.

Equipped with the information from the above research, Gruener poked into Kyocera's massive Japan operation, in the hope to find the man, woman or group behind the idea of the VP-210.

It took several weeks until I was able to find the right contact, reported Gruner. But eventually, Masumi Sakurai from Kyocera's PR department at the firm's Kyoto headquarters assisted us and I spoke to Kazumi Saburi.

Source Wolfgang Gruner, TGdaily Saburi led the VP-210 project back in 1997 as section manager of Kyocera's Yokohama research and development center and brought the device from idea to life. Today, Saburi is the manager of the Fifth Engineering Development Department for Mobile Communication Equipment in Kyocera's Corporate Mobile Communication Equipment Group.

Where did Saburi get the idea to build a camera into a cell phone?

"Around that time, cellular handsets with enabled voice and SMS communication capabilities were considered to be just one among many personal communication tools. One day a simple idea hit us - 'What if we were able to enjoy talking with the intended person watching his/her face on the display?' We were certain that such a device would make cell phone communications much more convenient and enjoyable."

So his initial thoughts were centered around a video rather than still cameras, something that is only recently become more popular in phones. "We knew that a handset must be equipped with a camera and a full-color display for visual communications of any kind - the former is required for catching the image of the speaker and the latter for showing it on the receiver's terminal. This pretty much outlines our first idea," he said.

Saburi's idea was to have users exchange pictures by email, using the SMS services of the phone network. He began working on the project back in 1997, and "the Kyocera visual phone came to the market in 1999, but we had nourished [the idea] for several years before we started development."

Looking back in the historical records 40 years ago, UK banks told John Edwards that no one ever would be interested in carrying a phone around all the time and ultimately denied him a loan to develop and market his cellphone idea. But Saburi didn't have any such difficulty. "Top management aggressively encouraged us to develop the product. We also believed that such a product would improve Kyocera's brand image."

Saburi's research on how consumers used cameras was a big reason for inventing the camera phone: "The cell phone's strongest feature is its portability. This alone creates the application opportunity for the camera phone: We imagined that users are likely to accept the minor weight addition of a camera in a cell phone and carry around 'weightless' photo albums to enjoy with friends. But they do not use heavy cameras or bring along traditional physical photo albums all the time. A cell phone with a camera and color display provided a completely new value for users: It could be used as a phone, a camera and a photo album. This is also where we started from a marketing point of view. From a technical view, we were also confident that we could develop such a device. The approach was to combine an existing teleconference system and a primary wireless data transmission technology."

The phone was a quick success in Japan and was the beginning of a cultural change in how phones were used. "After the release of Kyocera's visual phone for DDI pocket, now Willcom, and J-phone, now Vodafone K.K., introduced the service "SHA-mail" ["photo mail"], which greatly contributed to boost the sales of camera-installed cellular handsets in Japan. SHA-mail greatly changed Japanese telecommunication culture. A key factor to support the boom of camera-installed handsets was in my opinion the evolution of the camera module. We saw remarkable downsizing of the modules, the quality and function were amazingly improved - to a level that was almost equal to digital cameras."

As you can imagine, squeezing a camera into a phone wasn't easy. Saburi remembers plenty of challenges. "I remember that I had discussions with R&D staff, project members and top management several road blocks we came across - again and again. What is the best angle for camera mounting? How can we improve the data transmission speed for minimizing the time lag while talking? What is the best balance of the image quality and frame rate? What about the battery? We faced a pile of challenges that we had never had before." About a dozen engineers worked on the key technologies, taking about two years to build the first model.

Today, Saburi is in charge of Kyocera's cell handset R&D, and he is working on phones with auto focus and 3 megapixel resolution. "In Japan, we are currently enjoying taking pictures and playing music on our cell phones. We can even make payments with our handsets, just like you use a credit card. We call this the 'osaifu-keitai' function. My guess is that the next big thing would happen after a seamless ubiquitous network environment is established."

I asked Saburi if there is anything he could change about mobile phones and the way we use mobile phones, what would it be? "We should not forget the key function of phones. Cellular telephones enable us to communicate with anyone, at anytime, from anywhere. This is the strongest feature of cell phones. We should focus much more on this feature of communication, which would allow us to extend battery life. How much fun would it be, if we could enjoy endless multi-communication, without having to worry about the battery? Of course, this is my personal opinion. I myself prefer the simple cell phone models that focus on just the communication feature."

And there you have it, the ultimate irony: the inventor of the camera phone, for all his influence over how technology is used, is the ultimate cell Luddite! [edit] Schrage Motor

This request for creation has been reviewed. The result is below.
If your request was declined and you want to make changes and try again, copy and paste your article into a new submission and make your changes there.


In this type of motor, also called the Scharge or variable-brush AC commutator motor, a different method of injecting the speed-varying e.m.f. into the secondary winding is employed. Referring to Figure 45, the supply is led to the rotor winding through slip rings, the rotor winding being the primary. The secondary winding is on the stator. On the rotor is an additional regulating winding with a commutator. The ends of the stator (secondary) windings are connected to brushes disposed on the commutator.

The first point to notice is that when the rotor is at standstill, the rotating field sweeps past the secondary winding and the brushes at synchronous speed. As the motor gains speed, the frequency in the secondary windings and, due to the commutator, the frequency at the brushes are both reduced simultaneously. No matter at what speed the rotor is moving, the brush frequency ties up with the secondary frequency and, as a result, voltage can be picked up from the commutator and injected into the secondary windings.

The method of varying the e.m.f. injected into the secondary windings is by altering the amount of commutator spanned by each pair of brushes. The pairs of brushes are arranged so that they can be moved simultaneously equal distances from the in-line position. When in-line, each pair of brushes rests on the same commutator segment. At this position, each secondary phase winding is short-circuited and no phase voltage is injected; consequently the speed is that of a normal induction motor. Movement of the brushes in one direction or the other increases or decreases the speed. Brush movement can be done by hand wheel or by means of a small servo or pilot motor, remotely-controlled. The general characteristics of this rotor-fed motor are the same as those of the stator-fed type.

[edit] Grob 109B

The Grob 109B was developed out of the Grob 109 by Grob Aerospace of Mindelheim Mattsies of Germany.

The basic concept was that of a two seater self launching motorglider with a 57 foot wing span. The basic airframe construction was in fibreglass. Total weight was around 1870 lbs with a load of 506 lbs. Cruising speed was in the region of 100 knots on the 90 horsepower engine. Though Grob made the engine, it was based on a VW car engine The engine could be turned off in flight and propeller could be feathered. The aircraft then became a pure glider, with a best glide ratio of around 1:28. The pilot and passengers sat side by side and enjoyed excellent views from the large perspex cockpit.

[edit] IMF Financial Sector Assessment Program (FSAP)

As part of its activities, The International Monetary Fund (IMF) conducts Financial Sector Assessment Programs FSAP for member countries. It was established as a program by both the IMF and the World Bank in 1999 and provides member countries, upon request, a comprehensive assessment of the respective financial systems. The IMF conducts the program to assess the strengths and weaknesses of a country's financial sector. Participation in the program is entirely voluntary, however many countries deem the programme necessary, with over two thirds of the IMF member countries participating in the Program.[7]

The Program

When a country participates in an FSAP program, a technical team of financial experts from the IMF visit the respective country (mission) to hold meetings and gather economic and financial statistical information in order to generate an initial assessment. After an initial assesment, the IMF will subsequently provide FSAP updates on a regular basis. A member country's central bank or financial authority will specify whether they want the results of an FSAP review to be publicized.

[edit] Variable Analysis

A term used by Herbert Blumer in his theory of Symbolic Interactionism to refer to the positivistic (positivism), often quantitative, scientific methods of sociological research which he largely rejected in favour of qualitative approaches such as ethnography. Variable analysis would typically involve the isolation of two variables in a process, one 'dependent' variable to be measured and another 'independent' variable to be controlled, much as in the study of physical or biological science. Blumer viewed this approach as simplistic and as glossing over the complexities of social interaction, leading him to be viewed as the founder of a new sociological school known as symbolic interactionism. The term can equally be used by extension to refer to the use of such analysis, and the objections to its use, in other fields.

2.0 Statutory Duties

The County Auditor is an elected official who, by State and County Ordinance is the Chief Budget Officer of the County. The Auditor issues the Comprehensive Annual Financial Report (CAFR), prepares the annual budget and 5 year plan, reviews debt and bonding, pays County bills and issues payroll. The Auditor shares management duties regarding the real property tax system with the Recorder, Assessor, and Treasurer. The Auditor’s Tax duties include computing and issuing real property tax valuation notices, administering the appeals process, reconciling tax collections, and conducting the annual tax sale of delinquent properties. The Auditor also conducts audits of individual County organizations to evaluate both financial controls and program performance.

3.0 Accomplishments

The Salt Lake County Auditor's Office has been instrumental in helping the County to secure and maintain a "AAA" bond rating. This is the highest bond rating from all three major bond rating agencies. Salt Lake County is one of only 11 counties in the U.S. that maintain a AAA or "Triple Triple" bond rating from all three national bond rating agencies.

The Auditor's Office has also received numerous awards for excellence in financial reporting, including recognition for Excellence in Reporting by the Government Finance Officers Association, the principal organization for public finance professionals in the United States.

[edit] Caudete

[edit] Situation

Caudete is a Spanish town situated in the south- east of Spain, near the cities of Valencia and Alicante. Now, Caudete is part of the province of Albacete and the community of Castilla- la Mancha. Caudete has a 10.000 population.


[edit] Tourism in Caudete

Caudete is situated in a cross of ways. In their municipal term three independent communities are united , the Comunidad Valenciana, the Región de Murcia and Castilla- la Mancha. For this reason, Caudete has a lot of monuments to visit and many cities near from it. In first place, in Caudete there are the Church of Saint Catherine (iglesia de Santa Catalina) in the town centre. This church was built in the 14th century and it have examples of the gothic, renaissance and baroc styles. Above the Saint Catherine's Church there are the castle. This castle was built by the Muslims in the 12nd century. In town centre too there are the Saint Joseph's Monestry (monasterio de San José) built in the 16th century. Next to the monestry there are the Our Lady of Carmen's Church (iglesia del Carmen). This church is in the main square of the town. Outside the center town is the monumental district of San Francisco (el barrio de San Francisco). This district counts on the San Francisco' s Church, neobyzantine style, and the famous bullring "The Sands" of Caudete (Las Arenas de Caudete). These two works and all the district were constructed by the Count of San Carlos in the beginning of 20th century. Outside the town, following the pretty stroll of the Way of the Virgin (el Camino de la Virgen), is the hermitage of Our Lady of Grace. This hermitage was built between the 14th and the 18th centuries. This church counts with a valuable artistic treasures, from the mantles of the Virgin to gothic painting tables. Our Lady of Grace is the patroness of Caudete. But the greater touristic attractive of Caudete is their celebrations of Moors and Christians. This celebrations are celebrated between the 5th to the 10th of September, in honour of Our Lady of Grace and count with more of four hundred years of antiquity. In this celebrations the people of the town get dressed of moors and christians and remember the medieval time of the town. Other celebrations are the Dances of the Boy (Bailes del Niño) in Christmas, where the people of the town dance traditional dances in the squares, the popular celebrations of San Antón and San Blas, in January and February, the Easter, in March or April, and the celebration of Our Lady of Carmen, in the 16th July.

Aside from the touristic attractive of the town, Caudete counts with a communications network that unites the town with the beaches of the Costa Blanca (Benidorm, Torrevieja, Denia, etc) in less than one hour as well as with the big cities of Valencia, Alicante, Murcia or Albacete.

[edit] Retroperitoneal Lymph Node Dissection

Commonly referred to as RPLND, it is a procedure to remove abdominal lymph nodes to treat Testicular Cancer, as well as help establish its exact maturity and type.

Testicular Cancer spreads in a well-known pattern, and the lymph nodes in the retroperitoneum are a primary landing site during spread of the desease.

The procedue is becoming standard treatment for clinical stage I and II Non-Seminomatous Germ Cell Tumors (NSGCTT) because of the low mortality and relapse rate with this procedue, as compared with the alternative, which is observation. Also, NSGCTT is considered more aggressive than Seminomas, the "other" kind of Testicular Cancer.

The potential problems in RPLND have mostly to do with nerves: they might get damaged or cut, resulting in infertility, an inability to ejaculate, or even an inabiloty to have an erection. This is why most often, a nerve-sparing technique is used. A less invasive form using laparoscopic technique (L-RPLND) exists, which is more costly, time-consuming, and requires special equipment that not every hospital may have. Open RPLND (O-RPLND), which is perfeomed by opening the stomach to get inside, has more room for problems, but is an equally effective way to get the lymph node out.

Adjuvant Chemotherapy is considered equally effective, but complicates follow-up regimen, as it may complicate therapies in case of relapse.

    [edit] Amateur fire brigade

    An amateur fire brigade is a fire fighting force consisting of nonprofessional, sometimes volunteer members. Although largely superseded by professional fire fighters, amateur fire brigades were quite common [in the United States] during the American Civil War and can be seen in the film Gangs of New York among other places. They were originally organized by insurance companies, but fire houses eventually turned into a sort of social gathering place for working-class men.

    This led to a number of problems, however. Each fire brigade tended to have a "turf" it patrolled, putting out fires in order to reap the reward paid by the insurance company, but turf wars could lead to conflict among different brigades. In New York City and other places in the United States, for instance, it was not unheard of for two or more brigades to arrive at the scene of the fire and fight each other instead, while the building burned to the ground. Looting of the building in question was also quite common. See the incomplete article fire house and history of fire brigades.

    [edit] Jimmie Lewallen, one of NASCAR's earliest pioneers.

    Jimmie Lewallen (Born 22/8/1919 - 16/10/1995) was born and raised in High Point, North Carolina, Jimmie Lewallen started racing motorcycles in 1934 before switching to cars in the late 30's while helping to distribute "spirits" to those in other areas of the state and especially High Point, NC. Early moonshine running went hand in hand with racing. He competed on the old one-mile dirt track in his hometown in 1940 before going off to war.

    He entered the army in 1941 and between then and 1945 he fought in the ETO (European Theater Operations). He was at the Normandy landings and served in Europe, Africa and the Middle East Campaigns. He received the Bronze Star Medal, Good Conduct Medal, American Defense Service Medal, American Campaign Medal, European-African-Middle Eastern Campaign Medal & Bronze Star Attachment (Triple), World War II Victory Medal, the Honorable Service Lapel Button WWII and several Battlefield Commissions. He was wounded twice.

    After returning home from the war, he went to work at a local Machine Shop and continued to race at wherever a track could be found. On October 12, 1946 a meeting was held at the Rex Hotel on Peachtree Street in Atlanta Georgia. "Big Bill" France, Red Vogt, Buddy Shuman, Jimmie, Raymond Parks and others were in attendance. This was the meeting that actually "formed" NASCAR. There were 12 or 13 people present when it was formed. Bill France offered him a chance to "buy into NASCAR" for $500.00 and he turned him down. He said, "it would never amount to anything".

    Part of the appeal of the automobile to the average southern Piedmont boy, both before and after World War II, was the mechanical abilities that they had picked up in both the war, and in their own back yard. These backyard mechanics often worked late into the night, until they got their cars into shape from racing. Drivers frequently ran two or three races a week, requiring mechanical knowledge to keep the car running well.”

    Jimmie Lewallen was one of these shady tree mechanics. His son Gary remembers helping his father, working late into the night. "We’d work 'till one, two, three o’clock in the morning trying to get the car ready for the weekend, having to do it about every night after school. It was a family deal.” These drivers and their families put their heart and soul into racing.

    Jimmie Lewallen was one of the original drivers that competed in the first race that was sponsored under the NASCAR banner at the old Charlotte Speedway. Lewallen finished 16th in that race winning $25 for his efforts in a 1946 Ford.

    France and his group ran the 1948 season as a test year for the company by running strictly stockcars and a modified series. The actual first season of strictly stockcar racing occurred in 1949. Jimmie raced "officially" from the inception of NASCAR. The France family says it started in 1960 on the "Grand National Circuit", the "Sportsman" Circuit and the "Modified" Circuit. The GN circuit is now the Busch series but then it was the "CUP" series.

    He went on to compete in 139 Cup races through 1960. Though he is not credited with any Cup wins, he finished second four times. Jimmie did most of his racing in the Modified and Sportsman series, where he won dozens of events.

    In 1950 he won the Modified Championship at Bowman-Gray Stadium in Winston-Salem, NC.

    In 1953 Lee Petty Engineering running in the NASCAR Grand National Division expanded and ran a Dodge Diplomats for Jimmie at Palm Beach Speedway, finishing second to Petty. Both cars were prepared in the Randleman, NC Petty engineering shops.

    Jimmie's NASCAR career spanned some 12 years and from 1953 through 1955 he finished in the Top Ten in the points standings.

    Although he continued racing professionally until 1975, Lewallen joined what is now Vann York Pontiac and worked there for 39 years. He also was a co-founder of the Old-Timer Racing Club.

    Jimmie lost his battle with cancer in 1995.

    [edit] Maurice John

    Maurice John

    From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

    Maurice “Maury” John (born in 1919; died in October, 1974 in Ames, Iowa) coached at Moberly Junior College in Moberly, Missouri; Drake University in Des Moines, Iowa; and Iowa State University in Ames, Iowa. His teams compiled impressive numbers throughout his career.

    A graduate of Central Missouri State (1941), his teams first attracted national attention at Moberly. John was a breathtaking 280-79 (.780) in his twelve seasons there. His Greyhounds won two national junior college championships (1954, 1955). John was inducted into the National Junior College Athletic Association (NJCAA) Hall of Fame in 1984.

    Taking over at Drake for the 1958-1959 season, John had to compete in the stellar Missouri Valley Conference (MVC). Bradley, Cincinnati, Saint Louis, and Wichita were very worthy opponents; Tulsa and North Texas were not as strong. Basketball purists loved it when John would coach opposite the big names in the Valley --- Chuck Orsborn (Bradley), Ed Jucker (Cincinnati), John Benington (St. Louis), and Ralph Miller (Wichita). Outstanding competition notwithstanding, John was named MVC Coach-of-the-Year an incredible four times, and three years in a row (1964, 1968, 1969, 1970). On February 27, 1971 against St. Louis, Drake fans were chanting, “Don’t go, Maury, don’t go,” but it was too late. He had signed a contract that afternoon to take over at Iowa State after thirteen seasons in Des Moines. He was 211-131 (.617) as coach of the Bulldogs.

    John’s 1968-1969 Drake team was known probably more than any other. For serious basketball fans the names of some of the Drake players (Willie McCarter, Willie Wise, Dolph Pulliam) roll off the tongue, and the team is discussed in reverential tones to this day in Des Moines.

    Once at Iowa State, John described (James H.) Hilton Coliseum as “an $8 million palace,” and he was 43-35 in three seasons there. John ended his ties with the Cyclones in July, 1974 because of illness, and he died on October 16, 1974 in Ames.

    John may be best remembered both as a magnificent recruiter and as a coach who was ahead of his time. He was in step with Hall of Fame coaches Al McGuire of Marquette and Ralph Miller of Wichita --- they saw African-American players as skilled performers on a basketball court and amiable people off of it, while the general population may have seen them in a negative, noncontributing role off the court, but in an acceptable role on it.


    Notable Games and Accomplishments

    • the February 6, 1961 game in Peoria against Bradley, stopping the Braves’ 46-game winning streak there (DU won, 86-76),
    • the December 11, 1961 game in Des Moines against eventual national champion Cincinnati (DU lost, 60-59),
    • four championships in five tries at the Queen City Tournament in Buffalo, New York in the 1960’s,
    • four MVC titles won outright or shared (1964, 1969, 1970, 1971),
    • the December, 1968 game against Al McGuire’s Marquette Warriors in Des Moines (DU won, 68-63),
    • the March 8, 1969 MVC playoff game against Louisville in Wichita (DU won, 77-73),
    • the March 20, 1969 NCAA tournament game against John Wooden’s UCLA Bruins in Louisville in the Final Four (UCLA won, 85-82),
    • the November, 1970 publication of John’s book, Drake’s Belly-Button Defense,
    • the March 13, 1971 MVC playoff game against Louisville in Peoria (DU won, 86-71),
    • the March 18, 1971 NCAA tournament game against Notre Dame in Wichita (DU won, 79-72, in overtime),
    • the March 20, 1971 NCAA tournament game against Kansas in Wichita (DU lost, 73-71; the game was John’s last at DU), and
    • the magnificent three-year run at DU (1969, 1970, 1971) when the team went 69-20, and
    • the start of a turnaround at Iowa State the moment John arrived in Ames.

    John was named the United States Basketball Writers Association (USBWA) Coach of the Year in 1969 while taking Drake to the Final Four. He was chosen for the MVC Hall of Fame in 2000.

    John’s death drew scant attention in Sports Illustrated. It said simply that he was “a former national basketball coach of the year.”

    No coach has matched his winning percentage at Drake (. 617), and no Drake coach has been to the NCAA Final Four since he was (1969). No Drake coach has been to the NCAA tournament three years in a row like John was (1969, 1970, 1971), either.

    [edit] ANACS

    ANACS was created by the American Numismatic Association (ANA)to correct major problems facing the coin collecting hobby, namely counterfeit coins. The ANA is a not-for-profit educational association for collectors of coins, tokens, medals and paper money. The ANA is chartered by the U.S. Congress. In the 1970's, the ANA board of governors saw action was needed to remedy the confusion created by the counterfeit and altered coins that were flooding the coin collecting industry. Their solution was ANACS, the American Numismatic Association Certification Service. ANACS began with only two experts located in Washington D.C. Washington was chosen to be nearby to use the Smithsonian’s Numismatic Collection and the services of the Treasury Department. ANACS certified its first coin as genuine on June 15, 1972. As the need for its services grew additional experts were added to ANACS staff.

    In 1976, ANACS was moved to Colorado Springs where the ANA had its headquarters. Three years later, ANACS began grading coins using the book “The Official American Numismatic Association Grading Standards for United States Coins,” that the ANACS staff helped to create. Coins were given a technical grade for each side, "the obverse" and "the reverse". The service became very successful and within a few more years ANACS was grading nearly 3,000 coins a month. Today the ANACS grades about that many coins in a single day.

    [edit] Ellesmerocerida /Cephalopoda

    Ellesmerocerida comprise the ancestral nautiloid stock from which all other cephalopod orders were derived. All Cambrian and lower Canadian (Early Ordovician) nautiloids as well as many upper Canadian representatives are included.

    Ellesmerocerids exhibit wide morphologic variation. Primitive forms are minute cyrtocones with closely spaced septa and a marginal siphuncle bearing sort septal necks. Overall ellesmerocerids are characterized by moderate endogastric curvature, and a marginal siphuncle with irregular diaphragms, ellipochoanitic septal necks, and thick complex connecting rings. Variations include annulate orthoconic longicones, exogastric breviconic cyrtocones, forms with thin connecting rings, and those with subcentral siphuncle.

    Ellesmerocerida have been divided into three suborders, Plectronoceratina, Ellesmeroceratina, and Cyrtocerinina. Plectronoceratina form the ancestral stock which originated in the Late Cambrian. Only two families are known. Ellesmeroceratina form the bulk of the order with six families, three of which, the Ellesmeroceratidae, Baltoceratidae ,and Protocycloceratidae are major. Only three families, each represented by a single genus, are assigned to the Cyrtocerinina.

    Plectronoceratina consist of forms in which siphuncle material is expanded into the chambers where unbounded by septal necks, forming siphuncular bulbs. Siphuncles are fragile and poorly preserved, with rings composed mostly of organic material. Transverse partitions in the siphuncle known as diaphragms are irregular in form and erratic in distribution where found. Septa are close spaced, the siphuncle is small and ventrally located. Two families are assigned, the Plectronoceratidae for straight to endogastrically curved forms and the Balkoceratidae for those that are exogastrically curved. Plectronoceratina originated in the Late Cambrian and form the ancestral stock not only of the Ellesmerocerida, but of the entire cephalopoda.

    Ellesmeroceratina consist of ellesmerocerids in which the connecting rings are well calcified, are appreciably thickened, and may show layering. The siphuncle is ventral, diaphragms are rather common. The predominant and core family, the Ellesmeroceratidae, consists of laterally compressed shells with short phragmocones and rapidly expanding apices. They may be straight or endogastric.

    Cyrtocerinina are ellesmerocerids with well calcified connecting rings that extend as bulbs into the siphuncle cavity. Necks may be short or long. The three recognized genera are quite distinct from one another and are each assigned to its own family.

    Septa, dividing the chambers in ellesmerocerids, are generally closely spaced. In Plectronoceratina as many as 35 may occur in a distance equal to the corresponding diameter of the conch. The opposite extreme is encountered in some baltoceratids were the cameral interval approximates the diameter of the conch. Ellesmerocerid siphuncles range in size from one-half to one-fifteenth the corresponding conch diameter. Forms in which the siphuncle occupies one-third to one-tenth the conch diameter predominate. An exception to the usual ventral position is found in the Shideleroceratidae, in which the siphuncle is central or subcentral. However the inclusion of this family in the Ellesmerocerida is of doubtful validity.

    Five major nautiloid orders are derived from Ellesmerocerida. The Endocerida come most likely from within the family Endoceratidae with the lost of siphuncular diaphragms and the addition instead of endocones. Actinocerida came from the Cyrtocerinina, most likely from Bathmoceras, with further development of endosiphuncular deposits. Discosorida are though to be derived from Plectronoceratina. Michelinocerida (Orthocerida to some) are derived from the Baltoceratidae, a family of the Ellesmeroceratina. The fifth, Tarphycerida are thought to come from Bassleroceratidae, which are derived from Ellesmeroceratidae. Whether basslerocerids should be included in Tarphycerida or Ellesmerocerida (Ellesmeroceratina ) is debatable. Where the Barrandeocerida and Oncocerida fit with respect to Tarphycerida is yet uncertain although Tarphycerida seem to be the root stock. Oncocerida is considered the root stock of serious or orders leading to modern Nautilus . Thus we can trace Nautilus back to the Ellesmerocerida in the Early Ordovician.

    The Michelinocerida gave rise to a completely different sort of cephalopod, first to the Bactritida and Ammonoidea, and ultimately to modern coleoids – cuttlefish, squid , and octopus.

    Finally, the Endocerida, Actinocerida, and Discosorida are dead end groups, with no derived orders.

    Although the Ellesmerocerida are confined to the Ordovician Period, their progeny are still with us.

    [edit] ion chamber

    a radiation sensor consisting of a set of or multiple parallel plates that are placed in the path of a radiation beam. This sensor is commonly used in radiation therapy systems where it is necessary to monitor the delivered dose of radiation to a tumor.

    The plates, which enclose a gas, are subjected to a polarizing voltage. When a radiation beam crosses the plates, it creates ionization in the gas. The resulting ions will collide with the plates and create small currents that can be read by external amplifiers to which the plates are connected.

    [edit] fuckpaypal.net

    fuckpaypal.net is a PayPal Critical Site. The site is meant to show the problems that can happen while selling on eBay and using PayPal. It has gained a lot of attention, and has appeared in the Summer 2006 issue of [2600 Magazine].

    [edit] Beginnings

    The site is linked to the webmaster's main page, silic0nsilence.com.[76] The webmaster began the page in April 2006, after purchasing the fuckpaypal.net domain. The webmaster, who calls himself 'silic0nsilence', started the page after a buyer scammed him during a sale, and PayPal offered no assistance to gain back his money or property.

    [edit] The Story

    In October 2005, 'silic0nsilence' put a laptop up for bids on eBay. He had excellent feedback, and had sold in the past. The winning bid went to 'RH'. He uses this name to protect the buyer. After the buyer sent the payment via eBay's PayPal payment system, silic0nsilence shipped the laptop. When the buyer received it, he praised the seller. About a week later, the buyer placed a chargeback on his credit card stating the item was not as described. PayPal allowed the chargeback even though a return policy was not stated. The seller attempted contacting RH several times, with no luck. The seller was out $615, and the laptop. He contacted PayPal through email and their hard-to-find telephone number. He was continously told to 'wait 75 days' so that PayPal could attempt to get the funds back. After 75 days, he contacted PayPal again, only to have them tell him to 'wait 75 days.' Fed up, he contacted the Better Business Bureau.

    [edit] Fighting Back

    In April 2006, the website was online. silic0nsilence began telling his story to the world, and offered PDF documents and names as evidence. A picture of him is shown holding a sign that says, "FUCK PAYPAL." Imposed on the picture are the words, "They may win.. But I'm going down swinging'" Under the image, the words "Justice will be served is displayed." The page then tells his story, and offers several updates, the first being added on April 7th, 2006. Around this time, he was contacted by NCO Financial Systems, a company PayPal hired to obtain the money. Frustrated and getting no answers from PayPal, he decided to sue the company.

    [edit] silic0nsilence v. PayPal

    In May 2006, silic0nsilence filed a Small Claims suit in Clarkstown Justice Court. The trial date was June 13th, 2006. 5 days before the trial, PayPal's Senior Litigation Paralegalist Michelle Squires contacted silic0nsilence. She argued that PayPal did attempt to get the property back, but failed. She also told him that a clause in the PayPal User Agreement stated that he would have to file a claim, and travel to California where PayPal was located to dispute and problem. She threatened that if he went on with the suit, their lawyer would file a 'Motion To Compel', which would halt the trial, and force silic0nsilence to pay PayPal's lawyer fees. 20 years old, and threatened by PayPal, he agreed to settle as long as PayPal gave him information about the buyer so that he could commence in a suit against him. They complied and did so.

    [edit] Swarm of Attention

    In July 2006, silic0nsilence's article 'Getting Screwed by PayPal' was published in 2600 Magazine. This brought much attention and support to him and his site. Since receiving the buyer's information, he attempted contacting him several times with no luck. It was time to put an end to the frustration after almost a year, and file a suit with the buyer. silic0nsilence was contacted by The People's Court to show his case on television. He agreed to show the threat of PayPal fraud on national television. However, RH declined. The trial's set date was August 15th, 2006.

    [edit] Victory

    On August 15th, 2006, Clarkstown Justice Court Judge Flick awarded silic0nsilence default judgement against RH after he did not show in the amount of $671.12. This was the money silic0nsilence asked for, along with interest and court fees.

    [edit] What's Next

    silic0nsilence now has to wait for his money. If not paid, RH's wages can be garnished. After he is paid, he plans on paying back PayPal, and finally being able to close his PayPal account.
