[edit] VOC ship Rooswijk

The Rooswijk was a ship belonging to the VOC (Dutch East India Company) that sank in 1740.

[edit] History

The Rooswijk was built for the VOC "Chamber of Amsterdam" in 1737. On January 9 1740, during its second journey to the east, it sank on the sand bank of Goodwin Sands, about 8 km from the British mainland. There were no known survivors. At the time, it was captained by Daniel Ronzieres.

[edit] Recovery

In December 2005, it was made public that between May and September of that year a team led by Rex Cowan had recovered some of the ship and its contents. This was done in secrecy to avoid attracting looters. More work is planned for the 2006 diving season.

Lying at a depth of about 24 m, the Rooswijk had been discovered by an amateur diver in 2004. It is one of hundreds of wrecks on the treacherous Goodwin Sands.

Artifacts recovered included approximately one thousand bars of silver, gold coins and a mustard jar. When the VOC was disbanded in 1798, its possessions fell to the Batavian Republic, the legal successor of which is the current Dutch State, which therefore is entitled to the objects from the Rooswijk. They were presented to Junior minister of Finance Joop Wijn in Plymouth on December 11 2005.

[edit] Nicea-Constantinople Creed

The Nicea-Constatninope Creed, is the final revised version of the Nicean Creed used today by both the Catholic Churches and the Orthodox Churches. It was approved of by the Church at the Council of Chalcedon where it became the official Profession of Faith. Revisions, such as the addition to of the "Filioque" at the local council of Toledo III, would lead to the East West Schism of 1054. For more information on this creed research the first seven Ecumenical Councils, Nicea I through Constantinople II, also see Nicene Creed.

[edit] Rodney "Gypsy" Smith

Rodney (Gipsy) Smith, 1860-1947, British evangelist. He conducted evangelistic campaigns throughout the world but most notably in the United States and Great Britain for over 70 years. He was a contemporary of other admired Christians of the time such as Fanny Crosby and G. Campbell Morgan.

Rodney was born in a gypsy tent at Epping Forest just outside of London. The family made a living selling baskets, tinware and clothespegs. This was common practise for traveling gypsy families of the time. His father, Cornelius, and his mother, Mary Welch, provided a happy home in the gypsy wagon. Rodney's mother died from smallpox when he was child. The Smith children numbered four girls and two boys of which Rodney was the fourth in line. Although as an adult he was quite literate and known as a brilliant communicator, as a child he received no formal education but was self taught as a teenager.

Rodney's father was in and out of jail for various offenses. During one of these incarcerations he heard the gospel from a prison chaplain. Later Mr. Smith, along with his brothers were converted at a mission meeting. From 1873 on, "The Converted Gypsies" were involved in numerous evangelistic efforts.

Rodney's conversion came as a sixteen-year-old as a result of a combination of things. The witness of his father, hearing Ira Sankey sing, and a visit to the home of John Bunyan in Bedford all contributed. He taught himself to read and write and began to practice preaching. He would sing hymns to the people he met and was known as the singing gypsy boy.

At a convention at the Christian Mission (later called the Salvation Army) headquarters in London, William Booth noticed the gypsies but especially Rodney. On June 25, 1877, young Rodney accepted the invitation of Booth to be an evangelist with and for the Mission. For six years (1877-1882), he served on street corners and mission halls.

Gipsy was married on December 17, 1879 to Annie E. Pennock, one of his converts. They served in several assignments and saw memberships rise to hundreds, then a thousand. By June 1882, great crowds were coming and the work was growing. A gold watch was given him and about £20.00 was presented to his wife by the warm-hearted members of a local congregation. Acceptance of these gifts was a breach of the rules and regulations of the Salvation Army, and for this, he was dismissed from the Army. His eight assignments with the Salvation Army had produced 23,000 decisions and his crowds were anywhere up to 1,500.

He began to traveled extensively around the world on evagelistic crusades, drawing crowds numbering in the hundreds of thousands throughout his life. Busy as he was, he never grew tired of visiting gypsy encampments whenever he could on both sides of the Atlantic. Gipsy never wrote a sermon out for preaching purposes. Smith wrote several books and could sing as well as he preached. Sometimes he would interrupt his sermon and burst into song. Although he was a Methodist, ministers of all denominations loved him. It is said that he never had a meeting without conversions.

Duing World War I he ministered under the auspices of the Y.M.C.A. to the English troops in France, often visiting the front lines. As a result of this, King George VI made him a member of the Order of the British Empire.

Gipsy's wife, Annie, died in 1937 at the age of 79 while he was in America. Front page headlines in 1938 carried the news of the 78-year-old widower marrying Mary Alice Shaw on her 27th birthday. This, of course, brought some criticism. But it was a good marriage, for she helped him in his meetings, sang, did secretarial work, and later nursed him when his health failed.

Stricken by a heart attack, Gipsy died on the RMS Queen Mary in America, age 87. It was estimated that this was his 45th crossing of the Atlantic. His funeral was held August 8, 1947 in New York City. A memorial with a plaque was unveiled on July 2, 1949 at Mill Plain, Epping Forest, England, his birthplace. So ends the life of one who once said, "I didn't go through your colleges and seminaries. They wouldn't have me...but I have been to the feet of Jesus were the only true scholarship is learned."

[edit] Portaboat

Portaboat is a boat that folds to 4 inches (100 mm) flat. It comes in four lengths of hull length 8 feet 6 inches (2.85 meters), 10 feet 8 inches (3.27 meters), 12 feet 6 inches (3.85 meters) 14 feet (4.28 meters), and hull weight 47 pounds (21 kilograms), 58 pounds (26 kilograms), 69 pounds (31 kilograms), and 96 pounds (43.6 kilograms).

The Portaboat was originally invented in 1969 and has undergone four different generations of models and sizes.

It became popular in the last 1980s and 1990s when many boating and fishing enthusiasts started downsizing to condominiums and apartments. There was no storage room for an ordinary boat.

It is also known as Foldaboat, Foldboat, Foldingboat, and Portabote.
