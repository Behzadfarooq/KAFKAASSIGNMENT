[edit] Vic garth

Vic Garth was born September 9th 1902 and was a much loved citizen of Hobart, Tasmania. He is known for his service to the city as its Town Crier for over 20 years, until his death in April 2005 at the age of 92. Though battling with cancer for several years he continued to greet cruise ships as they arrived, and frequently appeared at Hobarts Salamanca Market on Saturdays. He was considered a great ambassador for Hobart, and was described as a very friendly and outgoing man. In 2003 he became known as the oldest town crier in the world.

[edit] Mt Pisgah (Oregon, USA)

Mt Pisgah is a hill in Lane County, Oregon, along the Willamette River immediately south of the city of Springfield and a few miles east of the city of Eugene. It hosts the Howard Buford Recreation Area, managed by Lane County, and the non-profit Mount Pisgah Arboretum sits at its base.

Mt. Pisgah rises over 1000 feet from the Willamette River to its summit at 1531 feet. The summit is accessible by a 1.4 mile trail from a parking area near the base of the hill. Several other trails wind through the adjacent arboretum and up the slopes. The summit offers a panoramic view of the Southern Willamette Valley and includes a bronze relief sculpture of the surrounding topography identifying many nearby geographic features.

Geology The hill consists of basalt or diabase and resembles many other hills throughout the Southern Willamette Valley. Small crystals of calcite and various zeolite minerals are frequently found where rock outcrops, especially near the summit.

An adjacent butte has been exploited as a gravel quarry.

[edit] Kananook 3198

The small beachside suburb of Kananook is located within the larger suburb of Seaford on the Mornington Peninsula, approximately 38km south of the Melbourne CBD. Its boundaries are comprised of to the west by the Seaford foreshore beach and Nepean highway, to the north by Seaford road, to the east by Wells road and south by Overton road. The suburb contains its own railway station, primary school, football club and reserve, tennis club and courts, retirement village, canoe ramp and the Kananook Creek Nature Reserve with several walking tracks. It is approximately 10 minutes walk to Seaford village shops to the north and about 20 minutes walk to the Frankston CBD to the south. As the demand for coastal living in Melbourne has gained in premuim over the last few years, property prices have risen sharply in the suburb of Kananook. The local city council is Frankston and the combined population of Kananook and Seaford in 2006 was approximately 16000 people with Kananook residents making up less than an estimated third of the total inhabitants.

[edit] Polytechnic School [GDR- East Germany]

The East German equivalent of the primary school and high school was the ten-year polytechnical school, for children six to sixteen. It was a very progressive school and students went to school six days a week. On Saturdays there were around four hours of lessons.

Polytechnical schools focused strongly on science, mathematics, geography, astronomy, chemistry, sport as well as practical work [Werken- works or PA = Produktive Arbeit- productive work], and until the tenth year there was little opportunity to deviate from either of these. The East German government considered these subjects to be of key importance to a fast growing, industrial country like East Germany and to give the young people of East Germany the best key and preparation for post-seondary education.

Special emphasis was also placed on learning Russian, as the GDR was a satellite state of the Soviet Union. Also available [fakultativ] were English and French. A majority of students had little motivation for Russian, and achievements were generally poor; few students reached fluency. In spite of the state-proclaimed friendship with the USSR, there were few opportunities for student exchanges and for experiencing the pleasure of communicating in the other's language. Appreciated by parents were the so-called head notes like behavior, industriousness, order, cooperation combined with a short teacher's essay about the student's character, success or progress often from a socialistic point of view.

From the seventh year onwards, students visited a factory, a power house or a farm one day per week, depending on their location. At either of these places, the student would work alongside regular employees.

There were annual championships on various subjects with the winners receiving prizes. The Russian language and mathematics championships were very prestigious and competitive as well as regular championchips in sport, called Spartakiade [from the word Spartacus ].

[edit] Academy of the Holy Names, Tampa, FL Founded 1881

The Academy of the Holy Names is a Catholic, independent co-educational elementary school and a college preparatory school for young women, sponsored by the Sisters of the Holy Names of Jesus and Mary. It is the oldest school in the Tampa Bay area and has operated for the past 126 years


History of AHN

On July 17, 1881, two Sisters of the Holy Names of Jesus and Mary arrived from Key West to open a two-room school house in a blacksmith shop on Zack Street in Tampa. Thirty-five pupils were enrolled initially, but by the end of the first academic year, their numbers had increased to 70 day scholars and two resident students.

By April 1889, the site of the school was moved to a two-story building at the corner of Franklin and Harrison Streets.

In 1891, the late Bishop John Moore, second Bishop of St. Augustine, purchased property on Twiggs Street where a larger school, which served the community for 34 years, was built.

By 1926, further growth necessitated larger accommodations, and the school was moved to temporary quarters on Central Avenue while the present building on Bayshore Boulevard was under construction. Bishop Barry, Bishop of St. Augustine, laid the cornerstone in 1928, and Florida's Governor-elect Carlton spoke at the ceremony.

The following September saw the opening of the new school, but financial difficulties halted building progress before the structure was completed. However, with increasing enrollment, growth of the school continued; the building was completed; and by 1952, two new school wings were added, along with the chapel and auditorium.

A separate boys division for grades one through eight was established in 1962, and in 1970 a kindergarten was added. Today the staff of Sisters and their lay associates instruct over 870 students in the coeducational elementary division and the high school for young women.

The school's centenary was celebrated during the 1980-81 school year. At that time an historic marker was unveiled in front of the school on Bayshore Boulevard to commemorate 100 years of service of the Sisters of the Holy Names on Florida's West Coast.

[edit] German Christmas Traditions

One of the most famous German Christmas traditions is the Christmas tree, or Weihnachtsbaum. The word tannenbaum means fir tree in German, and because a fir tree is the most common and most traditional Christmas tree, the words have become synonymous. In Germany, the traditions associated with the tree vary somewhat from American traditions. In Germany, the family does not decorate the tree together. It is usually done by one parent, most often the mother, while the father occupies the children. In fact, the children are not even allowed to see the tree until midday on Christmas day. The tree is decorated with candy, tinsel, lights, and special glass ornaments, each of which represent something. Some examples are a rabbit, which stands for hope, and a teapot, which stands for hospitality. These ornaments (there are twelve in total) are a traditional wedding gift to German couples. Glass ornaments came from Germany, and are still a major export of the country. The Christmas tree is believed to have originated in Germany, and has Pagen origins (Robson). According to Robson, the Germanic tribes in Germany worshipped the fir tree, because while all the other trees and plants seemed to die during the winter, the fir tree stayed green. They would cut off boughs and hang them in and around their houses to ward off evil spirits. In an effort to Christianize the Pagans, missionaries used the tree as a symbol for Christmas. The first Christmas trees were undecorated, and the tradition of putting lights or candles on the tree had been credited to Martin Luther, around 1500. The story goes that one Christmas Eve he was out walking, and he was struck by the beauty of a small fir tree, covered in snow and glistening in the moon light. When he got home, he set up a small fir tree in his house to share the story with his family, and decorated it with candles, which he lit the candles in honor of Christ‚Äôs birth (Robson). Another familiar Christmas tradition that is observed in Germany is Santa Claus, also known as Weihnactsmann or Christkindl, depending on where you come from. In Germany, Santa is not thought to be a ‚Äújolly old elf,‚Äù but the Christ Child. German children write him a letter, but not to ask him for gifts; the meaning is more religious. Children also get a visit from Saint Nikolaus on December sixth, his feast day. He leaves a gift for each child in a shoe or on a plate (Porter), unless they have been bad. In that case, his partner, Knecht Ruprecht, will leave twigs or coal in the shoes. Gifts from family members are exchanged on Christmas Eve, instead of Christmas Day, due to an old concept of the next day beginning at sunset instead of at midnight. The period leading up to Christmas is Advent. In Germany, both Advent wreathes and Advent calendars are common. Many children have day by day Advent calendars, with little bits of chocolate for each day. --205.188.116.72 21:24, 27 July 2006 (UTC)The idea of Advent wreathes is another tradition thought to have originated in Germany. The German Advent wreathe has five candles, usually four red and one white. One red candle is lit for each Sunday in Advent, and the white candle is lit on Christmas day. It is a great honor to light the candles, and when a child is allowed to do it, it is a sign of their maturity or good behavior. Food is an important part of the German Christmas celebration. As in the United States, a family meal is eaten on Christmas Eve and Christmas Day. Some traditional foods eaten by the Germans for Christmas dinner are some kind of pork as opposed to the ‚ÄúChristmas goose,‚Äù a cake called Stollen, which has lots of fruits in it, and, of course, beer or cider. A shared tradition in both the United States and Germany is the making of gingerbread houses, called Lebkuchenhaus. German gingerbread houses tend to be more ornate then American ones, with people spending entire days making them, but the ideas are the same. Germans are also big on Christmas cookies, and gingerbread is actually a German food. Cookies in Christmas shapes, such as trees and angels, are the most popular. Many Christmas traditions that people consider ‚ÄúAmerican‚Äù actually originated in Germany. Christmas trees, Advent wreathes and calendars, and many foods are all an important part of the German Christmas celebration.

Adult Basic Education / English for Speakers of Languages Other Than English (ABE/ESOL) specialists study and design interventions to help people develop reading, writing, and conversation skills and increasingly infuse curricula with health information to promote better health literacy. A range of approaches to adult education brings health literacy skills to people in traditional classroom settings, as well as where they work and live. (See the links at WorldEd http://www.worlded.org/us/health/lincs, and ‚ÄúA Selection of Health Literacy Articles and Research‚Äù published by Partnership for Clear Health Communication: http://www.askme3.org/pdfs/bibliography.pdf.)

The biomedical and psychological approach to health literacy dominant in the 1980s and 1990s often depicted individuals as lacking, or ‚Äúsuffering‚Äù from, low health literacy, assumed that recipients are passive in their possession and reception of health literacy, and believed that models of literacy and health literacy are politically neutral and universally applicable. This approach is found lacking when placed in the context of broader ecological approaches to health.

A more robust view of health literacy includes the ability to understand scientific concepts, content, and health research; skills in spoken, written, and online communication; critical interpretation of mass media messages; navigating complex systems of health care and governance; and knowledge and use of community capital and resources, as well as using cultural and indigenous knowledge in health decision making (Nutbeam, 2000; Ratzan, 2001; Zarcadoolas, Pleasant, & Greer, 2002).

This perspective defines health literacy as the wide range of skills, and competencies that people develop over their lifetimes to seek out, comprehend, evaluate, and use health information and concepts to make informed choices, reduce health risks, and increase quality of life (Zarcadoolas, Pleasant, & Greer, 2006).

Defining health literacy in that manner builds the foundation for a multi-dimensional model of health literacy built around four central domains:

    fundamental literacy, 
    scientific literacy, 
    civic literacy, and 
    cultural literacy.

Skills in one domain can contribute to developing literacy skill in another domain, and competencies in one area can compensate for a lack of competencies in another.

Finally, it must be stressed that health literacy skills are not only a problem of the public. Health care professionals (doctors, nurses, public health professionals) can also have poor health literacy skills; most often captured by a reduced ability to clearly explain health issues to patients and the public.
