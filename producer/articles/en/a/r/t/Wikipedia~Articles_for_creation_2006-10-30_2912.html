[edit] Spudd

Spudd is one of several informal "street" games that were popular among children during informal gatherings prior to the dawn of the Internet Era. It's exact origins are difficult to verify, but it seems to trace it's roots to Long Island in the early 1980s.

The game requires at least 3 players, an ample vertical space, such as a typical street block or basketball court, and a large, round, bouncy ball. A dodge ball, basketball or volleyball would be acceptable. Play begins with one of the players randomly declared to be "it". This player has no formal name, but for simplicity of explanation he or she will be referred to as the chooser. The chooser takes possession of the ball and selects a topic. Topics may be simplistic ("colors", "letters of the alphabet", "boys' names") or as complicated and personal as the group desires ("TV shows that Andrew wouldn't watch", "digit patterns of pi"). The remaining players then seperate from the chooser and each select a unit related to the topic.

Example: Andrew, Josh, Tony and Jerry are playing Spudd. Jerry is selected as the chooser, and he chooses the topic, "colors". Josh, Tony and Andrew move off to a corner and whisper their selections to each other - Andrew chooses "green", Tony chooses "yellow" and Josh chooses "white".

Once the players have made their choices, they return to the chooser and stand in a line in front of him. One of them calls out the choices, but does not do or say anything to indicate which choice belongs to which player. The chooser then performs a slight sing-song ritual, bouncing the ball and saying in rythym, "I declare war on the person who picked..." He finishes by yelling out one of the choices and throwing the ball high into the air. All participants then run away as quickly as they can, while the player whose choice was yelled out runs to catch the ball and yell out "SPUDD!" Once Spudd is yelled out, the running players stop and freeze in position.

Example: Andrew, Tony and Josh form a line in front of Jerry. Andrew tells Jerry, "The choices are green, yellow and white." Jerry takes a moment to decide which he is going to yell. He then bounces the ball between his hands, singing, "I declare war on the person who picked.... yellow!" When he says yellow, Jerry throws the ball into the air and starts running forward, towards where Tony, Andrew and Josh were standing. Josh and Andrew turn and also run in the same direction as Jerry. Tony, who had chosen yellow, runs forward and catches the ball. Once he has it, he yells, "SPUDD!" Jerry, Andrew and Josh all freeze in place.

Finally, the person who caught the ball will begin to approach the others. He is allowed 7 steps, each of which he will take while speaking the ritual words, "S-P-U-D-D Spells Spudd." He then gets two bunny hops, which he counts out as he jumps: "One, two." At this point, assuming he didn't have any problems catching the ball, he should be fairly close to one or more of the other players. At this point, his objective is to hit one of the other players with the ball. The player he is aiming for may attempt to dodge the ball, so long as one foot remains firmly planted in the spot where he was stopped. Alternatively, the player being aimed for may attempt to catch the ball. If the target player succeeds in dodging or catching the ball, or the throwing player misses the target completely, then the throwing player is penalized with a letter, and becomes the new chooser. If the throwing player succeeds in hitting his target, then the target player is penalized with a letter and becomes the new chooser. In either case, the new chooser selects a new topic, and the process is repeated from the beginning.

Example: Tony decides that Andrew is going to be his target, so he takes 7 steps towards Andrew and two bunny hops, repeating the mantra as he moves. "S." Step. "P." Step. "U." Step. "D." Step. "D." Step. "Spells." Step. "Spudd." Step. "One." Bunny hop. "Two." Bunny hop. This places him roughly three feet from Andrew, making it unlikely that Andrew will be able to dodge. Tony throws the ball at Andrew, and Andrew decides to try to catch it. However, Tony's throw is very hard and Andrew finds himself unable to hold on to the ball - it slips out of his hands and hits the ground. Andrew has been successfully hit, and retrieves the ball to become the next chooser. He now has a letter: S. He chooses a new topic, "Words that Begin with Q." The others form a huddle briefly, then return to Andrew and announce the choices: quick, quiet and quirk. Andrew throws up the ball while yelling quirk, which turns out to be Josh's word. Josh catches the ball, yells "Spudd", and then decides to approach Jerry. Jerry decides to dodge the ball, and succeeds in shifting his body to the side as the ball flies past him. Josh now has a letter S, and he becomes the chooser for the third round.

The letter penalties that each player gets from round to round spell out the name of the game - SPUDD. Once a player has acquired all 5 letters, he is either removed from the game or some alternative punishment is arranged (one of the more popular ones among the street children in the 80s was to put the player through the "paddywagon", where all of the other players would form a line and spread their legs wide apart, and the losing player would crawl through the tunnel and be slapped in the rear as he went through. Naturally, this form of physical punishment is unlikely to be condoned in a more formal setting). A player put through an alternative punishment usually has his letter penalties reset afterward, which allows the game to continue indefinitely or until the players tire of it. If the players are being removed from the game, then the game ends when there are only two players remaining and the player with the fewest letters is declared the winner.

Example: After 13 rounds of play, Andrew and Jerry both have SPUD, Tony has SPU, and Josh has S. On the fourteenth round, Andrew completely misses Tony with the ball, and winds up obtaining his final D. He is removed from play, randomly selecting Josh to replace him as the chooser (Josh is not penalized a letter for this). Josh picks the topic, "Animals", and Jerry and Tony huddle up. When they return to Josh, there are only two choices for him - "goat", selected by Jerry, and "Andrew", selected by Tony in order to make a playful joke at their friend's expense. Josh selects "Andrew" and throws up the ball, which Tony easily catches. Tony approaches Jerry and hits him with the ball, giving Jerry his final D. There are now only two players left, making it impossible to continue - the game ends, and Josh is the winner because he has the fewest letters.

[edit] Misattribution

Misattribution is a phenomenon where people attribute their current emotion based on a stimulus that does not actually cause that emotion. For example, rainy days have been linked to fouler moods, but an individual may attribute it to receiving a C on an exam.

    Michael O'Briant is an artist living and working in Chicago, Illinois.

    The paintings of Michael O’Briant reflect his desire to keep alive the tradition of the figurative narrative. His images are rooted in the timelessness of black and white photography. By combining figures from both found and original photographs, O’Briant is able to create his own unique compositions. These manufactured groupings reflect the solitude often encountered in crowds, while the individuals represent through their style and shape, the artist’s view of our times.

    O’Briant uses a technique of combining acrylic paint with glue to obtain a thick medium which dries semi-transparent, allowing for an aggressive layered surface while maintaining the soft visual effect typically created with oil glazing. O’Briant paints on wood panels, as well as elaborate panels he constructs from smaller wooden blocks, as well as other materials like glass, plexiglass, and canvas.

    A native of North Carolina, O’Briant received his B.F.A from East Carolina University and his M.F.A from the University of Wisconsin, Madison. He has lived in Chicago since 1998 and is represented in many private collections in the U.S. and Europe.

    He has exhibited his work at: The Hyde Park Art Center, Tojo Gallery, The Provincetown Art Association Museum, The Chicago School of Professional Psychology, The Randolph Arts Guild, April and George Gallery, Caro d'Offay Gallery, among other venues.

    [edit] Herbert Ackroyd Stuart

    Herbert Ackroyd Stuart was a British engineer who took out a patent (No.7146) in 1890 for an oil fired engine which was the result of his development work on low-compression oil engines. To quote the patent:

    "first of all compressing the necessary quantity of air for the charge, and then introducing into this quantity of compressed air the necessary supply of combustible liquid, vapour or gas, to produce the explosive mixture."

    This engine was in production in 1892. Ruston & Hornsby Ltd claimed, in a 1937 advertisement “Before Dr. Diesel took out his first oil engine patent, we were making successful oil engines at our Grantham Works.”

    Controversy

    It is thought by some technology historians that H.A. Stuart should be properly regarded as the inventor of the type of engine that hitherto borne Diesel's name. Rudolph Diesel is widely credited with developing the compression ignition (CI) engine. In 1892 - two years after Stuart's patent - he took out patent no.7241 on a CI engine which ran on coal dust this was built in 1893 but exploded when it was run. In 1894 Diesel filed a patent for a CI oil engine which he successfully ran in 1897. In 1900, at the world fair in Paris, a small diesel engine was demonstrated running on peanut oil. The Otto Company provided this demonstration at the request of the French Government which wanted to use peanut oil as an energy source in its African colonies.

    Stuart, a Yorkshireman, and one Charles Richard Binney took out patents in 1890 for a compression-ignition engine. The manufacturing rights for this engine were taken over in 1891 by Richard Hornsby & Sons Ltd of Grantham and they produced the "Hornsby-Akroyd Patent Oil Engine" to the inventor's original design. These engines were actually on sale before Diesel took out his first (impractical) patent. Diesel's first practical engine was not constructed (by Adolphus Busch of St Louis) until 1897.

    [edit] Sir Algernon Methuen

    Methuen, Sir Algernon (1856–1924)

    originally Stedman


    Born Algernon Methuen Stedman in London in 1856, he was a teacher of classics and French (1880–95), and founded a publishing company under the name of Methuen & Co in 1889 to market his textbooks. Soon afterwards he also changed his name to Methuen.

    Methuen & Co had an early publishing success with Kipling's "Barrack-Room Ballads" in 1892, and also published works of Hilaire Belloc, R L Stevenson, and Oscar Wilde, as well as many novelists, including Henry James.

    Algernon Methuen was created Baronet Methuen in 1916.
