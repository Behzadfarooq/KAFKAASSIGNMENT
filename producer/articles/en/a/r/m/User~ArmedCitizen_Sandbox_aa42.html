In 1998 there were 30 shall issue states and 12 may issue states; law-abiding Missouri residents wanted to join this growing majority in the United States.

Proposition B in Missouri began some years earlier than the Brady Handgun Violence Prevention Act by those within Missouri that were law-abiding citizens that perceived a need for self-protection. Some advise that Prop B started with the introduction of Jim Crow laws that were discriminatory in nature and widely practiced within major urban areas.[1]

Actually, a small portion of Jackson County started a petition to have the issue placed on a ballot for Missouri to consider. Another effort begins with hiring a 'professional lobbyist' to work in Jefferson City to introduce a bill. They soon discovered each other in 1991 and "the conference" was formed. That nickname was used until the Missouri Legislative Issues Council (MOLIC) was formed in 1995 for official recognition. In May 1998, there was enough support in the Missouri General Assembly to pass HB1891 with the House voting 123-33, and the Senate 26-8. Governor Mel Carnahan threatened veto and sought another solution. He offered to remain neutral and placed an offer to abide by the voice of the people in Missouri. This would be the first time in the Nation that such a decision would be put to the voter citizenry.[2]

[edit] MOLIC & Supporters

After author/columnist John Ross made his attempts to convince Missouri legislators, out came others as allies throughout Missouri. The NRA formed Missourians Against Crime(MAC) in 1998 as their spokesman into Missouri. This created an issue with the MOLIC membership. Negative campaigns seldom succeed in Missouri. The MOLIC organization was transformed into Missourians for Personal Safety(MPS) in 1998 for the 1999 Proposition B[3] campaign with Steve McGhee as the President. These combined efforts brought notoriety to Greg Jeffery, attorney/author Kevin Jamison, Tim Oliver, to the forefront of their regional news media. As the Missouri Sport Shooting Association (MSSA), joined (unofficially) with this vision, they all proceeded with their regional campaigning attempts. Eventually, individuals heard of these volunteers/members and took up the task upon themselves to fight the Gun Control advocates with smaller meetings and neighborhood 'grass roots' action groups.

"In a recent poll, more than eighty-five percent of our 1352 members favored Right-to-Carry.", Letter to St. Louis Police Chief Ron Henderson, from Sgt. John J. Johnson, President St. Louis Police Officers Association, 1/23/99. [4]


Later, The Gateway Civil Liberties Alliance (GCLA) gathered after the failure to pass the proposition in 1999; forming itself from MOLIC, MPS, and with WMSA giving support, to become the ‘team’ in leadership and the legislative clearing house for the next attempt. By not accepting failure and the endless trips to Jefferson City by Greg Jeffery (GCLA cofounder) earned him the nickname ‘braintrust’ to a newly created law that was "adopted" by Missouri in 2003.[5] There are many individuals that claim to be responsible for Missouri having the rights of self-protection. Those listed here have been selected as being in the forefront during the 1991-2003 political battle in Missouri.

[edit] HCI & Opponents

Handgun Control, Inc. (HCI), saw this as their ‘test case’ before the elections in 2000 to exercise their political influence [6] and even managed to use federal funding to help defeat this 'forced' referendum ballot. Political notables included the Clinton/Gore Administration[7] with Attorney General Janet Reno playing a secondary role to Hillary Clinton's activism, Governor Mel Carnahan and his daughter Robin Carnahan, with the 1994 appointed[8] Secretary of State, Rebecca Cook [9], and State Auditor, Claire McCaskill [10].

Robin Carnahan chaired the Safe Schools and Workplaces Committee (SSWC) and orchestrated television ads with amazing results (see Media Campaign below). Also, on the weekend prior to voting day, coordinated the taped phone message from Hillary Clinton to automatically dial 75,000 homes statewide with the message, "just too dangerous for Missouri families."[11]

[edit] Dilemma

“ Handgun Control, Inc. has a right to participate in the political process and to advance laws based on the belief that civilians should not have guns for defensive purposes. The gun control debate would be more productive, however, if HCI's moral intuition were not subsumed to the implausible claim that the very laws which HCI considers perfect for determining who may buy a gun suddenly become hopelessly flawed when used to determine who may carry a gun.[12] ”

[edit] Battle in Missouri

When the United States Justice Department started spending Federal funds to persuade law-enforcement supervisors and with toll-free phone messages to give misdirection to Missouri voters with political disinformation.[13]

"Even more offensive was the use of your tax dollars to lobby against Prop. B! [14]. Officials in Janet Reno's Justice Department, Missouri's two U.S. Attorneys, Edward Dowd and Stephen Hill, co-signed a letter on U.S. Department of Justice letterhead urging sheriffs and police chiefs across the state to rally resistance against Prop. B. Their letter not only listed a toll-free number in Dowd's office through which callers could receive anti-Prop. B materials, but also outrageously mischaracterized Prop B. The U.S. Attorneys went so far as to say that the measure would allow permit holders to carry firearms that were banned by the federal government in 1934!"[11]

Then came the Ballot Language to ‘educate’ voters. [15]

"Opponents used a loophole and manufactured flaw in the ballot language, and sued to have the ballot language changed to make it sound more confusing, vague and negative. The argument they used was that the ballot language contained 57 words, when such language is limited by law to 50 words. In the past, words such as 'a', 'the', or 'of' have not been counted in that total. The opponents found a judge favorable to their desires, and prevailed."[16]

[edit] Ballot Language

The ballot language that was approved by the General Assembly:

“ Shall state or local law enforcement agencies be authorized to issue permits to law-abiding citizens at least twenty-one years of age to carry concealed firearms outside their home for personal protection after having passed a state and federal criminal background check and having completed a firearms safety training course approved by the Missouri Department of Public Safety? ”

"The opposition conducted a poll of the ballot language which determined that 60% of the state would vote in favor of License to Carry. Outraged that the people might "speak wrong", the Governor's lawyer filed a suit with the Missouri Supreme Court to change the ballot language. The defendants in the suit were Attorney General Jay Nixon and State Auditor Claire McCaskill."[10]

“ Shall sheriffs, or in the case of St. Louis County, the chief of police, be required to issue permits to carry concealed firearms to citizens who apply if various statutory requirements are satisfied?" Because of the discretion given to local law enforcement to verify the accuracy of applications, the costs are uncertain. Application fees are estimated to cover most costs for the first three years. Subsequently, local governments, as a whole, may incur costs from $500,000 to $1,000,000 annually, not covered by fees. ”

[edit] Results

Passing in 110 of the 114 Counties displayed clearly that there were many in favor of Proposition B[21]. However, the administration staff of Governor Carnahan had done their polling research. [22]. The St. Louis Metro area brought enough votes to change/skew the simple majority percentages. Within the City of St. Louis; those found to be favorable precincts discovered a shortage of ballots and those precincts opposed were kept open for several hours past the closing time .[10] With that; the City managed to mass a 74.1% agreement to stop Proposition B. St. Louis County being the target of the NEW ballot language brought another 69.5% to the overall totals defeating Proposition B by 3.3% in the statewide totals[23].

1999 voting results.

The pledge of Governor Mel Carnahan's neutrality became a myth. The political ‘opponents’ managed a 1999 victory in Missouri. In 2003, the Missouri General Assembly passed new laws to enable law-abiding citizens the rights of self-protection [24].
