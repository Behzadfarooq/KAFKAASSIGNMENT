[edit] Early History

I first started using computers when I was around four years old. A very young age. I can still remember my first machine. It was a slow processor, very little memory, with an external hard drive, and one of those floppy drives that took those 5.25" discs. It ran DOS, and that was it. I learned enough so that I could quickly start it up, and get a game or something running, by myself.

That machine died of simple old age within six months or so of when I started on it. It very, very old at that time. Our new machine wasn't that much of an improvement as far as core hardware...basically, it had a 3.5" floppy in addition to the 5.25", and an internal hard drive. Wow. But the software was pwn...Windows Version 3.x. On the last year of its distribution, too. I took a great liking to the Windows system, and quickly mastered about as much as a five-year-old could.

[edit] Modernization

Of course, this machine would die soon enough, as well, when I was seven. I didn't have a new computer until 1998, two years later, at age 9. My father had purchased one of the latest Windows 98 machines. And, damn, was that thing awesome...for its times, anyway. 500 MHz AMD K6-2 processor, 128 MB RAM, 8 MB video, 19GB hard drive. However, over the years, this system degenerated. I began to encounter more and more hardware errors, and, eventually, beating the tower with my shoe, or if I was especially pissed at it, a hammer, became a regular occurrence.

Finally, at long last! The system died, in April of 2003. Four and a half years of service. Worst system I'd ever owned. But, I issued a declaration that day..."You are not going to escape me that easy, you slimy, errant son of a bitch." I sold the monitor for $35 on eBay, and shipped it halfway across the country, at a cost of $10. Of course, I charged $15 for shipping, giving me a total profit of $40. I tore apart the case, as well. I eventually discovered that the system's failure was not due to software issues, as I had thought, but rather, because of aging parts. Some electronic circuitry had finally blown, releasing large amounts of energy which fried large sections of the main board. I carefully extracted my hard drive, for safekeeping...and then I tore into that computer's remains like a vicious raptor. Most gratifying act ever.

[edit] More Recently...

I spent the entire summer without a computer, relying mostly on the public library's Intel P3 systems. They got the job done fairly well. But, when freshman year rolled around, things got more complicated. I needed a computer at home, but the parents apparently would not have the money for it for at least another month or so. So, I wait. And I wait. I visit the Dell website incessantly, and build at least fifteen different possible configurations.

What we're waiting for is a loan check from some 401k thing. To spare you the details...eventually it comes, and I'm at the library to order the system within fifteen minutes of learning that the money is in the account. Gogogo. I scout parts. I asseble. I pay. I go home and do nothing for a week or so.

Then...hey, boxes. I had it up and running in less than a half hour. I took minutes or so to put my old hard drive into the new system as a slave drive.

[edit] Currently

So, my system becomes a year old...and I decide to do it a favor...scrap it. I plan to upgrade the motherboard and processor. Mind you, as soon as you remove the motherboard, that system is no longer what it used to be, especially if it used to be a Dell. So...I buy a new motherboard, new processor, new computer case, and some cooling fans. End up spending about $400...sweet Jesus. I also end up with a new hard drive during this time, for a total of three. IDE only supports two, though. So, I tore the smallest and oldest one apart to have a look inside.

Sometime later down the line, I got a TV tuner card, which lets me watch TV, play PlayStation or other such consoles, or watch VHS tapes, on my computer.

The only problem with this system was the insufficient memory bus speed (not size)...it's running DDR266, when a computer of my sort should have at least 333, or, at best, DDR400. This problem was solved by the purchase of 1GB of Corsair low-latency PC3200/DDR400 RAM.

Later down the road, the video card became insufficient for some of the latest games, particularly Call of Duty 2. This was abated by the purchase of an nVidia 7800GTX card, top of the line, and a new PCI-e compliant motherboard.
