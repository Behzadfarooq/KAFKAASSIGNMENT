Arie Selinger

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Aryeh ("Arie") Selinger (born April 5, 1937) is a former volleyball coach from Israel, who guided the Dutch Men's Team to the silver medal at the 1992 Summer Olympics in Barcelona, Spain.

Born in Poland, Selinger and his family were separated by the Nazis in 1942. He and his mother were sent to a concentration camp and later rescued by the Americans. Through the American Red Cross, he was sent on the first boatload of immigrants to Israel.

There Selinger discovered his love of sports and his great athleticism. His quickness and jumping abilities earned him a spot on the Israeli National Volleyball Team (1954-1963), participating in the 1956 FIVB Men's World Championship in Paris.

In 1963 Selinger graduated from the Wingate Institute for Physical Education and began a career as a physical education teacher, while remaining an active player and coach. In 1967 he coached the Israeli Women's team to 8th place at the European Championship in Turkey. It was in Israel where he introduced new types of quicker offensive systems that include one-sets, crossing plays and combination attacks. These innovations brought Selinger worldwide recognition in the volleyball forum. He left Israel in 1969 to continue his studies at the University of Illinois, where he earned his B.S., M.S., and PhD degrees.

Selinger was offered the coaching position for the US Women's Team in 1975 – a team that would go on to win the silver medal in the 1984 Summer Olympics after being denied a shot at the gold in 1980 when an American boycott took the team out of competition in Moscow. The silver was the first medal won by an American Team in the Olympic volleyball competition.

Selinger has won his weight in gold, silver and bronze as a coach in games and championships worldwide, including a silver medal for the Men's Team of the Netherlands at the 1992 Olympics in Barcelona. One of the players he coached there was his son Avital, who later became a volleyball coach too.
