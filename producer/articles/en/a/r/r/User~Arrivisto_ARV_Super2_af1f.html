ARV Super2

(This page is still under development)

The ARV Super2 is a rugged all-metal side-by-side two-seater with a strutted forward-swept shoulder-wing. (Yes, a modern Bolkow Junior!) Twin control sticks, all controls are pushrod, except rudder (cable), handling very positive & nice. The shoulder-wing gives superb visbility. Wing is small (92sq.ft) so wing-loading is quite high. The fibre-glass wing-tips are said to reduce drag. Standard tyres a bit small, but fatter Trelleborgs will fit without any modification - big improvement. Flaps and trim are both manually controlled, simple and reliable. Steerable nosewheel, easy ground handling. Hand operated disc brakes do the job.

Stall is 45-52kts, Vfe 80kts, green arc 80-100kts, Vne 130kts. Approach 65kts, over-hedge at 60kts. MAUW 499kg.

PFA permit aircraft are more popular than CAA aircraft, which are stuck with the original spec: but the Hewland (a 3-cyl in-line liquid-cooled inverted 2-stroke) is arguably at least as good as its modern competitors, and is very smooth. Even so, power is a bit marginal; but the Hewland is being developed, I understand, to produce 90bhp. With engines of 80bhp or more, the ARV is transformed.

ARVs have also been succesfully fitted with the Rotax 912/912S/914 and the MidWest Rotary. The latter is excellent provided fuel injection is fitted, though the very hot exhaust does affect exhaust longevity. Fuel consumption (Midwest) is about 15 lph @85kts.

About 35 were produced at Sandown, and perhaps a dozen more have been produced first at ASL in Scotland, and then in Ohio. There is every likelyhood that the ARV will be back in production before very long under the moniker "Opus". Hopefully spares will also be plentiful again. [edit] Safety record

The ARV is a rugged aircraft of all alloy-construction, but with composite wingtips and cowlings. Aft of the cockpit bulkhead, the ARV is conventional build, with frames, longerons and stressed skin; ; but the cockpit is a monocoque pressing of superplastic alloy that gives great strength and crash protection. In the early days of the Hewland engine, there were a number of forced landings, due to gearbox failures nduced by propeller vibration. This matter was quickly sorted, although the CAA grounded the aircraft, leading to loss of confidence and the eventual closure of the Isle of Wight factory. [edit] Present day
